# Passive solar riverponic bioswale — design spec

**Interactive spec sheet (with figures): <https://claude.ai/code/artifact/37912a0e-9d47-401b-ade7-98706d386d61>**

A low-cost, tileable retrofit that turns an existing hardened **sheet-pile wall** into a
living, oxygenating, habitat-bearing edge — for the miles of channelized Chicago River reach
where dam removal, bank regrading, and riparian restoration aren't on the table. A small solar
pump doses river water in **tidal pulses** through a stacked, meandering planted terrace that
overwinters in place, while the wall's other faces carry layered wildlife habitat.

> **Honest positioning.** Where removal, bank regrading, or riparian restoration are possible,
> **those win decisively** and this shouldn't be built. This is for the hardened wall that will
> stay hard; it **complements floating wetlands** (which own the water-surface/root-mass habitat)
> and is a **distant second to real restoration**. Its genuine value is dissolved-oxygen polishing,
> distributed habitat/greening, and demonstration — **never a river-wide water-quality fix.**
> This is a concept design / research synthesis, **not a stamped engineering calculation.**

## Concept & operating mode — tidal fill-and-drain

Screened intake → PV pump lifts ~6–10 ft → **pulsed** dosing to the top row → fill-and-drain
through 3 switchbacking planted channels via notched weirs → aeration cascade → oxygenated
re-entry. Planters live in the **dry freeboard above the ice/normal-water line** (avoids the
ice-thrust load that forces floating wetlands to float).

The tidal cycle is the treatment engine: **drain = nitrification** (falling level pulls air in),
**flood = denitrification** (bed goes anoxic). Anoxia comes from the *cycle*, not recirculation.
- The **lowest channel is a weir-gated carbon cell** (biochar/coir-rich, deepened 200–300 mm) —
  **required** where nitrogen is a goal, or the bed nitrifies and then *exports* nitrate.
- Run **3–6 cycles/day** (~2 h flood / drain-to-dry); make **flood duration field-adjustable at
  the weirs** (the primary N knob); a passive dosing siphon multiplies pulses. **~70% less pump
  duty** than continuous flow. Recirculation is rejected (power/weight/winter).

## Geometry

Flow runs **along the wall length** (serpentine), dropping row to row; shelves project **≤12 in**
and meander the corrugation (+40–60% flow length & edge). Bay ~8–12 ft; 3 rows in 6–8 ft freeboard.

## Attachment — top-hung cap-saddle hanger

Hooks over the wall cap (vertical bearing + a light hook/toe couple over a long leg). **No clamp,
no penetrating waler.** ≤12 in projection keeps it light: ~209 lbf into the cap, ~85 lbf couple
(service). Bare pile tops must be capped first. **Wind uplift, not gravity, governs.**

## Mounting kit & cost

All-FRP/HDPE **click-in kit** (eliminates the galvanic couple with the steel pile); only bespoke
part is a flat-cut saddle bracket. **~$56–115/ft** (NDS channel, fiberglass strut, FG clips,
SendCutSend bracket).

## Media — reactive blend (~150 mm)

~50% biochar + ~15–20% clinoptilolite zeolite + ~20–25% pumice/LECA + coir top mat +
**iron-enhanced sand for phosphorus** (~0.9–2.9 mg P/g, river-safe) + the **carbon cell** in the
lowest row. **Reject** slag (pH 10–12), Al-based WTR (fish-toxic Al), and heavy sand/gravel/shale.
Wet blend ≈ **75–105 kg/m²** — verify against bracket capacity.

## Plants — winter-hardy, zoned

Crown-hardy to **USDA zone 3–4** (2-zone container rule): *Carex comosa*, *Schoenoplectus
tabernaemontani*, *C. stricta*, *Juncus effusus* (lower/wet); *Sedum*, *Allium cernuum*,
*C. lurida* (upper/dry). **Avoid** Pontederia, Sagittaria, tall forbs. Plant densely — vegetation
may be the dominant N pathway.

## Solar pump

6–8 L/min pulsed, ~11–12 ft head; brushless DC pump ~20–40 W; **100 W PV** (tidal cuts duty ~70%);
timer/float + dry-run cutoff; CuNi/316 wedge-wire intake. PV chosen over the Fresnel thermal pump
(15–50× more water/m², all-weather, freeze-drainable).

## Treatment & water quality — honest

| Per pass | Expectation |
|---|---|
| Dissolved oxygen | **<1–2 mg/L added** (the defensible benefit; ~0 near saturation) |
| TSS / turbidity | ~40–65% / ~15–40% |
| Metals (fresh media) | Zn 30–60%, Pb 15–45%, Cu 10–35% (finite) |
| Phosphorus | low, sorption-limited (needs iron sand) |
| Nitrogen (plain trickle) | ~0% |
| **Nitrogen (tidal + carbon cell)** | **TN ~40–60% warm-season; NH₄ ~60–90%; 0% winter** — in the treated slug, not the river |

**Scale reality:** one unit treats ~1:10,000 of river flow; even system-wide replication cannot
register against the mass flux. The only water-quality niche is a small, slow **backwater**; on the
open river this is a **per-foot habitat/DO upgrade to otherwise-dead wall** (report areal g/m²/day,
never river-wide %).

## Overwintering — stays up

Crown-in-media dieback/regrow (green-roof model); channel self-drains empty before freeze; fall
mulch cap; optional removable winter cover. Established survival ~85–95%; first winter ~15–30%
replacement. Winter N removal ≈ 0 (designed-off).

## Wildlife habitat add-ons — the full-height stack

The bioswale only uses the dry freeboard; the wall's other faces each carry cheap, bolt-on habitat
(feature set from the Wild Mile Framework Vision catalog):

- **Below water:** fish-shelter modules (shelves/cubbies/tubes/brush "reef"); densified hanging-root
  curtains; rough mussel/invertebrate substrate.
- **At the waterline:** turtle/frog **basking ramp** (impossible on a bare vertical wall).
- **Freeboard:** native bloom + a **pollinator nesting block**; leave standing stems over winter.
- **Above the cap:** **perch snag** (kingfisher/heron/cormorant); purple-martin/songbird/**bat** boxes.

Aggregated across miles of hardened wall, the honest payoff of the habitat stack is **distributed
habitat and greening — not water quality.**

## Open items

PE structural stamp (wind uplift governs; verify media density + cap geometry); one **instrumented
pilot winter**; permitting (USACE §10/§404 likely NWP 27, IEPA §401, IDNR OWR, MWRD WMO, CDOT, City
DPD, and **adjacent wall-owner consent** — the schedule driver).
