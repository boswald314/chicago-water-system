Chicago River · nature-based edge · concept design

Passive solar riverponic bioswale

A low-cost, tileable retrofit that turns an existing hardened sheet-pile wall into a living, oxygenating, habitat-bearing edge — for the miles of channelized reach where dam removal, bank regrading, and riparian restoration aren't on the table. A small solar pump doses river water in tidal pulses through a stacked, meandering planted terrace that overwinters in place, while the wall's other faces carry layered wildlife habitat. Honest mission: dissolved-oxygen polishing, habitat, and demonstration — a complement to floating wetlands and a distant second to real restoration, never a river-wide cleanup.

SEC 01

Concept & operating principle

River water is drawn through a screened intake, lifted 6–10 ft by a small PV-powered pump, and dosed in pulses to the top row; it fills and drains through a switchbacking series of shallow planted channels via notched weirs, then cascades back to the river. The planters live entirely in the dry freeboard above the ice and normal-water line — the move that lets a rigid, wall-mounted assembly avoid the ice-thrust load case that forces Wild Mile's wetlands to float.

Treatment is honest habitat-grade polishing, not municipal-scale removal. The genuinely defensible benefits are dissolved-oxygen addition at the cascade, ecological edge/habitat, and public demonstration; nitrogen removal is possible only through the tidal cycle (SEC 03), and no single unit measurably changes the river column (SEC 09). Where removal, bank regrading, or riparian restoration are possible, those win decisively — this is for the hardened wall that will stay hard, and it pairs with floating wetlands to cover such a reach better than either alone.

SEC 02

Module geometry

Flow runs back and forth along the length of the wall (elevation), dropping row to row; each shelf projects only ≤12 in off the face (section, and SEC 04). Shelves meander in/out through the corrugation (plan) to add ~40–60% flow length and habitat edge. The isometric shows one assembled module.

SEC 03

Operating mode — tidal fill-and-drain

The pump doses in pulses, not a continuous trickle: a timer/float gates flow so each channel floods, holds, then drains through its notched weirs. This tidal cycle is the treatment engine, and it earns its place on the merits:

  • Nitrogen removal without a saturated sump. The flooded/held phase goes anoxic (denitrification); the drain phase pulls air into the media (nitrification + O₂ transfer). Alternating redox does both in sequence — the standard tidal-flow-wetland mechanism — so anoxia comes from the cycle, not from recirculation. The lowest channel is a weir-gated carbon cell (biochar/coir-rich, locally deepened 200–300 mm) — required where nitrogen is a goal, or the bed nitrifies and then exports nitrate.
  • ~70% less pump duty than continuous flow — directly eases the 100 W PV budget.
  • Tune it at the weirs. 3–6 cycles/day (~2 h flood / drain-to-dry); make flood duration field-adjustable — it is the primary denitrification knob — and add a passive dosing siphon to multiply pulses without extra PV draw.
Recirculation is a separate, optional lever

Routing outflow back to a small head tank multiplies passes for more removal per unit of water, but treats less total water — a per-unit-quality vs total-volume tradeoff. The replication route (many single-pass units along the wall) favors single-pass tidal operation; the quality route favors recirculation. The scaling analysis (SEC 11) confirms single-pass is the right default — it can't move the open river, but it maximizes treated water per foot of wall.

SEC 04

Attachment — top-hung cap-saddle hanger

The assembly hooks over the wall cap: weight drops into vertical bearing on the cap, and the small overturning moment is a hook-tension/toe-bearing couple over a long front leg. No friction clamp, no penetrating waler. Narrowing the shelf to 12 in keeps the couple light. Precedent: Tractel parapet clamp (non-penetrating top+face grip), rated 1,500 lb.

SEC 06

Media — reactive blend

Revised after the deep media sweep: a light, biologically-active blend with a reactive phosphorus sorbent. Plain biochar removes P poorly (can even release it), so the iron sand is the decisive P addition.

SEC 09

Treatment & water quality — honest

Per-pass expectations (adversarially verified; most literature removal %s come from hours-to-days residence and do not transfer to a 7–15 min pass). Reference point: Wild Mile's own 90 m² mat achieved only ~6–7% local nutrient reduction, no detectable DO change, over 5 years.

The honest reframe

Genuinely good for DO polishing, habitat, and demonstration — not a measurable river-column intervention at one unit (~1:10,000 throughput vs river flow). The scaling analysis (SEC 11) confirms even system-wide replication cannot register against river flux: the only water-quality niche is a small, slow backwater. On the open river this is a per-foot habitat/DO upgrade to otherwise-dead wall — reported in areal terms (g/m²/day), never river-wide %.

SEC 10

Overwintering & operations — stays up

Never removed. Plants die back to the crown, which overwinters insulated in the media (green-roof model) and resprouts in spring. Bare hanging roots do not survive winter — crown-in-media is the survival unit.

  • Media ~150 mm; ends autumn moist-but-drained; channel self-drains empty before hard freeze. Fall coir/wood-chip mulch cap over crowns.
  • Consider a removable winter insulating cover for the exposed-crown risk (the one untested regime). Pump: drain / blow out lines.
  • Established survival ~85–95%; first winter ~15–30% replacement (heaving on new crowns).

SEC 12

Wildlife habitat add-ons

The bioswale only occupies the dry freeboard. The wall's other faces — below water, at the waterline, and above the cap — each carry cheap, bolt-on habitat, turning a dead vertical face into a full-height habitat stack. Feature set drawn from the Wild Mile Framework Vision habitat catalog; all mount to the same FRP/316 kit or a companion pole.

Why it fits the mission

The habitat stack is where this concept earns its keep: aggregated across miles of otherwise-lifeless hardened wall, the real, honest payoff is distributed habitat and greening — not water quality. First-season bird-protection push-fit fencing (BioMatrix practice) protects new plantings until established.
