# River habitat restoration on the Chicago River

Chicago spent a century engineering its rivers into a hydraulic machine — reversed, deepened,
walled in steel and concrete, and fed largely by treated wastewater. With the big water-quality
levers now in place (the [Deep Tunnel](deep-tunnel-timeline.html), effluent disinfection, and
phosphorus removal), **habitat has become the frontier**: rebuilding the aquatic and riparian
life a channelized river lost. This page maps who is doing that work, the methods they use, an
honest read on what each method can and can't accomplish, and the live proposals — including an
original design concept from this archive.

For the chronological story of how the rivers came back — from dead, crusted channels to 77 fish
species and nesting herons — see the **[Restoration timeline](restoration-timeline.html)**,
**[North Branch](north-branch-restoration-timeline.html)**, and
**[Bubbly Creek](bubbly-creek-restoration-timeline.html)** timelines.

## Current plans & programs

| Lead | Program / plan | Focus |
|---|---|---|
| **U.S. Army Corps of Engineers** | CAWS aquatic ecosystem restoration — Section 206 (Aquatic Ecosystem Restoration) and **GLFER** (Great Lakes Fishery & Ecosystem Restoration); the Bubbly Creek feasibility study | Fish habitat, sediment, backwater/embayment restoration |
| **MWRD** (Metropolitan Water Reclamation District) | **SEPA** sidestream/instream aeration stations; nutrient (phosphorus) removal at the WRPs; continuous dissolved-oxygen monitoring | Dissolved-oxygen compliance and nutrient load |
| **City of Chicago (DPD)** | Chicago River Corridor Design Guidelines & Development Control; the **Our Great Rivers** vision (2016) | Setbacks, naturalized edges, public access |
| **Friends of the Chicago River** | On-the-ground habitat projects — emergent plantings, fish habitat structures, cleanups | Riparian & shoreline habitat |
| **Urban Rivers** | The **[Wild Mile](north-branch-restoration-timeline.html)** floating eco-park; the Bubbly Creek floating gardens | Floating treatment wetlands, habitat, public engagement |
| **Chicago Park District · Openlands · Calumet partners** | Riverbank naturalization, land acquisition, Calumet-region wetland/ prairie restoration | Bank & watershed habitat |

## Methods — and an honest read on efficacy

- **Floating treatment wetlands (FTWs).** Anchored planted rafts (BioMatrix modules at the Wild
  Mile and Bubbly Creek). Excellent for **habitat, edge, and public engagement**; a peer-reviewed
  5-year study at the North Branch Canal found only **~6–7% *local* nutrient reduction and no
  detectable dissolved-oxygen change** — real habitat value, modest water-column effect.
- **Seawall softening / wall planters / vegetation pockets.** Retrofitting hardened vertical
  edges with vegetation (the [SOM 2019 Wild Mile Framework Vision](restoration-timeline.html)
  proposed sheet-pile "wall planters"). Conceptual to date; increases edge surface and habitat.
- **Bank naturalization & slope stabilization.** Regrading riprap to vegetated, geogrid-rooted
  slopes with accessible paths (e.g. the Framework Vision's Hobbie Cove); restores the
  land-to-water gradient native species need.
- **Fish habitat structures.** Cantilevered "lunker" shelves, submerged reefs, and cubbies on
  otherwise vertical walls — the only feasible habitat in fully channelized reaches.
- **Aeration.** MWRD's **SEPA stations** are the *mechanical* workhorse for dissolved oxygen — a
  reminder that engineered aeration, not vegetation, is what moves DO at compliance scale.
- **Dam removal & daylighting.** Restoring connectivity and reopening buried channels.
- **Native emergent plantings.** Water willow (*Justicia americana*) and lizard's tail
  (*Saururus cernuus*) are the workhorse Chicago-River shoreline species; the hardy palette also
  runs to *Carex*, *Juncus*, *Schoenoplectus*, and blue-flag iris.

## The honest scale reality

It is worth being clear-eyed about what nature-based edge projects do. The Chicago River's
nitrogen, phosphorus, and dissolved-oxygen problems are **watershed- and wastewater-scale** — MWRD
plants discharge phosphorus at roughly 20× the ambient target, and the dissolved-oxygen standard is
met only with mechanical aeration. Distributed nature-based units (floating wetlands, wall planters,
bioswales) contact a vanishingly small fraction of river flow: a single unit treats on the order of
1:10,000 of the daily discharge, and even system-wide replication cannot register against the
mass flux (a full scaling analysis is summarized in the proposal below). **Their genuine value is
habitat, ecological edge, localized dissolved oxygen in slow backwaters, and public demonstration —
not measurable river-wide water quality.** Honest framing is the point; overselling nature-based
treatment as river cleanup does the field no favors.

## Proposals & concepts

- **Wild Mile full build-out** — Urban Rivers' vision for a mile-long floating eco-park on the
  North Branch Canal (~$50M; ~700 ft built to date).
- **Wild Mile Framework Vision** (SOM, 2019) — the conceptual template for floating wetlands,
  modified seawalls, and vegetation pockets along the corridor.
- **USACE CAWS ecosystem restoration** — the federal aquatic-habitat track (Section 206 / GLFER).
