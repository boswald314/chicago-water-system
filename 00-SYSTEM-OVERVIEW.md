# Chicago's Water System: The Complete Picture

*Synthesis of the detail documents in `docs/` (now 15 — docs/15 on the Calumet-region lakes, incl. vanished Hyde Lake, was added after this synthesis). Compiled July 2026. Every figure below is traceable to a cited source in the detail docs; citations here point to the doc (and where useful, its numbered sources) rather than re-numbering the bibliography.*

---

## 1. The one-paragraph version

Chicago drinks from Lake Michigan and flushes to the Mississippi, and the whole system exists to keep those two facts from colliding. Raw lake water enters through two offshore intake cribs, falls down shafts, and flows by gravity through tunnels under the lakebed to two purification plants — Jardine, the world's largest conventional filtration plant, and Sawyer — which together are rated at about 2.1 billion gallons a day and actually deliver roughly 750 million to a billion gallons daily to 2.7 million Chicagoans and about 120 suburbs ([docs/03](docs/03-intake-cribs.md), 04). Twelve city pumping stations push that water through some 4,300–4,400 miles of mains to the tap ([docs/05](docs/05-water-pumping-distribution.md)). What goes down the drain enters a different system entirely: the city's ~4,400 miles of combined sewers — one pipe for sewage and stormwater alike, a design choice made in 1855 — drain into 560 miles of interceptor sewers owned by a separate government, the Metropolitan Water Reclamation District, which carries the flow to seven treatment plants anchored by Stickney, the largest wastewater plant on Earth ([docs/06](docs/06-city-sewer-network.md), 07, 11). Treated effluent goes into the Chicago River system, which since January 2, 1900 has flowed *backwards* — away from the lake, down the 28-mile Sanitary & Ship Canal, over a 40-foot drop at Lockport, and on via the Des Plaines and Illinois Rivers to the Mississippi ([docs/02](docs/02-river-reversal-canals.md)). When storms overload the combined sewers, 110 miles of deep rock tunnels and three giant reservoirs — the $3.9-billion Tunnel and Reservoir Plan, "Deep Tunnel" — catch up to 17.5 billion gallons of overflow for later treatment; only when *that* fills do the outfalls spill to the river, and only in the worst storms do the lakefront gates open and send the river, briefly, back into the lake ([docs/09](docs/09-tarp-deep-tunnel.md), 10, 14).

**A sense of scale.** The two halves of the system are strikingly mismatched in age and symmetry. The drinking-water side runs far below its engineering ceiling: 2.1 BGD of treatment capacity against ~750 MGD of average demand, headroom deliberately built in for heat waves and fire flow (the all-time peak day, 1.51 billion gallons, was set July 27, 1955 — before Jardine even existed) ([docs/04](docs/04-water-purification-plants.md)). The wastewater side runs the other way: Stickney alone can treat more than the entire drinking-water system typically delivers, because it must swallow not just sewage but the rain that falls on 300 square miles of pavement. And the storm side dwarfs both: North Branch and Racine Avenue together can pump ~3 billion gallons a day of combined sewage into the river — roughly triple what all twelve drinking-water stations deliver to taps on an average day — which is the arithmetic of a combined-sewer city in one sentence ([docs/05](docs/05-water-pumping-distribution.md), 07, 08).

## 3. Following a drop of water

### (a) A dry Tuesday

Start two and a half miles off North Avenue Beach, thirty-odd feet below the surface, where a drop of Lake Michigan slips through a screened port in the **William E. Dever Crib**. It falls down the crib's central shaft and rides gravity — no pumps yet — through the 16-foot-diameter Chicago Avenue tunnel, bored through limestone as much as 200 feet beneath the lakebed, 11.5 miles to the shore intake basin at the **Jardine Water Purification Plant** on its man-made peninsula north of Navy Pier ([docs/03](docs/03-intake-cribs.md), 04). Low-lift pumps raise it about 25 feet into the treatment train: traveling screens, alum coagulation, flocculation, settling that strips ~90% of remaining particulates, then a polish through one of 96 swimming-pool-sized sand filters, then chlorine, fluoride (0.7 mg/L), and a phosphate blend that coats the inside of old pipes to hold lead in place. About eight hours after it left the lake, the drop leaves the plant ([docs/04](docs/04-water-purification-plants.md)).

Gravity — Chicago keeps essentially no elevated storage — carries it through the finished-water tunnels to a district pumping station; on the North Side that might be the 1928 **Thomas Jefferson Pumping Station** in Lincoln Square, whose motors push it into the grid at 38–42 psi ([docs/05](docs/05-water-pumping-distribution.md)). It travels a few miles of the city's ~4,400 miles of main, up a service line (with luck, not one of the ~400,000 that are still lead), and out a kitchen tap in Ravenswood ([docs/05](docs/05-water-pumping-distribution.md)).

Down the drain, it changes governments. The house lateral drops it into a City of Chicago **combined sewer** under the street — the same pipe that takes the gutter's rainwater — where it flows by gravity to a connection with the MWRD's **North Side Intercepting Sewer** ([docs/06](docs/06-city-sewer-network.md)). At the riverbank at Lawrence Avenue the interceptor delivers it to the Art Deco **North Branch Pumping Station**, which in dry weather quietly lifts sanitary flow onward so it can run downhill again to the **O'Brien Water Reclamation Plant** in Skokie ([docs/08](docs/08-north-branch-pumping-station.md)). O'Brien takes about twelve hours to do its work: screens, grit removal, primary settling, activated-sludge aeration, final settling, and then a pass under 896 ultraviolet lamps — the largest UV disinfection installation in the world ([docs/12](docs/12-calumet-obrien-wrp.md)). (O'Brien keeps none of its sludge; solids are pumped to Stickney for digestion, joining a solids-consolidation chain that also drains Kirie via Egan and Lemont — [docs/11](docs/11-stickney-wrp.md), 13.)

The clean effluent enters the **North Shore Channel**, joins the North Branch at Foster Avenue, slides past downtown into the **South Branch** and the **Sanitary & Ship Canal** — moving *away* from the lake, as everything here has since 1900 — past Stickney's outfall at Cicero, 28 miles down the canal to **Lockport**, where it drops about 40 feet through the 1907 powerhouse turbines into the Des Plaines River ([docs/02](docs/02-river-reversal-canals.md)). From there it is ordinary river water: Des Plaines to Illinois to the **Mississippi** at Grafton, and eventually the Gulf. A drop that fell as rain on Lake Michigan's watershed has been permanently exported to another basin — which is why the U.S. Supreme Court caps Illinois' entire take, drinking water and diversion alike, at 3,200 cubic feet per second on a 40-year average ([docs/02](docs/02-river-reversal-canals.md)).

The solids the drop left behind have a journey of their own. At Stickney — which digests not only its own sludge but O'Brien's, plus the Kirie-via-Egan and Lemont streams — anaerobic digesters turn it into biogas that heats the plant and 119,234 dry tons a year (2024) of biosolids, of which none went to landfill: about a quarter is heat-dried into fertilizer pellets on site, some is composted or air-dried in the Lawndale and Harlem Avenue lagoons, and the rest goes to farmland, golf courses, and MWRD's 15,000-acre reclaimed strip-mine in Fulton County, 200 miles down the Illinois River ([docs/11](docs/11-stickney-wrp.md), 13). Stickney's Ostara reactors — the world's largest municipal nutrient-recovery facility — even crystallize the phosphorus into up to ~9,000 tons a year of marketable slow-release fertilizer before it can scale the pipes ([docs/11](docs/11-stickney-wrp.md)).

### (b) The big storm

Rain is forecast, so MWRD acts first: it opens up the Lockport gates and holds the lakefront gates shut, drawing the canals down to create roughly five billion gallons of empty channel — the cheapest storage it owns ([docs/14](docs/14-cso-outfalls-discharges.md), sources 14, 39).

The storm arrives. Street grates and 200,000-plus catch basins feed the city's combined sewers, which were sized generations ago for about two inches of rain in a day ([docs/06](docs/06-city-sewer-network.md)). Within the first hour of hard rain the interceptors are full, and at hundreds of MWRD connecting structures the regulating weirs begin diverting excess down the **drop shafts** — vertical plunge shafts, air vented up one side, water spiraling down the other — into the TARP tunnels 150–300 feet below the rivers ([docs/09](docs/09-tarp-deep-tunnel.md)). The tunnels' 2.32 billion gallons fill fast; gates open and flow passes onward to the reservoirs: Mainstream and Des Plaines tunnel water to **McCook**, Calumet water to **Thornton**, O'Hare-area water to **Majewski** ([docs/10](docs/10-tarp-reservoirs.md)).

If the storm keeps building, the big relief pumps come on — *before* TARP is full, which surprises people. In the July 2, 2023 storm, McCook was only 17% full at 2 a.m., yet CSOs began at 8:19 a.m. and the **North Branch Pumping Station** started pushing combined sewage into the river at 9:44 a.m. MWRD's own explanation: when TARP is filling rapidly, North Branch and **Racine Avenue** — 1,500 and 3,125 cfs respectively — are activated deliberately to pull sewer levels down and prevent a pressure surge through the neighborhood pipes the moment the tunnels top out. Most of the region's ~370 CSO outfalls spill passively over fixed weirs; these two pump on purpose ([docs/08](docs/08-north-branch-pumping-station.md), source 6; [docs/07](docs/07-mwrd-interceptors-pumping-stations.md), 14).

As tunnels and reservoirs saturate, gravity CSOs open along every reach — the Chicago River, the Cal-Sag, the Des Plaines — through the City's 184 permitted outfalls and the suburbs' remainder ([docs/06](docs/06-city-sewer-network.md), 14). The last resort comes when the waterway itself threatens to overtop into basements and streets: canal levels rise above the lake's, and MWRD opens the **Wilmette sluice gates**, then the **Chicago River Controlling Works**, then the **O'Brien Lock** — reversing the reversal and sending diluted sewage into Lake Michigan. The record release was 11.05 billion gallons over September 13–16, 2008 (remnants of Hurricane Ike); April 2013 came second at 10.7 billion ([docs/14](docs/14-cso-outfalls-discharges.md), source 12). In July 2023 all three points opened, and over 1.1 billion gallons reached the lake ([docs/09](docs/09-tarp-deep-tunnel.md)).

Then the slow unwinding. The **Mainstream Pumping Station** lifts McCook's contents 300 feet up to Stickney; the **Calumet TARP station** sends Thornton's to Calumet WRP — a plant that can treat less than a third of what Stickney can per day, which is why draining a full Thornton takes about a month, as south-suburban residents smelling hydrogen sulfide off 7.4 billion stored gallons learned in July 2026, when McCook hit 100% for the sixth time in a single year and Thornton set its all-time record at 94% ([docs/10](docs/10-tarp-reservoirs.md), 07). The paperwork unwinds too: every activation of a major CSO pumping station triggers NPDES sampling of the discharge, and two days after each North Branch pumping event MWRD sends boat crews downriver to check for fish kills and report to the Illinois EPA ([docs/08](docs/08-north-branch-pumping-station.md)). The whole choreography works — TARP has captured over a trillion gallons since its tunnels went into service, cut CSO days from ~100 to ~50 a year, and eliminated overflows entirely in Thornton's service area since 2020 — right up until the rain outruns it, which climate statistics say it now does more often: the region's storms deliver totals the 2-inch-design sewers and 1970s-sized tunnels never anticipated, and the July 2023 storm flooded 70,000 basements with the entire finished system online ([docs/09](docs/09-tarp-deep-tunnel.md), 10, 06, 14).

## 4. How it was built — seven eras

**1855–1889: Chesbrough's sewers and the raised city.** After cholera killed 1,424 Chicagoans in 1854 (a 53.9-per-1,000 death rate), the legislature created a Board of Sewerage Commissioners on February 14, 1855; its engineer, Ellis S. Chesbrough, delivered the first comprehensive sewer plan for any American city that December. Because the city sat barely above lake level, the sewers went in nearly at the surface and the streets were raised four to fourteen feet around them — buildings jacked up on thousands of jackscrews, hotels operating mid-lift. Fifty-four miles were in service by 1861 (death rate: down to 18.8), 888 miles by 1892 — all draining, fatally, to the river and thence the lake that supplied the drinking water. Chesbrough's answer was to chase clean water offshore: the Two-Mile Crib and lake tunnel of 1865–67 ($380,784), a template repeated eight times through 1935. The 1871 I&M Canal "deep cut" reversed the river's flow briefly and feebly; the storm of August 1885 pushed the sewage field out to the crib and made a permanent fix politically unstoppable ([docs/01](docs/01-early-sewerage-history.md), 03).

**1889–1910: the Sanitary District and the reversal.** The Sanitary District of Chicago was chartered May 29, 1889 and ratified by referendum that November, 70,958 to 242. From "Shovel Day" (September 3, 1892) some 8,500 men dug the 28-mile Main Channel — 41 million cubic yards of earth and rock, $23.7 million, machinery so novel the trade press called it the "Chicago school of earth moving," later exported to Panama. On January 2, 1900, ahead of a Missouri injunction, trustees quietly cut the dam and the Chicago River began flowing backwards for good. The Lockport Controlling Works (1899) and Powerhouse (1907) fixed the downstream end; the North Shore Channel (1910) extended dilution to the northern lakefront ([docs/02](docs/02-river-reversal-canals.md), 01).

**1900–1930: interceptors and the first treatment plants.** Dilution alone couldn't protect the lakefront north of the river mouth, so from 1906 the District built intercepting sewers along the shore, feeding a pumping station at Lawrence Avenue (1907; rebuilt 1928–30 as the present **North Branch Pumping Station**). The Cal-Sag Channel (1922) reversed the Calumet rivers, and the Supreme Court's diversion rulings of 1925–1930 — capping the lake water available for dilution — forced actual treatment: Calumet (1922, the first plant), North Side/O'Brien (1928), with the 95th Street PS (1925) lifting Calumet-area flow. On the drinking-water side, chlorination began experimentally at the cribs in 1912 and citywide by 1916 ([docs/07](docs/07-mwrd-interceptors-pumping-stations.md), 08, 12, 04).

**1930–1950: Stickney and filtration begin.** The West Side works opened June 2, 1930 on Imhoff-tank technology and the Southwest works followed on September 7, 1939 — a single day's bulkhead pull diverting 75 MGD of raw sewage out of the canal — the pair, part of a ~$162-million program, later becoming **Stickney**, the world's largest treatment plant. The Racine Avenue Pumping Station (1939) fed it. The Dever Crib (1935) and 16-foot Chicago Avenue tunnel (1936, three million pounds of dynamite) modernized the intake side; the Chicago Harbor Lock (1938) sealed the river mouth per the 1930 decree; and voters' 1930 mandate for filtration — delayed by Depression and war — was finally delivered when the South District plant (now **Sawyer**) reached full operation May 14, 1947, then the world's largest filtration plant ([docs/11](docs/11-stickney-wrp.md), 03, 02, 04).

**1950–1970: Jardine and the suburban build-out.** The Central District plant — **Jardine** — survived a lawsuit to the Illinois Supreme Court (1954), rose on a 61-acre artificial peninsula, entered full operation in fall 1964, and at 1.4 billion gallons a day took the world-title from Sawyer (itself expanded to 480 MGD in 1964); final cost about $105 million against an $85-million estimate. Fluoridation went citywide May 1, 1956. USACE modernized the Cal-Sag (widened 1955–65) and opened the O'Brien Lock (1965). The MWRD's suburban satellites began: Lemont (1961), Hanover Park (1963) — whose tertiary filters drew President Nixon and four governors to a sewage plant in February 1970. And Chicago's Bureau of Engineering built the Lawrence Avenue Underflow Sewer (1967–75): 12-to-17-foot tunnels 250 feet down that cut local overflows 92% — the acknowledged prototype of Deep Tunnel, finished before TARP was even adopted ([docs/04](docs/04-water-purification-plants.md), 02, 13, 06).

**1972–2006: TARP Phase I.** With combined sewers overflowing 100+ days a year, a four-government committee weighed 23 alternatives (full sewer separation: rejected at "upwards of four billion dollars") and on October 20, 1972 MWRD adopted the Chicago Underflow Plan — TARP — at an estimated $1.223 billion, timed to the brand-new Clean Water Act's 75% federal grants. Boring began in 1975; the O'Hare tunnels went live in 1981, Mainstream in 1985 — the year GAO-fueled "boondoggle" headlines ($10.2 billion and climbing) nearly killed the program and Reagan-era grants dried up. An October 1986 storm shot 65-foot geysers out of the new tunnels. Egan (1975) and Kirie (1980) completed the plant roster. The full 110.4-mile, 2.32-billion-gallon tunnel network was done in 2006: $2.33 billion in construction contracts, capturing ~85% of CSO pollution and halving overflow days to ~50 a year ([docs/09](docs/09-tarp-deep-tunnel.md), 13).

**1998–2032: reservoirs, disinfection, and the stormwater era.** Phase II added the storage TARP always needed: Majewski (1998), Thornton Composite (2015, world's largest CSO reservoir, excavated essentially for free by quarry miners who bought the 76 million tons of stone), and McCook (Stage 1, 2017; Stage 2 slipping from a 2011 consent-decree deadline of 2029 to an estimated 2032 because reservoir excavation rides the commercial stone market). MWRD gained countywide stormwater authority (2004), and — ending Chicago's status as the last major U.S. city releasing undisinfected effluent — added chlorination at Calumet (2015) and UV at O'Brien (2016). The results are visible: 70+ fish species where there were fewer than ten in the 1970s, and in September 2025 the first sanctioned Chicago River swim in 98 years. The counterweight is climate: storms the system was never sized for filled McCook six times in the first half of 2026 alone, and the July 2023 flood put 70,000 basements underwater despite everything built since 1855 ([docs/10](docs/10-tarp-reservoirs.md), 14, 06).

## 5. Who owns what

The single most confused fact about this system: **two separate governments run it**, with the U.S. Army Corps of Engineers as the third player on the waterway. DWM is a city department funded by water/sewer rates; MWRD is an independent district with its own elected board covering nearly all of Cook County. The boundary runs, literally, at the pipe flange where a city sewer meets a District interceptor ([docs/06](docs/06-city-sewer-network.md)).

| | **City of Chicago DWM** | **MWRD** | **USACE (federal)** |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Drinking water** | Everything: cribs, lake tunnels, Jardine & Sawyer, 12 pumping stations, 4,400 mi of mains, suburban wholesale | Nothing | Nothing |
| **Local sewers** | ~4,400 mi of combined sewers, catch basins, manholes, house-connection permits; 184 city CSO outfall structures | — | — |
| **Interceptors & treatment** | — | 560 mi of interceptors, 23 sewage pumping stations, all 7 WRPs, biosolids program | — |
| **TARP** | — | Tunnels, drop shafts, reservoirs, TARP pumping stations; the federally approved CSO Long-Term Control Plan for the City's own outfalls | Designed/built Majewski & McCook features under the Chicagoland Underflow Plan cost-share |
| **The waterway** | — | Hydraulic control: lakefront sluice gates, Wilmette PS, Lockport Powerhouse & Controlling Works; reversal decisions | Navigation: Chicago Harbor Lock (since 1984), O'Brien Lock, Lockport Lock; electric carp barriers; Brandon Road project |
| **Stormwater policy** | Green alleys, city stormwater ordinance (2008) | Countywide stormwater authority (2004), Watershed Management Ordinance | Flood-control cost-share partner |
| **The diversion** | Uses it (domestic pumpage) | Holds the 305/270→220-cfs lakefront allowance | Runs the official Lake Michigan diversion accounting under the 3,200-cfs decree (allocated by Illinois DNR) |

Private owners hold one more tier: the house lateral from the building to the main, and — for now — most of the lead service lines ([docs/05](docs/05-water-pumping-distribution.md), 06).

## 6. The open questions

The ten most interesting unresolved figures and disputes, distilled from the detail docs' Open Questions sections:

1. **Who conceived Deep Tunnel?** Vinton Bacon (MSD superintendent 1962–70) is credited as originator in several accounts; Frank Dalton's own MWRD obituary says *he* "conceived and led" it. The 1968 Harza-Bauer report is the earliest concrete design. Genuinely contested, not just under-researched ([docs/09](docs/09-tarp-deep-tunnel.md)).
2. **What did TARP really cost?** $1.223B (1972 estimate) vs. $2.33B (Phase I contracts, nominal) vs. $3.86B (MWRD's cumulative ledger through 2025) vs. GAO's $10.2–12.5B full-program projections of 1981–83. The figures bundle financing, inflation, and Phase II differently and no source reconciles them ([docs/09](docs/09-tarp-deep-tunnel.md)).
3. **How many times has the river reversed into the lake?** MWRD's own 1985–2021 table counts 22 years with events totaling ~51.3 billion gallons; press accounts attribute "27 reversals / 44 billion gallons" and "about 50 events" to the same agency. Counting rules (gates vs. locks, dates vs. events) are undocumented, and no post-2021 ledger exists ([docs/14](docs/14-cso-outfalls-discharges.md)).
4. **When was the Dunne Crib built — 1909 or 1911?** Wikipedia and most histories say 1909; the City's own 1933 and 1956 engineering reports say 1911. The archive carries both ([docs/03](docs/03-intake-cribs.md), 04).
5. **How many died in the 1909 crib fire?** Estimates run 47 (early count) to ~60 (coroner's jury) to 67–70 (press); the contractor never fully documented its temporary laborers, so Chicago's worst forgotten disaster has no fixed toll ([docs/03](docs/03-intake-cribs.md)).
6. **When was Jardine finished — 1964 or 1968?** Trade press and the Encyclopedia of Chicago say full operation fall 1964; ENR, Structurae, Wikipedia and Gizmodo all say 1968 ("13 years, $105 million"). Probably online-1964/complete-1968, but no dedication record confirms it ([docs/04](docs/04-water-purification-plants.md)).
7. **Racine Avenue Pumping Station: 1939 or 1964?** MWRD's own press release says built 1939; South Side Weekly describes a 1964 station that "replaced" a smaller one. Rebuild, expansion, or error — unresolved ([docs/07](docs/07-mwrd-interceptors-pumping-stations.md), 11).
8. **When did North Branch PS get its five 300-cfs pumps?** The 1970s demonstration project installed only ~48 cfs of pumps at the site; the 1,500-cfs bank first appears, as pre-existing, in a 2018 rehab contract. No document dates the expansion — the TARP era is only a hypothesis ([docs/08](docs/08-north-branch-pumping-station.md)).
9. **What can the Mainstream Pumping Station actually pump?** No MWRD document publishes an MGD rating for the world's largest sewage pumping station; the "billion gallons a day / 17,500 hp" figures trace to an enthusiast blog's pre-opening tour ([docs/09](docs/09-tarp-deep-tunnel.md), 10).
10. **Brown's 1894 history disagrees with itself** on the I&M deep-cut's cost — $3,300,883.71 in the narrative, $3,800,883.71 in its own summary table — a genuine internal contradiction in the canonical 19th-century source, resolvable only in the Board of Public Works' 1871 audit ([docs/01](docs/01-early-sewerage-history.md)).

Honorable mentions: MWRD's own fact sheets give the Calumet WRP site as both 275.4 and 470 acres ([docs/12](docs/12-calumet-obrien-wrp.md)); the City's suburban-customer count floats between 118 and 126 across its own current materials ([docs/04](docs/04-water-purification-plants.md), 05); nobody can cite the single largest city-owned sewer ([docs/06](docs/06-city-sewer-network.md)); and DWM's structure counts (56,000 vs. 205,000+146,000 vs. 340,000) have never been reconciled ([docs/06](docs/06-city-sewer-network.md)).
