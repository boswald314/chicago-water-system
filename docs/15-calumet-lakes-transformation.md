# The Calumet-Region Lakes: How Lake Calumet, Wolf Lake, and Hyde Lake Were Re-Engineered

## Role in the system

Every other document in this archive follows engineered infrastructure — canals, cribs, plants, tunnels — built *around* Chicago's natural waterways. This document covers something different: three natural lakes at the far southeast corner of the system that were themselves reshaped into infrastructure. Lake Calumet, Wolf Lake, and the vanished Hyde Lake sat at the historic headwaters of the Little Calumet/Calumet River system that doc [02](02-river-reversal-canals.md) describes being reversed by the Cal-Sag Channel in 1922. That reversal did not simply redirect water — it also supplied the legal and economic rationale for a 60-year campaign of dredging and filling that cut Lake Calumet's open-water area roughly in half, eliminated Hyde Lake entirely, and left Wolf Lake divided into diked pools by causeways built for reasons that had nothing to do with the lake itself [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[2\]](#src-2).

Geologically, all three lakes occupy the same shallow, poorly drained plain: a former arm of glacial Lake Michigan left behind sandy beach ridges (the Tolleston and Calumet ridges) enclosing broad marshy flats after the lake receded roughly 4,000–2,000 years ago [\[1\]](#src-1). Surveyors in 1834 found Lake Calumet at the center of an "impassable" wetland roughly three miles long and a mile and a half wide, six to ten feet deep, ringed by cattail marsh that extended east to the Calumet River and west to the Blue Island moraine [2 p.14-17]. Wolf Lake sat in a similar depression hemmed in by beach ridges straddling what became the Illinois-Indiana state line, its basin floored with twenty to thirty inches of peat over sandy soil [2 p.15]. Hyde Lake did not even exist on the earliest surveys — it emerged sometime between 1872 and 1881 as the water stage of Wolf Lake and neighboring Berry Lake dropped, exposing sand ridges that separated a shallow remnant pool from the rest of Wolf Lake and gave it a name of its own [1 p.14-15]. That pool then had one of the shortest lifespans of any named lake in the Chicago region: formed in the 1870s, it was "almost completely filled by 1902" and gone for good by 1927 [1 p.15,24-25].

This document tells the physical-transformation story that the rest of the archive assumes but does not narrate: the dredging and diking that created Calumet Harbor and the Port of Chicago; the slag, dredge spoil, and municipal refuse that did the filling; the causeways that split Wolf Lake into managed pools; and the disappearance of Hyde Lake as documented on USGS topographic maps. It also traces where the spoil from the Cal-Sag Channel and the Calumet River federal navigation project actually went — a question the canal-construction narrative in doc [02](02-river-reversal-canals.md) leaves open. The short answer: much of it went into these same lakes.

## Original construction

### Lake Calumet: from wild-rice-and-cattail marsh to harbor

Congress authorized the first federal money for a harbor at the mouth of the Calumet River in 1869 — $50,000 to cut through the sandbar and build two piers into Lake Michigan [3 p.204][2 p.19]. For the next two decades, improvement of the river upstream toward Lake Calumet was blocked less by engineering difficulty than by property law: the Corps of Engineers refused to dredge until riparian landowners surrendered their rights, and a handful of speculators held out for higher prices, delaying real work until 1888 [2 p.19]. Once underway, dredging moved quickly: a 16-foot-deep, 20-foot-wide channel reached 108th Street by 1891, and by 1896 a shallower channel reached Lake Calumet itself [2 p.19-20]. By 1912 a 21-foot federal channel extended all the way to the lake [3 p.207].

Industry did not wait for the Corps. Landowners along the river dredged their own frontage and used the spoil to raise building sites — the Calumet and Chicago Canal and Dock Company reported spending $4,800 on filling/grading and $12,000 on dredging in 1879 alone [2 p.19-20]. When George Pullman built his factory town starting in spring 1880, his engineers filled a two-by-half-mile section of the western shore of Lake Calumet, raising it five feet above the surrounding marsh — while simultaneously mining the exposed lake bed for brick clay at a rate described in Corps correspondence as "100,000 [bricks] per day," with a company agent suggesting the excavation could "go far towards excavating the lake" for dock frontage [2 p.20-21]. At the river mouth, North Chicago Rolling Mill began "systematically" dumping slag and mill refuse into the lake and shoreline starting in 1878-79; by 1882 the practice had created over 30 acres of new land, and it eventually built out roughly 300 acres there, with Iroquois Iron and American Smelting & Refining creating another 400 acres just south of the river mouth using the same method [2 p.21-22]. This pattern — slag and dredge spoil dumped directly into the water to manufacture buildable land — became, in Colten's words, "the norm throughout the Calumet region" and is why the 1911 Sanborn insurance map for the Sherwin-Williams plant on Lake Calumet's shore carries the frank label "Land in Making" [2 p.22].

By the early 1900s, momentum built for a formal harbor plan rather than piecemeal industrial filling. On June 29, 1920, engineer Arend Van Vlissingen submitted a "Report on a Plan for the Construction of a Harbor at Lake Calumet and on the Public Need of Such a Harbor" to the Chicago City Council's Committee on Harbors, Wharves, and Bridges; the City of Chicago formally adopted the Van Vlissingen Plan in 1921 [\[19\]](#src-19)[\[20\]](#src-20). The plan — illustrated with an artist's sketch of the reshaped lake held today by the Chicago History Museum — called for dredging Lake Calumet into a true inland harbor connected to Lake Michigan by the improved Calumet River channel [\[19\]](#src-19)[\[22\]](#src-22). The Army Corps' own project history describes Calumet Harbor's "project with modifications" as complete by 1922 — the same year the Cal-Sag Channel opened and the Sanitary District's Calumet plant (doc [12](12-calumet-obrien-wrp.md)) came online, making 1922 the single pivotal year in the Calumet region's engineered transformation [3 p.234][\[16\]](#src-16).

Filling of the lake margins continued for decades after the harbor plan's initial construction. When the Corps built a turning basin at the lake's south end, dredge spoil was pumped in behind a purpose-built slag dike to manufacture new dock land — a technique the USGS later found repeated at multiple points around the lake's perimeter [1 p.24][2 p.41]. Between 1902 and 1927 "the western half of Lake Calumet continued to be infilled," and by the time the USGS mapped the region's fill deposits in the early 1990s, its plate-based analysis found that "about half of Lake Calumet" had been filled overall [1 p.24, 27]. Public Works Administration funds accelerated harbor construction during the Depression: on September 6, 1933, the Corps began building a detached breakwater and dredging the outer harbor to 26 feet, while also straightening and widening the Calumet River channel and deepening its five turning basins to 21 feet [3 p.235-236]. In 1940 the City of Chicago opened a 300-acre municipal landfill at the lake's north end, operated by the Illinois Development Company; concerned that leachate would reach open water, the operators built a composite slag-and-ash dike across the lake at 110th Street — 40 feet wide and 6 feet high, reinforced with slag after a 1945 storm washed out part of the ash fill — and behind it delivered roughly 1,800 cubic yards of refuse per day for decades, eventually filling in the lake's entire northern quarter [2 p.59][1 p.27].

### Hyde Lake: formed, mapped, and erased inside 55 years

Hyde Lake's entire documented existence — appearance, industrial use, and disappearance — fits within about five decades, and it happened almost entirely within the fill zone this document has already described. It was not an ancient feature: USGS geologists, working from an 1882 congressional survey document, place its formation "sometime between 1872 and 1881," when falling water levels in Wolf Lake exposed a sand ridge that separated off a shallow western remnant pool and gave it a name [1 p.14-15]. A U.S. Army Corps of Engineers map of "Calumet River Improvements, 1894," reproduced in Colten's history, labels "HYDE LAKE" clearly at this location, immediately southwest of Wolf Lake and northeast of the Calumet River's turning basins — and the earliest USGS topographic coverage of the area, the 1892 Calumet 15-minute quadrangle, shows the same small lake between Calumet Lake and Wolf Lake, already squeezed by the railroad and industrial grid pressing in from South Chicago [2 fig. 2-3][\[5\]](#src-5). That map is the clearest surviving cartographic record of Hyde Lake as a distinct, named water body.

Its filling followed the same playbook as the rest of the region: steel-industry slag and river dredge spoil, deposited to "create land for steel manufacturing, petroleum refining, and storage" — the USGS's fill-characterization study groups Hyde Lake with two nearby Indiana-side lakes (Deer Lake and Berry Lake) that were filled by the identical process in the same window [1 p.24]. The USGS's map-comparison methodology is unusually precise about the timing: "Hyde Lake was almost completely filled by 1902," the year the agency treats as the boundary of its earliest reliable topographic coverage, and by 1927 Hyde Lake (along with Deer and Berry Lakes) "had been filled" outright [1 p.15, 24]. A small residual wet spot apparently lingered for decades — the same study notes that "small amounts of fill were dumped... at the northern tip of what was once Hyde Lake" as late as the 1978-1993 period, suggesting a marshy remnant persisted long after the main lake was gone [1 p.28]. This document could not identify a single named company or a specific year as "the" party responsible for the final filling — the sources describe a decades-long, multi-operator process typical of the whole Calumet shoreline, driven by the steel mills and rail yards that surrounded the site (Wisconsin Steel/International Harvester and the Iroquois Landing/CID industrial corridor sat immediately adjacent, though no source consulted names either operator specifically in connection with Hyde Lake's fill) — see Open questions.

### Wolf Lake: shared, diked, and slagged

Wolf Lake's transformation ran on a different track from Lake Calumet's, because it was never dredged into a shipping harbor — it was instead progressively diked, filled at the margins, and pressed into service for projects that had nothing to do with the lake itself. Colten's history notes that peat deposits up to thirty inches deep "are most common in the Wolf Lake basin," reflecting its origin as a shallow, marsh-fringed depression among beach ridges — described by naturalist Donald Culross Peattie as "a famous hunting ground for aquatic wildlife... where the plants form one of the most remarkable assemblages of aquatics in the country" [2 p.15][\[7\]](#src-7). Industrial filling reached Wolf Lake later and less completely than it reached Lake Calumet: the USGS's fill-deposit mapping finds "the northern and southeastern parts of Wolf Lake" had been filled by the early 1990s, and identifies slag as "the principal fill material" around the lake, with especially heavy ash-and-cinder deposits — flue dust from steel manufacturing — on its west side [1 p.24, 27]. The single largest filling episode came during the Cold War: several hundred acres of wetland on the lake's northwest side were filled with ferrous slag to build a Nike Ajax anti-aircraft missile site; that land is now the Cook County Forest Preserve District's Eggers Woods [\[7\]](#src-7).

The lake's present-day division into separate diked pools dates specifically to the late 1950s and had nothing to do with water management — it was fill logistics for a highway project. The Illinois portion's five interconnected impoundments and the Indiana portion's three impoundments (the longest carrying Indiana Harbor Belt Railroad track roughly parallel to State Line Road) were built so that individual sections of the lake could be drained and dredged for fill material used in constructing the Chicago Skyway, which opened in 1958 [\[7\]](#src-7). The dikes remain the lake's defining physical feature today.

### Cal-Sag Channel: deepening the construction story

Doc [02](02-river-reversal-canals.md) already covers the Cal-Sag Channel's dates and dimensions; the additional construction detail relevant here concerns *how* it was built and *where the spoil went* — because much of that spoil reshaped the lakes described above. The Sanitary District divided the 16.2-mile channel into fourteen sections, each roughly 1.125 miles long, let out to separate contractors [\[18\]](#src-18)[3 p.206]. Construction proceeded from the main channel above Lemont eastward toward Blue Island and the Little Calumet River; the ceremonial start of excavation came on July 4, 1913, when the contracting firm of Green & Sons broke ground with a steam shovel near Worth [\[18\]](#src-18). Photographs from the build (reproduced by local historians from Sanitary District/MWRD archives) show steam shovels repeatedly mired in the region's soft clay, dredges being assembled on-site as early as December 1911, and — in the channel's rock-cut sections through the Sag Valley — a traveling bridge crane that dredged material and sorted it to either side of the cut, stone to one side and earth to the other, so it could be re-used or trucked away [\[17\]](#src-17). Contractor A. Guthrie & Co. cast the channel's original concrete retaining walls, later removed during the 1955-1971 widening [\[17\]](#src-17). Neither the Colten history nor the Corps' own district history gives a comprehensive workforce headcount for the 1911-1922 build; that remains an open question below.

Disposal of the material excavated from both the original 1911-1922 Cal-Sag dig and the subsequent decades of Calumet River/Harbor maintenance dredging followed an evolving, and increasingly contested, path. Colten's history states that prior to the 1920s, river-dredging spoil was "typically dumped indiscriminately along the banks of the rivers from which the spoil was removed" — exactly the practice already described above as building "made land" along the Calumet River shore [1 p.28][2 p.19-22]. Beginning in the 1920s new regulations required open-lake disposal roughly 12 miles offshore instead, a practice that continued (with berms occasionally built to contain material left on riverbanks) until the late 1960s [1 p.28]. The Corps' own history confirms the scale of this open-lake dumping: from 1924 onward, Chicago, Calumet, and Indiana Harbors shared "one common underwater dumpground" nine miles east of Calumet Harbor's breakwater entrance, a rectangular tract roughly 5 by 17 miles and 69 feet deep on average [3 p.271]. Rising environmental concern in the mid-1960s ended open-lake dumping for polluted material: a July 1965 internal Corps memo warned that "public attitudes toward pollution are becoming more critical," and by 1966 the Chicago District Engineer was studying diked land-disposal alternatives, estimating that roughly 2 million cubic yards of material would need to be dredged from Calumet Harbor and River alone over the following decade [3 p.271-273]. By 1967, land-based settling ponds for dredging spoil had opened southwest of the O'Brien Lock and Dam, immediately north of the confluence of Lake Calumet and the Calumet River — meaning the CID/Confined Disposal Facility complex used today traces directly back to this 1960s environmental-policy shift [1 p.28][3 p.273]. Congress formalized the practice nationally with Public Law 91-611 (signed December 31, 1970), authorizing the Corps to build permanent contained spoil-disposal facilities; the present-day Chicago Area Confined Disposal Facility at 3600 East 95th Street — 43 acres, built 1982-1984, still receiving Calumet River/Harbor dredge material under an Illinois EPA permit — is the direct descendant of that law [\[14\]](#src-14)[3 p.276]. In short: much of the material dredged out of the Cal-Sag Channel, the Calumet River, and Calumet Harbor over more than a century did not leave the region at all. It was piled along the riverbanks to build industrial sites, pumped behind slag dikes to fill Lake Calumet's margins, dumped in Lake Michigan for roughly forty years, and finally, from the 1970s-80s onward, sealed permanently into diked disposal cells on the lake's own shoreline.

## Upgrades & modernization timeline

- **1869** — Congress appropriates $50,000 for the first federal harbor work at the mouth of the Calumet River [3 p.204].
- **1872-1881** — Hyde Lake and Deer Lake form as falling water levels expose sand ridges within the Wolf Lake/Berry Lake basins [1 p.14-15].
- **1878-1882** — North Chicago Rolling Mill begins systematic slag dumping into Lake Michigan/the Calumet River shoreline; ~30 acres of new land created by 1882 (eventually ~300 acres) [2 p.21].
- **1880** — Pullman fills a two-by-half-mile tract on Lake Calumet's western shore for factory-town construction, mining the exposed lakebed for brick clay [2 p.20-21].
- **1888** — Riparian right-of-way disputes finally resolved; Corps dredging of the Calumet River toward Lake Calumet begins in earnest [2 p.19].
- **1891-1896** — Federal channel reaches 108th Street (1891) and Lake Calumet (1896) [2 p.19-20].
- **ca. 1902** — Hyde Lake "almost completely filled," per USGS comparison of the earliest reliable topographic surveys [1 p.15].
- **1911-1922** — Cal-Sag Channel dug in 14 contractor sections; groundbreaking July 4, 1913; gates opened August 18-26, 1922 [\[18\]](#src-18)[3 p.202-206].
- **1920-1921** — Arend Van Vlissingen submits, and the City of Chicago adopts, the plan to dredge Lake Calumet into a formal harbor [\[19\]](#src-19)[\[20\]](#src-20).
- **1922** — Calumet Harbor federal project "complete with modifications"; Cal-Sag Channel opens; Calumet sewage treatment works (doc [12](12-calumet-obrien-wrp.md)) opens — the single most consequential year in the region's engineering history [3 p.234][\[16\]](#src-16).
- **1924** — Chicago/Calumet/Indiana Harbors begin sharing a single 90-square-mile open-lake dredge-spoil dumpground [3 p.271].
- **1927** — Hyde Lake, Deer Lake, and Berry Lake fully filled [1 p.15, 24].
- **1933-1937** — PWA-funded detached breakwater, outer-harbor dredging to 26 ft, and turning-basin deepening at Calumet Harbor [3 p.235-236].
- **1940** — City of Chicago opens a 300-acre municipal landfill on Lake Calumet's north shore behind a new slag-and-ash dike at 110th Street; eventually fills the lake's entire northern quarter [2 p.59][1 p.27].
- **1945** — Storm washes out part of the 1940 landfill's ash dike; rebuilt with slag [2 p.59].
- **1951-1952** — Illinois General Assembly creates the Chicago Regional Port District; the state and city relinquish title to roughly 1,500 acres of Lake Calumet marshland to the District to enable harbor development [\[23\]](#src-23).
- **Late 1950s** — Wolf Lake diked into separate pools (5 in Illinois, 3 in Indiana) so sections could be drained and dredged for Chicago Skyway fill (Skyway opens 1958) [\[7\]](#src-7).
- **1958** — Lake Calumet deepened and new slips added in anticipation of St. Lawrence Seaway ocean-freighter traffic that "never truly materialized" [\[6\]](#src-6).
- **1957-1965** — T.J. O'Brien Lock & Dam built on the Calumet River, replacing the Sanitary District's Blue Island Lock (full detail in doc [02](02-river-reversal-canals.md)) [3 p.226-228][\[4\]](#src-4).
- **1955-1971** — Cal-Sag Channel widened from 60 to 225 ft, removing roughly 1 million cubic yards of material per mile [3 p.226-229].
- **1960/1962** — Turning Basin No. 5 at Lake Calumet's entrance widened (completed 1962); Little Calumet River bend near the basin widened (completed 1960) [3 p.229].
- **1967** — Land-based dredge-spoil settling ponds open southwest of the O'Brien Lock and Dam, north of the Lake Calumet/Calumet River confluence — the direct ancestor of today's CID/Confined Disposal Facility [1 p.28].
- **Dec. 31, 1970** — Public Law 91-611 authorizes the Corps to build permanent contained dredge-spoil disposal facilities nationwide [3 p.276].
- **1971** (per one source) or **1974** (per another) — Paxton I Landfill opens on filled wetland south of Lake Calumet; sources disagree on the year [source notes below].
- **1973** (per one source) or **1976** (per another) — Paxton II Landfill opens adjacent to Paxton I; sources again disagree [source notes below].
- **1982-1984** — U.S. Army Corps of Engineers builds the 43-acre Chicago Area Confined Disposal Facility at 3600 East 95th Street for Calumet River/Harbor dredge material [\[14\]](#src-14)[1 p.28].
- **1985** — Illinois International Port District Act creates the modern IIPD, superseding the 1951 Chicago Regional Port District [\[23\]](#src-23).
- **1992** — City of Chicago closes Paxton II over structural/safety concerns about its capping [source notes below].
- **1996** — The Calumet Expressway (built partly on former Lake Calumet lakebed in the 1950s) is renamed the Bishop Ford Freeway [\[6\]](#src-6).
- **1999-2000** — 2.5 million gallons of contaminated liquid pumped from the closed Paxton landfill complex; Illinois EPA plants prairie grass over the capped site the following year [\[28\]](#src-28).
- **2007** — $7.25 million ecosystem restoration completed at Wolf Lake / William W. Powers State Recreation Area: natural water-level regime restored, ~25 acres of new wetland created, ~5,000 ft of shoreline rebuilt [\[7\]](#src-7).

- **2021** — Army Corps applies to renew the CDF's discharge permit and to add roughly 20 feet of vertical containment capacity, extending its operating life by about 20 more years; public review remains open, with enhanced outreach citing the site's Environmental Justice designation [\[15\]](#src-15)[\[14\]](#src-14).
- **2025-2028 (planned)** — Lake Calumet Cluster remedial investigation/feasibility studies to conclude, with a groundwater-remedy Record of Decision tentatively set for spring 2028 [\[13\]](#src-13).

## Current operations

Lake Calumet today functions as a working harbor rather than a natural lake. The Illinois International Port District — successor since 1985 to the 1951 Chicago Regional Port District — controls roughly 1,600 acres of land around Senator Dan Dougherty Harbor (the Lake Calumet terminal) plus the separate Iroquois Landing Terminal at the Calumet River's mouth on Lake Michigan; combined, the Port of Chicago handled about 894,832 tons of cargo annually as of the most recent figures located, moving grain (two elevators totaling 14 million bushels of capacity), bulk liquids (800,000 barrels of storage), petcoke, and cement through roughly 3,000 linear feet of ship/barge berthing [\[23\]](#src-23)[\[10\]](#src-10). The lake remains connected to Lake Michigan by the dredged Calumet River channel, deep enough that 600-foot lake freighters still back up the river into the harbor [\[25\]](#src-25). Immediately south and east of the open water, the industrial land created by a century of slag and dredge-spoil filling now carries a very different designation: the 87-acre Lake Calumet Cluster Superfund site (US Drum, Alburn Incinerator, an unnamed parcel, and part of the old Paxton Lagoons) is in active EPA remediation, with a partial landfill cap already built and groundwater investigation ongoing through the mid-2020s [\[13\]](#src-13). The separate CID Landfill Superfund site, on filled land between 122nd Street and the Calumet River, remains a distinct, actively managed cleanup [\[12\]](#src-12). The Army Corps' Confined Disposal Facility at 95th Street continues to receive Calumet River and Harbor maintenance-dredging material under an Illinois EPA water-pollution-control permit, most recently renewed in 2016 with a temporary extension through 2021 while a vertical-expansion permit is reviewed [\[14\]](#src-14).

Wolf Lake operates under split jurisdiction reflecting its diked, cross-border layout: the Illinois portion is managed by the Illinois Department of Natural Resources as the 580-acre William W. Powers State Recreation Area (419 acres of which is open water), offering fishing, ice fishing, 25 lottery-assigned waterfowl-hunting blinds, and shoreline shelters, while the Indiana portion is municipal parkland around Hammond [\[7\]](#src-7)[\[8\]](#src-8). A 2007 restoration project reset the lake's water-level regime and rebuilt wetland habitat, and the lake today supports nesting habitat for four state-listed endangered bird species alongside a sport fishery (largemouth bass, muskie, walleye, northern pike) [\[7\]](#src-7). The former Nike missile site on the lake's northwest shore is Cook County Forest Preserve land (Eggers Woods) [\[7\]](#src-7).

Hyde Lake, having no physical existence to manage, has no current operations — but the ground it once occupied is squarely inside the same active-cleanup corridor described above. Whether that ground today underlies open industrial land, the CID Landfill site, or restored open space nearer Hegewisch remains genuinely unresolved in the sources consulted for this document; see Open questions.

## Notable events & incidents

- **1912-1916 — Discovery and disappearance of *Thismia americana*.** Botanist Norma Etta Pfeiffer discovered this chlorophyll-lacking, mycoheterotrophic flowering plant in 1912 in a wet-mesic sand prairie at 119th Street and Torrence Avenue, in the wetlands then still surrounding Lake Calumet — the only location it has ever been recorded anywhere in the world. It was last seen in 1916 and is presumed extinct, its habitat destroyed by the same filling and industrial development described throughout this document [\[11\]](#src-11).
- **1945 — Landfill dike failure.** Heavy rain washed out a section of the 1940 municipal landfill's ash-and-slag dike across Lake Calumet at 110th Street; the Sanitary District/city rebuilt it with slag [2 p.59].
- **1958 — St. Lawrence Seaway bet that didn't pay off.** Lake Calumet was deepened and large new slips built specifically to accommodate ocean-going freighters expected after the Seaway's opening; that traffic "never truly materialized," leaving Lake Calumet's harbor infrastructure oversized relative to the shipping it actually attracted for decades [\[6\]](#src-6).
- **1965-1966 — The Corps' pivot away from open-lake dumping.** Internal Corps correspondence in 1965 (Division Engineer Brigadier General Roy T. Dodge to Chief of Engineers Lieutenant General William F. Cassidy) marks the moment federal dredging policy for the Calumet/Chicago/Indiana Harbor complex turned decisively away from a forty-year practice of open-lake spoil disposal — the policy shift that eventually produced the shoreline disposal facilities described above [3 p.271-272].
- **1999 — Contaminated-liquid pumping at the closed Paxton landfill.** The state pumped 2.5 million gallons of contaminated liquid from the closed Paxton complex, followed in 2000 by IEPA prairie-grass planting over the cap — an early instance of the active remediation regime that continues on this land today [\[28\]](#src-28).
- **2009-2010 — Asian carp emergency.** As documented in doc [02](02-river-reversal-canals.md), a live bighead carp was found in Lake Calumet in June 2010 amid the broader Asian-carp eDNA/electric-barrier crisis, prompting lawsuits from Michigan and other Great Lakes states seeking closure of the Chicago and O'Brien locks — placing Lake Calumet briefly at the center of a Supreme Court-level interstate dispute over Great Lakes ecological protection [source: doc 02, sources 8, 22, 31].
- **2010 — Lake Calumet Cluster Superfund listing.** EPA's National Priorities List designation formalized decades of unregulated slag, dredge-spoil, and industrial-waste dumping documented throughout this history as a federally managed cleanup [\[13\]](#src-13).
- **2015 — "Chicago's tallest hill."** The capped Paxton landfill complex, at roughly 170 feet, became known locally as the tallest nonstructural point in Cook County, now overrun with a large deer herd and slated for redevelopment as the Big Marsh Bike Park [\[28\]](#src-28).

## Open questions

- **Lake Calumet's original acreage cannot be reconciled with its current acreage using the sources consulted.** Colten's "3 mi × 1.5 mi" description and the frequently cited 442-acre current figure and "about half filled" USGS estimate do not add up to consistent numbers under any straightforward reading; a primary Chicago Regional Port District or Illinois International Port District survey document (not located in this pass) would likely resolve this.
- **No source consulted names a specific company, contractor, or precise final-fill date for Hyde Lake.** The USGS fill-deposit study groups it with Deer Lake and Berry Lake and dates its filling to the 1902-1927 window generally, attributing the fill type to "steel manufacturing, petroleum refining, and storage" development without naming individual operators [1 p.24]. The strongest circumstantial link is geographic and corporate rather than documentary: EPA's own community history of the adjacent South Deering neighborhood records that Joseph H. Brown Iron & Steel Co. began operating on the Calumet River in 1875, was bought by International Harvester in 1902 (becoming the plant popularly known as Wisconsin Steel) to supply steel for farm machinery, and was joined soon after by Gold Medal Flour Co., Illinois Slag and Ballast Co., and the Federal Furnace Co. — an industrial cluster whose 1875-1902 growth window brackets Hyde Lake's own 1902-1927 disappearance and sits immediately adjacent to its mapped location [\[34\]](#src-34). No source consulted states outright that these companies' slag or waste filled Hyde Lake specifically, so this remains informed inference rather than established fact.
- **What occupies the historic Hyde Lake footprint today is unresolved.** The 1892-1901 USGS quadrangles and the 1894 Corps map place Hyde Lake close to Stony Island and the Calumet River turning basins — inside today's industrial/CID Landfill corridor — while the task brief's suggested location (roughly 122nd-130th & Torrence/Brainard) sits closer to the present-day Hegewisch Marsh Park (13200-13298 S. Torrence Avenue), a restored 130-acre natural area [\[27\]](#src-27). These may be describing overlapping but not identical ground; this document could not confirm whether any part of Hegewisch Marsh sits within Hyde Lake's historic footprint, or whether the entire historic lakebed instead lies further north/west under still-industrial land. A parcel-level GIS overlay of the 1892/1901 quadrangle against modern Hegewisch Marsh and CID Landfill boundaries would settle this.
- **Paxton I and Paxton II opening dates conflict across sources.** One set of secondary sources gives Paxton I opening in 1971 and Paxton II "two years later" (1973); another gives Paxton I in 1974 and Paxton II in 1976. This document could not locate a primary IEPA permit record to adjudicate between them, and presents both above.
- **No comprehensive workforce figures survive (in the sources consulted) for the original 1911-1922 Cal-Sag Channel excavation** — number of laborers, wage rates, or contractor-by-contractor headcounts. The fourteen-section, contractor-built structure of the project is documented, and individual equipment (steam shovels, dredges, a traveling bridge crane) is described in surviving photographs, but a Sanitary District annual report or contractor payroll record (likely in the Sanitary District's own *Proceedings of the Board of Trustees* for 1911-1922, only partially held in this archive's sources — see `sources/MANUAL-RETRIEVAL.md` item 18) would be needed to quantify the workforce.
- **The exact composition and total volume of fill placed specifically at Lake Calumet (as opposed to the whole Calumet region) is not broken out separately** in the USGS's regional fill-characterization study, which reports fill area/volume for the *entire* Calumet region (60.2 mi², 2.1×10¹⁰ ft³) rather than per water body [1 p.25]. A lake-specific volume estimate was not located.
- **Whether the current CID Landfill Superfund site and the Army Corps' Chicago Area Confined Disposal Facility at 95th Street are the same facility, adjacent facilities, or successive names for evolving infrastructure was not fully resolved.** Sources describe both a "CID" landfill (Waste Management of Illinois' Calumet City facility, cited as a data source by the USGS study) [1 p.20] and a Corps-operated "CDF"/"Confined Disposal Facility" (built 1982-1984, 43 acres, at 3600 E. 95th St.) [\[14\]](#src-14), and one EPA Superfund profile for a "CID Landfill" site separately [\[12\]](#src-12). These may be three distinct things sharing similar names and a small geographic area; a site-boundary map was not located to confirm.
