# North Branch (Lawrence Avenue) Pumping Station

## Role in the system

The North Branch Pumping Station (NBPS), also widely known by its informal name the **Lawrence Avenue Pumping Station**, sits on the east bank of the North Branch of the Chicago River at 4860 N. Francisco Avenue, immediately south of the Lawrence Avenue bridge and directly across the river from Ronan Park, in the Lincoln Square/Ravenswood area of Chicago's North Side [10][11][21]. It is owned and operated by the Metropolitan Water Reclamation District of Greater Chicago (MWRD), successor to the Sanitary District of Chicago, which built it [10][11]. In the District's own river-mile referencing system it sits at USGS river mile 333.1 upstream of Lockport, just upstream of the North Branch Dam (river mile 333.3) [9].

NBPS performs two distinct hydraulic functions that correspond to its two eras of construction:

**Dry-weather / sanitary lift function (original, 1928–1930).** Before the station existed, the Sanitary District's lakefront intercepting sewers — built 1900–1907 to seal off lakefront outfalls and stop sewage from reaching Lake Michigan — carried flow south and inland to a point where it had to be lifted before it could reach a treatment works [10][19]. NBPS was built at the Lawrence Avenue crossing of the North Branch to receive that flow (arriving via a tunnel bored under the North Shore Channel that connects to the North Side Intercepting Sewer) and lift it into the interceptor system feeding the newly built North Side Sewage Treatment Works, opened October 3, 1928 and today known as the Terrence J. O'Brien Water Reclamation Plant (WRP), 3500 Howard Street, Skokie [2][10][15]. In dry weather the station still pumps this collected sanitary flow onward to O'Brien WRP, which currently treats an average of 230 million gallons per day (MGD) with a maximum hydraulic capacity of 450 MGD, serving roughly 1.3 million people over 143 square miles of the North Side and North Shore suburbs [2].

**Storm relief / combined-sewer-overflow (CSO) function (expanded, TARP era).** The area NBPS serves is a combined sewer district — stormwater and sanitary sewage share the same pipes. During heavy rain, when local combined sewers and the downstream interceptor/tunnel system cannot convey everything fast enough, NBPS's five large discharge pumps lift the excess mixture of sewage and stormwater directly over the riverbank and into the North Branch of the Chicago River through arched outfall openings in the building's river face [1][10][11]. This discharge point is a permitted NPDES outfall under O'Brien WRP's discharge permit, carried in MWRD's own CSO inventory as outfall/tide-gate ID **107**, structures **DS-M90 and DS-M91**, one of a small number of CSO outfalls on the North Branch of the Chicago River that MWRD itself owns (as opposed to the roughly 86 total North Branch outfalls, most of which belong to the City of Chicago) [5][4].

NBPS sits close to — but functions differently from — the district-wide Tunnel and Reservoir Plan (TARP). A leg of TARP's Mainstream Tunnel System runs beneath the North Branch corridor near the station (built under contract 73-060-2H, "North Branch Chicago River, Tunnel & Connecting Structures," at a cost of $167,907,130, part of the larger $1.142-billion Mainstream System) [7], with a drop shaft located across the river in Ronan Park that intercepts local sewer flow into the deep tunnel by gravity [17]. NBPS is not that drop shaft and does not pump flow into the tunnel. Instead, MWRD activates NBPS's pumps to actively lower local combined-sewer elevations and prevent a surcharge/surge in the neighborhood sewer network — a proactive, powered intervention that is different from most of the District's CSO points, which merely spill by gravity once the interceptor or TARP tunnel is already full [6]. In MWRD's own words, responding to the Chicago Tribune in 2023: "During extreme events where the TARP system is rapidly filling, we activate the North Branch Pumping Station and Racine Avenue Pumping Station so that the large pumps can control the sewer elevations and prevent a rapid surge in the sewer system once TARP fills. These locations are different than most of our outfalls that are activated via gravity once TARP fills or conveyance limitations occur." [6]

The Mainstream Tunnel System that passes nearby is itself enormous: 220 square miles of tributary area, 40.5 miles of tunnel with 1.2 billion gallons (BG) of storage capacity, ultimately draining by gravity to the Mainstream Pumping Station (6100 South River Road, Hodgkins) which lifts captured combined sewage to the Stickney WRP, and backstopped by the McCook Reservoir (10 BG at full build-out) [7][8][22]. Sources disagree on the completed system's tunnel diameter and depth: MWRD's own post-completion TARP Status Report tables (2020 and 2025 editions) give the Mainstream Tunnel's diameter as 8 to 33 feet, consistent with the District's system-wide figure (all four TARP tunnels combined) of "8 to 33 feet in diameter and 150 to 300 feet underground" [4][7]; an EPA-published 1980 interim report, citing a partial 1979 snapshot of Mainstream contracts awarded to that point (21.4 of the eventual 40.5 miles), instead gives that partial award's tunnels as 13 to 33 feet in diameter with associated drop shafts 200 to 266 feet deep [3]. Both figures are presented here because they come from different stages of construction and it is not possible to fully reconcile them from the sources reviewed. NBPS is a small, local, much older facility layered onto the edge of this much larger 20th-century system, not a component of the Mainstream Tunnel's own pumping works.

## Original construction

The Sanitary District of Chicago built the North Branch Pumping Station between 1928 and 1930 to solve a specific problem left over from the district's turn-of-the-century sanitation work. Between 1900 and 1907 the district had built large intercepting sewers along the lakefront to stop wastewater from draining directly into Lake Michigan; that captured flow needed a place to go, and — until a treatment plant existed — the intercepting sewer discharged into the North Branch of the Chicago River at this point on Lawrence Avenue [10][19]. Construction of the permanent riverside pumping station is documented in a long run of MWRD's own dated construction photographs: excavation is visible by April 9, 1928 (photographed from the roof of Lawrence Hall, 4833 N. Francisco Avenue, across the street); further excavation for the connection to the "Lawrence Avenue conduit" is dated April 20, 1928; superstructure progress is documented on May 25, August 8, September 4, September 15, and October 26, 1928; the building appears "nearly complete" in a photograph dated October 26, 1929; and a photograph captioned "newly constructed" is dated April 26, 1930 [10]. MWRD's own captions state plainly that "the station was completed in 1929 and went into service in 1930" [10]. On October 26, 1929, MWRD also photographed miners working in a tunnel under the North Shore Channel connecting to the North Side Intercepting Sewer near the pumping station — evidence of the tunneled connection that ties the lakefront intercepting-sewer system into the new station [10].

One commenter on the historical-photo blog that documents this construction (an anonymous, unverified source, not an MWRD record) states that an earlier "Lawrence Avenue Pumping Station" had existed since 1907 on the north side of Lawrence Avenue between Broadway and the Red Line embankment, and that operations were moved to the new riverside building in 1930 [10]. This is plausible given that lakefront intercepting sewers were substantially complete by 1907 and needed somewhere to discharge in the intervening two decades, but it could not be corroborated against a primary Sanitary District source in this research pass; it is flagged in Open Questions below.

Architecturally, the building is an Art Deco (Art Deco–Moderne) structure of red brick on a granite base with limestone accents, glass-block windows, and bronze sconce lighting, deliberately designed to read as a park fieldhouse rather than an industrial facility from the Lawrence Avenue bridge, though its true function is unmistakable from the riverside, where large arched openings allow storm discharge to pour directly into the North Branch [11]. No named architect or engineer for the building could be confirmed in the sources reviewed for this report; MWRD's photo captions and secondary sources credit the builder only as "the Sanitary District of Chicago" [10][11]. No original construction cost figure was located.

David M. Solzman's *The Chicago River: An Illustrated History and Guide to the River and Its Waterways* (2nd ed., 2006, p. 75) is cited as a secondary academic source for the basic sequence — lakefront intercepting sewers built 1900–07 feeding a pumping station that discharged to the North Branch, later superseded in function once the North Side treatment plant opened [10][19]. Richard Lanyon, MWRD's former executive director, devotes his second history volume, *Draining Chicago: The Early City and the North Area* (2016), specifically to "the early years of city sewer building, eliminating sewers discharging to the lake, improvement of the North Branch, constructing the North Shore Channel and building an intercepting sewer network and water reclamation plant" — precisely the historical arc that produced NBPS — but this report was not able to access the book's text directly and cites it only bibliographically [18].

## Upgrades & modernization timeline

- **1900–1907** — Sanitary District builds lakefront intercepting sewers, eliminating direct lakefront sewage outfalls and creating the need for a pumping point on the North Branch [10][19].
- **(Disputed/unverified) 1907** — A commenter's account describes an earlier "Lawrence Avenue Pumping Station" on Lawrence Avenue near the CTA Red Line embankment, predating the riverside building; not independently confirmed [10].
- **1928–1930** — Permanent North Branch Pumping Station built at 4860 N. Francisco Avenue by the Sanitary District of Chicago; construction documented in dated photographs April 1928–April 1930; in service by 1930; tunnel connection to the North Side Intercepting Sewer under the North Shore Channel completed in the same period [10].
- **October 3, 1928** — North Side Sewage Treatment Works (today's O'Brien WRP) begins operation at 3500 Howard Street, Skokie, giving NBPS's lifted sanitary flow an actual treatment destination [2][15].
- **1963–1980 — Lawrence Avenue Underflow Sewer System (Chicago's first deep-tunnel demonstration project, forerunner of TARP).** The City of Chicago's 1963 Five-Year Capital Improvements Program called for a new auxiliary outlet sewer to relieve basement and underpass flooding bounded by the North Branch of the Chicago River, Irving Park Road, Oriole Avenue and Devon Avenue; the City's Bureau of Engineering retained Harza Engineering Company to study alternatives, and in 1966 the project (originally "Eastwood-Wilson") was redesignated the "Lawrence Avenue System" and committed to deep-rock-tunnel construction 220–250 feet below Lawrence Avenue [3]. Key contracts:
  - *Contract No. 1 — Main Shaft and Rock Tunnels*: joint venture of James McHugh Construction Co., S.A. Healy Co., and Kenny Construction Co.; bid $10,792,094; started November 6, 1967; completed May 28, 1973; built 16,638 ft of 12-ft-diameter and 9,126 ft of 17-ft-diameter concrete-lined rock tunnel and a 29-ft (earth)/27-ft (rock) diameter, ~250-ft-deep main shaft [3].
  - *Contract No. 3 — Pumping Station and Outfall*: same contractor joint venture; bid $1,977,000; location given as "Lawrence Avenue at North Branch of Chicago River" — the NBPS site; started April 17, 1972; ~96.65% complete as of February 1979 after repeated delays from submersible-pump cable and junction-box failures; installed four German-made KSB submersible dewatering pumps (5,000 gpm / 535 hp each) to drain the tunnel to the sanitary interceptor after storms [3].
  - *Contract No. 4 — Additional High-Level Sewers*: contractor Jay-Dee Contractors, Inc.; bid $2,644,875; started May 1, 1974; completed June 30, 1975 [3].
  - The overall system, funded partly by U.S. EPA Demonstration Grant No. 11020 EMD, was projected (and later measured in a multi-year computer/field study) to cut combined-sewer overflow events on this drainage area from an average of 59 per year (235 hours) to about 5 per year (18 hours) — a roughly 92 percent reduction — while cutting suspended-solids and BOD loading reaching the North Branch by 73 and 78 percent respectively [3].
- **1975–1980s — TARP Mainstream System construction.** Contract 73-060-2H, "North Branch Chicago River, Tunnel & Connecting Structures" ($167,907,130), builds the Mainstream Tunnel System's North Branch leg near the station, including a drop shaft in adjacent Ronan Park [7][17]. It is a reasonable but unconfirmed inference — flagged in Open Questions — that NBPS's storm-pump capacity was substantially enlarged to its current five-pump, 1,500-cfs configuration in this period, since that capacity vastly exceeds the ~48-cfs 1970s KSB pump bank and matches the scale of the much larger Mainstream System tributary area (220 sq mi, versus the 3,620-acre/5.7-sq-mi Lawrence Avenue pilot tunnel) [1][3][7].
- **March 1, 2002** — MWRD begins a continuing NPDES sampling program for every discharge from NBPS into the North Branch of the Chicago River, per O'Brien WRP's discharge permit [4][5].
- **August 2, 2018** — MWRD awards Contract 16-079-3D, "Rehabilitation of North Branch Pumping Station, NSA," to Path Construction Company, Inc., for $4,613,450 (500 calendar days), financed through the Illinois Clean Water State Revolving Fund. The project justification states that the five 300-cfs discharge pumps' high-velocity flow had eroded the concrete building columns below the waterline by more than 20 percent, and in some cases up to 50 percent, of their original cross-section; scope included grouted fiberglass-reinforced-plastic jackets around the columns, underwater repair of the east wall and under-deck wall, repair of deteriorated concrete stairs and deck, and restoration of the balustrade and deck lighting [1].
- **2023–2026 (in progress; specific MWRD contract number not identified in sources reviewed)** — A larger structural rehabilitation, again performed by Path Construction, addresses continued deterioration of the "100-year-old" facility: partial-depth refacing of all exterior concrete walls, full replacement of the cantilevered boat dock and staircase over the river, and replacement of elevated concrete slabs above the storm discharge pumps, along with new architectural masonry to restore the building's original appearance. The project has an approximately 2.5-year duration; as of a February 5, 2026 project profile, roughly 80 percent of exterior wall repairs were complete and the old boat dock had been fully demolished. Work requires professional divers (winter water temperatures of 30–40°F), barges, and boom lifts, and the station remains operational and continues to function as an overflow facility throughout construction [12].

## Current operations

NBPS is operated as an outstation of the Terrence J. O'Brien WRP; an "OWRP Operating Engineer" at O'Brien WRP monitors the station remotely and is the point of contact (via extensions x88380/x88381) for entry, a silent intrusion alarm, and coordination of NPDES sampling [4]. Field Services/Industrial Waste Division (IWD) North Office personnel hold the Medeco key needed for physical entry [4]. Upon activation of the station's first storm pump, the OWRP Operating Engineer notifies the District's Systems Dispatcher, who assigns IWD-North staff to sample the discharge from an automatic composite sampler mounted on the south wall of the building's lower level [4]. Sampling requirements under the O'Brien WRP NPDES permit include a one-gallon composite for general chemistry, a composite for trace metals, and a grab sample for pH (taken 30 minutes to 2 hours after the first pump activates) for every 24-hour period of discharge; results for BOD5/TOC, suspended solids, cadmium, and hardness are reported by MWRD's Monitoring & Research laboratory at the Lue-Hing R&D Complex [4].

Operationally, NBPS is treated by MWRD as one of a short list of "major pumping stations" (alongside Racine Avenue Pumping Station on the South Fork of the South Branch of the Chicago River, and the 95th Street and 122nd Street Pumping Stations on the Calumet River) whose discharges into local waterways are separately, specifically monitored under the WRPs' NPDES permits [4]. Every CSO event at NBPS also triggers a standing fish-kill response protocol: two days after a pumping event (or the following Monday if it falls on a weekend), MWRD's Channel Maintenance Unit dispatches boat crews to inspect the river downstream for fish kills, cleans up if warranted, and files both an oral and written report to the Illinois EPA [4]. Water-quality context for the surrounding stretch of river is also actively managed: an instream aeration station at Webster Avenue adds oxygen to the North Branch (a companion station at Devon Avenue serves the North Shore Channel), controlled using a dissolved-oxygen probe read remotely at O'Brien WRP from the Ohio Street bridgehouse on the main Chicago River [4].

The five storm-discharge pumps are activated on an as-needed basis during large storms specifically to draw down local combined-sewer elevations before they surcharge — a proactive, powered response rather than a passive weir overflow — which MWRD describes as distinguishing NBPS (along with Racine Avenue Pumping Station) from most of its other CSO points [6]. The station's discharge is tracked as MWRD-owned CSO outfall/telemetry ID 107 (tide-gate structures DS-M90 and DS-M91) as part of the District's Consent Decree–mandated CSO telemetry network, which uses proximity switches on tide gates reporting to workstations at the Stickney, Calumet, and Kirie WRPs (with a move to cellular communications underway under a separate District contract) [4].

## Notable events & incidents

- **July 2–3, 2023 storm.** The National Weather Service recorded rainfall totals of roughly 3 to 7 inches across greater Chicago, with some localized areas exceeding 8 inches; the worst flooding hit the west and southwest sides and near-west/southwest suburbs [6]. Even though the McCook Reservoir was only 17 percent full as of 2 a.m. on July 2, CSOs began by 8:19 a.m., and NBPS began actively releasing sewage and stormwater into the North Branch at 9:44 a.m. — before the region's primary TARP reservoir was anywhere near capacity. MWRD, responding publicly to the Chicago Tribune, used this event to explain that NBPS and Racine Avenue Pumping Station pump proactively to control local sewer elevations, rather than waiting for TARP itself to fill and spill by gravity [6].
- **March 11, 2026.** Residents across several North Side neighborhoods along the North Branch of the Chicago River and North Shore Channel — including Lincoln Square, Albany Park, North Center, Irving Park, Horner Park, and Roscoe Village — reported a strong sewage-like ("skunk"-like, per some residents) odor. MWRD spokesperson Allison Fore confirmed District staff investigated and also detected the smell; search-indexed excerpts of the WTTW report state that "many pointed blame in the direction of a nearby pumping station" and that a Deep Tunnel drop shaft located near the North Branch Pumping Station was linked to the problem, and MWRD invited residents to report odors via its hotline (800-332-3867) or online system [16]. Because NBPS sits directly on this stretch of river and adjoins one of the area's TARP drop shafts (Ronan Park), it is very likely implicated in or adjacent to this incident, but the full WTTW article text remained access-blocked (HTTP 403) on repeated retrieval attempts, so this is sourced only to search-engine-indexed excerpts rather than the primary text and is still flagged as an open question below.
- **2018–2020 concrete erosion repair.** MWRD's own contract documents describe the storm pumps' high-velocity discharge as having eroded the below-waterline concrete building columns by 20–50 percent of their original cross-section, necessitating the $4.6 million Contract 16-079-3D repair — itself a notable operational consequence of the station's storm-relief role [1].
- **2023–2026 major structural rehabilitation**, described above, undertaken because of "widespread and visible structural degradation" after roughly a century of service, including replacement of the river-spanning boat dock and staircase and the elevated slabs directly above the storm pumps [12].
- **Circa 1999 (low-confidence, minor/cultural).** A commenter on the Industrial History blog recalls seeing film actors Kathleen Turner and Jason Alexander filming a scene inside the building around 1999, for what the commenter describes as "a terrible movie." No film title, date, or independent confirmation was found; included here only as a documented local anecdote, not a verified fact [10].

### Disambiguation: this is NOT a City of Chicago drinking-water pumping station

Because Lincoln Square has more than one historic "pumping station" from roughly the same era, it is worth stating plainly what NBPS is not. The **Thomas Jefferson Pumping Station**, at 2250 W. Eastwood Avenue — about a mile from NBPS — is a City of Chicago Department of Water Management facility that pressurizes purified Lake Michigan drinking water for distribution through the city's water mains; it was designed by city architect Argyle Robinson (city architect 1926–1929) and became operational in August 1928, almost exactly contemporaneous with NBPS's construction, but it has nothing to do with sewage or stormwater [13]. Similarly, the **Mayfair Pumping Station**, at 4850 W. Wilson Avenue, is another City drinking-water pumping station (opened 1913 per some sources, 1915–1918 per city records — see the water-pumping document in this archive; originally steam-driven, later upgraded) supplying the northwest side and suburbs [14]. Both are operated by the City of Chicago; NBPS is operated by MWRD, a separate unit of government, and moves wastewater and stormwater, not drinking water. General web searches for "North Branch Pumping Station" or "Lawrence Avenue Pumping Station" occasionally surface unrelated facilities of the same generic name elsewhere in the country (for example, a "North Pumping Station" in South Bend, Indiana, and a "North Branch" commuter-rail station in New Jersey turned up as false-positive image matches during this research and are not related in any way) [confirmed by direct verification during this research].

## Open questions

- **Exact date and contract number for the pump-capacity expansion to five 300-cfs pumps.** The 1970s Lawrence Avenue Underflow Sewer System installed only four ~11-cfs KSB submersible pumps (≈48 cfs combined) at this site [3], while the station's current, much larger 1,500-cfs (5×300 cfs) discharge capacity is documented only in a 2018 rehabilitation contract [1] that treats the pumps as pre-existing. No source located in this research directly documents when or under what contract the larger pumps were installed — the TARP Mainstream System era (1975–1980s) is a plausible but unconfirmed hypothesis based on scale and chronology.
- **Whether the 1970s KSB submersible dewatering pumps are still in service, were replaced by the current storm pumps, or coexist as a separate dry-weather system.** Not resolved by available sources.
- **Capacity of the dry-weather sanitary lift pumps** that move normal flow onward to the O'Brien WRP interceptor — no MGD or gpm figure was found; only the five storm-discharge pumps (300 cfs each) are documented numerically.
- **Named architect and/or chief engineer of the 1928–1930 building.** Not identified in any source reviewed; MWRD photo captions and secondary sources credit only "the Sanitary District of Chicago" as builder.
- **Original construction cost.** No figure located.
- **Verification of the "1907 Lawrence Avenue Pumping Station" predecessor claim**, sourced only to an anonymous blog comment, not to a primary Sanitary District record.
- **The exact contract number, award date, and dollar value of the 2023–2026 boat-dock/wall-refacing rehabilitation**, known only through a contractor's own project profile [12], not yet cross-confirmed against an MWRD board-award record or SRF public notice.
- **Direct confirmation that the March 2026 North Side odor incident implicated NBPS specifically** (as opposed to other nearby Deep Tunnel shafts) — the primary WTTW reporting could not be fully retrieved due to access restrictions during this research; only search-engine summaries were available.
- **National Register of Historic Places or Chicago Landmark status.** No evidence was found that the building carries any formal historic-preservation designation; a search explicitly for this returned no confirming record, despite the building's evident architectural and historical significance.
- **Whether Richard Lanyon's *Draining Chicago: The Early City and the North Area* (2016) contains additional primary-source detail on the station's design and engineers** — the book was identified as directly on-topic but its full text could not be accessed in this research pass.
