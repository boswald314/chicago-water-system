# North Branch (Lawrence Avenue) Pumping Station

## Role in the system

The North Branch Pumping Station (NBPS), also widely known by its informal name the **Lawrence Avenue Pumping Station**, sits on the east bank of the North Branch of the Chicago River at 4860 N. Francisco Avenue (building footprint 4840–4860 N.), immediately **north** of the Lawrence Avenue bridge and roughly across the river from Ronan Park's natural area, in the Lincoln Square/Ravenswood area of Chicago's North Side [\[10\]](#src-10)[\[11\]](#src-11)[\[21\]](#src-21)[\[26\]](#src-26)[\[27\]](#src-27). It is owned and operated by the Metropolitan Water Reclamation District of Greater Chicago (MWRD), successor to the Sanitary District of Chicago, which built it [\[10\]](#src-10)[\[11\]](#src-11). In the District's own river-mile referencing system it sits at USGS river mile 333.1 upstream of Lockport — about 0.2 mile **downstream** of the site of the former North Branch Dam (river mile 333.3, south of Foster Avenue at River Park), where the North Shore Channel met the natural North Branch until the dam was removed in 2018 [\[9\]](#src-9)[\[25\]](#src-25). *(Correction, July 2026: an earlier version of this document inverted this relationship, placing the station "just upstream" of the dam; the cited MWRD table [\[9\]](#src-9) lists Lawrence Avenue at mile 332.9, the station at 333.1, and the dam at 333.3 — miles increase moving upstream.)* *(Second correction, July 2026: this pass re-verified the station's position relative to the Lawrence Avenue bridge, which an earlier version of this document also had backwards. The building's Chicago street-grid address (4840–4860 N.) is entirely north of the 4800 N baseline that Lawrence Avenue itself occupies, and direct measurement of the mapped building footprint against the mapped Lawrence Avenue bridge crossing confirms the station's near edge sits roughly 230 feet, and its far edge roughly 480 feet, north of the bridge — well under a city block, but north, not south, as previously stated. The station's east-bank position and its rough alignment across the river from Ronan Park's natural area — which runs along the west bank from Lawrence Avenue north to Argyle Street — are both independently confirmed and unchanged from the earlier text [\[26\]](#src-26)[\[27\]](#src-27).)*

NBPS performs two distinct hydraulic functions that correspond to its two eras of construction:

**Dry-weather / sanitary lift function (original, 1928–1930).** Before the riverside station existed, the original inland Lawrence Avenue Sewage Pumping Station (1907, see below) lifted local North Side sewage about 10 feet into the head of the westward 16-foot Lawrence Avenue conduit, which then ran the rest of the way **by gravity** to a straight, untreated outfall on the North Branch at this same Lawrence Avenue site [\[33\]](#src-33)[\[34\]](#src-34). The Sanitary District's own 1928 construction and engineering publications resolve exactly what changed hydraulically when the new station replaced it: NBPS was designed to **receive that flow by gravity**, through the same 16-foot conduit — now also serving flow newly added from the City's Broadway relief sewer and other sewers reaching the conduit west of Broadway — and the inland station was formally "abandoned upon the completion of the North Branch Station" [\[23\]](#src-23)[\[24\]](#src-24). The lift was therefore not relocated or paralleled but eliminated at the inland site and moved to the river end: NBPS's own dry-weather pumps lift the gravity-delivered flow up into the North Side Intercepting Sewer, crossing under the North Shore Channel via the tunnel documented under construction in 1929 (below), after which the interceptor carries it onward **by gravity** — with no further pumping at NBPS — north along its west-bank alignment to the newly built North Side Sewage Treatment Works, opened October 3, 1928 and today known as the Terrence J. O'Brien Water Reclamation Plant (WRP), 3500 Howard Street, Skokie [\[2\]](#src-2)[\[10\]](#src-10)[\[15\]](#src-15)[\[23\]](#src-23)[\[24\]](#src-24). A second, wholly separate lift occurs only at the plant itself, where an influent pumping station unrelated to NBPS raises the arriving interceptor flow about 44 feet into the grit chambers before the rest of the treatment train proceeds by gravity [\[24\]](#src-24); MWRD describes its 560-mile intercepting-sewer network overall as "designed to flow downhill by gravity, with a couple of exceptions where pumping stations help keep the water moving" — NBPS is one of those exceptions [\[39\]](#src-39). In dry weather the station still pumps this collected sanitary flow onward to O'Brien WRP, which currently treats an average of 230 million gallons per day (MGD) with a maximum hydraulic capacity of 450 MGD, serving roughly 1.3 million people over 143 square miles of the North Side and North Shore suburbs [\[2\]](#src-2). Full documentation for this hydraulic profile — original pump specifications, the interceptor's specific route, and a discrepancy between the two 1928 sources on storm-pump capacity — is in "The 1928 hydraulic reconfiguration," below.

**Storm relief / combined-sewer-overflow (CSO) function (part of the original 1928–1930 design, capacity since modestly increased).** *(Correction, July 2026: an earlier version of this document labeled this function "expanded, TARP era" and speculated in Open Questions that the five-pump storm configuration dated to the 1975–1980s TARP buildout. Newly reviewed Sanitary District primary sources from 1928 — see "The 1928 hydraulic reconfiguration," below — document five large storm-flow pumps as part of NBPS's original design, predating TARP by decades; that speculation is withdrawn.)* The area NBPS serves is a combined sewer district — stormwater and sanitary sewage share the same pipes. During heavy rain, when local combined sewers and the downstream interceptor/tunnel system cannot convey everything fast enough, NBPS's five large discharge pumps lift the excess mixture of sewage and stormwater directly over the riverbank and into the North Branch of the Chicago River through arched outfall openings in the building's river face [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[10\]](#src-10)[\[11\]](#src-11). This discharge point is a permitted NPDES outfall under O'Brien WRP's discharge permit, carried in MWRD's own CSO inventory as outfall/tide-gate ID **107**, structures **DS-M90 and DS-M91**, one of a small number of CSO outfalls on the North Branch of the Chicago River that MWRD itself owns (as opposed to the roughly 86 total North Branch outfalls, most of which belong to the City of Chicago) [\[5\]](#src-5)[\[4\]](#src-4).

NBPS sits close to — but functions differently from — the district-wide Tunnel and Reservoir Plan (TARP). A leg of TARP's Mainstream Tunnel System runs beneath the North Branch corridor near the station (built under contract 73-060-2H, "North Branch Chicago River, Tunnel & Connecting Structures," at a cost of $167,907,130, part of the larger $1.142-billion Mainstream System) [\[7\]](#src-7), with a drop shaft located across the river in Ronan Park that intercepts local sewer flow into the deep tunnel by gravity [\[17\]](#src-17). NBPS is not that drop shaft and does not pump flow into the tunnel. Instead, MWRD activates NBPS's pumps to actively lower local combined-sewer elevations and prevent a surcharge/surge in the neighborhood sewer network — a proactive, powered intervention that is different from most of the District's CSO points, which merely spill by gravity once the interceptor or TARP tunnel is already full [\[6\]](#src-6). In MWRD's own words, responding to the Chicago Tribune in 2023: "During extreme events where the TARP system is rapidly filling, we activate the North Branch Pumping Station and Racine Avenue Pumping Station so that the large pumps can control the sewer elevations and prevent a rapid surge in the sewer system once TARP fills. These locations are different than most of our outfalls that are activated via gravity once TARP fills or conveyance limitations occur." [\[6\]](#src-6)

The Mainstream Tunnel System that passes nearby is itself enormous: 220 square miles of tributary area, 40.5 miles of tunnel with 1.2 billion gallons (BG) of storage capacity, ultimately draining by gravity to the Mainstream Pumping Station (6100 South River Road, Hodgkins) which lifts captured combined sewage to the Stickney WRP, and backstopped by the McCook Reservoir (10 BG at full build-out) [\[7\]](#src-7)[\[8\]](#src-8)[\[22\]](#src-22). Sources disagree on the completed system's tunnel diameter and depth: MWRD's own post-completion TARP Status Report tables (2020 and 2025 editions) give the Mainstream Tunnel's diameter as 8 to 33 feet, consistent with the District's system-wide figure (all four TARP tunnels combined) of "8 to 33 feet in diameter and 150 to 300 feet underground" [\[4\]](#src-4)[\[7\]](#src-7); an EPA-published 1980 interim report, citing a partial 1979 snapshot of Mainstream contracts awarded to that point (21.4 of the eventual 40.5 miles), instead gives that partial award's tunnels as 13 to 33 feet in diameter with associated drop shafts 200 to 266 feet deep [\[3\]](#src-3). Both figures are presented here because they come from different stages of construction and it is not possible to fully reconcile them from the sources reviewed. NBPS is a small, local, much older facility layered onto the edge of this much larger 20th-century system, not a component of the Mainstream Tunnel's own pumping works.

## The North Branch Dam next door

Two-tenths of a mile upstream of NBPS, at the confluence where the North Shore Channel meets the natural North Branch of the Chicago River (river mile 333.3, immediately south of Foster Avenue at River Park), the Sanitary District of Chicago — MWRD's predecessor — built a low dam in 1910 as part of the North Shore Channel project, the same construction campaign that tied the lakefront intercepting-sewer system into the North Branch corridor NBPS would later serve [\[9\]](#src-9)[\[25\]](#src-25). The North Shore Channel's own bed, and the lower North Branch it fed into, were engineered to a lower, canal-controlled water elevation than the natural, un-channelized North Branch immediately upstream; multiple accounts put the resulting difference at roughly four feet, and the dam was built to hold the upper river at its own separate elevation and keep that four-foot head differential from eroding the riverbed and banks working upstream from the confluence [\[25\]](#src-25)[\[28\]](#src-28)[\[31\]](#src-31). A U.S. Army Corps of Engineers spokesman quoted around the time of removal gave a complementary account of the same function, describing the dam as preventing water from draining out of the upper river too rapidly and guarding against reversed flow into the system's northern reaches [\[31\]](#src-31).

Physically the dam was modest — a low-head concrete structure with a single four-foot vertical drop — but for the century it stood it was hydraulically and ecologically significant out of proportion to its size: it was the only waterfall on the entire Chicago River system, a complete barrier to upstream fish passage, and an obstacle canoeists and kayakers had to portage around [\[25\]](#src-25)[\[28\]](#src-28). A fisheries survey using electrofishing above the dam before removal found a single green sunfish in the North Branch upstream of it, against 35 native species already documented in the North Shore Channel just downstream [\[28\]](#src-28).

Friends of the Chicago River began campaigning for the dam's removal in 2000, opening an 18-year effort that combined feasibility studies, fisheries surveys, and sustained advocacy with the Army Corps, MWRD, and the Chicago Park District [\[25\]](#src-25)[\[30\]](#src-30). The Corps' Chicago District ultimately executed the physical removal under its Section 506 Great Lakes Fishery and Ecosystem Restoration (GLFER) Program authority — a 65 percent federal / 35 percent local cost-share program — with MWRD and the Chicago Park District as the local cost-share partners [\[29\]](#src-29)[\[32\]](#src-32). Reported figures for the dam-removal phase itself range from roughly $2.1 million (the Phase 1 estimate approved in 2017) to $4.5–4.6 million (figures reported around the July 2018 demolition, likely reflecting a later, fuller accounting of the same phase); a costlier Phase 2 — riverbank and habitat restoration extending into the adjoining Legion and Ronan Parks, estimated near $12.4 million — remained contingent on additional federal funding as of the removal date and is not confirmed as complete in sources reviewed [\[29\]](#src-29)[\[31\]](#src-31). Demolition began July 31, 2018, marked with a public ceremony at River Park attended by the Corps, MWRD, the Park District, and Friends of the Chicago River, and the concrete dam and apron were replaced with a graded, naturalized channel of riffles, step pools, cobbles, gravel, and sand — trading the single four-foot drop for a series of roughly one-foot steps fish can ascend — alongside restoration of 14 acres of riverbank and 5 acres of aquatic beds [\[25\]](#src-25)[\[28\]](#src-28)[\[30\]](#src-30)[\[31\]](#src-31). A 2016 environmental assessment had already found the dam's secondary flood/erosion-control function "mostly obsolete," in part because a separate Foster Avenue stormwater diversion tunnel had taken over that role — one reason the structure could be removed without a hydraulic replacement, leaving the river's post-removal elevation control to the riffle-pool grading itself rather than to any single structure [\[31\]](#src-31).

The results were immediate and measurable: a pre-removal survey above the dam found essentially no fish, while a 2019 post-removal survey documented 266 fish across 17 native species in the same stretch, part of a longer-term MWRD trend that has seen the number of fish species recorded across the whole Chicago Area Waterway System grow from 10 in 1974 to 77 by the early 2020s [\[30\]](#src-30). Removal also opened roughly 20 additional miles of the North Branch to upstream fish passage and made the River Park stretch safer for paddlers, who no longer need to portage [\[25\]](#src-25)[\[28\]](#src-28). NBPS itself, two-tenths of a mile downstream, was not part of the dam or its removal project and did not need to be — but its own stretch of river sits immediately downstream of the dam's former pool, and the same River Park/Ronan Park riverbank-restoration program that grew out of the dam's removal directly touches the natural area described above, directly across the river from NBPS [\[29\]](#src-29).

## Original construction

The Sanitary District of Chicago built the North Branch Pumping Station between 1928 and 1930 to solve a specific problem left over from the district's turn-of-the-century sanitation work. Between 1900 and 1907 the district had built large intercepting sewers along the lakefront to stop wastewater from draining directly into Lake Michigan; that captured flow needed a place to go, and — until a treatment plant existed — the intercepting sewer discharged into the North Branch of the Chicago River at this point on Lawrence Avenue [\[10\]](#src-10)[\[19\]](#src-19). Construction of the permanent riverside pumping station is documented in a long run of MWRD's own dated construction photographs: excavation is visible by April 9, 1928 (photographed from the roof of Lawrence Hall, 4833 N. Francisco Avenue, across the street); further excavation for the connection to the "Lawrence Avenue conduit" is dated April 20, 1928; superstructure progress is documented on May 25, August 8, September 4, September 15, and October 26, 1928; the building appears "nearly complete" in a photograph dated October 26, 1929; and a photograph captioned "newly constructed" is dated April 26, 1930 [\[10\]](#src-10). MWRD's own captions state plainly that "the station was completed in 1929 and went into service in 1930" [\[10\]](#src-10). On October 26, 1929, MWRD also photographed miners working in a tunnel under the North Shore Channel connecting to the North Side Intercepting Sewer near the pumping station — evidence of the tunneled connection that ties the lakefront intercepting-sewer system into the new station [\[10\]](#src-10).

A commenter on the historical-photo blog that documents this construction (an anonymous, unverified source, not an MWRD record) states that an earlier "Lawrence Avenue Pumping Station" had existed since 1907 on the north side of Lawrence Avenue between Broadway and the Red Line embankment, and that operations were moved to the new riverside building in 1930 [\[10\]](#src-10). That predecessor is now corroborated by primary sources on both its existence and its precise date, location, and capacity — see "The original Lawrence Avenue Pumping Station, 1907–1930" below. The Sanitary District's own 1928 publications, obtained for this archive as page-image harvests, separately caption a construction photograph of the new riverside station as replacing "the Lawrence Avenue station" at **four times its capacity** [\[23\]](#src-23), and the companion *North Side Sewage Treatment Project* volume captions an excavation-phase photograph — showing the six intake wells under construction — as "North Branch Pumping Station, a $2,500,000 unit in the great North Side Project… note the six huge intakes for its double-barreled suction pumps" [\[24\]](#src-24).

Architecturally, the building is an Art Deco (Art Deco–Moderne) structure of red brick on a granite base with limestone accents, glass-block windows, and bronze sconce lighting, deliberately designed to read as a park fieldhouse rather than an industrial facility from the Lawrence Avenue bridge, though its true function is unmistakable from the riverside, where large arched openings allow storm discharge to pour directly into the North Branch [\[11\]](#src-11). No named architect or engineer for the building could be confirmed in the sources reviewed for this report; MWRD's photo captions and secondary sources credit the builder only as "the Sanitary District of Chicago" [\[10\]](#src-10)[\[11\]](#src-11). The Sanitary District's own 1928 *North Side Sewage Treatment Project* volume describes the station as "a $2,500,000 unit" (nominal 1928 dollars) in one caption; the same volume's own final project cost-summary table gives a more precise total construction cost of $2,336,980.00, nearly matching *Engineering Works*' independent contemporaneous estimate of $2,337,000.00 — see "The 1928 hydraulic reconfiguration," below [\[23\]](#src-23)[\[24\]](#src-24).

David M. Solzman's *The Chicago River: An Illustrated History and Guide to the River and Its Waterways* (2nd ed., 2006, p. 75) is cited as a secondary academic source for the basic sequence — lakefront intercepting sewers built 1900–07 feeding a pumping station that discharged to the North Branch, later superseded in function once the North Side treatment plant opened [\[10\]](#src-10)[\[19\]](#src-19). Richard Lanyon, MWRD's former executive director, devotes his second history volume, *Draining Chicago: The Early City and the North Area* (2016), specifically to "the early years of city sewer building, eliminating sewers discharging to the lake, improvement of the North Branch, constructing the North Shore Channel and building an intercepting sewer network and water reclamation plant" — precisely the historical arc that produced NBPS — but this report was not able to access the book's text directly and cites it only bibliographically [\[18\]](#src-18).

### The original Lawrence Avenue Pumping Station, 1907–1930

NBPS's own construction record starts in 1928, but the pumping function it took over did not — and the true predecessor was not on the river at all. Two primary sources newly reviewed for this archive, previously unavailable, turn what had been only an anonymous blog comment into a well-documented station in its own right.

C. D. Hill, in a paper on "The Sewerage System of Chicago" read to the Western Society of Engineers and published in its *Journal* in September 1911, describes the city's north-side lakefront intercepting sewer converging at "a pumping station at Lawrence Avenue east of Evanston Avenue" [\[34\]](#src-34) — Evanston Avenue was renamed Broadway in 1913, so "east of Evanston Avenue" is equivalent to "east of Broadway." The Sanitary District's own July 1921 *Engineering Data on the Sanitary District of Chicago* tabulates the same plant outright as the "Lawrence Avenue Sewage Pumping Station," giving its location as "Lawrence Ave. and C. M. & St. P. R. R. (N. W. El.)" — the crossing of Lawrence Avenue with the railroad/elevated embankment immediately east of Broadway [\[33\]](#src-33). Both descriptions match the 2024 blog commenter's account of a site "on the north side of Lawrence between Broadway and the Red Line" almost exactly, from sources the commenter could not have seen [\[10\]](#src-10).

The date is also now independently corroborated, and resolves in favor of 1907 rather than 1909. The Sanitary District's 1919 *History of its Growth and Development* states that the north-side and south-side (39th Street) intercepting-sewer systems, "with their respective pumping stations, ... were completed about the same time in the year 1907 and placed in operation" [\[35\]](#src-35) — an institutional account from the District's own law department, written a little over a decade after the fact and independent of both the 1921 engineering tabulation and the 2024 blog comment, yet in agreement with both. The 1921 *Engineering Data* volume further identifies the station's builder as the **City of Chicago**, not the Sanitary District [\[33\]](#src-33) — consistent with Hill's 1911 account of an agreement, reached following Mayor Swift's 1896 sewer commission, between the City and the District under which the City's 39th Street and Lawrence Avenue intercepting-sewer conduits were each built oversized, so that beyond carrying intercepted sewage they could also carry lake water pumped through to flush the South and North Branches of the river [\[34\]](#src-34).

The 1921 *Engineering Data* table also gives the plant's equipment in useful detail: a boiler house with two 280-horsepower Edgemoor water-tube boilers on Green chain-grate stokers; a 585-cubic-foot-per-second screw pump (13-foot-6-inch, six-blade propeller, driven by a triple-expansion vertical engine) dedicated to forcing lake water through to flush the North Branch; and three vertical-shaft sewage pumps (one rated at 37.5 cfs against a 10-foot lift, two more at 25 cfs each against a 5-foot lift), all Allis-Chalmers-built and Tomlinson-condensed, together documented as pumping "25 to 192 million gallons" of sewage per 24 hours from a roughly 6-square-mile drainage area [\[33\]](#src-33). (This 1907 station is unrelated to the separate 1972–79 "Lawrence Avenue" tunnel-dewatering pumps listed in the Facilities & capacities table above, which were installed decades later at the *riverside* NBPS site itself, not at this inland predecessor location [\[3\]](#src-3).)

How the sewage actually reached the river from this inland site is answered by the same sources: the station discharged into the **Lawrence Avenue conduit**, a gravity conduit running west beneath Lawrence Avenue to an outfall on the North Branch at Lawrence Avenue — the site where the riverside NBPS stands today, and the same conduit the 1928 construction photographs show the new station being connected to (excavation "for the connection to the 'Lawrence Avenue conduit'," April 20, 1928 [\[10\]](#src-10)). Hill's 1911 paper treats the conduit as a major artery in its own right: the Ravenswood-area relief sewers were tied into it "at Robey Street and at Western Avenue," and he weighs it directly against the older Fullerton Avenue conduit — a lake-to-river flushing conduit with a mid-route pumping station — noting that "since the opening of the Lawrence Avenue conduit it has been suggested that the operation of the Fullerton Avenue pumping station should be abandoned" [\[34\]](#src-34). The pairing worked like the South Side's better-known 39th Street conduit and pumping station: the three vertical-shaft pumps lifted intercepted sewage about 10 feet into the westward conduit, while the huge low-head screw pump (585 cfs at only ~3 feet of lift) drove **lake water** down the same corridor to flush the North Branch [\[33\]](#src-33). *(Correction/extension, July 2026: an earlier version of this document treated the conduit's lake connection as an inference drawn only from Hill's Fullerton Avenue comparison, since no source then in hand described the conduit's full alignment explicitly. Newly reviewed Sanitary District Board of Trustees proceedings for 1901 and 1902 remove that ambiguity — see below.)*

**The conduit's authorized alignment and dimensions.** The conduit's east end, length, and diameter were fixed by the same city–district agreements that created the pumping station, not left to inference. A Chicago City Council ordinance adopted January 31, 1898, and approved by Mayor Carter H. Harrison II on February 2, 1898, authorized the Commissioner of Public Works to fix the final size of the two intercepting-sewer conduits only tentatively sized by the 1896–97 Pure Water Commission, specifying "a 20-foot conduit on Thirty-ninth Street, from Lake Michigan to the Halsted Street slip" and "a 16-foot conduit on Lawrence Avenue, from Lake Michigan to the Chicago River" [\[36\]](#src-36). The Sanitary District's reciprocal ordinance of February 4, 1898, and a formal Agreement the two governments executed November 18, 1899, restate the same alignment, obligating the City to build and eventually turn over to the District "the conduit from Lake Michigan in Lawrence Avenue through the Lawrence Avenue Pumping Station...to the North Branch of the Chicago River," complete with "intakes for water from Lake Michigan" and "protecting piers" [\[36\]](#src-36). This settles, from the governing legal instruments rather than by analogy to Fullerton Avenue, that the conduit's east end was designed from the outset as a Lake Michigan shore intake.

**Construction status, 1901: dimensions, an intake, and a proposal to abandon it.** A Board of Engineers exhibit dated April 26, 1901, prepared for the Sanitary District's Special Commission of Experts, gives the fullest technical description of the conduit located in this research: as then under construction by the City, it was 14,026 feet (2.657 miles) long and 16 feet inside diameter, of which 4,871 feet had been completed by December 31, 1900 (cost $220,463), with the remainder — the source's own subtotals for the remaining footage disagree by roughly 500 feet, giving either about 9,635 or 9,655 feet — to be finished by December 31, 1902 at an estimated further cost of $604,756 "for conduit, intake and regulating works, and an additional sum for the pumping station" [\[36\]](#src-36). The same exhibit's pumping-head calculations use "Sheridan Road" as the datum point for the lake end of the line, placing the conduit's lakefront intake in the vicinity of Sheridan Road on the Uptown lakefront, east of the inland pumping station itself; no further description of the intake structure (a crib, a shore-connected pier, or otherwise) was located in this source [\[36\]](#src-36).

That same 1901 exhibit shows the District's own engineers seriously weighing whether to build the lake intake at all. With the not-yet-built North Shore Channel offered as an alternative way to flush the North Branch, the Special Commission costed out a scaled-back alternative that would have dropped the lake connection entirely — an 11-foot sewer-only outfall running 12,776 feet from Sheridan Road to the North Branch, with no intake — at roughly half the gross capital of the full lake-to-river design ($1,828,822 versus $3,084,028) [\[36\]](#src-36). The Sanitary District's own subsequent accounting (the 1902 proceedings already cited above for this same $1,828,822 figure) adopted the reduced, sewer-outfall-only number as its official cost estimate for "completing" the conduit [\[36\]](#src-36). Yet the station as actually built and operated retained the full dual-purpose design: Hill's 1911 account of the two conduits having been "built oversized" so they could carry lake water as well as sewage, and the 1921 *Engineering Data* table's 585-cfs lake-water screw pump, both describe a completed station with a working lake connection, not the descoped sewer-only version the 1901 committee had costed as an economy measure [\[33\]](#src-33)[\[34\]](#src-34). Whether the full lake-intake plan was formally reinstated by a later, unlocated agreement, or the 1901 committee's alternative was simply never adopted despite appearing in the District's own capital accounting, is not resolved by the sources reviewed for this research — but the lake connection was, on this evidence, actually built and put into service, answering the user-facing question of whether it was "ever completed" in the affirmative.

**Why completion took so long.** Progress on the conduit did not merely lag the City's promised May 1900 completion date — it stopped outright for years. The Sanitary District's Board of Trustees, in a resolution adopted March 18, 1901 protesting the City's non-performance under the 1898 agreement, found "considerably less than fifty per cent" of the Thirty-ninth Street conduit complete and "less than thirty-three and one-third per cent of the Lawrence Avenue conduit work" done, noting that work on the sister Thirty-ninth Street conduit had "absolutely ceased on April 10, 1900" [\[36\]](#src-36). The District's president reported to the Board again on December 3, 1901 that "less than forty per cent" of the Lawrence Avenue system was complete [\[36\]](#src-36), and then, a full year later, on December 2, 1902, that "no progress has been made upon the Lawrence Avenue Conduit, which remains as it was a year ago," attributing the standstill to "the failure of the original contractors and to resulting legal complications" [\[37\]](#src-37). That same December 1902 message urged the District to keep deepening the North Branch "as far north as the site of the proposed Lawrence Avenue Pumping Station" regardless of the conduit delay, since a deeper channel would reduce the pumps' operating head once the station was eventually built [\[37\]](#src-37) — confirming that, two and a half years past the conduit's original 1900 target, the pumping station itself was still only "proposed," not yet under construction. The Sanitary District's own 1921 tabulation dates the related North Branch channel-straightening project between Belmont and Lawrence Avenues (2.2 miles) to May 10, 1904 – December 2, 1907 [\[33\]](#src-33) — a timeframe that aligns closely with the station's independently documented 1907 completion (Hill 1911; the 1919 *History of its Growth and Development*) [\[34\]](#src-34)[\[35\]](#src-35), suggesting the conduit, the pumping station, and the North Branch channel work in this reach were ultimately finished together as one delayed, interlocking project, roughly seven years behind the schedule first contracted for in 1898.

**A December 1902 site relocation.** The North Branch channel-straightening project itself forced a late change to the pumping station's site. A Board resolution adopted December 17, 1902 records that the City's originally acquired parcel (Lot 56 in Sam Shakeford's Subdivision, Section 12, Township 40 North, Range 13 East) had been rendered unusable by the Sanitary District's own April 23, 1902 ordinance rerouting the North Branch's channel in this reach, and approved a land exchange with the "Northwest Land Association" for a substitute parcel in the adjoining Section 13, described as bordering "the east line of said new channel of the North Branch of the Chicago River" and "preferable to the site first selected" [\[37\]](#src-37). Whether this river-adjacent 1902 parcel is the same site Hill (1911) and the 1921 *Engineering Data* table later locate "east of Evanston Avenue [Broadway]," at the Lawrence Avenue crossing of the C.M.&St.P.R.R. — a location well inland from the river — could not be confirmed from the sources reviewed; it is possible the site changed again before the 1907 completion, or that "east line of said new channel" in the 1902 resolution describes a right-of-way or easement boundary rather than the immediate riverbank. This is flagged as an open question below rather than resolved either way.

Operations moved to the new riverside NBPS building in 1930 [\[10\]](#src-10). That flushing role also explains the original station's obsolescence in two steps: when the North Shore Channel — whose official terminus the District records as "North Branch Chicago River at Lawrence Ave." — opened on November 29, 1910, Wilmette's gravity lake-water supply made steam-driven flushing redundant [\[33\]](#src-33); and when the North Side Treatment Works opened in 1928, the new riverside NBPS was built at the conduit's outfall to intercept its flow for treatment rather than let it pour into the river [\[10\]](#src-10)[\[24\]](#src-24). What became of the original Lawrence Avenue building and its site afterward — whether it was demolished, repurposed, or left standing under a different use — is not documented in any source located for this research and remains an open question.

### The 1928 hydraulic reconfiguration: from mid-route lift to river-end lift

This report set out to test a specific hypothesis: that the 1928–1930 project moved the system's single lift from mid-route (the inland station) to the river end (NBPS), so that the conduit now flows to NBPS's wet well by gravity and NBPS lifts once into a gravity interceptor running toward the treatment works — versus the alternative that flow direction reversed entirely. Two Sanitary District publications from 1928, newly OCR'd for this research pass — the promotional *North Side Sewage Treatment Project: An Outline of Its Construction, Operation and Effect* and the more technical *Engineering Works* — describe, in the District's own contemporary language, exactly what changed, and the evidence supports the hypothesis.

**Before 1930: gravity conduit, mid-route lift.** As established above, the inland station lifted local sewage about 10 feet into the head of the westward 16-foot Lawrence Avenue conduit, which then ran by gravity the rest of the way to a straight outfall on the North Branch at the river [\[33\]](#src-33)[\[34\]](#src-34).

**After 1930: gravity to the river end, one lift there.** The *Outline* states plainly that the new station "will receive the sewage and storm-water flow by gravity through the 16-foot Lawrence Avenue conduit," now serving an enlarged area — "not only... the area now served by the present Lawrence Avenue Station but also... the area served by the Broadway relief sewer and sewers reaching the conduit west of Broadway" — and that "[t]he Lawrence Avenue Sewage Pumping Station at Lawrence Avenue and Broadway will be abandoned upon the completion of the North Branch Station" [\[24\]](#src-24). *Engineering Works* confirms the same outcome independently: the new station is "replacing the Lawrence Avenue Sewage Pumping Station with a new electrically operated station of four times the present capacity" [\[23\]](#src-23). The inland lift was not relocated or duplicated — it was eliminated once the conduit could deliver flow all the way to the river by gravity, and the system's sole remaining lift point moved to NBPS itself.

**The route beyond NBPS.** *Engineering Works* documents the North Side Intercepting Sewer's actual alignment, resolving how NBPS connects onward to the treatment works. The main interceptor runs along the **west** bank of the North Branch and North Shore Channel — a southern branch beginning at Fullerton Avenue and following Elston Avenue, Washtenaw Avenue, Melrose Street, California Avenue, and Manner Avenue to the west side of the North Shore Channel, and a northern branch running south along the channel's west side from near the Wilmette Pumping Station — both converging on the treatment works at Howard Street [\[23\]](#src-23). Because NBPS sits on the **east** bank, local sewage collected there has to cross to reach the west-bank interceptor; *Engineering Works* names three such crossings, one of which runs "from Montrose Avenue along Rockwell Street, Sunnyside Avenue and Virginia Avenue to Lawrence Avenue, to the North Branch Sewage Pumping Station" — i.e., NBPS is the specific point where this east-bank branch reaches, and is lifted into, the main interceptor, consistent with the 1929 tunneling-under-the-channel photograph already in this record [\[10\]](#src-10)[\[23\]](#src-23). The interceptor itself — nearly 14 miles long, built almost entirely in tunnel — flows by gravity the rest of the way to Howard Street, where a wholly separate pumping station at the plant (Division P, the "Pump and Blower House," at Howard Avenue and McCormick Road, Niles Center) lifts the arriving flow about 44 feet into the grit chambers to begin treatment [\[23\]](#src-23)[\[24\]](#src-24).

**What the "twin sewer" under the building actually is — partly resolved.** No source located in this research names the structure beneath NBPS's vaulted foundation explicitly; the rehabilitation contractor's own description is a "twin sewer," not "twin interceptor sewer" as an earlier version of this document stated [\[12\]](#src-12). The best-documented candidate is the 16-foot Lawrence Avenue conduit itself: the Sanitary District's 1928 photo captions describe it arriving at "six huge intakes for its double-barreled suction pumps," and the station's original pumps were built "single inlet, double suction" — a doubled configuration at both the conduit's intake structure and internal to each pump [\[23\]](#src-23)[\[24\]](#src-24). Whether today's "twin sewer" is this same double-barreled 1928 conduit/intake structure, the separate interceptor tunnel under the North Shore Channel, or an undocumented later structure could not be resolved from sources reviewed; see Open Questions.

**Original pump specifications (1928 design).** The *Outline* and *Engineering Works* agree exactly on the dry-weather pumps — three, 48-inch suction/42-inch discharge, 75 cfs each at a 9-foot head, 100-hp synchronous motors, horizontal split-case centrifugal, single-inlet double-suction, ≈225 cfs (≈145 MGD) combined design capacity — but disagree on the storm pumps: the *Outline* gives five 72-inch pumps at 250 cfs each (17-ft head, 800-hp motors), while *Engineering Works* gives the same five pumps at 300 cfs each (also 17-ft head, 800-hp motors) [\[23\]](#src-23)[\[24\]](#src-24). Today's documented capacity — five pumps at 300 cfs each, per MWRD's own 2018 rehabilitation contract — matches *Engineering Works* exactly, suggesting either that 300 cfs was the true as-built 1930 figure and the *Outline*'s 250-cfs figure was an uncorrected early planning estimate, or that a later, otherwise-undocumented change happened to land on the same number *Engineering Works* had already published in 1928; sources reviewed cannot distinguish between these. What both 1928 sources agree on is that a five-pump storm-relief capacity, whatever its exact original rating, was part of NBPS's design from the start — not, as an earlier version of this document speculated, a later TARP-era expansion built on a small 1970s dewatering-pump base. (The four ~11-cfs KSB submersible pumps installed in the 1970s, discussed in the Upgrades timeline, were a separate, much smaller system serving the unrelated Lawrence Avenue Underflow tunnel-dewatering project, at the same site but for a different purpose.) *Engineering Works* explains the storm pumps' scale directly: "[t]he increase of capacity is necessary because of the greater flow from relief storm sewers being built by the City of Chicago" [\[23\]](#src-23) — i.e., sized for the City's own 1920s storm-sewer expansion in the area, which also included a temporary stopgap: the Clifton Avenue Sewage Pumping Station, built by the Sanitary District in 1927 at Clifton Avenue just south of Lawrence Avenue (two pumps, 3,600 and 9,000 gpm) to provide an outlet for the City's Clifton Avenue relief sewer until NBPS could take over, expected in the summer of 1929 [\[23\]](#src-23).

**Cost.** The *Outline*'s own final project cost-summary table gives the North Branch Sewage Pumping Station's total construction cost as $2,336,980.00 (contractors Allis-Chalmers Mfg. Co. and Tully-Costello Co.) — against the North Side Intercepting Sewer's $11,048,225.06 and the treatment works' $18,190,190.94, for a project total of $31,575,396.00 — refining the "$2,500,000" figure already cited elsewhere in this document as only an approximate, contemporary description [\[24\]](#src-24). *Engineering Works*' own contemporaneous estimate of "$2,337,000.00" for the pumping station is nearly identical, cross-validating both figures [\[23\]](#src-23). NBPS's pumping-unit equipment was let under contract May 6, 1926, to Allis-Chalmers Mfg. Co. and Tully-Costello Co. — predating the earliest excavation photograph (April 1928) already in this record — while the North Side Intercepting Sewer's first construction contract was awarded November 23, 1921 [\[24\]](#src-24).

## Upgrades & modernization timeline

- **1898–1902 — new detail (July 2026).** The City and Sanitary District's governing ordinances and agreements (Jan. 31 & Feb. 4, 1898; Nov. 18, 1899) fix the Lawrence Avenue conduit's design as a 16-foot-diameter conduit running "from Lake Michigan...to the Chicago River," but construction — contracted May 2, 1898 for a promised May 1900 completion — falls far behind (under 33% done by March 1901, under 40% by December 1901) and then stalls completely through December 1902 amid "the failure of the original contractors and...resulting legal complications"; the Sanitary District separately relocates the pumping station's site by land exchange in December 1902 after its own ordinance rerouting the North Branch channel renders the originally acquired parcel unusable [\[36\]](#src-36)[\[37\]](#src-37).
- **1900–1907** — Sanitary District builds lakefront intercepting sewers, eliminating direct lakefront sewage outfalls and creating the need for a pumping point on the North Branch [\[10\]](#src-10)[\[19\]](#src-19).
- **1907 — Resolved (July 2026).** The City of Chicago completes the original, inland Lawrence Avenue Sewage Pumping Station (Lawrence Ave. at the C.M.&St.P.R.R./Northwestern Elevated crossing, east of old Evanston Avenue/Broadway), which pumps intercepted North Side sewage onward and forces lake water through to flush the North Branch; confirmed by two independent 1911/1919/1921-era Sanitary District primary sources, resolving both the date (1907, not 1909) and the site previously known only from an anonymous blog comment [\[33\]](#src-33)[\[34\]](#src-34)[\[35\]](#src-35). The Sanitary District's own 1921 tabulation separately dates the related North Branch channel-straightening work between Belmont and Lawrence Avenues to May 10, 1904 – December 2, 1907, consistent with the conduit, pumping station, and river work in this reach finishing together after the earlier delays [\[33\]](#src-33).
- **1910** — The Sanitary District builds the North Branch Dam at River Park, where the newly dug North Shore Channel meets the natural North Branch, to hold the two waterways' differing elevations apart; it stands as the only "waterfall" on the Chicago River system until removed in 2018 (see "The North Branch Dam next door," above) [\[25\]](#src-25).
- **1921–1930 — resolved (July 2026), see "The 1928 hydraulic reconfiguration" above.** North Side Intercepting Sewer Contract No. 1 awarded November 23, 1921; NBPS pumping-unit equipment contracted May 6, 1926 (Allis-Chalmers Mfg. Co., Tully-Costello Co.); permanent North Branch Pumping Station built 1928–1930 at 4860 N. Francisco Avenue, construction documented in dated photographs April 1928–April 1930, in service by 1930; tunnel connection to the North Side Intercepting Sewer under the North Shore Channel completed in the same period [\[10\]](#src-10)[\[23\]](#src-23)[\[24\]](#src-24). The Sanitary District's own 1928 publications establish that this project eliminated the inland station's ~10-ft mid-route lift rather than relocating it: the inland station's drainage area, plus newly added flow from the Broadway relief sewer, now reached the new riverside station **by gravity** through the existing 16-foot Lawrence Avenue conduit, and the old station was formally "abandoned upon the completion of the North Branch Station" [\[23\]](#src-23)[\[24\]](#src-24).
- **October 3, 1928** — North Side Sewage Treatment Works (today's O'Brien WRP) begins operation at 3500 Howard Street, Skokie, giving NBPS's lifted sanitary flow an actual treatment destination [\[2\]](#src-2)[\[15\]](#src-15).
- **1963–1980 — Lawrence Avenue Underflow Sewer System (Chicago's first deep-tunnel demonstration project, forerunner of TARP).** The City of Chicago's 1963 Five-Year Capital Improvements Program called for a new auxiliary outlet sewer to relieve basement and underpass flooding bounded by the North Branch of the Chicago River, Irving Park Road, Oriole Avenue and Devon Avenue; the City's Bureau of Engineering retained Harza Engineering Company to study alternatives, and in 1966 the project (originally "Eastwood-Wilson") was redesignated the "Lawrence Avenue System" and committed to deep-rock-tunnel construction 220–250 feet below Lawrence Avenue [\[3\]](#src-3). Key contracts:
  - *Contract No. 1 — Main Shaft and Rock Tunnels*: joint venture of James McHugh Construction Co., S.A. Healy Co., and Kenny Construction Co.; bid $10,792,094; started November 6, 1967; completed May 28, 1973; built 16,638 ft of 12-ft-diameter and 9,126 ft of 17-ft-diameter concrete-lined rock tunnel and a 29-ft (earth)/27-ft (rock) diameter, ~250-ft-deep main shaft [\[3\]](#src-3).
  - *Contract No. 3 — Pumping Station and Outfall*: same contractor joint venture; bid $1,977,000; location given as "Lawrence Avenue at North Branch of Chicago River" — the NBPS site; started April 17, 1972; ~96.65% complete as of February 1979 after repeated delays from submersible-pump cable and junction-box failures; installed four German-made KSB submersible dewatering pumps (5,000 gpm / 535 hp each) to drain the tunnel to the sanitary interceptor after storms [\[3\]](#src-3).
  - *Contract No. 4 — Additional High-Level Sewers*: contractor Jay-Dee Contractors, Inc.; bid $2,644,875; started May 1, 1974; completed June 30, 1975 [\[3\]](#src-3).
  - The overall system, funded partly by U.S. EPA Demonstration Grant No. 11020 EMD, was projected (and later measured in a multi-year computer/field study) to cut combined-sewer overflow events on this drainage area from an average of 59 per year (235 hours) to about 5 per year (18 hours) — a roughly 92 percent reduction — while cutting suspended-solids and BOD loading reaching the North Branch by 73 and 78 percent respectively [\[3\]](#src-3).
- **1975–1980s — TARP Mainstream System construction.** Contract 73-060-2H, "North Branch Chicago River, Tunnel & Connecting Structures" ($167,907,130), builds the Mainstream Tunnel System's North Branch leg near the station, including a drop shaft in adjacent Ronan Park [\[7\]](#src-7)[\[17\]](#src-17). *(Correction, July 2026: an earlier version of this document speculated here that NBPS's storm-pump capacity was substantially enlarged to its current five-pump, 1,500-cfs configuration during this TARP-era period, reasoning that the capacity vastly exceeds the ~48-cfs 1970s KSB pump bank. Newly reviewed Sanitary District primary sources from 1928 show this speculation was wrong in kind, if not necessarily in exact number: NBPS's five large storm-flow pumps are documented as part of the station's original 1928–1930 design, decades before TARP — see "The 1928 hydraulic reconfiguration," above. The ~48-cfs KSB pumps were never the storm pumps' predecessor; they were always a separate, smaller system built for the unrelated Lawrence Avenue Underflow tunnel-dewatering project described just above.)*
- **March 1, 2002** — MWRD begins a continuing NPDES sampling program for every discharge from NBPS into the North Branch of the Chicago River, per O'Brien WRP's discharge permit [\[4\]](#src-4)[\[5\]](#src-5).
- **July 31, 2018** — The U.S. Army Corps of Engineers, MWRD, and the Chicago Park District begin demolition of the North Branch Dam 0.2 mile upstream, the culmination of Friends of the Chicago River's 18-year removal campaign (see "The North Branch Dam next door," above) [\[25\]](#src-25)[\[31\]](#src-31).
- **August 2, 2018** — MWRD awards Contract 16-079-3D, "Rehabilitation of North Branch Pumping Station, NSA," to Path Construction Company, Inc., for $4,613,450 (500 calendar days), financed through the Illinois Clean Water State Revolving Fund. The project justification states that the five 300-cfs discharge pumps' high-velocity flow had eroded the concrete building columns below the waterline by more than 20 percent, and in some cases up to 50 percent, of their original cross-section; scope included grouted fiberglass-reinforced-plastic jackets around the columns, underwater repair of the east wall and under-deck wall, repair of deteriorated concrete stairs and deck, and restoration of the balustrade and deck lighting [\[1\]](#src-1).
- **2024–2027 — resolved (July 2026): MWRD Contract 22-094-3D, "Rehabilitation of Elevated Deck and Boat Dock at North Branch Pumping Station, NSA."** An earlier version of this document could identify this larger structural rehabilitation only through the contractor's own project profile, with no confirmed contract number. Three independent MWRD records now cross-confirm the contract: the District's public "Contracts Awarded" search tool, the Board of Commissioners' own Legistar record (File #24-0156), and the District's 2026 capital budget book [\[40\]](#src-40)[\[42\]](#src-42). Bids were opened January 16, 2024 against the District's own engineer's estimate of $17,250,000.00; three bids were received, all above the estimate — Path Construction Company, Inc. of Arlington Heights, IL, at $22,593,795.00 (low bid, roughly 31 percent over the estimate), IHC Construction Companies, LLC of Elgin, IL, at $25,690,000.00, and Granite Construction Co. of Des Plaines, IL, at $29,642,000.00 — out of 1,307 bidders notified and 79 planholders [\[41\]](#src-41). The Board of Commissioners authorized award to Path Construction as the lowest responsive bidder on March 7, 2024 (agenda item #25 of that meeting), for a base amount not to exceed $22,593,795.00 plus a five percent change-order allowance of $1,129,689.75, for a total authorized amount not to exceed $23,723,484.75 (Account 401-50000-645720) [\[40\]](#src-40). Unlike the 2018 predecessor contract (16-079-3D, financed through an Illinois EPA Clean Water SRF loan), Contract 22-094-3D does not appear on MWRD's own running list of SRF-financed projects, indicating it was funded through the District's general Capital Improvement Program rather than an SRF loan [\[43\]](#src-43). MWRD's 2026 budget book, using in-house engineering design (no outside consultant), officially describes the contracted scope more narrowly than the contractor's own public project profile: "replace deteriorated exterior concrete decking, stairs, boat dock, and balustrades" at NBPS, plus replacement of the flap gates at the nearby connecting structure to TARP Dropshaft DS-91, with new cast stone balustrade and light pylons to follow — a scope statement that does not explicitly mention the "partial-depth refacing of all exterior concrete walls" language from the contractor's own profile, though both plainly describe the same single contract and project timeline [\[12\]](#src-12)[\[42\]](#src-42). Per the same budget book, the contract's duration is 1,253 calendar days from the March 2024 award, giving a contracted substantial completion date of August 2027 — notably longer than the "approximately 2.5-year duration" the contractor's own profile describes; as of that February 5, 2026 profile (about 23 months into the contract), roughly 80 percent of exterior wall repairs were complete and the old boat dock had been fully demolished [\[12\]](#src-12)[\[42\]](#src-42). Work requires professional divers (winter water temperatures of 30–40°F), barges, and boom lifts, and the station remains operational and continues to function as an overflow facility throughout construction [\[12\]](#src-12). A separate, smaller MWRD contract at the same station — 24-601-21, "Mechanical Process Improvements, Various Locations," awarded November 2024 to Independent Mechanical Industries, Inc. and John Burns Construction Company, LLC — concurrently covers replacement of the seal water break tank and piping at NBPS; it is a distinct contract from 22-094-3D and outside this rehabilitation's scope [\[42\]](#src-42).

## Current operations

NBPS is operated as an outstation of the Terrence J. O'Brien WRP; an "OWRP Operating Engineer" at O'Brien WRP monitors the station remotely and is the point of contact (via extensions x88380/x88381) for entry, a silent intrusion alarm, and coordination of NPDES sampling [\[4\]](#src-4). Field Services/Industrial Waste Division (IWD) North Office personnel hold the Medeco key needed for physical entry [\[4\]](#src-4). Upon activation of the station's first storm pump, the OWRP Operating Engineer notifies the District's Systems Dispatcher, who assigns IWD-North staff to sample the discharge from an automatic composite sampler mounted on the south wall of the building's lower level [\[4\]](#src-4). Sampling requirements under the O'Brien WRP NPDES permit include a one-gallon composite for general chemistry, a composite for trace metals, and a grab sample for pH (taken 30 minutes to 2 hours after the first pump activates) for every 24-hour period of discharge; results for BOD5/TOC, suspended solids, cadmium, and hardness are reported by MWRD's Monitoring & Research laboratory at the Lue-Hing R&D Complex [\[4\]](#src-4).

Operationally, NBPS is treated by MWRD as one of a short list of "major pumping stations" (alongside Racine Avenue Pumping Station on the South Fork of the South Branch of the Chicago River, and the 95th Street and 122nd Street Pumping Stations on the Calumet River) whose discharges into local waterways are separately, specifically monitored under the WRPs' NPDES permits [\[4\]](#src-4). Every CSO event at NBPS also triggers a standing fish-kill response protocol: two days after a pumping event (or the following Monday if it falls on a weekend), MWRD's Channel Maintenance Unit dispatches boat crews to inspect the river downstream for fish kills, cleans up if warranted, and files both an oral and written report to the Illinois EPA [\[4\]](#src-4). Water-quality context for the surrounding stretch of river is also actively managed: an instream aeration station at Webster Avenue adds oxygen to the North Branch (a companion station at Devon Avenue serves the North Shore Channel), controlled using a dissolved-oxygen probe read remotely at O'Brien WRP from the Ohio Street bridgehouse on the main Chicago River [\[4\]](#src-4).

The five storm-discharge pumps are activated on an as-needed basis during large storms specifically to draw down local combined-sewer elevations before they surcharge — a proactive, powered response rather than a passive weir overflow — which MWRD describes as distinguishing NBPS (along with Racine Avenue Pumping Station) from most of its other CSO points [\[6\]](#src-6). The station's discharge is tracked as MWRD-owned CSO outfall/telemetry ID 107 (tide-gate structures DS-M90 and DS-M91) as part of the District's Consent Decree–mandated CSO telemetry network, which uses proximity switches on tide gates reporting to workstations at the Stickney, Calumet, and Kirie WRPs (with a move to cellular communications underway under a separate District contract) [\[4\]](#src-4).

## Notable events & incidents

- **July 2–3, 2023 storm.** The National Weather Service recorded rainfall totals of roughly 3 to 7 inches across greater Chicago, with some localized areas exceeding 8 inches; the worst flooding hit the west and southwest sides and near-west/southwest suburbs [\[6\]](#src-6). Even though the McCook Reservoir was only 17 percent full as of 2 a.m. on July 2, CSOs began by 8:19 a.m., and NBPS began actively releasing sewage and stormwater into the North Branch at 9:44 a.m. — before the region's primary TARP reservoir was anywhere near capacity. MWRD, responding publicly to the Chicago Tribune, used this event to explain that NBPS and Racine Avenue Pumping Station pump proactively to control local sewer elevations, rather than waiting for TARP itself to fill and spill by gravity [\[6\]](#src-6).
- **March 11, 2026.** Residents across several North Side neighborhoods along the North Branch of the Chicago River and North Shore Channel — including Lincoln Square, Albany Park, North Center, Irving Park, Horner Park, and Roscoe Village — reported a strong sewage-like ("skunk"-like, per some residents) odor. MWRD spokesperson Allison Fore confirmed District staff investigated and also detected the smell; search-indexed excerpts of the WTTW report state that "many pointed blame in the direction of a nearby pumping station" and that a Deep Tunnel drop shaft located near the North Branch Pumping Station was linked to the problem, and MWRD invited residents to report odors via its hotline (800-332-3867) or online system [\[16\]](#src-16). Because NBPS sits directly on this stretch of river and adjoins one of the area's TARP drop shafts (Ronan Park), it is very likely implicated in or adjacent to this incident, but the full WTTW article text remained access-blocked (HTTP 403) on repeated retrieval attempts, so this is sourced only to search-engine-indexed excerpts rather than the primary text and is still flagged as an open question below.
- **2018–2020 concrete erosion repair.** MWRD's own contract documents describe the storm pumps' high-velocity discharge as having eroded the below-waterline concrete building columns by 20–50 percent of their original cross-section, necessitating the $4.6 million Contract 16-079-3D repair — itself a notable operational consequence of the station's storm-relief role [\[1\]](#src-1).
- **2024–2027 major structural rehabilitation** (MWRD Contract 22-094-3D, awarded to Path Construction Company, Inc., March 7, 2024, for $22,593,795.00; contracted substantial completion August 2027), described above, undertaken because of "widespread and visible structural degradation" after roughly a century of service, including replacement of the river-spanning boat dock and staircase and the elevated slabs directly above the storm pumps [\[12\]](#src-12)[\[40\]](#src-40)[\[42\]](#src-42).

### Disambiguation: this is NOT a City of Chicago drinking-water pumping station

Because Lincoln Square has more than one historic "pumping station" from roughly the same era, it is worth stating plainly what NBPS is not. The **Thomas Jefferson Pumping Station**, at 2250 W. Eastwood Avenue — about a mile from NBPS — is a City of Chicago Department of Water Management facility that pressurizes purified Lake Michigan drinking water for distribution through the city's water mains; it was designed by city architect Argyle Robinson (city architect 1926–1929) and became operational in August 1928, almost exactly contemporaneous with NBPS's construction, but it has nothing to do with sewage or stormwater [\[13\]](#src-13). Similarly, the **Mayfair Pumping Station**, at 4850 W. Wilson Avenue, is another City drinking-water pumping station (opened 1913 per some sources, 1915–1918 per city records — see the water-pumping document in this archive; originally steam-driven, later upgraded) supplying the northwest side and suburbs [\[14\]](#src-14). Both are operated by the City of Chicago; NBPS is operated by MWRD, a separate unit of government, and moves wastewater and stormwater, not drinking water. General web searches for "North Branch Pumping Station" or "Lawrence Avenue Pumping Station" occasionally surface unrelated facilities of the same generic name elsewhere in the country (for example, a "North Pumping Station" in South Bend, Indiana, and a "North Branch" commuter-rail station in New Jersey turned up as false-positive image matches during this research and are not related in any way) [confirmed by direct verification during this research].

## Open questions

- **Whether the storm pumps' rated capacity was ever formally increased from 250 to 300 cfs each — timing resolved, exact figure unresolved (July 2026).** An earlier version of this document speculated that NBPS's five-pump, 1,500-cfs storm configuration dated to the TARP Mainstream System era (1975–1980s), reasoning from the small ~48-cfs 1970s KSB dewatering-pump bank. That is now known to be wrong: the Sanitary District's own two 1928 publications both document five large storm-flow pumps as part of NBPS's *original* 1928–1930 design — the *North Side Sewage Treatment Project* outline rates them at 250 cfs each (17-ft head), while *Engineering Works* rates the same five pumps at 300 cfs each (also 17-ft head); today's documented figure (300 cfs each, per the 2018 rehabilitation contract) matches *Engineering Works* exactly [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[23\]](#src-23)[\[24\]](#src-24). **Still unresolved:** whether 300 cfs was the true 1930 as-built figure (with the *Outline*'s 250-cfs figure an uncorrected early estimate) or whether some later, undocumented change produced today's number; see "The 1928 hydraulic reconfiguration," above.
- **Whether the 1970s KSB submersible dewatering pumps are still in service, were replaced by the current storm pumps, or coexist as a separate dry-weather system.** Not resolved by available sources; now further clarified (see above) that these KSB pumps were never the storm pumps' predecessor in any case — the storm pumps existed as a distinct, much larger system from 1928.
- **Capacity of the dry-weather sanitary lift pumps — resolved (July 2026):** three pumps, 75 cfs each at a 9-foot head (≈225 cfs / ≈145 MGD combined), 100-hp synchronous motors, per the Sanitary District's own matching 1928 design specifications in both the *Outline* and *Engineering Works* [\[23\]](#src-23)[\[24\]](#src-24). **Still unresolved:** whether this capacity has since been changed.
- **Named architect and/or chief engineer of the 1928–1930 building.** Not identified in any source reviewed; MWRD photo captions and secondary sources credit only "the Sanitary District of Chicago" as builder.
- **Original construction cost — resolved (July 2026):** the *Outline*'s own final project cost-summary table gives $2,336,980.00 as the North Branch Sewage Pumping Station's total construction cost, nearly matching *Engineering Works*' contemporaneous $2,337,000.00 estimate [\[23\]](#src-23)[\[24\]](#src-24) — refining the "$2,500,000" figure the station is elsewhere described by only approximately.
- **Discrepancy in the original Lawrence Avenue Sewage Pumping Station's completion date and pump capacities across two Sanitary District sources — unresolved (July 2026).** Hill (1911), the 1921 *Engineering Data* tabulation, and the 1919 SD History date the inland station's completion to 1907 (see above); the Sanitary District's own 1928 *Engineering Works* instead states it "was...completed in 1908, and turned over to the Sanitary District to operate on December 6, 1910" [\[23\]](#src-23). Separately, this document now reads the 1921 *Engineering Data* table's two smaller sewage pumps as 125 cfs each (following *Engineering Works*, and internal consistency with the 1921 document's own 192-MGD/≈297-cfs peak-flow figure) rather than the 25 cfs each read in an earlier pass; the two published figures cannot be reconciled from sources reviewed.
- **Exact identity of the "twin sewer" beneath NBPS's vaulted foundation — unresolved (July 2026).** The rehabilitation contractor's own term is "twin sewer" [\[12\]](#src-12), not "twin interceptor sewer" as an earlier version of this document stated. The best-documented candidate is the double-barreled 16-foot Lawrence Avenue conduit/intake structure described in the station's 1928 design sources, but this is not confirmed; see "The 1928 hydraulic reconfiguration," above.
- **The original "Lawrence Avenue Pumping Station" predecessor — resolved (July 2026):** the 1911 Hill paper, the 1921 *Engineering Data* tabulation, and the 1919 *History of its Growth and Development* together confirm the predecessor's existence, its 1907 completion date (not 1909), its site (Lawrence Ave. at the C.M.&St.P.R.R./Northwestern Elevated crossing, east of old Evanston Avenue/Broadway — matching the blog commenter's account almost exactly), its builder (City of Chicago), its dual sewage-lift/lake-flushing function, and its detailed pump/boiler specifications [\[33\]](#src-33)[\[34\]](#src-34)[\[35\]](#src-35); see "The original Lawrence Avenue Pumping Station, 1907–1930" above. **Still unresolved:** what became of the building and its site after operations moved to the riverside NBPS in 1930 — no source located in this research documents its demolition, repurposing, or survival.
- **The Lawrence Avenue conduit's alignment, dimensions, and lake connection — largely resolved (July 2026):** 1898–1901 ordinances, agreements, and a Special Commission of Experts exhibit establish the conduit's authorized design (16-foot inside diameter, 14,026 feet/2.657 miles, running from a Lake Michigan shore intake near Sheridan Road, west under Lawrence Avenue, through the pumping station, to a North Branch outfall), and confirm — together with the already-cited 1911 Hill account and 1921 *Engineering Data* screw-pump specification — that the lake connection was actually built and used, not merely proposed [\[33\]](#src-33)[\[34\]](#src-34)[\[36\]](#src-36); see "Construction status, 1901" and "Why completion took so long" above. **Still unresolved:** (1) whether the 1901 Special Commission's alternative proposal to abandon the intake and downsize the conduit to an 11-foot sewer-only outfall was ever formally rescinded, or simply quietly superseded by continued construction of the original 16-foot design; (2) the exact reconciliation of the exhibit's own internal ~500-foot discrepancy in remaining-footage figures; (3) no description of the physical intake structure itself (crib, pier, or shore connection) was located beyond its being referenced relative to Sheridan Road; and (4) whether the intake and its 585-cfs screw pump continued in active operation after the North Shore Channel opened in November 1910, or were retained only as unused standby equipment through the 1921 tabulation date.
- **Site of the original inland pumping station — possible tension, unresolved (July 2026):** a December 17, 1902 Board resolution documents a land-exchange relocation of the pumping station's site to a parcel (Section 13, Township 40 North, Range 13 East) described as bordering "the east line of said new channel of the North Branch of the Chicago River," following the Sanitary District's own April 23, 1902 ordinance rerouting the North Branch channel in this reach [\[37\]](#src-37). Whether this river-adjacent 1902 parcel is the same site the 1911 Hill paper and 1921 *Engineering Data* table locate "east of Evanston Avenue [Broadway]" — well inland from the river, at the Lawrence Avenue crossing of the C.M.&St.P.R.R. — could not be confirmed from sources reviewed; the site may have changed again before 1907, or "east line of said new channel" may describe a right-of-way/easement boundary rather than the immediate riverbank. Not resolved in this research pass.
- **A possible 1913 photograph of the conduit's lake intake — unconfirmed (July 2026):** a glass-plate negative in the Chicago History Museum's collection, indexed via Explore Chicago Collections, is catalogued as "[Water intake crib on Lake Michigan at Lawrence Avenue]" (Uptown community area), dated 1913. This could plausibly depict the Lawrence Avenue conduit's own lake intake described above, but the museum's page returned an HTTP 403 error on repeated retrieval attempts during this research, so neither its full caption text nor confirmation that it depicts this conduit's intake (rather than an unrelated City of Chicago drinking-water crib coincidentally near the same street) could be verified. Flagged here only as an unconfirmed lead, sourced to search-engine-indexed metadata rather than the primary record [\[38\]](#src-38).
- **The exact contract number, award date, and dollar value of the boat-dock/wall-refacing rehabilitation — resolved (July 2026):** MWRD Contract 22-094-3D, "Rehabilitation of Elevated Deck and Boat Dock at North Branch Pumping Station, NSA," awarded to Path Construction Company, Inc. on March 7, 2024 (bid opening January 16, 2024; engineer's estimate $17,250,000.00; low bid $22,593,795.00 of three bids received; total authorized amount not to exceed $23,723,484.75; contracted substantial completion August 2027), cross-confirmed against MWRD's Legistar record, its public Contracts Awarded and Contract Bid Opening Results search tools, and its 2026 capital budget book — see the 2024–2027 entry in the Upgrades & modernization timeline above for full detail [\[40\]](#src-40)[\[41\]](#src-41)[\[42\]](#src-42)[\[43\]](#src-43). **Still open:** whether the contractor's own "partial-depth refacing of all exterior concrete walls" language and MWRD's own narrower official scope description ("exterior concrete decking, stairs, boat dock, and balustrades") describe identical work or a scope that has evolved/expanded since contract award, and why the contractor's public "approximately 2.5-year duration" is shorter than the contract's own 1,253-calendar-day (~3.4-year) term from award to contracted substantial completion.
- **Direct confirmation that the March 2026 North Side odor incident implicated NBPS specifically** (as opposed to other nearby Deep Tunnel shafts) — the primary WTTW reporting could not be fully retrieved due to access restrictions during this research; only search-engine summaries were available.
- **National Register of Historic Places or Chicago Landmark status.** No evidence was found that the building carries any formal historic-preservation designation; a search explicitly for this returned no confirming record, despite the building's evident architectural and historical significance.
- **Whether Richard Lanyon's *Draining Chicago: The Early City and the North Area* (2016) contains additional primary-source detail on the station's design and engineers** — the book was identified as directly on-topic but its full text could not be accessed in this research pass.
- **North Branch Dam removal, Phase 2.** Sources describe a planned second phase of riverbank and habitat restoration extending into Legion Park and the Ronan Park natural area directly across the river from NBPS, estimated near $12.4 million and contingent on further federal Great Lakes Fishery and Ecosystem Restoration Program funding as of 2017 [\[29\]](#src-29). Whether this second phase was ever funded and completed, and in what form, was not confirmed in sources reviewed for this research.
- **North Branch Dam removal cost.** Reported figures range from a $2.1 million Phase 1 estimate (2017) to $4.5–4.6 million in figures reported around the actual July 2018 demolition; these likely represent different stages of the same accounting, but no single source reviewed reconciles them explicitly.
