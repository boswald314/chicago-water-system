# Water Purification: Jardine WPP, Sawyer WPP, and Chicago's Drinking-Water Treatment History

## Role in the system

Chicago's drinking water begins in Lake Michigan, not in the river or sewer system covered elsewhere in this archive — the drinking-water intake system and the wastewater/drainage system (operated by the separate Metropolitan Water Reclamation District) are distinct infrastructures that happen to touch the same lake. Raw lake water enters through offshore **intake cribs** — armored concrete-and-steel structures two to three miles from shore, in 32–37 feet of water — through screened ports 20–36 feet below the surface [1][11]. From each crib, water drops through a vertical shaft and travels through a rock tunnel roughly 50–200 feet below the lakebed to shore, arriving at one of the city's two purification plants [1][5][9]. Two cribs remain in active service today: the **William E. Dever Crib** (built 1935), which together with the older Four Mile Crib historically supplied the Central District/Jardine plant, and the **Edward F. Dunne Crib** (built 1911, per the city's own 1956 engineering account, though some secondary sources give 1909), which supplies the South District/Sawyer plant [1][11][22]. At each plant, low-lift pumps raise the raw water 20–25 feet into the treatment train; after coagulation, sedimentation, filtration and disinfection, high-lift pumps push the finished water by gravity and pressure through tunnels to a ring of pumping stations, and from there into the distribution grid of water mains [1][5].

**Downstream**, the two plants are the sole source of potable water for the City of Chicago and — via wholesale contracts — more than 120 suburban municipalities and regional water agencies across Cook, DuPage, and Will counties, serving over 5 million people in total [5][7][9]. The **James W. Jardine Water Purification Plant** (north of Navy Pier) supplies the northern, downtown and western parts of the city and northern/western suburbs; the **Eugene Sawyer Water Purification Plant** (South Shore, near Rainbow Beach) supplies the south side and southern suburbs [1][9][10]. One well-documented downstream link is the **DuPage Water Commission**, which taps Chicago's system at the city's Central Park Avenue Pumping Station, sends water 2.4 miles through a 12-foot-diameter bedrock tunnel to the Lexington Pumping Station, and then 9.3 miles farther west through 90-inch and 72-inch mains to its own DuPage Pumping Station in Elmhurst, from which it redistributes to DuPage County communities [25]. No water leaves Chicago's system as wastewater at the plants themselves — used water re-enters the separate sewer/drainage system documented elsewhere in this archive and is conveyed to MWRD's water reclamation plants, not back through Jardine or Sawyer.

*Design vs. average vs. peak, explicitly:* the City of Chicago's Bureau of Water Supply currently states it "provides approximately 750 million gallons" of treated water per day on average, while the two plants' combined rated/design capacity is stated as "approximately 2.1 billion gallons of water per day" [5][7]. In 1955, before Jardine existed, total system average daily pumpage (filtered South District water plus chlorinated-only North/Central District water) was 1,037,074,000 gallons/day, with a record peak day of 1,513,140,000 gallons on July 27, 1955 [1]. These figures show the system has long run well under its engineered peak/design capacity — headroom built in for fire flow, heat-wave demand spikes, and redundancy.

## Original construction

**Pre-filtration era (1834–1912).** Chicago's water system predates any filtration or disinfection by nearly a century. The village's first public well was dug in 1834; a privately owned system went into service in 1842 at a cost of **$24,000**, drawing water through a pipe extending 150 feet into the lake off Lake Street, lifted by a 25-horsepower steam pump into a wooden elevated tank, and distributed through wooden mains bored from 10-foot cedar logs [1]. The city obtained legislative authority to build its own works in 1851, laid its first cast-iron distribution pipe on Clark Street in 1852, and put its own municipally owned North Pumping Works into service in February 1854, powered by a walking-beam steam pump nicknamed "Old Sally" — an engine tended, from age 25, by future Chicago mayor **DeWitt Clinton Cregier**, who went on to serve as the water works' chief engineer (1861–1880), Commissioner of Public Works, and Superintendent of Water Works before his 1889 election as mayor [1][33]. Rapid population growth (Chicago's population reached 178,500 by the end of the Civil War) and worsening lakefront pollution from the Chicago River drove **Ellis Sylvester Chesbrough** — born Baltimore County, Maryland, July 8, 1813; formerly chief engineer of the Boston Water Works; appointed chief engineer of Chicago's sewerage commission in 1855 and the city's first Commissioner of Public Works in 1861 — to design an offshore intake [33]. Construction of the first lake tunnel began March 17, 1864 (some secondary accounts say the crib work began May 1864), and water first reached the North Pumping Works through the completed 2-mile, 5-foot-diameter brick-lined tunnel and its timber crib on March 25, 1867, at a reported construction cost of **$380,784** [1][11][33][31]. The Chicago Water Tower and pumping station at the tunnel's shore terminus were completed in early 1869; the tower survived the Great Chicago Fire of October 8–9, 1871, though the pumping works were knocked offline for eight days and an estimated 15,000 lead service connections were melted by the fire, destroying underground service records and making leak location difficult for years afterward [1][33]. Chesbrough's approach — pushing intakes progressively farther offshore rather than treating the water — remained Chicago's primary defense against contamination for the rest of the 19th century; the city extended it repeatedly, including a new Wilson Avenue crib and tunnel system (crib floated into position and sunk July 1915, tunnel completed for inspection March 1918, total length about 8.2 miles, cost including the crib approximately **$3,900,000**) and the Chicago Avenue Lake and Land Tunnel (intake crib adjacent to the Carter H. Harrison crib, tunnel extending 7.81 miles inland through solid limestone about 200 feet below lake level, project cost approximately **$13,500,000**, largely built through the early 1920s) [33]. This buildout was supplemented after 1900 by construction of the Sanitary and Ship Canal, which reversed the Chicago River's flow away from the lake (a separate drainage-engineering story covered elsewhere in this archive) [31].

**Chlorination begins (1912–1916).** As the city's cribs multiplied (Four Mile Crib 1892, 68th Street Crib 1892, Carter H. Harrison Crib 1900) but still delivered untreated lake water, City Engineer **John Ernst Ericson** — a Swedish-born engineer (b. Stockholm, October 21, 1857) who emigrated in 1884, joined Chicago's Department of Public Works in 1892, and served as City Engineer from 1897 until his death in 1927 (with a gap from 1919–1923) — began experimental chlorination using the hypochlorite-of-lime method in early 1912 at the Edward F. Dunne intake crib, feeding the Roseland Pumping Station [1][4][33]. The treatment was extended to the 68th Street Crib in July 1912 and to the Lake View Crib in August 1913 [4]. Ericson's own account, presented to the American Public Health Association, describes serious early problems: freezing of chemical solutions in winter, anchor ice blocking distribution pipes, unreliable hypochlorite deliveries to the offshore cribs, and consumer complaints about taste, causing periodic shutdowns [4]. The Encyclopedia of Chicago dates *systematic, sustained* disinfection — chlorine added at the pumping stations rather than experimentally at the cribs — to **1916** [31]. The City of Chicago's own 1956 retrospective simply states "chlorination of the water supplied to Chicago, as a safeguard to public health, was started in 1912" without distinguishing the experimental from the permanent phase [1]. Both dates are defensible depending on what is being measured (see Open Questions). Ericson himself is credited with pushing for rigid filtration of the city's water for years before his death in 1927 — he was still preparing a paper titled "A Program for Improving Water Service in Chicago" for the American Water Works Association when he died, three years before voters finally approved the South District plant [33].

**South District/Sawyer Filtration Plant (1930–1947).** Turbidity, algae and industrial pollution (including from the Indiana Harbor Ship Canal) continued to plague the unfiltered North and Central districts' water even after chlorination began. An experimental filtration plant was tried in 1928, and in **1930** Chicago voters approved construction of a full-scale filtration plant on the South Side [1][31]. The Great Depression and then World War II stretched what should have been a few years of construction into seventeen: the plant began **partial treatment in October 1945** and reached **complete operation on May 14, 1947** — becoming, at that moment, the largest water filtration plant in the world [1][2][10]. As built, it comprised a low-lift pumping station with eight pumps (combined raw-water capacity 750 MGD), three chemical mixing basins, three settling basins (3–5 hours' detention, removing 70–90% of coagulated solids), eighty rapid sand filters, two filtered-water reservoirs (combined 141-million-gallon capacity), a chemical building, laboratories, an administration building, shops and a garage, largely topped by a landscaped green roof exceeding 10 acres [1][10]. It served three South Side pumping stations — 68th Street, Roseland, and Western Avenue — supplying 1,543,000 people by 1955, with average pumpage that year of 345 MGD and a 1955 peak day of 478 MGD [1]. Treatment chemicals in 1955 included chlorine (6,069 tons, for disinfection), aluminum sulfate and chlorinated ferrous sulfate (coagulation), lime (corrosion control), activated carbon (taste/odor), sodium silicate (coagulation aid), ammonium sulfate (to suppress chlorinous taste), and — notably — hydrofluosilicic acid (2,700 tons), i.e., fluoride, already in use for dental-caries control by the mid-1950s [1]. No named private architect or engineering firm is identified in the sources located for this report; the plant appears to have been designed in-house by the city's own water engineering staff under the Bureau of Water (see Open Questions).

**Central District/Jardine Filtration Plant (1954–1968).** As Chicago's North and Central districts kept growing on chlorination-only treatment, the city moved to build a second, larger filtration plant on a 61-acre artificial peninsula in Chicago's outer harbor, just north of Navy Pier. The project was contested in court: in **Bowes v. City of Chicago**, decided by the Illinois Supreme Court on **May 24, 1954**, opponents had won a lower-court injunction blocking construction in the harbor; the Supreme Court reversed, clearing the city to proceed. The court's opinion records the site as roughly 61 acres at the surface of Lake Michigan, immediately north of and 400 feet from Navy Pier, with an "indicated cost of construction" at that time of **$85,000,000** [2][3]. Site preparation began soon after: by the city's own account, published in July 1956, a coffer dam had been completed and the enclosed area dewatered and graded during 1955, with construction "now under way" [1]. The plant as planned would have a "rated capacity" of 960 million gallons per day and would comprise tunnel connections to existing tunnels, a low-lift pumping station, sixteen mixing units, sixteen settling basins, ninety-two filters with two filter beds each, filtered-water storage reservoirs, a chemical building, laboratories, an administration building, and shops [1]. Construction was carried out by Chicago's own Bureau of Engineering [9][20]; landscape architect **Dan Kiley** and architect **Stan Gladych** of **C.F. Murphy Associates** designed the adjoining public grounds, later named Milton Lee Olive Park, including five circular fountains (evoking the five Great Lakes) capable of spraying up to 100 feet high, and the site incorporated the bronze sculpture "Hymn to Water" by **Milton Horn** [9][12][35]. The park was dedicated June 19, 1966, to Pfc. Milton Lee Olive III, the first African American Medal of Honor recipient of the Vietnam War [35]. Sources disagree, sometimes internally, on the completion date: the plant reached **full operation in the fall of 1964** according to the trade press (Wastewater Digest states directly: "the City of Chicago started full operation of the Jardine facility in the fall of 1964") and the Encyclopedia of Chicago, which credits it with overtaking Sawyer as the world's largest filtration plant that year, while Wikipedia ("began functioning in 1968"), Gizmodo ("Opened in 1968..."), the structural-engineering database Structurae, and Engineering News-Record all independently give **1968** as the year construction finished or the plant opened, with ENR additionally reporting that "construction lasted 13 years and cost $105 million" [9][13][20][21][28][31]. Reconciling these, the most likely reading is that the plant came online and assumed the bulk of its treatment load in 1964, while ancillary construction, additional filter units (built out to 96 filter beds rather than the 1956 plan's 92), and site finishing continued for several more years, with total project cost rising from the 1954 estimate of $85 million to a final figure near $105 million [1][2][13].

**Fluoridation.** Community water fluoridation was politically approved by the Chicago City Council via a resolution on **June 16, 1954**, with the program "set into operation" citywide on **May 1, 1956**. These dates — previously traceable only to a secondary aggregator (ActionPA.org) whose specific page 404'd on re-verification — are now confirmed by a primary legal source: the Illinois Supreme Court's opinion in the 1964 taxpayer suit *Schuringa v. City of Chicago*, 30 Ill. 2d 504 (1964), which recites that a special city council committee began studying fluoridation on October 31, 1951, that the council "passed a resolution on June 16, 1954, declaring the fluoridation of the water supply to be in the interest of the public health," and that after approval by the Illinois Department of Health the program "was set into operation on May 1, 1956" [36]. However, the city's own July 1956 technical report already records fluoride (applied as hydrofluosilicic acid) among the chemicals used in South District filtration treatment for calendar year 1955, which — if that dating is accurate — would place fluoride use at the South District plant specifically at least a year before the confirmed May 1956 *citywide* program start; this narrower discrepancy is flagged, not resolved, in Open Questions below [1][36]. As of the 2023 Consumer Confidence Report, Chicago maintains fluoride at the Illinois Department of Public Health's recommended optimal level of 0.7 mg/L (range 0.6–0.8 mg/L) [34].

- **1973** — James W. Jardine retires as Commissioner of Water (and Sewers), a post he had held since 1953 [9].
- **1977 (April 5)** — James W. Jardine dies; the Central District Filtration Plant is renamed the James W. Jardine Water Purification Plant in his honor [9][20].
- **2007–2011** — New 37,000-sq-ft chlorine-processing building constructed at the South/Sawyer plant (Contract C1339); general contractor George Sollitt Construction Co., engineering/architect of record Greeley and Hansen; achieved LEED Silver certification [17].
- **2012 (Aug.)–2014 (Sept.)** — Complete re-roofing of the Jardine filtration building: demolition of the original ~50-year-old coal-tar/gravel roof over precast concrete channel slabs (which by then showed spalling, corrosion and cracking), removal of 6,100 tons of debris, installation of 712,000 board-feet of cellular glass insulation, replacement of 30,000 precast concrete roof channels, and application of a thermoplastic (later described as PVC/Flex FB Elvaloy KEE) membrane over 10.3 acres / roughly 448,000 sq ft; condition assessment performed by CTLGroup [4][14][15].
- **2016 (Sept. 19–20)** — South Water Purification Plant renamed the Eugene Sawyer Water Purification Plant, honoring Chicago's 53rd mayor; resolution introduced by Aldermen Roderick Sawyer, Greg Mitchell and Edward Burke; commemorated by Mayor Rahm Emanuel on Sept. 20, 2016 [8][10].
- **2010s (undated more precisely)** — $10 million dehumidification/ventilation system replacement at Jardine's Filter Galleries and Main Gallery (16 conditioners, 3 concentrators), construction engineering oversight by Milhouse Engineering & Construction, prime contractor CTR JV [16].
- **2020 (July, $74.5 million)** — Major renovation begins at the Sawyer plant: roof restoration of the original 1941(/1947) building and the 1964 addition, facade work, and mechanical/electrical/plumbing system overhauls, managed by IHC Construction Companies LLC [9][18].
- **2020 (May)** — Over 300 Rotork IQ multi-turn electric actuators and 200+ IQT part-turn actuators installed at Sawyer, replacing aging hydraulic cylinders on 12–30-inch butterfly valves controlling raw water, filter saturation, backwash/drain and surface-wash flows [19].
- **2011–present** — Proactive PFAS ("forever chemicals") sampling at Chicago's treatment plants, initiated in 2011, with levels at Jardine reported below federal limits in recent testing [per secondary aggregation of chicago.gov PFAS data; see Open Questions].
- **2020 (early)** — New Ultrasonic Meter Pilot program launched to evaluate a new water-meter technology for citywide use [32].
- **2022–2026** — DWM's five-year, $1.7 billion Water System Capital Improvement Program funds, among other work: at Jardine, a sediment force-main project, HVAC improvements, replacement of turbidity meters and actuators, and the start of a filter-building brick rehabilitation project; at Sawyer, the "Joliet Tunnel Connection" project; system-wide, filter underdrain replacement and filter-building roof replacement at both plants, plus continued lead service line replacement (program scope: approximately 380,000 lead lines citywide) and pumping-station upgrades at Central Park, Western Avenue and Mayfair stations. Combined 2022–2026 investment at the two purification plants totals approximately **$469.6 million** [32].
- **2024 (Fall, planned)** — DWM begins transitioning corrosion-control chemistry at both plants from a blended-phosphate formula to orthophosphate, which the department's research found "significantly better at reducing lead levels at the homeowner's tap"; new chemical feed facilities were constructed at both Jardine and Sawyer to support the switch, approved by the Illinois EPA [34]. A CWPAC meeting-minutes document additionally describes a December 16, 2024 phased step in this transition at Sawyer specifically, moving from a 60%/40% polyphosphate/orthophosphate blend toward 30%/70% and raising the target orthophosphate dose from 0.5 to 0.85 mg/L [per secondary aggregation; see Open Questions].
- **Ongoing (through 2025)** — Sawyer plant boiler system overhaul (~$4 million over four years), replacing high-pressure steam boilers with a decentralized hot-water, dual-fuel boiler system [per secondary aggregation; see Open Questions].
- **2025 (Jan.)** — ABC7 Chicago's I-Team conducts a rare, exclusive media tour of the normally closed Jardine plant [26] (secondary aggregation; see Open Questions).

## Current operations

Today, the Bureau of Water Supply (one of three bureaus within the Chicago Department of Water Management, alongside the Bureau of Engineering and the Bureau of Administrative Support) operates both plants around the clock, drawing raw water through the Dever and Dunne cribs, treating it, and delivering it by gravity and pumping through roughly 65 miles of tunnel and 4,300–4,400 miles of water main to 12 pumping stations serving Chicago and approximately 118–125 suburban customers — the count varies slightly by source and year: 118 (older trade/journalism sources), 120 (chicago.gov, current), 123 (2022–2026 CIP), and 125 (Chicago Public Library's archival description) — and onward to customers [1][5][6][7][24][32]. DWM as a whole employs approximately 2,100 full-time staff on an annual operating budget of about $1 billion, with administrative offices located at the Jardine plant, 1000 E. Ohio Street [6]. The treatment train at both plants follows the conventional sequence: coarse screening (eight traveling screens per plant catch fish and debris), chemical coagulation with aluminum sulfate (alum) and, at times, polymer, flocculation in paddle-agitated basins, sedimentation (removing roughly 90% of remaining particulates), rapid sand filtration through gravel-and-sand beds for a "polishing" effect, and finally disinfection with chlorine, plus post-treatment dosing of fluoride (for dental health) and phosphate (to coat aging pipe interiors and inhibit lead and copper leaching) [1][9][13][21]. Water quality staff reportedly run on the order of 900 tests a day at Jardine alone, and DWM chemists and technicians conduct more than 600,000 water-quality analyses annually system-wide [26] (secondary aggregation; see Open Questions). Both plants reportedly operate on an isolated internal control network without direct internet connectivity, coordinate daily cybersecurity diagnostics with federal partners, and permit public access only by appointment — a posture tightened after the September 11, 2001 attacks [26] (secondary aggregation; see Open Questions).

The Department's own 2023 Consumer Confidence (Water Quality) Report — a document every U.S. water utility must file annually under the Safe Drinking Water Act — recorded zero regulatory violations across all monitored contaminants that year. Reported 2023 figures include: total trihalomethanes (TTHM) at a highest locational running annual average of 32.6 ppb (range 15.9–51.0 ppb; limit 80 ppb); haloacetic acids (HAA5) at 16.4 ppb (range 6.0–26.9 ppb; limit 60 ppb); lead below the EPA's 15 ppb action level system-wide (zero monitored sites exceeded the action level); copper likewise below its 1.3 ppm action level; and free chlorine residual within the required 0–4 ppm range. The same report confirmed continuous voluntary monitoring for Cryptosporidium, Giardia and E. coli in source water, with neither Cryptosporidium nor Giardia detected in 2023 source-water samples, and separate ongoing monitoring for hexavalent chromium (chromium-6) and PFAS/PFOS, for which Illinois EPA has not yet set enforceable standards [34]. Lake Michigan itself — Chicago's sole source water — is the second-largest Great Lake by volume (roughly 1,180 cubic miles) and third-largest by surface area [34].

Lead-service-line replacement is the dominant current infrastructure story touching both plants. DWM's own 2022–2026 Capital Improvement Program puts the Lead Service Line Replacement Program's scope at approximately **380,000 lead lines** citywide (a 2025 Chicago Sun-Times investigation independently estimated the lead-line share at roughly 70% of all service lines and put the total nearer 400,000, illustrating how the count varies by source and methodology) [29][32]. Both treatment plants add phosphate specifically to reduce leaching from those pipes while the city works through several free replacement programs (income-qualified "Equity LSLR," leak/break-triggered replacement, block-long replacement tied to water/sewer main work, and a fee-waived homeowner-initiated option) [34]. Estimates for full program completion — and the applicable regulatory deadline — vary by source between the mid-2040s (under the EPA's Lead and Copper Rule Improvements, which reportedly granted Chicago until 2047) and as late as 2076 at then-current replacement pace, with total program cost estimated in the $12–14 billion range [29, and secondary aggregation — see Open Questions]. Current treated-water pricing is about $4.89 per 1,000 gallons for water alone (roughly $9.78 combined with wastewater charges), among the lower rates of major U.S. cities [30].

## Notable events & incidents

- **January 20, 1909** — Fire aboard a wooden workers' barracks on the crib under construction for the Dunne intake tunnel system killed an estimated 70 tunnel workers, by fire or drowning while attempting to escape — one of the deadliest incidents in the system's construction history [11].
- **October 8–9, 1871** — The Great Chicago Fire damaged the North Pumping Works (Chicago Avenue), taking the pumps out of service for eight days even as the adjacent Water Tower survived largely intact [1].
- **1954** — *Bowes v. City of Chicago* reached the Illinois Supreme Court after opponents won a lower-court injunction against building the Central District plant in Chicago's harbor; the Supreme Court's reversal on May 24, 1954, was necessary before construction of what became the Jardine plant could proceed [2][3].
- **1955** — Record system pumpage: a peak day of 1,513,140,000 gallons on July 27, 1955, and a peak hour of 1,783,000,000 gallons (annualized rate) on July 23 at 3 p.m. [1].
- **1955** — Chicago Health Department reported zero deaths from waterborne disease citywide; eight typhoid cases and 49 amoebic dysentery cases were recorded but all were traced to infection outside Chicago (foreign travel or rural areas), reflecting the city's transition from a historically high typhoid rate to one of the lowest among major U.S. cities [1].
- **December 1931** — The U.S. Lighthouse Service completed a nine-month, $92,000 project installing 240,000-candlepower rotating navigation beacons and fog-signal equipment on two of Chicago's water cribs, along with fourteen miles of submarine cable for power and remote control from a shore breakwater light station — the city permitted the federal navigation infrastructure on its cribs in exchange for the protective and navigational benefit, at no separate construction cost to the government [23].
- **1997–1998** — The Carter H. Harrison Crib tunnel (by then supplemented/superseded by the Dever Crib) was taken out of service in 1997; when drained for inspection in 1998 portions of the tunnel had collapsed, prompting the city to spend $5.3 million filling sections beneath Lake Shore Drive and triggering litigation against engineering and construction firms involved in the original work [11].
- **2016** — Renaming ceremony for the Eugene Sawyer Water Purification Plant, led by Mayor Rahm Emanuel with the Sawyer family present, marking the first time a Chicago water plant was named for a former mayor [8].
- **2024** — AFSCME Council 31 publicly commended Chicago DWM staff at the Jardine plant for maintaining reliable, safe water delivery through severe storms and flooding events that year [26] (secondary aggregation; see Open Questions).
- **2025 (Jan.)** — ABC7 Chicago's I-Team was granted a rare on-camera tour inside the normally closed Jardine plant, the first such media access in decades, describing it as a "hidden gem" of American infrastructure [26] (secondary aggregation; see Open Questions).

## Open questions

- **Exact chlorination start date.** Primary/agency sources conflict on whether 1912 (Chicago Dept. of Water & Sewers, 1956, and City Engineer John Ericson's own account of experimental trials) or 1916 (Encyclopedia of Chicago, describing sustained/systematic disinfection) is the correct "start" date. Both are likely correct for what they describe — 1912 for first experimental use, 1916 for permanent citywide adoption — but no single source in this research states that reconciliation explicitly [1][4][31].
- **Jardine completion date: 1964 vs. 1968.** Multiple credible sources give different "completion" years — full operation in fall 1964 (Wastewater Digest trade article; Encyclopedia of Chicago) versus construction completion/"began functioning" in 1968 (Structurae's structural database, Engineering News-Record, Wikipedia, and Gizmodo — each independently re-verified by direct fetch in July 2026 to state 1968, not 1964; an earlier draft of this report had miscited Wikipedia and Gizmodo as supporting the 1964 date, which they do not). This report presents both and offers a plausible reconciliation (operational by 1964, fully finished/expanded by 1968) but could not locate an unambiguous primary-source dedication record confirming either date definitively [9][13][20][21][28][31].
- **Jardine total construction cost.** The 1954 Illinois Supreme Court opinion in *Bowes v. City of Chicago* cites an "indicated cost of construction" of $85,000,000 at that early stage; Engineering News-Record (2017) states the completed project "cost $105 million." No source bridges these two figures with a documented cost history, bond issuance records, or itemized final accounting [2][13].
- **Original South District (Sawyer) Plant architect/engineer of record.** No source located in this research names a specific chief engineer, architect, or engineering firm for the 1930–1947 South District Filtration Plant design, in contrast to the well-documented design team (Bureau of Engineering; landscape architect Dan Kiley; architect Stan Gladych of C.F. Murphy) for the later Jardine plant. This is likely recoverable from Chicago Public Library's Municipal Reference Collection or the Bureau of Engineering's own annual reports, which were not accessible within this research pass.
- **Fluoridation start date — resolved.** The June 16, 1954 council resolution and May 1, 1956 citywide program start, originally traced only to an anti-fluoridation advocacy site (ActionPA.org) whose specific page returned a 404 error on re-verification, are now independently confirmed by a primary legal source: the Illinois Supreme Court's opinion in *Schuringa v. City of Chicago*, 30 Ill. 2d 504, 198 N.E.2d 326 (1964), a taxpayer suit seeking to enjoin the city's fluoridation program, which recites both dates in its background section [36]. One residual discrepancy remains unresolved: the city's own July 1956 technical report already lists fluoride (as hydrofluosilicic acid) among 1955 treatment chemicals at the South District plant specifically, which would predate the confirmed May 1956 *citywide* program start by roughly a year — possibly reflecting South District-only pilot use ahead of the citywide rollout, but no source located in this research confirms that reconciliation explicitly [1][36].
- **Several current-operations figures still rely on secondary aggregation rather than a document this research could independently verify**, including: ~900 daily water-quality tests at Jardine; 600,000+ annual system-wide analyses; the 2011 PFAS-monitoring start date; the exact December 16, 2024 corrosion-control percentages/dosing at Sawyer specifically (the *general* fall-2024 blended-phosphate-to-orthophosphate transition and new chemical feed facilities at both plants are now confirmed directly by the city's own 2023 Consumer Confidence Report, source [34]); the ongoing multi-year boiler overhaul at Sawyer; the January 2025 ABC7 tour; and the 2024 AFSCME commendation. These appear to trace to identifiable primary documents (chicago.gov PFAS pages, CWPAC meeting minutes, news coverage) that this research could not independently load and confirm verbatim within the time available; they are flagged rather than asserted as fully verified.
- **Current DWM Commissioner — resolved.** The 2023 Consumer Confidence Report names Randy Conner as DWM Commissioner under Mayor Brandon Johnson's administration; this is confirmed still current as of the July 2026 access date by contemporaneous sources, including coverage of Conner testifying as sitting Commissioner at a 2026 city budget hearing and a Chicago Build 2026 conference speaker listing identifying him in that role [37][38].
- **No Historic American Engineering Record (HAER) or Historic American Buildings Survey (HABS) documentation was found for either the Jardine or Sawyer plant specifically.** The closest related Library of Congress HAER/HABS records located are for the Lakeview Pumping Station (HAER IL-4, built 1915, demolished 1979) and the Chicago Water Tower/Pumping Station (HABS IL-1041, built 1869) — both predecessor/adjacent infrastructure, not the purification plants themselves.
- **Suburban customer count varies by source and year**: "118 suburbs" (several tertiary/older sources), "120 suburbs" (City of Chicago's current official pages), and "125 suburbs" (Chicago Public Library archival description) all appear in sourced material; the true current count was not independently confirmed against a single authoritative current list. A July 2026 re-check found the same split still live across current sources (120 per chicago.gov and its Facebook/LinkedIn accounts; 125 per the 44th Ward's August-2025-updated constituent page), suggesting this is a persistent inconsistency in the city's own public materials rather than a stale citation.
- **Sawyer settling-basin count before the 1960s expansion.** The city's own 1956 technical report states the South District (Sawyer) plant as built had *three* chemical mixing basins and *three* settling basins (matching its then-eighty filters) as of 1955–56 [1]. Engineering News-Record (2017) and Grokipedia instead describe the plant as originally built with *four* settling basins, to which "a fifth settling basin and 40 more filters" were added in 1964 to reach 480 MGD [13][27]. These are not necessarily contradictory — a fourth basin could have been added between 1956 and the 1964 expansion, outside the window either source describes — but no source located here documents that intermediate step, and this report could not independently confirm the current (post-1964) total filter count at Sawyer, which "40 more filters" would imply is roughly 120 rather than the 80 recorded by the 1956 primary source for the original plant.
