# The City of Chicago Combined Sewer Network

## Role in the system

The City of Chicago's combined sewer network is the local collection layer of a three-tier regional wastewater and stormwater system. It is owned and operated by the Chicago Department of Water Management (DWM), a city agency, and is legally and physically distinct from the much larger interceptor and tunnel system owned by the Metropolitan Water Reclamation District of Greater Chicago (MWRD), a separate unit of government [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[10\]](#src-10).

**What flows in.** Roof downspouts, yard and area drains, sump pumps, and street catch basins feed stormwater into the system; toilets, sinks, and industrial process drains feed sanitary sewage into the same pipes through private "house drains" (building sewers) that connect to the public main in the street or parkway [\[5\]](#src-5)[\[6\]](#src-6). Because more than 95 percent of the city's sewer system is combined — that is, it carries sanitary sewage and stormwater runoff in the same pipe — nearly all of this flow mixes immediately downstream of the property line [\[4\]](#src-4). A small minority of the city's drainage area (mainly Lake Shore Drive and non-industrial portions of O'Hare International Airport) is served by separate storm sewers regulated as a Municipal Separate Storm Sewer System (MS4) under NPDES Permit No. IL0002283 for O'Hare, rather than the combined-sewer NPDES permit that governs the rest of the city [\[4\]](#src-4).

**What flows out, and to whom.** City-owned local sewers do not go to a treatment plant directly. They discharge into MWRD-owned intercepting sewers — described by MWRD as the "superhighway of sewers" — which are typically much larger, run along or under major streets and waterways, and are fed by roughly 10,000 connections from local municipal systems across the region [\[10\]](#src-10)[\[12\]](#src-12). From there, dry-weather flow is pumped to one of MWRD's water reclamation plants (the Chicago combined-sewer area is served principally by the Stickney, Calumet, and O'Brien/North Side plants) [\[11\]](#src-11). In wet weather, when the combined flow exceeds what the interceptors and treatment plants can carry, MWRD's Tunnel and Reservoir Plan (TARP, "Deep Tunnel") intercepts the excess through vertical drop shafts and routes it into deep rock tunnels and reservoirs for temporary storage until treatment capacity is available [\[12\]](#src-12)[\[2\]](#src-2). Only when TARP itself is full does the system discharge untreated combined sewage directly to the Chicago River, the Calumet River, or the Des Plaines River through a combined sewer overflow (CSO) [\[2\]](#src-2)[\[12\]](#src-12). The City of Chicago holds its own NPDES discharge permit (No. IL0045012) covering 184 city-owned CSO outfalls into these waterways; 73 of them are jointly monitored with MWRD under a telemetric tide-gate monitoring and data-sharing agreement, and TARP itself serves as the federally approved Long-Term Control Plan for the city's outfalls even though MWRD, not the City, is responsible for building and operating TARP [\[14\]](#src-14)[\[15\]](#src-15). The physical hardware at each of these junctions — the **connecting structures (regulators), tide gates, and drop shafts** that decide, purely by water level, whether a local sewer's flow continues into the interceptor, plunges down into the Deep Tunnel, or spills out the old outfall to the river — is described in detail in the CSOs and discharges document in this archive ([docs/14](14-cso-outfalls-discharges.md), "How each outfall actually works").

**Ownership boundary in plain terms:** the City of Chicago DWM owns everything from the building's connection point in the street out to (but not including) the MWRD intercepting sewer; MWRD owns the intercepting sewers, the CSO structures on the interceptors, the TARP tunnels/dropshafts/reservoirs, and the treatment plants [\[10\]](#src-10)[\[1\]](#src-1). This is a distinct, coordinate relationship, not a hierarchy — DWM and MWRD are both independent units of local government with their own elected/appointed leadership, budgets, and capital programs [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[10\]](#src-10). That coordinate relationship has occasionally produced open friction: under a February 1898 agreement the City was to build 20-ft (Thirty-ninth Street) and 16-ft (Lawrence Avenue) outlet conduits for its own intercepting sewers into the Sanitary District's new Drainage Canal, but City construction stalled for years, and by March 1901 the District's trustees had adopted a board resolution threatening legal action to compel "specific performance" of the City's contract — a dispute chronicled in full, along with the conduits' complete construction history, in the North Branch Pumping Station document in this archive ([docs/08](08-north-branch-pumping-station.md)) [\[51\]](#src-51).

## Original construction

Chicago's sewer system began as a direct response to repeated public-health catastrophes. Six consecutive years of typhoid and dysentery outbreaks, capped by a cholera epidemic in 1854 that (by contemporary Chicago Tribune reporting) killed roughly six percent of the city's population, pushed the Illinois legislature to create a municipal Board of Sewerage Commissioners on February 14, 1855 [\[20\]](#src-20)[\[21\]](#src-21). The Board hired Ellis Sylvester Chesbrough — previously chief engineer of the Boston Water Works — as its engineer in December 1855; he delivered his "Report and Plan of Sewerage for the City of Chicago" to the Board on December 31, 1855, and it was formally adopted [\[21\]](#src-21)[\[25\]](#src-25). This plan is generally credited as the first comprehensive, engineered sewer system built for an American city [\[20\]](#src-20)[\[17\]](#src-17).

Chesbrough's plan faced a fundamental problem: Chicago's terrain was so flat, and so close to the level of Lake Michigan, that sewers could not be laid at a depth sufficient for gravity flow without pipes literally sitting below the level of the lake into which they had to drain [\[19\]](#src-19)[\[21\]](#src-21). His solution — debated and revised through 1856 and formally approved by the Common Council as the "High Grade" plan on May 4, 1857 — was to build the sewers largely above the existing ground surface and then raise the grade of the streets around them by roughly six to fourteen feet (accounts vary: contemporary Tribune coverage and modern secondary sources cite figures from "6 to 10 feet" up to "as much as 14 feet" in places), filling in around the new pipes and re-paving at the new elevation [\[21\]](#src-21)[\[19\]](#src-19)[\[22\]](#src-22). Existing masonry buildings were jacked up in place on hundreds to thousands of jackscrews — the Tremont House, a six-story hotel, was raised six feet on roughly 1,000 jackscrews operated by about 500 men while remaining open for business; the 27,000-ton, iron-framed Robbins Building was raised 27.5 inches on 1,584 jackscrews over three days in November 1865 — while smaller wood-frame buildings were more often moved bodily on rollers to new lots [\[21\]](#src-21). This "Raising of Chicago" continued in a piecemeal fashion into the 1860s and beyond, and construction on the sewer system itself began in earnest in 1856 (roughly six miles built by the end of that year), with the City's own modern engineering materials describing sewer-system construction as "beginning in 1857" [\[22\]](#src-22)[\[4\]](#src-4). Two independent primary sources close most of that gap: the City's own 1891 Department of Public Works annual report and a 1927 Sanitary District consulting-engineers' report both independently corroborate 1856 as the year construction actually began, suggesting the City's modern "1857" figure most likely refers to the Common Council's May 4, 1857 formal approval of the High Grade street-raising plan rather than to the start of construction itself [\[44\]](#src-44)[\[50\]](#src-50).

Chesbrough's adopted 1855 plan specified real numbers, not just a general concept: main sewers ran in alternate streets roughly 800 feet apart — three to six feet in diameter, built of brick with 8.5-inch walls, discharging into the river — with two-foot brick sub-main sewers in the cross streets feeding into them at right angles; the originally-sewered district was far smaller than the modern city, bounded (per an 1877 sketch by principal assistant engineer W. H. Clark) by Taylor Street on the south, a Halsted–Madison–Sangamon–Fulton line on the west, and Franklin Street/Chicago Avenue/Rush Street on the north [\[48\]](#src-48). The first Board of Sewerage Commissioners — William B. Ogden (president), James D. Webster, and Sylvester Lind, with Chesbrough as Chief Engineer and Clark as principal Assistant Engineer — sold the first $100,000 of sewerage bonds March 19, 1856 and awarded the first three sewer contracts that year: State Street from Randolph Street to the river (Ives & Lonergan), North Clark Street from North Water to Erie (S. S. Wiltsee & Co.), and West Randolph Street from the river to Desplaines Street (S. S. Wiltsee & Co.) [\[44\]](#src-44). The Commission's own later tally confirms and refines the "roughly six miles" figure above — 6.02 miles were built in 1856, followed by 4.868 in 1857, 19.29 in 1858, 10.45 in 1859, and 18.09 in 1860, for 53.71 total miles built by the Board of Sewerage Commissioners through 1860; by December 31, 1876 the city had 265.80 miles of sewers in place [\[44\]](#src-44).

Water supply and sewerage were administered by separate bodies at first — a Water Board (1851–1861) alongside the Sewerage Board (1855–1861) — before the two merged on May 6, 1861 into a single Board of Public Works; that Board was in turn abolished September 18, 1876 in favor of a Department of Public Works with dedicated Bureaus of Water and of Sewers, a structure that persisted for decades (Chesbrough himself, having stepped down as the city's engineer, briefly returned to public service as the Department's second Commissioner of Public Works, January–May 1879) [\[47\]](#src-47). This is the earliest documented point at which Chicago's water and sewer functions shared a single governing body — a partial answer to the open question, below, about when a combined "water and sewers" department first existed, though the modern Department of Water Management's own founding date was not traced past this point [\[47\]](#src-47).

The earliest sewer mains were brick, built as large masonry conduits (particularly the downtown trunk lines), supplemented from early on by vitrified clay pipe for smaller laterals and house connections — vitrified clay was cheap, locally producible, and resistant to the sulfuric acid that forms naturally inside sewer lines from hydrogen sulfide gas, and it remained the dominant material for city mains into the 20th century [\[17\]](#src-17)[\[19\]](#src-19). Both brick and clay-pipe sewers from this era remain in active service today and are explicitly addressed in DWM's current construction regulations, which require a licensed structural engineer's sign-off for any connection that breaks into half or more of a brick sewer's diameter [\[5\]](#src-5). The shift away from brick came gradually and is independently dated by a 1917 University of Illinois engineering thesis surveying Chicago's own practice: as of that year, laterals were still tile pipe and "submains or trunk sewers and outfall sewers are of brick construction with a few exceptions," but the Board of Local Improvements had already built Chicago's first plain-concrete sewer system in 1908 (South Chicago district, sizes down to 2.5 feet, including a 10.5-foot concrete outfall sewer), and in 1910 the City and the Sanitary District jointly built Chicago's first large reinforced-concrete sewer — a 12-by-14-foot conduit in South Western Avenue running from the main Sanitary District channel to Thirty-ninth Street [\[49\]](#src-49).

The Chesbrough sewers were built to drain directly into the Chicago River and, from there, into Lake Michigan — the same body of water that supplied the city's drinking water [\[20\]](#src-20)[\[4\]](#src-4). This created an escalating pollution problem as the city's population grew explosively (from about 4,470 in 1840 to nearly 1.7 million by 1900) and as meatpacking and other heavy industry added their waste to the river [\[38\]](#src-38). Chesbrough's later projects — a two-mile water-intake tunnel extending 60 feet under the lake bed to a new offshore crib (begun 1863–64, roughly three years' round-the-clock construction) and, ultimately, the reversal of the Chicago River's flow away from the lake — were attempts to manage the same underlying conflict between a combined sewer system and a lake-based water supply [\[21\]](#src-21)[\[22\]](#src-22)[\[38\]](#src-38). River reversal itself was accomplished by the Chicago Sanitary District (created 1889, predecessor to today's MWRD) through construction of the 28-mile Chicago Sanitary and Ship Canal, completed January 2, 1900 — Main Channel construction proper cost about $23.7 million, roughly $33 million including land and auxiliary works (see the river-reversal document in this archive) — a project separate from — though downstream of and dependent on — the city's own sewer mains [\[11\]](#src-11)[\[20\]](#src-20).

A 2025 economic-history study by researchers including a Federal Reserve Bank of Philadelphia economist used land-value data to estimate the benefit of Chicago's 1874–1880 expansion of piped water and sewer service into then-unsettled land a few miles from downtown. It found that parcels receiving water and sewer access saw land values more than double, that households with access paid roughly 36 percent of income for a serviced parcel versus 12 percent for an unserved one, and that the benefit-to-cost ratio of the 1874–1880 expansion was on the order of 62 times the construction cost — a data point illustrating how highly the 19th-century market valued sewer and water access, though it does not itself report the dollar cost of that specific expansion [\[26\]](#src-26).

## Upgrades & modernization timeline

- **1855–1857** — Board of Sewerage Commissioners created; Chesbrough appointed engineer; his sewerage plan adopted Dec. 31, 1855; "High Grade" street-raising plan formally approved May 4, 1857; construction of the first sewers begins (contemporary sources put ~6 miles built by end of 1856) [\[20\]](#src-20)[\[21\]](#src-21)[\[22\]](#src-22)[\[4\]](#src-4).
- **1863–1867** — Two-mile, 60-foot-deep lake tunnel built to a new offshore water intake crib to escape sewage-contaminated nearshore water — an attempt to manage the consequences of a combined system draining to the drinking-water source [\[21\]](#src-21)[\[22\]](#src-22).
- **1889** — Illinois legislature creates the Chicago Sanitary District (predecessor of MWRD), beginning the separation of large-interceptor/treatment functions from local city sewer construction [\[11\]](#src-11)[\[20\]](#src-20).
- **1900** — Chicago River flow reversed via the new Sanitary and Ship Canal, redirecting the endpoint of the combined-sewer system's overflow away from Lake Michigan [\[11\]](#src-11)[\[20\]](#src-20).
- **Early 20th century (1900s–1930s)** — Sanitary District begins building (from 1906) what is now a ~560-mile network of intercepting sewers (6 in.–27 ft diameter) to capture flow from local city and suburban sewers before it reaches the rivers, and begins large-scale treatment-plant construction (Calumet plant, 1922, was the district's first; several more plants merged over time into what is now the Stickney Water Reclamation Plant) [\[10\]](#src-10)[\[11\]](#src-11). This is the point at which most of the modern city/district ownership boundary — city mains feeding district interceptors — was established.
- **1920s–1960s** — Continued city sewer main construction and periodic relief-sewer projects as the built-up area of Chicago expanded; Chicago Public Library's Chicago Sewers Collection photographic record (bulk dates 1922–1969) documents numerous individual relief and trunk-sewer construction and repair projects during this period, including WPA-funded work in the 1930s [\[17\]](#src-17)[\[18\]](#src-18).
- **1963–1978 (Lawrence Avenue Underflow project)** — City of Chicago's 1963 Five-Year Capital Improvements Program calls for a new "Eastwood-Wilson" auxiliary outlet sewer on the North Side; re-designated the Lawrence Avenue Underflow Sewer System effective November 1, 1966, and — per Harza Engineering Company's appraisal — lowered into a 200-to-250-foot-deep rock tunnel (12-to-17-ft diameter, engineer's 1966 cost estimate $12.3 million vs. $14.35 million for open-cut) rather than built as a conventional shallow sewer; construction proceeded under a $1.5 million EPA demonstration grant (approved April 25, 1967) across five separate contracts from November 1967 (start of the main-shaft/rock-tunnel contract) to June 1975, with the pumping-station contract still ~97% complete as of a February 1979 status check. Documented by the City of Chicago and EPA as "the first section of the Chicago deep tunnel to be constructed" — a direct forerunner, roughly a decade earlier, of MWRD's TARP [\[39\]](#src-39).
- **1967** — MWRD, the State of Illinois, Cook County, and the City of Chicago form the Flood Control Coordinating Committee in response to combined sewer overflows that by the 1960s were occurring more than 100 days a year; the committee evaluates 50 alternatives (including full separation of the combined system, rejected as too costly and disruptive) before recommending what becomes TARP [\[12\]](#src-12).
- **1972** — MWRD formally adopts the Tunnel and Reservoir Plan (TARP / "Deep Tunnel") as the region's combined-sewer-overflow and flood-control strategy; TARP is a MWRD asset, downstream of and separate from city sewers, but its capacity directly determines how often the city's own CSO outfalls discharge [\[12\]](#src-12)[\[2\]](#src-2).
- **1975–2006** — TARP tunnel construction; Upper Des Plaines segment (near O'Hare) online 1981; full tunnel system operational by 2006 [\[12\]](#src-12).
- **1995** — Illinois EPA approves TARP as the federally recognized Long-Term Control Plan (LTCP) for combined sewer overflows in the Chicago area, including the City of Chicago's own permitted outfalls, under the "presumptive approach" of the 1994 federal CSO Control Policy [\[14\]](#src-14).
- **1998 / 2015 / 2017–present** — TARP reservoirs come online: Gloria Alitto Majewski Reservoir (1998), Thornton Composite Reservoir (2015, "nearly eliminated" CSOs in its Calumet-system service area), McCook Reservoir Stage 1 (2017) and Stage 2 (under construction as of the most recent MWRD fact sheet obtained) [\[12\]](#src-12).
- **2001–2010** — City of Chicago Department of Environment pilots the Green Alley program (four alley reconstructions, 2001–2006) using permeable pavement, open-bottom catch basins, and recycled materials to reduce local flooding without enlarging sewers; the Green Alley Handbook is published in 2010 [\[9\]](#src-9). By the mid-2020s, CDOT reports "more than 400" green alleys built since the program's expansion (roughly 2007 onward), at a typical cost of $250,000–$500,000 per block [\[9\]](#src-9).
- **December 13, 2006 / effective January 1, 2008** — Chicago City Council adds Chapter 11-18, the "Chicago Stormwater Management Ordinance," to the Municipal Code, requiring stormwater management plans and on-site detention/retention for regulated new development and redevelopment disturbing 15,000+ square feet [\[7\]](#src-7). *Note on sourcing:* this 2008-effective city ordinance, not a "2014 ordinance," is Chicago's primary stormwater-detention regulation; the year 2014 more likely corresponds either to (a) the City's April 2014 Green Stormwater Infrastructure Strategy (below) or (b) MWRD's regional Watershed Management Ordinance, introduced in May 2014, which governs stormwater detention for new development in suburban Cook County outside the City of Chicago and is a different instrument administered by a different agency [\[7\]](#src-7)[\[8\]](#src-8). Where the original research brief for this project referenced a "Stormwater Management Ordinance (2014)," that date appears to conflate these two/three distinct measures; readers should treat 2008 as the City ordinance's operative date.
- **April 2014** — Mayor Rahm Emanuel's administration releases the City of Chicago Green Stormwater Infrastructure Strategy, committing $50 million over five years to green infrastructure construction (an investment the strategy projected could provide about 10 million gallons of stormwater storage and reduce annual runoff by roughly 250 million gallons), and reporting that existing green roofs and permeable pavement were already capturing more than 85 million gallons of stormwater per year, that over 350 green roofs covering more than 5.5 million square feet had been built (Chicago City Hall's own green roof, one of the program's earliest, helped launch the initiative), and that the 2008 Stormwater Management Ordinance had already reduced impervious surface citywide by more than 3 million square feet [\[8\]](#src-8).
- **2012–2021** — Under its Sewer Lining and Sewer Main Improvement Programs, DWM lines 503 miles of sewer and more than 97,000 structures, and replaces approximately 206 miles of sewer main [\[4\]](#src-4).
- **2022** — DWM plans to line ~42 additional miles of sewer and ~8,000 structures, and to replace ~22 miles of sewer main, as part of that year's program [\[4\]](#src-4).
- **2022–2026** — DWM's 5-year, $1.2 billion Sewer System Capital Improvement Program funds sewer lining ($264.5M), rehabilitation ($144.7M), and replacement/construction ($789.9M), financed mainly by sewer revenue bonds (62%) and state IEPA revolving-loan proceeds (30%) [\[3\]](#src-3).
- **2025–2029** — DWM's newer 5-year Sewer System Program grows to $1.9 billion (10.3% of the citywide CIP), superseding the figures above: $1.225 billion (65%) for sewer replacement/construction, $374.9 million (20%) for lining, and $293.0 million (15%) for rehabilitation, funded roughly 59% by bond proceeds, 30% by state funds, 7% by federal WIFIA loans, and the remaining 4% by sewer-fee revenue and other sources — a funding mix markedly more state/federal-dependent than 2022–2026's, consistent with the post-2023-flood disaster funding described below [\[52\]](#src-52).
- **April 1, 2024** — Illinois EPA reissues the City of Chicago's NPDES CSO permit (IL0045012) with strengthened terms: enhanced monitoring, a Nine Minimum Controls Enhancement Plan requirement, evaluation of green stormwater infrastructure, expanded public notification, and prioritized attention to "communities of environmental concern"; the permit continues to authorize the City's 184 CSO outfalls [\[14\]](#src-14)[\[15\]](#src-15).
- **July 2, 2023** — Record short-duration rainfall (up to 9.0 inches in 18 hours in parts of the city and near suburbs; 3.35 inches in a single day at O'Hare, a new daily record) overwhelms the combined system; at least 70,000 basements flood, damages across northeastern Illinois are estimated at $500+ million, and a federal disaster is declared August 15, 2023 — triggering, in turn, a subsequent DWM capital-funding surge (see Current operations) [\[31\]](#src-31)[\[32\]](#src-32).
- **2025–2026** — DWM's FY2026 budget, at $774.0 million, includes a new $318.2 million Disaster Recovery Fund (federal CDBG-RF money) explicitly tied to long-term recovery from flooding impacts in 2023 and 2024, alongside the Sewer Fund's ordinary $108.1 million allocation [\[36\]](#src-36).

## Current operations

DWM's Bureau of Operations and Distribution is responsible for day-to-day maintenance of the sewer system, alongside water mains, hydrants, and valves, and fields roughly 145,000 customer calls a year on issues including water/sewage in basements, water on the street, and broken mains [\[1\]](#src-1). Routine sewer maintenance includes cleaning approximately 25,000 catch basins annually and responding to 311-generated service requests for sewer cleaning, inspection, and cave-ins [\[4\]](#src-4). Capital rehabilitation runs on a rolling multi-year cycle combining cured-in-place-pipe (CIPP) and geopolymer trenchless lining (chosen because it is cheaper and less disruptive than open-cut replacement), targeted replacement of hydraulically undersized or structurally deficient mains, and structure (manhole/catch basin) lining and rehabilitation; DWM's FY2022–2026 program alone represents roughly $1.2 billion, and multi-year trenchless-lining contracts awarded to outside contractors (e.g., a reported $148 million package covering roughly 575,000 linear feet of 10-to-60-inch sewer lining, awarded to Granite Inliner) illustrate the scale of this ongoing work [\[3\]](#src-3)[\[4\]](#src-4).

Development review and permitting for any new sewer connection or drain is governed by the City's Regulations for Sewer Construction and Stormwater Management (most recently reissued August 2024) and by Municipal Code Chapters 11-16 (Public Sewers and Drains) and 11-18 (Stormwater Management); regulated developments must submit stormwater management plans, meet volume- and rate-control requirements (commonly implemented with 3-inch and 4-inch "vortex restrictor" outlet devices calibrated to a maximum release rate, typically 0.15 cfs per half-acre), and, where feasible, disconnect roof downspouts from the sewer system entirely [\[5\]](#src-5)[\[7\]](#src-7)[\[6\]](#src-6). Sewer connections and repairs on public property require a permit from the Department of Buildings; minor drain-layer work and short in-kind repairs are exempted subject to registration [\[6\]](#src-6).

The City manages its own combined sewer overflow program under NPDES Permit IL0045012, including a Nine Minimum Controls Enhancement Plan, a Pollution Prevention Plan, and public CSO notification coordinated through MWRD's online and text/email alert systems; members of the public can report a dry-weather CSO discharge by calling 311 [\[2\]](#src-2). To address chronic basement flooding, DWM has historically paired a "Rainblocker" sewer-inlet-control program (mechanical inlet restrictor valves) with public encouragement of downspout disconnection, and more recently has partnered with MWRD, CDOT, and community groups on neighborhood-specific flood-risk assessments — for example a joint pilot evaluation in the Chatham neighborhood on the South Side, a chronically flood-prone area situated near the low end of its local sewershed [\[4\]](#src-4)[\[38\]](#src-38). The nonprofit Center for Neighborhood Technology's RainReady program supplements the City's own efforts with free home flood-risk inspections and green-infrastructure retrofit assistance for homeowners [not independently verified against a City source in this research pass].

Green stormwater infrastructure is now a standing element of nearly every City capital project category — road reconstruction, streetscape work, schoolyard restoration, sewer/water replacement, and dedicated Green Alley projects all specify bioswales, infiltration trenches, permeable pavement, rain gardens, or planter boxes as applicable, per DWM's 2022 MS4 Stormwater Management Plan [\[4\]](#src-4). Green Alleys remain a signature program: CDOT selects roughly one location per ward per year, with the pace of construction accelerating in the mid-2020s thanks to dedicated capital-bond funding (36 blocks added in 2024, with more projected for subsequent years) [\[9\]](#src-9).

FY2026 marks an unusually large jump in DWM's budget — to $774.0 million, an 85.5 percent increase over FY2025's $417.2 million — driven almost entirely by $318.2 million in new federal Community Development Block Grant Disaster Recovery (CDBG-RF) funding tied to long-term recovery from the 2023 and 2024 flood events, on top of the department's standing $108.1 million Sewer Fund (14 percent of the 2026 total) and $326.3 million Water Fund (42.1 percent); departmental staffing is essentially flat at 2,492 positions [\[36\]](#src-36).

## Notable events & incidents

- **1937 Fullerton–Milwaukee Avenue manhole explosions (May 29, 1937).** Naphtha or gasoline vapor that had accumulated in the sewer ignited, blowing 17 manhole covers out of the Fullerton Avenue sewer along roughly a mile of pipe between Kedzie and Western avenues; one of the 155-pound manhole covers was hurled onto and through the roof of a five-story building, falling down an elevator shaft and killing the elevator operator. Residents had reported a gas odor in the area in the days before the blast [\[28\]](#src-28).
- **September 12–16, 2008 flood.** Remnants of Hurricane Ike combined with a slow-moving low-pressure system to drop a then-all-time-record 6.64 inches of rain in a single calendar day (Sept. 13) at O'Hare, with storm totals ranging from about 2.4 to 10.5 inches across northeastern Illinois; MWRD diverted roughly 11.2 billion gallons into Lake Michigan and about 50 billion gallons into the Des Plaines River to relieve the system, roughly 10,000 homes were evacuated, and insured property losses were estimated at $155 million [\[33\]](#src-33)[\[34\]](#src-34).
- **April 18, 2013 sinkhole.** A severe rainstorm ruptured a water main in Chicago's South Deering neighborhood on the Southeast Side; the resulting sinkhole on Houston Street grew from about 20 to about 40 feet across and swallowed three cars, injuring one driver [not directly a sewer failure, but illustrative of combined subsurface-utility risk in older infrastructure corridors].
- **2019 Greater Grand Crossing sinkhole.** A cave-in on the side of an aging brick sewer main near 73rd Street and Woodlawn Avenue opened a street sinkhole, illustrating the continued in-service presence — and periodic failure risk — of 19th/early-20th-century brick trunk sewers within the modern system.
- **July 2, 2023 flash flood.** One of the heaviest rainfall events in Chicago's recorded history (per the National Weather Service), with localized totals up to 9.0 inches over 18 hours and a new daily rainfall record at O'Hare (3.35 inches, surpassing the prior record of 2.06 inches set July 2, 1982); rainfall exceeded the combined capacity of the local sewers and MWRD's Deep Tunnel/McCook Reservoir system, at least 70,000 basements flooded, the Chicago River rose above flood stage near the Loop, and 311 logged flooding complaints from roughly 7,000 addresses in the first week of July alone, disproportionately concentrated in majority-Black West Side neighborhoods such as Austin; the event was declared an Illinois disaster area July 11, 2023 and a federal disaster area August 15, 2023, with regional damages estimated at $500+ million [\[31\]](#src-31)[\[32\]](#src-32).
- **2023–2024 flood recovery funding.** The scale of the July 2023 event (and additional 2024 flood impacts not independently detailed in sources reviewed here) is directly reflected in DWM's FY2026 budget, which allocates a new $318.2 million federal Disaster Recovery Fund specifically for long-term neighborhood recovery [\[36\]](#src-36).
- **For disambiguation — the 1992 "Great Chicago Flood."** This well-known event, in which roughly 250 million gallons of Chicago River water breached an 1899-vintage freight tunnel beneath the river and flooded Loop basements and the Pedway, is frequently associated with "Chicago's underground" in popular accounts but was **not** a sewer-system failure: the freight tunnels were a separate, disused private railway/telephone-cable tunnel network unrelated to the combined sewer system, and the breach point was a pier repair that punctured the tunnel roof rather than a sewer defect. It is included here only to head off a common conflation, not as a sewer-network incident [\[37\]](#src-37).
- **Ongoing: climate-driven overload.** Multiple 2026 news accounts (drawing on state climatologist and University of Illinois research, including "Bulletin 76") report that Chicago's combined sewers were designed decades ago to handle roughly 2 inches of rain in 24 hours, that the likelihood of intense rainstorms in the Chicago area has increased roughly sevenfold over the past century, and that modern storms can now drop more than 8.5 inches in 24 hours — a structural mismatch between 19th/20th-century design assumptions and 21st-century rainfall intensity that both DWM and MWRD cite as the central operational challenge facing the network [\[35\]](#src-35). A 2015 Illinois DNR/State Water Survey engineering study of statewide stormwater design standards puts the same trend in more technical, quantified terms: a Chicago-area storm sewer sized to the once-standard 10-year, 2-hour design storm under the older "TP-40" rainfall statistics (based on data mostly predating the 1950s) now corresponds to only a 6.6-year design storm under the state's currently-recommended Bulletin 70 statistics, or an 8-year storm under the newer NOAA Atlas 14 statistics; the same comparison for a 100-year, 24-hour detention-design storm shows the old TP-40 standard now corresponds to only a 31-to-84-year event depending on which updated rainfall dataset is used — i.e., infrastructure engineered decades ago to a nominal "100-year" storm standard now floods more often than once a century under current rainfall data [\[53\]](#src-53).

## Open questions

- **Reconciling DWM's own structure counts.** DWM materials variously report ~205,000 "collection structures" + ~146,000 manholes (2022 CIP), ~340,000 "related structures" (2003 Water Agenda language still quoted in the 2022 MS4 plan), and ~56,000 "sewer structures" (Operations & Distribution web page) for what should be roughly the same underlying inventory. It was not possible from the sources gathered here to determine whether these reflect different definitions (e.g., catch basins only vs. all structure types), different snapshot years, or simple inconsistency in DWM's own public communications. A records request to DWM or comparison against the City's GIS/sewer-atlas layer would resolve this. *Partial update (this pass):* the City's FY2025–2029 CIP repeats the same "approximately 4,500 miles...205,000 collection structures, and 146,000 manholes" language nearly verbatim, reinforcing these (rather than the 2003-vintage 4,400-mile/340,000-structure figures) as DWM's current standard public figures — though this still does not explain the 56,000-structure Operations & Distribution page [\[52\]](#src-52).
- **Total system mileage: 4,400 vs. 4,500 miles.** Both figures recur across DWM's own materials from different years/pages; it is unclear whether this reflects real system growth, a rounding convention, or inclusion/exclusion of separate storm sewers. *Partial update (this pass):* the FY2025–2029 CIP again uses 4,500 miles, consistent with the FY2022–2026 figure, suggesting 4,400 miles is the outdated (2003-era) figure rather than a rounding artifact — though this remains unconfirmed against DWM directly [\[52\]](#src-52).
- ~~**Largest single city-owned sewer.**~~ **Resolved in this pass.** The City's own FY2025–2029 Capital Improvement Program states that DWM's Sewer Lining Program covers "large-diameter trunk sewers (72" to 204")" — i.e., up to 17 feet — confirming city-owned mains in that range remain active today [\[52\]](#src-52). Historically, the City itself (not the Sanitary District) built an even larger sewer: a 20-ft-diameter, 1,593-ft segment of the Thirty-ninth Street intercepting-sewer conduit (Butler St. to Lake Ave., completed 1900) and a 16-ft conduit under Lawrence Avenue, both built under 1898–99 city–district agreements that required the City to hand the finished conduits over to the Sanitary District — see the Facilities & capacities table above, the "Role in the system" section above, and [docs/08](08-north-branch-pumping-station.md) for the full history [\[45\]](#src-45)[\[46\]](#src-46)[\[51\]](#src-51).
- **Precise founding date of "Department of Water Management" as a merged sewer-and-water agency.** Archival catalog records reference an earlier "City of Chicago Department of Water & Sewers," implying sewers and water supply were at some point administered separately (or under a different combined name) before arriving at the current DWM structure; the exact date(s) of reorganization were not determined here. *Partial answer (this pass):* the earliest documented merger of Chicago's water and sewer governance was May 6, 1861, when a separate Water Board (1851–1861) and Sewerage Board (1855–1861) combined into the Board of Public Works; that Board was replaced September 18, 1876 by a Department of Public Works with dedicated Bureaus of Water and of Sewers. This establishes the administrative lineage but not the date DWM itself took its current name and form [\[47\]](#src-47).
- ~~**Lawrence Avenue Underflow Sewer System report.**~~ **Resolved in this pass** by OCR-processing the archived 92-page scan. The report is Louis Koncza, Donald H. Churchill, and G.L. Miller (City of Chicago Department of Public Works, Bureau of Engineering), *Lawrence Avenue Underflow Sewer System, Interim Report: Planning and Construction*, EPA-600/2-80-014 (March 1980), covering the "Interim 1966-1978" study period under EPA Demonstration Grant No. 11020 EMD. Consulting engineer: Harza Engineering Company (Dr. David S. Louie, Chief Hydraulic Engineer; W.T. Bristow, Project Manager), under a $621,142 design contract. The project — a re-designation, effective November 1, 1966, of the earlier "Eastwood-Wilson" auxiliary outlet sewer proposal — is described in the report as "the first section of the Chicago deep tunnel to be constructed," i.e., a direct forerunner of TARP built roughly a decade before MWRD adopted TARP in 1972. Construction proceeded under five separate contracts, each independently dated and priced: Contract No. 1 (Main Shaft and Rock Tunnels — 16,638 ft of 12-ft-diameter and 9,126 ft of 17-ft-diameter concrete-lined rock tunnel, plus a ~250-ft-deep, 27-to-29-ft-diameter main shaft), bid at $10,792,094 to a joint venture of James McHugh Construction Co., S.A. Healy Co., and Kenny Construction Co., started November 6, 1967, completed May 28, 1973; Contract No. 2-A (high-level earth tunnels), $2,393,645, completed June 1, 1972; Contract No. 2-B (ten drop shafts), $3,415,688, completed October 13, 1974; Contract No. 3 (pumping station and outfall, four German-built KSB submersible pumps rated 5,000 GPM/228 ft head each), $1,977,000, started April 17, 1972 (96.65% complete as of February 1979); and Contract No. 4 (additional high-level sewers), $2,644,875, completed June 30, 1975 — summing to roughly $21.2 million across the five construction contracts, on top of a separate $1,500,000 federal demonstration grant (approved April 25, 1967) and the $621,142 design contract [\[39\]](#src-39).
- ~~**Total CSO outfall count across the full metropolitan system.**~~ **Partially resolved in this pass.** EPA's summary of its enforcement settlement with MWRD states "MWRD owns 36 CSO outfalls located on Chicago area river ways" and "the 51 satellite communities own 334 CSO outfalls also located on Chicago area river ways" (370 total), and separately identifies the City of Chicago as "the largest satellite community in the MWRD service area" — implying the City's 184 permitted outfalls are counted as a subset of, not additional to, that 334-outfall satellite-community figure (184 of 334 would be a plausible ~55% share for the largest single satellite community), though EPA's summary does not say this in so many words [\[43\]](#src-43). The generic "300+" figure used by advocacy sources (e.g., Friends of the Chicago River [\[16\]](#src-16)) is consistent with this same 370-outfall universe, likely rounded down or reflecting an earlier count.
- **Exact figure for the smallest historic sewer pipe still in service** was not found in a single authoritative source. ~~**Material-by-decade transition timeline.**~~ **Partially resolved in this pass.** A 1917 University of Illinois thesis surveying Chicago sewer-construction practice gives concrete anchor dates: laterals of tile pipe and brick trunk/outfall sewers "with a few exceptions" as the norm through at least 1917; Chicago's first plain-concrete sewer system built 1908 (South Chicago); its first large reinforced-concrete sewer built 1910 (a 12-by-14-ft conduit in South Western Avenue, jointly by the City and Sanitary District) [\[49\]](#src-49). The transition to PVC/HDPE linings and ductile iron in the mid-to-late 20th century, and any national-vs.-Chicago-specific divergence in that later period, remain synthesized from general histories rather than a single authoritative Chicago-specific source.
- **RainReady/CNT program's formal relationship to DWM** (grant-funded partner vs. independent nonprofit effort) was not verified against a City of Chicago primary source in this pass.
- ~~**TARP reservoir-capacity conflict.**~~ **Resolved (cross-document harmonization pass):** the 4.8 BG (TARP overview fact sheet) and 7.9 BG (dedicated Thornton/McCook fact sheets) figures describe different things. The Thornton fact sheet itself splits the reservoir's 7.9-BG total capacity into 4.8 BG of CSO/TARP storage plus 3.1 BG of Thorn Creek overbank flood-control (non-TARP) storage, so the 17.5-BG TARP system total counts only the 4.8-BG TARP share.
