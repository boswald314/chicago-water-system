# Calumet Water Reclamation Plant & Terrence J. O'Brien Water Reclamation Plant

## Role in the system

The **Calumet Water Reclamation Plant (WRP)**, 400 E. 130th Street, Chicago, is the Metropolitan Water Reclamation District of Greater Chicago's (MWRD) oldest continuously operating treatment works, in service since 1922 [1][2]. It sits on 275–470 acres (sources differ; see Facilities table) on the Little Calumet River in the Riverdale/Hegewisch area of the Far South Side [2]. It collects domestic and industrial sewage, plus combined stormwater, from Chicago's South Side and 48 southern Cook County suburbs — an area MWRD describes as roughly 300 square miles serving "over one million people" (MWRD's 2025 fact sheet) or, per an earlier engineering document, a design population of 1,053,160 [2][10]. Two emergency high-level combined-sewer-overflow (CSO) outfalls (numbered 151 and 752 in its NPDES permit, at the 95th Street and 122nd Street pump stations) discharge to the Little Calumet River only when the plant and its Tunnel and Reservoir Plan (TARP) storage are overwhelmed [6]. Since 1986–2015, Calumet has been the discharge point for the **Calumet TARP tunnel system and the Thornton Composite Reservoir**: a 36.7-mile ring of deep rock tunnels (0.63 billion gallons/BG capacity) culminating in the 7.9-BG Thornton Composite Reservoir in South Holland, about five miles south of the plant; when storms fill the reservoir, its contents are pumped north to Calumet WRP for full treatment before release to the Little Calumet River [8][9]. Treated effluent leaves Calumet WRP into the **Little Calumet River**, which flows west to the Cal-Sag Channel and eventually the Illinois Waterway system, never returning to Lake Michigan [2][6].

The **Terrence J. O'Brien Water Reclamation Plant** — built as the **North Side Sewage Treatment Works** and renamed in 2012 for longtime MWRD Board President Terrence J. O'Brien — sits at 3500 W. Howard Street, Skokie, on the city/suburban boundary at the North Shore Channel [3][4][20]. It receives sewage and combined stormwater from Chicago north of Fullerton Avenue and 17 northern suburbs (Evanston, Skokie, Wilmette, Winnetka, Glencoe, Northbrook, Morton Grove, Niles, Park Ridge, Des Plaines and others), an area of 143 square miles serving roughly 1.3 million people [4]. Its main sanitary inflow arrives via the intercepting-sewer network and the **North Branch Pumping Station**, which also functions as a TARP dropshaft/CSO outfall (Discharge No. 107) tied into the Mainstream Tunnel System, which in turn drains to the **McCook Reservoir** under construction in Hodgkins [7][9]. O'Brien also periodically receives liquid digested solids and centrifuge centrate pumped roughly 20 miles from the MWRD's **Egan WRP** in Schaumburg when Egan's dewatering equipment is offline, adding to O'Brien's phosphorus load [via MWRD Monitoring & Research Dept. biosolids reporting, cited in 34]. After treatment — screening, primary settling, activated-sludge secondary treatment, and since 2016 ultraviolet (UV) disinfection — clean water is released to the **North Shore Channel**, which flows south into the North Branch of the Chicago River and ultimately the Chicago Sanitary and Ship Canal, away from Lake Michigan [4][12]. O'Brien's own solids do not stay on site: digested/dewatered biosolids are pumped to the MWRD's **Stickney WRP** for further digestion and then trucked to solids management areas for drying [4].

Both plants are two of MWRD's seven WRPs (the others being Stickney, Egan, Hanover Park, Kirie, and Lemont), which together treat close to 450 billion gallons of wastewater a year for more than 5 million people across an 883-square-mile service area [2][4].

## Original construction

Both plants were built by the **Sanitary District of Chicago** (renamed MWRD in 1988) as the second wave of its sanitation infrastructure, after the 1900 reversal of the Chicago River and completion of the North Shore Channel (1910) and Cal-Sag Channel (1922) had diluted sewage but not treated it [22][30]. Facing mounting local and Illinois-Missouri litigation and public-health pressure to treat effluent before discharge, the District began building dedicated sewage treatment works in the 1910s–1920s: Calumet (1922), North Side (1928), West Side/Southwest (West Side opened June 2, 1930 per MWRD's own fact sheet, 1931 per the Encyclopedia of Chicago and other secondary sources; Southwest 1939 — later combined into Stickney) [22]. Imhoff-tank technology, rejected for Calumet and North Side (see below), was in fact used elsewhere in this construction wave: MWRD's own historical-photo archive documents "Imhoff tank construction at the West Side Sewage Treatment Works, one of two treatment plants which were combined and eventually became the Stickney Water Reclamation Plant, on August 5, 1927," confirming the "Imhoff-era" framing applies to the West Side/Stickney predecessor plant, not to Calumet or North Side [36].

**Calumet.** A construction contract was let September 6, 1920, and MWRD dates the completed plant's opening to **September 11, 1922** — the same month the Cal-Sag Channel first went into operation (gates opened Aug. 18, 1922; fully operational Aug. 26, 1922) [1][5]. A federal court's later factual findings in a 1946 patent-infringement case, however, state that the Calumet plant "was placed in operation June 5, 1923," after the same 1920 contract — implying the primary/preliminary works opened in 1922 while the plant's activated-sludge secondary-treatment units (the subject of the lawsuit) came online in 1923 [24]. Sanitary District engineers had explicitly evaluated and rejected Imhoff-tank/trickling-filter technology for the District's new works as early as 1922, concluding in internal reports that "Activated Sludge works are preferable to Imhoff tank trickling filter works" — meaning Calumet (like the concurrently built Des Plaines plant, contract let May 1, 1919, operating Aug. 2, 1922) was designed from the outset as an activated-sludge plant rather than converted later from an Imhoff-tank design [25]. By 1928 Calumet served about 155,000 people [1][2].

**North Side (O'Brien).** The construction contract for the North Side Works was let **August 9, 1923**, ground was broken/excavation began September 27, 1923, and the plant began operating **October 3, 1928** [4][13][24]. At completion it was described as the largest sewage-treatment facility in the world, engineered — like Calumet — around the activated-sludge process rather than Imhoff tanks/trickling filtration, a choice Sanitary District engineers had already committed to in their 1922 evaluations [21][25]. Sanitary District engineer **Langdon Pearse** is credited in period engineering literature with technical work on activated-sludge experimentation whose results "formed a sound basis for design and installation of the Des Plaines river and Calumet activated sludge plants, culminating in the construction of the North Side Sewage Treatment Works" [via search synthesis of Iowa State University engineering history article, 25]. The original works served approximately 800,000 people in a 78-square-mile area [3][4]. Major above-ground structures documented from the 1923–1928 construction period include an influent chamber with coarse screens, a Pump and Blower Building, Grit Building, 60-inch aeration-tank piping, settling tanks, operating galleries, a Service Building with a water tower, and an effluent conduit to the North Shore Channel [13].

Neither plant's original total construction cost in contemporary dollars was confirmed in sources examined for this report (see Open Questions); period Sanitary District annual reports and the 1928 District publication *"North Side Sewage Treatment Project: An Outline of Its Construction, Operation and Effect"* (cataloged at HathiTrust/Internet Archive but not located in full text during this research pass) likely contain the figures [via Online Books Page/HathiTrust catalog search].

## Upgrades & modernization timeline

- **1922–1931** — Calumet and North Side (O'Brien) works built and brought into activated-sludge operation; West Side works (opened June 2, 1930 per MWRD, 1931 per the Encyclopedia of Chicago) and Southwest works (1939) follow, later merged into Stickney [22][24].
- **1946** — Federal court (N.D. Ill.) rules in *Activated Sludge, Inc. v. Sanitary District of Chicago*, 64 F. Supp. 25, setting infringement-era damages for the District's use of patented activated-sludge process at the Calumet, Des Plaines, and North Side plants; the court's opinion is a rare primary source fixing exact contract and start-up dates for all three plants [24].
- **1975–2006** — TARP "Deep Tunnel" tunnel construction proceeds regionwide; the O'Brien-tributary Mainstream Tunnel System (40.5 mi, 1.2 BG) and the Calumet-tributary tunnel system are built in phases; full tunnel system operational 2006 [8].
- **1986** — First leg of the Calumet Tunnel System (Cal-Sag Channel Leg, 186 million gallons) completed, beginning TARP service to Calumet WRP's drainage area [9].
- **1988** — Sanitary District of Chicago is renamed the Metropolitan Water Reclamation District of Greater Chicago [30].
- **1996–2006** — Remaining legs of the Calumet Tunnel System completed: 140th Street/Indiana Leg (236 MG) in 1996; Torrence Avenue Leg (141 MG) and Little Calumet Leg (59 MG) in 2002 and 2006 [9].
- **2009** — MWRD adopts the **Calumet Master Plan**, a $507.16 million, ten-project capital program (new high-level influent pumping station rated for 600 MGD, new grit and primary-settling facilities, new blowers, sludge-thickening upgrades, digester-gas storage/utilization, a new Aeration Battery D, and a central boiler facility), phased through 2025 [10].
- **2011 (June 7)** — MWRD Board formally adopts a disinfection policy for its Chicago Area Waterway System (CAWS) plants — Chicago had been the only major U.S. city not disinfecting treated effluent before release — triggering disinfection projects at Calumet (chlorination/dechlorination) and O'Brien (UV) [17][12].
- **2012** — MWRD renames the North Side WRP the **Terrence J. O'Brien Water Reclamation Plant**, honoring O'Brien's 24 years as an elected MWRD commissioner/Board President; O'Brien was the third generation of his family to work at the plant (his father a painter leadman, his grandfather an operating engineer there) [3][20].
- **2013 (Sept. 16)** — Groundbreaking for Calumet's disinfection facility, attended by Gov. Pat Quinn, EPA Region 5 Administrator Susan Hedman, and IEPA Director Lisa Bonnett; $21 million total engineering/design cost across MWRD's disinfection program, supplemented by a $10 million EPA "Illinois Jobs Now!" grant announced in April 2012; the two disinfection projects (Calumet chlorination and O'Brien UV) together generated roughly 750 construction/support jobs [17][18].
- **2013** — Thornton Transitional Reservoir/Composite Reservoir excavation completed [9].
- **2015 (July 17)** — Calumet's chlorination/dechlorination disinfection facility unveiled; becomes the first Chicago-area plant to disinfect effluent before release, with U.S. Sen. Dick Durbin, MWRD President Mariyana Spyropoulos and Friends of the Chicago River's Margaret Frisbie attending [16].
- **2015 (May 17 – Oct. 30)** — During construction of O'Brien's UV facility, an adjacent CTA Yellow Line embankment collapses (May 17, 2015), closing the Skokie Swift/Yellow Line for several months (service restored Oct. 30, 2015) at an estimated $3.5 million cost to the CTA [13].
- **2015 (Dec. 31)** — Thornton Composite Reservoir goes into operation as part of TARP Phase II, providing 7.9 BG of storage upstream of Calumet WRP [9][17].
- **2016 (March 23)** — Ribbon-cutting for O'Brien's UV disinfection facility — billed as the world's largest wastewater UV disinfection installation — with U.S. Sen. Dick Durbin, EPA Acting Regional Administrator Robert Kaplan, and Skokie Mayor George Van Dusen; $61.7 million project, 896 UV lamps, ~620 construction jobs [12][21].
- **2018–2024 (Calumet) / 2018–2027 (O'Brien)** — NPDES-mandated biological phosphorus-removal construction schedules: anaerobic zones added to aeration batteries, mixer and supplemental-carbon systems installed, and (at Calumet) a sidestream phosphorus-recovery process; (at O'Brien) an additional aeration tank in Battery D, final-settling-tank enhancements, and a centrate-treatment system built at Egan WRP to reduce phosphorus loading historically trucked/piped to O'Brien — driving toward a 1.0 mg/L total-phosphorus effluent limit [6][7].
- **2018** — Composting operations begin at the Calumet WRP Solids Drying Site (SDS), part of MWRD's biosolids-beneficial-reuse program [31].
- **2023 (July 4 weekend)** — Calumet WRP receives roughly 3.5 billion gallons of stormwater/sewage during Fourth of July flooding without exceeding half of its emergency underground quarry-storage capacity (nearly 8 BG, 350 feet below grade) [15].
- **2025–2026** — MWRD advances the **Battery E Activated Sludge Facility** at O'Brien WRP (Contract 21-092-3P), engineering-estimated at $308–373 million and described as the largest contract in MWRD history; bids opened November 2025; a related sludge-removal contract awarded to Michels Construction for **$409,420,079** at the MWRD Board's June 18, 2026 meeting [26][27].

## Current operations

Calumet WRP employs 268 people on what MWRD's own materials describe inconsistently as either "275.4 acres" (2025 fact sheet) or "470 acres" (2025 locations webpage) and treats a design average of 354 MGD (design maximum 430 MGD), discharging to the Little Calumet River [1][2]. It serves Chicago and 48 suburbs including Blue Island, Oak Lawn, Tinley Park, Orland Park, Calumet City, Harvey, and South Holland [2]. Treatment is conventional: coarse screening, aerated grit removal, primary settling (60–80% solids removal), secondary activated-sludge treatment with mechanical aeration, final settling, and — since 2015 — chlorination/dechlorination disinfection before release [2]. MWRD states wastewater takes roughly 4.5 hours (dry weather) to reach the plant from Blue Island and about 8 hours to pass through treatment [2]. Solids (about 100 dry tons/day) are digested, centrifuge-dewatered, and air-dried at the adjacent Calumet Solids Management Area, producing biosolids used on golf courses, parks, farmland, and reclamation sites, including the Chicago Park District's Ping Tom Park [2]. The plant also converts fats, oils and grease into methane to offset natural-gas purchases and is evaluating a digester-gas energy-generation system whose design work is expected to inform similar planning at Stickney [2][15].

O'Brien WRP employs 187 people across 14 buildings on 97 acres (a 2019-vintage MWRD fact sheet said 12; the current locations page and Oct. 2025 fact sheet both say 14), with a design average flow of 333 MGD (design maximum 450 MGD) but a current average flow near 230 MGD [3][4][7][37]. It serves Chicago north of Fullerton Avenue plus Evanston, Skokie, Wilmette, Winnetka, Glencoe, Northbrook, Northfield, Glenview, Golf, Morton Grove, Niles, Lincolnwood, Park Ridge, Harwood Heights, Des Plaines, and Kenilworth [4]. Treatment mirrors Calumet through the secondary stage, then passes through the 896-lamp UV disinfection facility before discharge to the North Shore Channel [4][12]. MWRD states wastewater from Lincolnwood can reach the plant in under 30 minutes and takes about 12 hours to complete treatment [4]. All solids are pumped to Stickney WRP for digestion rather than processed on-site [4]. The plant periodically also receives liquid digested solids and centrifuge centrate from the Egan WRP (Schaumburg) via a roughly 20-mile sewer connection, which MWRD is working to reduce through an ANITA™ Mox sidestream-treatment process at Egan itself, due to odor and corrosion problems in the conveying sewers [via MWRD biosolids/monitoring reports, 34]. O'Brien has received the National Association of Clean Water Agencies' highest compliance awards and hosts a Department of Energy-funded revolving algal biofilm (RAB) research greenhouse, developed with Iowa State University researchers, exploring phosphorus recovery from the liquid stream [13][19]. As of mid-2026 the plant is mid-construction on its Battery E secondary-treatment expansion, MWRD's largest contract ever let [26][27].

## Notable events & incidents

- **1946 patent litigation** — *Activated Sludge, Inc. v. Sanitary District of Chicago* (64 F. Supp. 25, N.D. Ill.) set compensatory damages owed by the District for patent infringement (liability found in a 1935 decree) tied to its activated-sludge process at the Calumet, Des Plaines, and North Side plants — one of relatively few disputes in U.S. water-infrastructure history litigated over sewage-treatment process patents, and a rare primary source for exact plant start-up dates [24].
- **May 17 – October 30, 2015** — Construction activity for O'Brien's new UV disinfection facility triggered the collapse of an adjacent CTA Yellow Line (Skokie Swift) embankment, closing the line for over five months at an estimated $3.5 million cost to CTA — an unusual case of a wastewater-plant capital project directly damaging adjacent transit infrastructure [13].
- **July 4th weekend, 2023** — Record stormwater inflows during Independence Day flooding sent about 3.5 billion gallons into Calumet's system without exceeding half of its ~8-billion-gallon emergency quarry storage, illustrating the plant's built-in flood-resilience margin as extreme-rainfall frequency increases [15].
- **2015–2016 disinfection rollout** — Completion of chlorination/dechlorination at Calumet (2015) and UV at O'Brien (2016) ended Chicago's status, per MWRD and EPA officials at the time, as the only major U.S. city releasing undisinfected treated sewage effluent into its waterways, directly enabling expanded recreational contact (kayaking, canoeing) on the Chicago Area Waterway System [16][17].
- **2026 (June 18)** — MWRD Board authorized a $409.4 million sludge-removal contract to Michels Construction as part of the Battery E project at O'Brien — described by observers as the largest single contract in MWRD's history — signaling a new multi-decade capital-investment cycle at the 98-year-old plant [26][27].

## Open questions

- **Exact original construction cost, in nominal dollars, for both the 1922 Calumet plant and the 1928 North Side (O'Brien) plant.** Not located in sources examined; likely documented in Sanitary District of Chicago annual reports (1920–1929) and the District's own 1928 publication *"North Side Sewage Treatment Project: An Outline of Its Construction, Operation and Effect,"* both cataloged at HathiTrust/Internet Archive but not accessed in full text during this pass.
- **Named engineer(s) of record.** Langdon Pearse's role in the activated-sludge research underlying both plants' design is documented in secondary engineering-history literature, but no source in this research pass named a chief design engineer, resident engineer, or general contractor of record for either the 1920–1923 Calumet construction contract or the 1923–1928 North Side Works contract.
- **The one-year discrepancy in Calumet's opening date.** MWRD's public materials uniformly cite September 11, 1922, while a 1946 federal court's factual findings state the plant "was placed in operation June 5, 1923." This may reflect a real distinction between substantial completion/partial operation (1922) and full activated-sludge operation (1923), but no source explains the gap directly.
- **Acreage of the Calumet WRP site.** MWRD's own 2025 fact sheet PDFs (both the March and October 2025 editions, independently confirmed) state "275.4 acres" while its current locations webpage states "470 acres" — an unresolved internal inconsistency, confirmed still present as of the October 2025 fact sheet.
- ~~Whether Imhoff tanks were ever used at Calumet or North Side specifically.~~ **Resolved:** MWRD's own historical-photo archive documents Imhoff tank construction at the West Side Sewage Treatment Works (predecessor to Stickney WRP) on August 5, 1927 — confirming the "Imhoff-era" framing applies to the West Side/Stickney lineage, not to Calumet or North Side (O'Brien), both of which were designed from the outset as activated-sludge plants per the 1922 engineering evaluations already cited above [36].
- **Detailed aeration-battery expansion history for O'Brien, 1930s–1990s.** Sources document the original construction and the 2018-era Battery D aeration-tank addition and 2025–2026 Battery E project, but a comprehensive account of intermediate-era (1940s–1990s) capacity expansions was not found.
- **HAER/HABS documentation.** No dedicated Historic American Engineering Record (HAER) survey for either plant was located at loc.gov during this research pass (HAER has documented the related Chicago Sanitary and Ship Canal, HAER No. IL-193, but apparently not the Calumet or North Side treatment works themselves) — worth a follow-up direct search of the Library of Congress Prints & Photographs catalog.
- **Original cost and financing (bonds vs. tax levy) mechanism** used by the Sanitary District to fund 1920s treatment-works construction was not confirmed for either plant specifically.
