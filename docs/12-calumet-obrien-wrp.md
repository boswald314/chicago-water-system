# Calumet Water Reclamation Plant & Terrence J. O'Brien Water Reclamation Plant

## Role in the system

The **Calumet Water Reclamation Plant (WRP)**, 400 E. 130th Street, Chicago, is the Metropolitan Water Reclamation District of Greater Chicago's (MWRD) oldest continuously operating treatment works, in service since 1922 [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[2\]](#src-2). It sits on 275–470 acres (sources differ; see Facilities table) on the Little Calumet River in the Riverdale/Hegewisch area of the Far South Side [\[2\]](#src-2). It collects domestic and industrial sewage, plus combined stormwater, from Chicago's South Side and 48 southern Cook County suburbs — an area MWRD describes as roughly 300 square miles serving "over one million people" (MWRD's 2025 fact sheet) or, per an earlier engineering document, a design population of 1,053,160 [\[2\]](#src-2)[\[10\]](#src-10). Two emergency high-level combined-sewer-overflow (CSO) outfalls (numbered 151 and 752 in its NPDES permit, at the 95th Street and 122nd Street pump stations) discharge to the Little Calumet River only when the plant and its Tunnel and Reservoir Plan (TARP) storage are overwhelmed [\[6\]](#src-6). Since 1986–2015, Calumet has been the discharge point for the **Calumet TARP tunnel system and the Thornton Composite Reservoir**: a 36.7-mile ring of deep rock tunnels (0.63 billion gallons/BG capacity) culminating in the 7.9-BG Thornton Composite Reservoir in South Holland, about five miles south of the plant; when storms fill the reservoir, its contents are pumped north to Calumet WRP for full treatment before release to the Little Calumet River [\[8\]](#src-8)[\[9\]](#src-9). Treated effluent leaves Calumet WRP into the **Little Calumet River**, which flows west to the Cal-Sag Channel and eventually the Illinois Waterway system, never returning to Lake Michigan [\[2\]](#src-2)[\[6\]](#src-6).

The **Terrence J. O'Brien Water Reclamation Plant** — built as the **North Side Sewage Treatment Works** and renamed in 2012 for longtime MWRD Board President Terrence J. O'Brien — sits at 3500 W. Howard Street, Skokie, on the city/suburban boundary at the North Shore Channel [\[3\]](#src-3)[\[4\]](#src-4)[\[20\]](#src-20). It receives sewage and combined stormwater from Chicago north of Fullerton Avenue and 17 northern suburbs (Evanston, Skokie, Wilmette, Winnetka, Glencoe, Northbrook, Morton Grove, Niles, Park Ridge, Des Plaines and others), an area of 143 square miles serving roughly 1.3 million people [\[4\]](#src-4). Its main sanitary inflow arrives via the intercepting-sewer network and the **North Branch Pumping Station**, which also functions as a TARP dropshaft/CSO outfall (Discharge No. 107) tied into the Mainstream Tunnel System, which in turn drains to the **McCook Reservoir** under construction in Hodgkins [\[7\]](#src-7)[\[9\]](#src-9). O'Brien also periodically receives liquid digested solids and centrifuge centrate pumped roughly 20 miles from the MWRD's **Egan WRP** in Schaumburg when Egan's dewatering equipment is offline, adding to O'Brien's phosphorus load [via MWRD Monitoring & Research Dept. biosolids reporting, cited in 34]. After treatment — screening, primary settling, activated-sludge secondary treatment, and since 2016 ultraviolet (UV) disinfection — clean water is released to the **North Shore Channel**, which flows south into the North Branch of the Chicago River and ultimately the Chicago Sanitary and Ship Canal, away from Lake Michigan [\[4\]](#src-4)[\[12\]](#src-12). O'Brien's own solids do not stay on site: digested/dewatered biosolids are pumped to the MWRD's **Stickney WRP** for further digestion and then trucked to solids management areas for drying [\[4\]](#src-4).

Both plants are two of MWRD's seven WRPs (the others being Stickney, Egan, Hanover Park, Kirie, and Lemont), which together treat close to 450 billion gallons of wastewater a year for more than 5 million people across an 883-square-mile service area [\[2\]](#src-2)[\[4\]](#src-4).

## Original construction

Both plants were built by the **Sanitary District of Chicago** (renamed MWRD in 1988) as the second wave of its sanitation infrastructure, after the 1900 reversal of the Chicago River and completion of the North Shore Channel (1910) and Cal-Sag Channel (1922) had diluted sewage but not treated it [\[22\]](#src-22)[\[30\]](#src-30). Facing mounting local and Illinois-Missouri litigation and public-health pressure to treat effluent before discharge, the District began building dedicated sewage treatment works in the 1910s–1920s: Calumet (1922), North Side (1928), West Side/Southwest (West Side opened June 2, 1930 per MWRD's own fact sheet, 1931 per the Encyclopedia of Chicago and other secondary sources; Southwest 1939 — later combined into Stickney) [\[22\]](#src-22). Imhoff-tank technology, long assumed by this document to have been rejected for both Calumet and North Side in favor of pure activated-sludge design, was in fact used at Calumet as well as at West Side: MWRD's own historical-photo archive documents "Imhoff tank construction at the West Side Sewage Treatment Works, one of two treatment plants which were combined and eventually became the Stickney Water Reclamation Plant, on August 5, 1927" [\[36\]](#src-36), while independent primary sources — the District's own consulting engineers' 1927 report and its 1928 *Engineering Works* volume — separately confirm Calumet ran Imhoff tanks (55 MGD capacity) alongside a smaller experimental activated-sludge component well into the late 1920s (see Calumet paragraph and Open Questions, below) [\[39\]](#src-39)[\[41\]](#src-41). North Side, by contrast, remains confirmed as activated-sludge from the outset.

**Calumet.** A construction contract was let September 6, 1920, and MWRD dates the completed plant's opening to **September 11, 1922** — the same month the Cal-Sag Channel first went into operation (gates opened Aug. 18, 1922; fully operational Aug. 26, 1922) [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[5\]](#src-5). A federal court's later factual findings in a 1946 patent-infringement case state that the Calumet plant "was placed in operation June 5, 1923," after the same 1920 contract; a contemporary December 1923 Sanitary District memorandum independently confirms and refines this, stating "The Calumet Treatment Plant was begun in 1920, the sedimentation tanks completed and put in operation in September, 1922, and the tanks for the Activated Sludge Process put in service in May, 1923" — primary-source proof (within about a month of the court's later-cited date) that primary/sedimentation treatment opened in 1922 while activated-sludge secondary treatment followed in 1923 [\[24\]](#src-24)[\[38\]](#src-38). Sanitary District engineers had evaluated Imhoff-tank/trickling-filter technology against activated sludge as early as 1922 [\[25\]](#src-25), but **correcting this document's prior "resolved" framing** (see Open Questions), Calumet was not in fact built exclusively as an activated-sludge plant: the District's own consulting engineers, Alvord, Burdick & Howson, described it in a 1927 report to the Board as "the Calumet Imhoff tank plant," rating its Imhoff-tank capacity at 55 MGD and treating its activated-sludge and sprinkling-filter units as a smaller "experimental" adjunct whose cost was tracked separately; the District's own August 1928 *Engineering Works* volume corroborates this, describing live 1928 experiments with gas collection "from an Imhoff tank unit," Imhoff-sludge drying on "glass-covered sand beds," and equipment changes to "one of the activated sludge tanks" (implying only a small number of activated-sludge tanks existed alongside the larger original Imhoff-tank system) [\[39\]](#src-39)[\[41\]](#src-41). A 1934 federal review of District plans put Calumet's capacity at 186 MGD as activated-sludge construction continued to expand the plant beyond its Imhoff-dominated 1920s design [\[44\]](#src-44). Total construction cost of the Calumet Sewage Treatment Works alone reached $6,971,916 by Dec. 31, 1927, with the full Calumet Project (treatment works plus intercepting sewer, pumping station and power station) at $18,991,669 by the same date — the most precise nominal-dollar construction-cost figures located for the plant [\[39\]](#src-39). Albert W. Dilling was the Sanitary District's Chief Engineer at the time of the 1920 Calumet contract (per the District's own July 1921 engineering-data report), having succeeded G. M. Wisner, who held the post as of May 1919; Langdon Pearse, credited elsewhere with the activated-sludge research underlying both plants, held the subordinate title of Sanitary Engineer throughout this period rather than Chief Engineer [\[42\]](#src-42)[\[43\]](#src-43). By 1928 Calumet served about 155,000 people [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[2\]](#src-2).

**North Side (O'Brien).** The first contract of the overall North Side Project — for the North Side Intercepting Sewer — was let November 23, 1921; the construction contract specifically for Division A of the treatment works was let **August 9, 1923**, ground was broken/excavation began September 27, 1923, pumping units for the North Branch Pumping Station were contracted May 6, 1926, and the plant began operating **October 3, 1928**, on a 97-acre site at McCormick Road and Howard Street [\[4\]](#src-4)[\[13\]](#src-13)[\[24\]](#src-24)[\[40\]](#src-40). At completion it was described as the largest sewage-treatment facility in the world, engineered — like Calumet's activated-sludge component — around the activated-sludge process rather than Imhoff tanks/trickling filtration, a choice Sanitary District engineers had already committed to in their 1922 evaluations [\[21\]](#src-21)[\[25\]](#src-25). The plant's construction was driven not only by litigation but by a specific federal deadline: a March 3, 1925 War Department permit authorizing the Sanitary District's Lake Michigan diversion made continued diversion contingent on completing "a program of sewage treatment by artificial processes which will provide the equivalent of the complete (100%) treatment of the sewage of a human population of at least 1,200,000" by December 31, 1929 — a condition the District's own 1928 dedication brochure cites explicitly as a rationale for the North Side Works [\[39\]](#src-39)[\[40\]](#src-40). Sanitary District engineer **Langdon Pearse** is credited in period engineering literature with technical work on activated-sludge experimentation whose results "formed a sound basis for design and installation of the Des Plaines river and Calumet activated sludge plants, culminating in the construction of the North Side Sewage Treatment Works" [via search synthesis of Iowa State University engineering history article, 25]; the District's 1928 dedication brochure states more broadly that the plant "was designed entirely by engineers of the Sanitary District, no outside help having been called in," and names **Edward J. Kelly** — later Mayor of Chicago (1933–1947) — as the District's Chief Engineer at the time of the plant's completion [\[40\]](#src-40). The original works served approximately 800,000 people in a 78-square-mile area [\[3\]](#src-3)[\[4\]](#src-4). Major above-ground structures documented from the 1923–1928 construction period include an influent chamber with coarse screens, a Pump and Blower Building, Grit Building, 60-inch aeration-tank piping, settling tanks, operating galleries, a Service Building with a water tower, and an effluent conduit to the North Shore Channel [\[13\]](#src-13). Construction on the project was carried out under 42 major contracts publicly advertised and opened by the District; the treatment-works contracts alone (excluding the intercepting sewer and pumping station) went to a roster of contractors led by general contractor **John Griffiths & Son Co.**, with major equipment supplied by **The Dorr Co.** (activated-sludge/settling equipment), **Allis-Chalmers Mfg. Co.**, **General Electric Co.**, and some twenty other named firms [\[40\]](#src-40). From the outset the plant's waste activated sludge was not processed on site: it was pumped through a roughly 15–17-mile, 14-inch cast-iron force main (sources differ) to the then-under-construction West Side Sewage Treatment Works at Stickney for Imhoff-tank digestion and sand-bed drying — the direct historical predecessor of today's arrangement, in which O'Brien's solids are still piped to Stickney WRP for digestion (see Current operations, below) [\[40\]](#src-40).

Original total construction costs, previously flagged as an open question, are now substantially resolved from primary sources. Per the District's own 1928 dedication brochure — *"North Side Sewage Treatment Project: An Outline of Its Construction, Operation and Effect,"* now located in full text — the completed North Side Project's total construction cost, by contract, was **$31,575,396** (North Side Intercepting Sewer $11,048,225.06; North Branch Sewage Pumping Station $2,336,980.00; North Side Sewage Treatment Works $18,190,190.94), rising to a **grand total of $34,118,840.26** once predecessor structures folded into the project (North Shore and Evanston intercepting sewers, Evanston Pumping Station, Niles Center Outfall Sewer) are included; the brochure separately rounds the "total cost of the plant, including the sludge pipe line" to $18,200,000 [\[40\]](#src-40). For Calumet, the District's 1928 *Engineering Works* cost tables (cumulative to Dec. 31, 1927) show $6,971,915.68 for the Calumet Sewage Treatment Works alone and $18,991,669.39 for the full Calumet Project [\[39\]](#src-39). For district-wide context, the same 1928 North Side brochure states the Sanitary District's total sewage-disposal expenditure reached $182,169,000 by January 1928 — more than the *combined* $122,401,858 spent to that date by every other U.S. city of over 500,000 population (New York, Philadelphia, Detroit, Cleveland, Boston, Baltimore, Los Angeles, and others) [\[40\]](#src-40).

## Upgrades & modernization timeline

- **1922–1928** — Calumet (dual Imhoff-tank/activated-sludge process, 55-MGD Imhoff capacity per an independent 1927 engineering report) and North Side/O'Brien (activated sludge from the outset) works built; West Side works (opened June 2, 1930 per MWRD, 1931 per the Encyclopedia of Chicago) and Southwest works (1939) follow, later merged into Stickney [\[22\]](#src-22)[\[24\]](#src-24)[\[41\]](#src-41).
- **1924** — *Activated Sludge, Inc.* files suit against the Sanitary District of Chicago in federal court over its patented activated-sludge process (a parallel suit was also filed against Milwaukee's sewerage commission), opening a legal dispute that would not reach final judgment for 22 years [\[44\]](#src-44).
- **1925 (March 3)** — U.S. War Department issues the Sanitary District's Lake Michigan diversion permit (8,500 cfs), Condition 4 of which requires the District to complete sewage-treatment works providing the equivalent of 100% treatment for a population of at least 1,200,000 by December 31, 1929 — a concrete federal deadline driving the North Side Works' completion timeline [\[39\]](#src-39).
- **1932–1935** — *Activated Sludge, Inc.* patent-infringement trial against the Sanitary District is heard (May 1932 – June 1933) and argued (March 1934); liability is found in a 1935 decree, with damages not finally set until 1946 [\[24\]](#src-24)[\[44\]](#src-44).
- **1934** — A federal review of District plans records Calumet's capacity at 186 MGD and North Side's at 200 MGD, reflecting post-1920s expansion of activated-sludge capacity at both plants [\[44\]](#src-44).
- **1946** — Federal court (N.D. Ill.) rules in *Activated Sludge, Inc. v. Sanitary District of Chicago*, 64 F. Supp. 25, setting infringement-era damages for the District's use of patented activated-sludge process at the Calumet, Des Plaines, and North Side plants; the court's opinion is a rare primary source fixing exact contract and start-up dates for all three plants [\[24\]](#src-24).
- **1975–2006** — TARP "Deep Tunnel" tunnel construction proceeds regionwide; the O'Brien-tributary Mainstream Tunnel System (40.5 mi, 1.2 BG) and the Calumet-tributary tunnel system are built in phases; full tunnel system operational 2006 [\[8\]](#src-8).
- **1986** — First leg of the Calumet Tunnel System (Cal-Sag Channel Leg, 186 million gallons) completed, beginning TARP service to Calumet WRP's drainage area [\[9\]](#src-9).
- **1988** — Sanitary District of Chicago is renamed the Metropolitan Water Reclamation District of Greater Chicago [\[30\]](#src-30).
- **1996–2006** — Remaining legs of the Calumet Tunnel System completed: 140th Street/Indiana Leg (236 MG) in 1996; Torrence Avenue Leg (141 MG) and Little Calumet Leg (59 MG) in 2002 and 2006 [\[9\]](#src-9).
- **2009** — MWRD adopts the **Calumet Master Plan**, a $507.16 million, ten-project capital program (new high-level influent pumping station rated for 600 MGD, new grit and primary-settling facilities, new blowers, sludge-thickening upgrades, digester-gas storage/utilization, a new Aeration Battery D, and a central boiler facility), phased through 2025 [\[10\]](#src-10).
- **2011 (June 7)** — MWRD Board formally adopts a disinfection policy for its Chicago Area Waterway System (CAWS) plants — Chicago had been the only major U.S. city not disinfecting treated effluent before release — triggering disinfection projects at Calumet (chlorination/dechlorination) and O'Brien (UV) [\[17\]](#src-17)[\[12\]](#src-12).
- **2012** — MWRD renames the North Side WRP the **Terrence J. O'Brien Water Reclamation Plant**, honoring O'Brien's 24 years as an elected MWRD commissioner/Board President; O'Brien was the third generation of his family to work at the plant (his father a painter leadman, his grandfather an operating engineer there) [\[3\]](#src-3)[\[20\]](#src-20).
- **2013 (Sept. 16)** — Groundbreaking for Calumet's disinfection facility, attended by Gov. Pat Quinn, EPA Region 5 Administrator Susan Hedman, and IEPA Director Lisa Bonnett; $21 million total engineering/design cost across MWRD's disinfection program, supplemented by a $10 million EPA "Illinois Jobs Now!" grant announced in April 2012; the two disinfection projects (Calumet chlorination and O'Brien UV) together generated roughly 750 construction/support jobs [\[17\]](#src-17)[\[18\]](#src-18).
- **2013** — Thornton Transitional Reservoir/Composite Reservoir excavation completed [\[9\]](#src-9).
- **2015 (July 17)** — Calumet's chlorination/dechlorination disinfection facility unveiled; becomes the first Chicago-area plant to disinfect effluent before release, with U.S. Sen. Dick Durbin, MWRD President Mariyana Spyropoulos and Friends of the Chicago River's Margaret Frisbie attending [\[16\]](#src-16).
- **2015 (May 17 – Oct. 30)** — During construction of O'Brien's UV facility, an adjacent CTA Yellow Line embankment collapses (May 17, 2015), closing the Skokie Swift/Yellow Line for several months (service restored Oct. 30, 2015) at an estimated $3.5 million cost to the CTA [\[13\]](#src-13).
- **2015 (Dec. 31)** — Thornton Composite Reservoir goes into operation as part of TARP Phase II, providing 7.9 BG of storage upstream of Calumet WRP [\[9\]](#src-9)[\[17\]](#src-17).
- **2016 (March 23)** — Ribbon-cutting for O'Brien's UV disinfection facility — billed as the world's largest wastewater UV disinfection installation — with U.S. Sen. Dick Durbin, EPA Acting Regional Administrator Robert Kaplan, and Skokie Mayor George Van Dusen; $61.7 million project, 896 UV lamps, ~620 construction jobs [\[12\]](#src-12)[\[21\]](#src-21).
- **2018–2024 (Calumet) / 2018–2027 (O'Brien)** — NPDES-mandated biological phosphorus-removal construction schedules: anaerobic zones added to aeration batteries, mixer and supplemental-carbon systems installed, and (at Calumet) a sidestream phosphorus-recovery process; (at O'Brien) an additional aeration tank in Battery D, final-settling-tank enhancements, and a centrate-treatment system built at Egan WRP to reduce phosphorus loading historically trucked/piped to O'Brien — driving toward a 1.0 mg/L monthly-average total-phosphorus limit by Aug. 2027 and a stricter 0.5 mg/L annual geometric-mean limit by Jan. 2030 [\[6\]](#src-6)[\[7\]](#src-7)[\[45\]](#src-45).
- **2018** — Composting operations begin at the Calumet WRP Solids Drying Site (SDS), part of MWRD's biosolids-beneficial-reuse program [\[31\]](#src-31).
- **2022 (August)** — MWRD awards the Calumet WRP Chemical Phosphorus Removal Facility (ferric chloride dosing), placed in service February 2025 as an interim/complementary measure alongside ongoing biological phosphorus-removal research at the plant [\[46\]](#src-46).
- **2023 (June) – 2025 (July)** — Battery D phosphorus-removal modifications at O'Brien WRP (Contract 21-091-3P, $15.34M), adding a RAS-fermenter pump building, piping, mixers and baffles for sidestream enhanced biological phosphorus removal [\[45\]](#src-45).
- **2023 (July 4 weekend)** — Calumet WRP receives roughly 3.5 billion gallons of stormwater/sewage during Fourth of July flooding without exceeding half of its emergency underground quarry-storage capacity (nearly 8 BG, 350 feet below grade) [\[15\]](#src-15).
- **2025 (April)** — MWRD awards the O'Brien WRP Chemical Phosphorus Removal Facility (alum dosing), targeted for service by June 2027 [\[46\]](#src-46).
- **2025–2028** — MWRD advances the **Battery E Activated Sludge Facility** at O'Brien WRP (Contract 21-092-3P), with a $367,750,800 estimated construction cost and AECOM Technical Services as engineering consultant per MWRD's FY2025 capital budget book — described as the largest contract in MWRD history; bids opened November 2025, with a February 2028 substantial-completion target; a related sludge-removal contract awarded to Michels Construction for **$409,420,079** at the MWRD Board's June 18, 2026 meeting [\[26\]](#src-26)[\[27\]](#src-27)[\[45\]](#src-45).

## Current operations

Calumet WRP employs 268 people on what MWRD's own materials describe inconsistently as either "275.4 acres" (2025 fact sheet) or "470 acres" (2025 locations webpage) and treats a design average of 354 MGD (design maximum 430 MGD), discharging to the Little Calumet River [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[2\]](#src-2). It serves Chicago and 48 suburbs including Blue Island, Oak Lawn, Tinley Park, Orland Park, Calumet City, Harvey, and South Holland [\[2\]](#src-2). Treatment is conventional: coarse screening, aerated grit removal, primary settling (60–80% solids removal), secondary activated-sludge treatment with mechanical aeration, final settling, and — since 2015 — chlorination/dechlorination disinfection before release [\[2\]](#src-2). MWRD states wastewater takes roughly 4.5 hours (dry weather) to reach the plant from Blue Island and about 8 hours to pass through treatment [\[2\]](#src-2). Solids (about 100 dry tons/day) are digested, centrifuge-dewatered, and air-dried at the adjacent Calumet Solids Management Area, producing biosolids used on golf courses, parks, farmland, and reclamation sites, including the Chicago Park District's Ping Tom Park [\[2\]](#src-2). The plant also converts fats, oils and grease into methane to offset natural-gas purchases and is evaluating a digester-gas energy-generation system whose design work is expected to inform similar planning at Stickney [\[2\]](#src-2)[\[15\]](#src-15). Since February 2025, a ferric-chloride Chemical Phosphorus Removal Facility (awarded August 2022) has supplemented treatment to meet NPDES phosphorus limits, while MWRD's Monitoring & Research Department continues experimental work on a fully biological phosphorus-removal alternative for Calumet's influent stream [\[46\]](#src-46).

O'Brien WRP employs 187 people across 14 buildings on 97 acres (a 2019-vintage MWRD fact sheet said 12; the current locations page and Oct. 2025 fact sheet both say 14), with a design average flow of 333 MGD (design maximum 450 MGD) but a current average flow near 230 MGD [\[3\]](#src-3)[\[4\]](#src-4)[\[7\]](#src-7)[\[37\]](#src-37). It serves Chicago north of Fullerton Avenue plus Evanston, Skokie, Wilmette, Winnetka, Glencoe, Northbrook, Northfield, Glenview, Golf, Morton Grove, Niles, Lincolnwood, Park Ridge, Harwood Heights, Des Plaines, and Kenilworth [\[4\]](#src-4). Treatment mirrors Calumet through the secondary stage, then passes through the 896-lamp UV disinfection facility before discharge to the North Shore Channel [\[4\]](#src-4)[\[12\]](#src-12). MWRD states wastewater from Lincolnwood can reach the plant in under 30 minutes and takes about 12 hours to complete treatment [\[4\]](#src-4). All solids are pumped to Stickney WRP for digestion rather than processed on-site [\[4\]](#src-4). The plant periodically also receives liquid digested solids and centrifuge centrate from the Egan WRP (Schaumburg) via a roughly 20-mile sewer connection, which MWRD is working to reduce through an ANITA™ Mox sidestream-treatment process at Egan itself, due to odor and corrosion problems in the conveying sewers [via MWRD biosolids/monitoring reports, 34]. O'Brien has received the National Association of Clean Water Agencies' highest compliance awards and hosts a Department of Energy-funded revolving algal biofilm (RAB) research greenhouse, developed with Iowa State University researchers, exploring phosphorus recovery from the liquid stream [\[13\]](#src-13)[\[19\]](#src-19). An alum-based Chemical Phosphorus Removal Facility was awarded in April 2025, targeted for service by June 2027, to complement the biological program. As of mid-2026 the plant is mid-construction on its Battery E secondary-treatment expansion, MWRD's largest contract ever let, with an estimated construction cost of $367,750,800 (AECOM Technical Services, engineering consultant) and a February 2028 substantial-completion target [\[26\]](#src-26)[\[27\]](#src-27)[\[45\]](#src-45)[\[46\]](#src-46).

## Notable events & incidents

- **1924–1946 patent litigation** — *Activated Sludge, Inc. v. Sanitary District of Chicago* (64 F. Supp. 25, N.D. Ill.) ran from the suit's 1924 filing through trial (May 1932 – June 1933), argument (March 1934), a 1935 liability decree, and finally the 1946 compensatory-damages ruling — a 22-year case tied to the District's use of the patented activated-sludge process at the Calumet, Des Plaines, and North Side plants; one of relatively few disputes in U.S. water-infrastructure history litigated over sewage-treatment process patents, and a rare primary source for exact plant start-up dates [\[24\]](#src-24)[\[44\]](#src-44).
- **May 17 – October 30, 2015** — Construction activity for O'Brien's new UV disinfection facility triggered the collapse of an adjacent CTA Yellow Line (Skokie Swift) embankment, closing the line for over five months at an estimated $3.5 million cost to CTA — an unusual case of a wastewater-plant capital project directly damaging adjacent transit infrastructure [\[13\]](#src-13).
- **July 4th weekend, 2023** — Record stormwater inflows during Independence Day flooding sent about 3.5 billion gallons into Calumet's system without exceeding half of its ~8-billion-gallon emergency quarry storage, illustrating the plant's built-in flood-resilience margin as extreme-rainfall frequency increases [\[15\]](#src-15).
- **2015–2016 disinfection rollout** — Completion of chlorination/dechlorination at Calumet (2015) and UV at O'Brien (2016) ended Chicago's status, per MWRD and EPA officials at the time, as the only major U.S. city releasing undisinfected treated sewage effluent into its waterways, directly enabling expanded recreational contact (kayaking, canoeing) on the Chicago Area Waterway System [\[16\]](#src-16)[\[17\]](#src-17).
- **2026 (June 18)** — MWRD Board authorized a $409.4 million sludge-removal contract to Michels Construction as part of the Battery E project at O'Brien — described by observers as the largest single contract in MWRD's history — signaling a new multi-decade capital-investment cycle at the 98-year-old plant [\[26\]](#src-26)[\[27\]](#src-27).

## Open questions

- ~~Exact original construction cost, in nominal dollars, for both the 1922 Calumet plant and the 1928 North Side (O'Brien) plant.~~ **Resolved:** the District's own 1928 publications (now located in full text) give a North Side Project total of $31,575,396 ($34,118,840.26 grand total with predecessor structures folded in; treatment works alone $18,190,190.94) and a Calumet Project total of $18,991,669.39 through Dec. 31, 1927 ($6,971,915.68 for the treatment works alone) [\[39\]](#src-39)[\[40\]](#src-40).
- **Named engineer(s) of record — substantially resolved.** The Sanitary District's Chief Engineer during the Calumet contract (1920) was Albert W. Dilling (in office by July 1921), succeeding G. M. Wisner (in office 1919); by the North Side Works' 1928 completion the Chief Engineer was Edward J. Kelly, later Mayor of Chicago. Langdon Pearse held the subordinate title of Sanitary Engineer throughout. The District's 1928 brochure states the North Side design work was done entirely in-house ("no outside help having been called in"); the treatment-works construction contracts were led by general contractor John Griffiths & Son Co., with Dorr Co., Allis-Chalmers Mfg. Co. and General Electric Co. among roughly twenty other named equipment contractors [\[39\]](#src-39)[\[40\]](#src-40)[\[42\]](#src-42)[\[43\]](#src-43). Still open: a specific general contractor/resident engineer of record for the 1920–1923 Calumet contract itself was not located.
- ~~The one-year discrepancy in Calumet's opening date.~~ **Resolved:** a contemporary December 1923 Sanitary District memorandum confirms the two-stage reading directly — "the sedimentation tanks completed and put in operation in September, 1922, and the tanks for the Activated Sludge Process put in service in May, 1923" — closely matching (within about a month of) the 1946 federal court's separately reported June 5, 1923 date for full activated-sludge start-up [\[38\]](#src-38).
- **Acreage of the Calumet WRP site.** MWRD's own 2025 fact sheet PDFs (both the March and October 2025 editions, independently confirmed) state "275.4 acres" while its current locations webpage states "470 acres" — an unresolved internal inconsistency, confirmed still present as of the October 2025 fact sheet.
- **Whether Imhoff tanks were ever used at Calumet or North Side specifically — corrected.** The prior edition of this document marked this "Resolved" (Imhoff tanks used only at West Side/Stickney) based on a secondary engineering-history source and an MWRD photo caption. Primary sources now contradict that: the District's own consulting engineers (Alvord, Burdick & Howson, 1927) describe Calumet as "the Calumet Imhoff tank plant" with a 55-MGD Imhoff-tank capacity and only a small "experimental" activated-sludge/sprinkling-filter component, and the District's 1928 *Engineering Works* volume documents live Imhoff-tank experiments (gas collection, sand-bed sludge drying) at Calumet that same year. Imhoff tanks were *also* used at West Side/Stickney (confirmed by the 1927 MWRD photo already cited), but Calumet was not a pure activated-sludge plant in its first decade-plus of operation — it ran a genuine dual Imhoff/activated-sludge process, with activated-sludge capacity growing over time (186 MGD by 1934) as the Imhoff-era design was progressively supplemented [\[36\]](#src-36)[\[39\]](#src-39)[\[41\]](#src-41)[\[44\]](#src-44). North Side/O'Brien, by contrast, remains confirmed as activated-sludge from the outset, with its own waste sludge piped to West Side/Stickney for Imhoff-tank digestion rather than treated with Imhoff tanks on site [\[40\]](#src-40).
- **Detailed aeration-battery expansion history for O'Brien, 1930s–1990s.** A 1934 federal review records North Side's capacity at 200 MGD (up from 175 MGD as reported under construction in 1927), showing early-1930s growth, but a comprehensive account of intermediate-era (1940s–1990s) capacity expansions between that point and the 2018-era Battery D addition was still not found [\[41\]](#src-41)[\[44\]](#src-44).
- **HAER/HABS documentation.** No dedicated Historic American Engineering Record (HAER) survey for either treatment plant was located at loc.gov during this research pass. Note for disambiguation: HAER *does* document a same-site-adjacent but unrelated structure — HAER No. IL-164-1, the Thomas J. O'Brien Lock and Control Works on the Illinois Waterway at East 130th Street, immediately next to Calumet WRP — which is a different "O'Brien" (Thomas J., not Terrence J.) than the O'Brien WRP in Skokie; worth a follow-up direct search of the Library of Congress Prints & Photographs catalog for the treatment works themselves.
- ~~Original cost and financing (bonds vs. tax levy) mechanism~~ **partially resolved for North Side:** the District's 1928 dedication brochure states the North Side Works "was financed out of the general corporate funds, and involved no burdensome special assessments on the taxpayers of the Sanitary District of Chicago" — ruling out special-assessment financing, though it does not specify the bonds-vs.-tax-levy split. Not confirmed for Calumet specifically [\[40\]](#src-40).
