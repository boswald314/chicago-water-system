# Chicago's Water Pumping Stations and Distribution Tunnels

*Research compiled July 2026. All sources accessed July 2026.*

## Role in the system

This is the delivery half of Chicago's drinking-water chain — the machinery that takes purified Lake Michigan water and pushes it out to taps across the city and the region. (The offshore intake cribs and lake tunnels that bring *raw* water in are covered in depth in the companion document on intake cribs; this document begins where that one leaves off, at the two purification plants, and follows the water outward.)

Raw lake water enters through the offshore cribs and flows by gravity through tunnels roughly 200 feet under the lakebed to shore-side intake basins at Chicago's two purification plants: the **James W. Jardine Water Purification Plant** (1000 E. Ohio St., serving the north, central and downtown areas) and the **Eugene Sawyer Water Purification Plant** (3300 E. Cheltenham Pl., serving the south side), formerly named the Central District and South District Filtration Plants respectively [\[12\]](#src-12)[\[13\]](#src-13)[\[33\]](#src-33). After filtration and chlorination, gravity carries the treated water onward — Chicago has essentially no elevated storage tanks — to a citywide network of **12 pumping stations**, each responsible for a geographic "pumping district," where high-lift pumps raise the water's pressure and push it into the street-level grid of cast-iron and ductile-iron mains [\[7\]](#src-7)[\[9\]](#src-9). From the grid mains, service pipes carry water to roughly 2.7 million Chicago residents and, on a wholesale basis, to more than 5 million people combined in surrounding suburban communities — about 43% of Illinois's population. Sources disagree on the precise current count of those suburban customers: the FY2022–2026 Capital Improvement Program, the most recent official figure available, states 123; the 2011 DWM transition report states 125; a 2021 University of Chicago Chicago Studies paper states 126; and chicago.gov's own current suburban-customers page gives no specific count at all [\[4\]](#src-4)[\[5\]](#src-5)[\[8\]](#src-8)[\[34\]](#src-34). Return flow — sanitary sewage and stormwater — is an entirely separate system operated by the Metropolitan Water Reclamation District of Greater Chicago (MWRD), not the Department of Water Management (DWM); DWM's sewer mains simply convey wastewater to MWRD's treatment plants, a boundary that is a frequent point of public confusion [\[8\]](#src-8)[\[34\]](#src-34).

The tunnels described in this document are therefore of two kinds: the long offshore *lake* tunnels (crib to shore — treated at length in the companion crib document) and the shorter inland *land* or "crosstown" tunnels that link the shore intake basins to each pumping station and, in several cases, link pumping stations to one another so that any station can help supply another's district in an emergency [\[1\]](#src-1). Above ground, roughly 4,300–4,400 miles of water mains, 47,000-plus fire hydrants and 48,000 water control valves complete the distribution system [\[4\]](#src-4)[\[7\]](#src-7)[\[8\]](#src-8).

## Original construction

Chicago's pumping-and-tunnel system is the direct engineering legacy of **Ellis Sylvester Chesbrough** (1813–1886), chief engineer of the Board of Public Works, who in 1863 proposed running a tunnel two miles out under Lake Michigan to intercept clean water beyond the sewage plume fouling the shoreline [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[6\]](#src-6). The tunnel and the accompanying **Chicago Avenue Pumping Station** were built simultaneously and "piecemeal," so as not to interrupt the existing water supply from the 1854 works on the same site [\[6\]](#src-6). The pumping station's original engine, nicknamed **"Old Sally,"** was an 8-million-gallon-per-day (MGD) vertical condensing steam engine with a single-acting pump, installed under the direction of a young engineer named **DeWitt Clinton Cregier** in 1853; it served the city faithfully for more than 50 years [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[6\]](#src-6). A third pump of 18 MGD was added on Cregier's recommendation as the present Chicago Avenue building rose in 1867–69 [\[6\]](#src-6).

The architect of both the **Chicago Avenue Pumping Station** and the adjoining **Chicago Water Tower** was **William W. Boyington** (1818–1898), one of the most prolific Midwestern architects of the era, who designed the pair in "castellated Gothic" style using rough-faced, cream-yellow Joliet limestone [\[6\]](#src-6)[\[9\]](#src-9)[\[35\]](#src-35). The pumping station's engine room measured 142 by 60 feet with a 36-foot ceiling and an operating gallery; a 131-foot smokestack rose from twin boiler rooms at the rear [\[6\]](#src-6). Because 1860s pumping engines delivered water in surges rather than a steady stream, Chesbrough specified a standpipe — an 138-foot, 3-foot-diameter vertical iron pipe — to absorb the pressure spikes; rather than leave the standpipe exposed, the city built the ornamental tower around it, with 168 foundation piles and a 154-foot limestone shaft (237 interior steps, no elevator) [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[6\]](#src-6). Both buildings were dedicated March 25, 1867 (tower cornerstone) and opened for service in 1869; they are the only public structures in the burned district to survive the Great Chicago Fire of October 8–9, 1871 substantially intact — although the fire's wood-shingle roof over the pumping station burned, gutting the machine shop and putting the pumps out of service for eight days — from the afternoon of October 9 until Cregier's crews restored them roughly a week later — leaving the city without water in the aftermath [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[6\]](#src-6)[\[9\]](#src-9).

The rest of the 20th-century station network followed a broadly repeating pattern of tunnel-then-station construction, driven by successive waves of annexation (the 1889 annexation of Lake View, Hyde Park, Jefferson and Lake townships alone expanded the city from 37 to 170 square miles in a single year) and by the discovery that shoreline pollution kept outrunning each new intake [\[1\]](#src-1). The **West Side (22nd Street/Ashland Avenue) station**, Chicago's second, opened November 6, 1876 under an October 1874 contract to Murphy & Co. and the Quintard Iron Works of New York, for $243,500, with two Corliss compound engines of 15 MGD each — 48-inch high-pressure and 76-inch low-pressure cylinders on a 6- and 10-foot stroke, with 32-foot, 60-ton flywheels [\[16\]](#src-16). The **Harrison Street** (1890) and **14th Street** (1891) stations were built as part of the Four-Mile Tunnel project, equipped with Allis-Chalmers triple-expansion engines still running decades later [\[1\]](#src-1). **Central Park** (1899–1900) and **Springfield Avenue** (1901) were built together as the terminus of the Carter H. Harrison crib's northwest land tunnel system, each originally fitted with three Worthington vertical triple-expansion engines of 20 MGD apiece [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[18\]](#src-18). **Roseland** (1911) served the newly tunneled southwest side. **Mayfair** (1913/1918), designed by city architect Charles Kallal, was built at the end of an eight-mile, hand-dug rock tunnel from the new Wilson Avenue crib after Lake View-area residents complained that pressure from the Lake View station three miles south was so weak that only ground floors of buildings received water [\[14\]](#src-14)[\[37\]](#src-37). **Western Avenue** (August 1927) — at 300 MGD rated capacity from four 75-MGD steam-turbine DeLaval centrifugal pumps — was, on completion, "the largest municipally owned water pumping station in the world" [\[1\]](#src-1). **Thomas Jefferson** (1928, architect Argyle Robinson) was built in under a year and was, as of the 1933 official history, "the twelfth and newest" of Chicago's stations [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[36\]](#src-36).

A 1901 Western Society of Engineers paper (delivered by engineer J. H. Spengler) adds contractor-level detail the 1933 booklet omits about the Four-Mile Tunnel stations: Harrison Street and 14th Street's engines were ordered under an **April 13, 1888 contract with the E. P. Allis Company** of Milwaukee for five vertical triple-expansion engines of 15 MGD each — two for Harrison Street, three for 14th Street — with nine return-tubular boilers installed at 14th Street and six at Harrison Street; the four-mile tunnel itself was completed December 7, 1892, though Harrison Street had already begun service July 14, 1890, temporarily fed through the old seven-foot land tunnel connecting Chicago Avenue and 22nd Street [\[41\]](#src-41). By 1917 Harrison Street's original two 15-MGD engines had been supplemented by a 25-MGD steam-driven centrifugal reserve pump (1913) for 55 MGD total, and 14th Street — which added a 30-MGD engine in 1898 on top of its original three 15-MGD units, plus its own 25-MGD reserve pump in 1913 — reached 100 MGD across five pumping engines; together with Chicago Avenue, the two stations supplied the Loop district [\[39\]](#src-39). The 22nd Street/West Side station, meanwhile, had two of its four original 1876/1884 Corliss engines replaced by electrically driven pumps in 1912, and by 1933 was fully electrified with four 32-MGD centrifugal units [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[39\]](#src-39) — making 22nd Street, not Harrison or 14th Street, the single already-"electric" station the 1933 booklet means when it says the new Central Pumping station would be "supplanting two steam and one electric stations" [\[1\]](#src-1); see the 1956 table below for evidence 22nd Street kept pumping for two more decades regardless.

Two of the twelve stations were not built by Chicago at all but arrived ready-made: Lake View and 68th Street were acquired through the June 29, 1889 annexation of 126 square miles — the City of Lake View, the Village of Hyde Park, and the Towns of Lake and Jefferson — which added 220,000 residents in a single stroke and gave Chicago 22 miles of lake frontage overnight [\[41\]](#src-41). The Lake View works, built in 1875 at Clarendon and Montrose Avenues, arrived with 19.5 MGD of pumping capacity, which grew through additions in 1892 (12 MGD), 1897 (14 MGD) and 1909 (25 MGD) before a full **$1,400,000 rebuild** — four new 25-MGD triple-expansion engines and a new boiler house — was substantially complete in 1915 [\[39\]](#src-39). The 68th Street site began as two *separate* jointly built waterworks: the Town of Lake and Village of Hyde Park first constructed a shared plant there in 1873, split into independent systems in 1882, and were both taken over by the city in 1889, after which the Town of Lake plant was abandoned and the Hyde Park plant — enlarged in 1891 specifically to help supply the 1893 World's Columbian Exposition, and again in 1905 — became the modern 68th Street station, rated 134 MGD by 1917 [\[39\]](#src-39)[\[41\]](#src-41).

By 1933 Chicago's Bureau of Engineering counted 12 pumping stations with a combined rated capacity of **1,954.5 MGD**, against a single 1.8-MGD pump at the city's very first (private) waterworks in 1842 — itself built by the Chicago Hydraulic Company at a cost of $24,000 under an Illinois state charter granted January 18, 1836 [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[38\]](#src-38). Of the twelve, only Lake View was by then equipped entirely with triple-expansion engines; seven ran centrifugal pumps only and four ran both types, with four of the twelve dependent on electric power and eight still steam-operated [\[1\]](#src-1). The system's total appraised value at the end of 1932 was **$130,303,548.64**, with **$111,534,762.48** already spent on capital construction [\[1\]](#src-1). A **new Central Pumping Station**, financed by a $2,327,000 loan from the federal Reconstruction Finance Corporation in 1932, was under construction on the site of the old Harrison Street station to consolidate and replace the obsolete 22nd Street, Harrison Street and 14th Street plants; it was expected in 1934, opened fully electric from the start — six 50-MGD motor-driven centrifugal pumps — and by 1952 was the cheapest station in the system to operate, at $8.05 per million gallons pumped, versus $47.48 at the outmoded 22nd Street station [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[2\]](#src-2). That Central Pumping Station survives today, renamed the **Cermak Pumping Station**, at 735 W. Harrison Street [\[2\]](#src-2). The renaming itself, previously reconstructed in this document only indirectly, is now confirmed by a 1956 city report, which states plainly that "the Harrison Street Pumping Station (rebuilt and called Cermak Station) was placed in service in 1889" — identifying Cermak as a rebuild of Harrison Street specifically — while separately dating the Cermak Pumping Station itself, elsewhere in the same report, to 1936 rather than 1934–35 [\[38\]](#src-38); see Open Questions for the remaining 1934/1935/1936 date discrepancy.

Chicago's aboveground distribution network has its own pre-Fire history, documented in the 1933 booklet's origin-story chapter: the first cast-iron main was laid in Clark Street in 1852 (4 inches in diameter), followed by a 24-inch main in La Salle Street in 1854 that was still found in "excellent condition" when it was finally removed in 1932 after 78 years' service; total mileage grew from two miles of bored cedar-log pipe (2–6 inches, laid in 1840 under the private Chicago Hydraulic Company's system) to the 3,712 miles of cast-iron main reported on January 1, 1933, ranging from 4 to 54 inches in diameter [\[1\]](#src-1). All 28 of the system's river crossings are made in dedicated brick- or concrete-lined tunnels rather than exposed pipe laid across the riverbed — a design response to repeated anchor-strike damage that had led City Engineer Chesbrough to push for tunneled crossings even before the 1871 fire [\[1\]](#src-1).

Alongside the pumping stations, the connecting *land* tunnels grew from a single 1867 five-foot bore to a citywide network. By 1933 the system totaled **63.04 miles of tunnel in service** (30.19 miles brick-lined, 32.85 miles concrete-lined), plus 8.56 miles abandoned and 11.43 miles under construction, served by 77 completed tunnel shafts [\[1\]](#src-1). Notable land tunnels included the **North West Land Tunnel** (1900, 9.60 miles, 10-ft main bore with 8-ft branches, serving Central Park and Springfield Avenue via the Carter Harrison crib), the **Four-Mile Lake Tunnel** (1892, 6.64 miles, feeding 14th Street and Harrison Street), and the **Southwest Land and Connecting Tunnel** (1911, 10.72 miles, 8–14-ft diameter, feeding 68th Street, Roseland and Western Avenue from the Dunne crib) [\[1\]](#src-1). The single largest 20th-century tunnel project was the new **Chicago Avenue (Dever) Tunnel**, authorized in 1922, budgeted at roughly $13.5 million, and completed in 1936: 16 feet in diameter, 11.5 miles long, running 130–200 feet below the surface through solid limestone, excavated with an estimated 3,000,000 pounds of dynamite and hailed by the *Chicago Tribune* in 1938 as "one of the greatest single engineering jobs in the world" [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[15\]](#src-15). By 1956 the tunnel network had grown only modestly further, to 63.6 miles (including the 2.80-mile Sawyer connector) — most of the mid-century growth went into re-equipping existing stations rather than digging new tunnel [\[38\]](#src-38).

### Which plant feeds which station: the three pre-filtration tunnel districts

Before either purification plant existed, the city's own engineering staff organized the tunnel network — and the 12 (then 11) pumping stations it fed — into three self-contained "Districts," each with its own crib(s) and dedicated land-tunnel system: a **North District** (Lake View and Wilson Avenue tunnels, serving the Lake View, Mayfair and Thomas Jefferson stations), a **Central District** (Four Mile Lake, Chicago Avenue, Blue Island Avenue, Polk Street and Desplaines Street tunnels, serving Chicago Avenue, 22nd Street/Cermak, Central Park Avenue and Springfield Avenue), and a **South District** (Southwest Lake and Land, Western Avenue and Stewart Avenue tunnels, serving 68th Street, Roseland and Western Avenue) — a grouping laid out explicitly, station by station and tunnel by tunnel, in the city's own 1956 technical report [\[38\]](#src-38). That report predates Jardine (whose predecessor, the Central District Filtration Plant, was then still under construction) and Sawyer had only just reached full capacity, so the North and Central Districts were still running on chlorination-only lake water piped in from their old cribs, while the South District's tunnels had already been physically spliced into the new South District (Sawyer) plant via a dedicated 2.80-mile, 1945 "Connection to Filtration Plant" tunnel — the direct ancestor of every finished-water feeder described below [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[38\]](#src-38). The same 1956 report describes the mechanism at the Sawyer end concretely: the South District plant's low-lift pumping station used eight large motor-driven pumps with a combined 750 MGD capacity to lift raw water roughly 20–25 feet from the intake basin into the treatment train, after which filtered water flowed by gravity to two main filtered-water reservoirs connected "by a tunnel through solid rock" directly to the three South District pumping stations (68th Street, Roseland, Western Avenue) — i.e., the 2.80-mile connector *is* that tunnel [\[38\]](#src-38). Once Jardine came online in the 1960s, its combined "north, central and downtown" service territory absorbed both the old North and Central District tunnel networks, meaning **8 of the 12 modern stations — Chicago Avenue, Central Park, Springfield, Mayfair, Thomas Jefferson, Cermak, Lakeview and (indirectly, via Central Park) Lexington — draw finished water that originates at Jardine**, while **the other 4 — 68th Street, Roseland, Western Avenue and Southwest — draw from Sawyer**, continuing the old South District network Sawyer was built to feed [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[38\]](#src-38). No source located in this research documents the specific connector tunnel(s) built after 1964 to splice the old North and Central District networks into Jardine's output, the way the 1945 tunnel splices the South District network into Sawyer's — this is flagged as an open question below. What the 1956 report does establish is a firm *lower bound* on when such a connector had to be built: as of July 1956 the old North and Central District tunnels were confirmed still running raw, chlorination-only water with no filtration-plant splice of any kind, narrowing the construction window for the Jardine connector(s) to the c. 1956–1968 span rather than anytime after 1945 [\[38\]](#src-38).

Several of these routes are unusually well documented at street level. The 1917 Chicago Bureau of Public Efficiency report describes the **Northwest Land and Lake Tunnel** (completed 1900) running from the Carter H. Harrison crib southwest to Green Street and Grand Avenue, where it splits into an 8-ft branch southwest to Central Park Avenue and an 8-ft branch northwest to Springfield Avenue — explaining why those two stations were built together as a pair [\[39\]](#src-39). The same source traces the **Southwest Land and Lake Tunnel** (completed 1911, "designed to supply the Roseland pumping station") from the Dunne crib southwest to 73rd Street and Yates Avenue, then west along 73rd Street to State Street, then south along the State Street corridor to Roseland — with a separate 1916 connection extended north to 68th Street and a 1927 extension built to reach the new Western Avenue station [\[39\]](#src-39). Mayfair's eight-mile hand-dug rock tunnel from the Wilson Avenue crib is documented elsewhere in this report; a short 1913 branch tunnel in Clarendon Avenue off that same Wilson Avenue Tunnel supplied the old Lake View Pumping Station, made permanent by a full spur completed in 1918 [\[2\]](#src-2)[\[14\]](#src-14). Lexington is the cleanest modern case: rather than a direct plant connection, it draws Jardine-sourced water relayed through the **Central Park Avenue Pumping Station**, via a 12-foot-diameter bedrock tunnel the DuPage Water Commission's own materials put at 2.4 miles — a figure that lines up almost exactly with the straight-line distance between the two stations' addresses [\[23\]](#src-23). Southwest Pumping Station's tie to Sawyer is the most recent and least historically documented of the twelve: no source gives a construction date for the station itself, but the City's FY2025–2029 Capital Improvement Program lists a "Southwest Pumping Station – Joliet Tunnel Connection" project (#44019, design 2022–2023, construction 2023–2026) filed under Sawyer's own capital program specifically, tying the two together as part of the buildout for the Chicago–Grand Prairie Water Commission regional pipeline described above [\[25\]](#src-25)[\[40\]](#src-40). A machine-readable version of these 12 plant-to-station routes, with per-connection sourcing and confidence flags, is maintained at `map-data/water-supply-feeders.json` for the archive's interactive map.

## Upgrades & modernization timeline

- **1853–1869** — Chicago Avenue site progresses from "Old Sally" (8 MGD, 1853) through a 12-MGD second engine (1857) to the present castellated-Gothic station and Water Tower (designed by W.W. Boyington, dedicated 1867, in full service 1869) [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[6\]](#src-6).
- **1876** — West Side (22nd St./Ashland Ave.) pumping station opens with two 15-MGD Corliss compound engines; two more of the same type added 1884 [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[16\]](#src-16).
- **1889–1892** — Annexation of Lake View, Hyde Park, Jefferson and Lake townships (170 sq. mi. total) brings existing satellite pumping infrastructure into the Chicago system; Harrison Street (1890) and 14th Street (1891, "Four-Mile Tunnel" project) open with Allis-Chalmers triple-expansion engines [\[1\]](#src-1).
- **1899–1901** — Central Park and Springfield Avenue stations built together at the end of the Carter H. Harrison crib's northwest land tunnels, each with three 20-MGD Worthington engines [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[18\]](#src-18).
- **1909** — Southwest Lake and Land Tunnel begun; Edward F. Dunne Crib in service by December 1911, supplying the new Roseland station, though a contemporary 1917 city report states the station itself "has been in operation since 1912" [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[39\]](#src-39).
- **1913/1918** — Mayfair pumping station opens at the terminus of the new eight-mile Wilson Avenue crib tunnel, ending decades of low water pressure on the far North Side [\[14\]](#src-14).
- **1922–26** — Central Park and Springfield Avenue re-equipped, replacing their original 1900–01 triple-expansion engines with steam-turbine-driven DeLaval centrifugal pumps of 60 MGD each [\[1\]](#src-1).
- **1927** — Western Avenue station opens — briefly "the largest municipally owned water pumping station in the world" at 300 MGD [\[1\]](#src-1).
- **1928** — Thomas Jefferson station (architect Argyle Robinson) opens in under a year, the newest of the "classic twelve" [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[36\]](#src-36).
- **1932–1936** — New Central Pumping Station (fully electric, six 50-MGD pumps) rises on the old Harrison Street site with $2.327M in RFC financing, opening 1934–35 (later renamed Cermak Pumping Station); the new 16-ft, 11.5-mile Chicago Avenue tunnel is completed in 1936 [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[2\]](#src-2).
- **1952** — System-wide statistics: 12 stations, population served 4,226,000, 967,937,000 gallons pumped per day, rated capacity 2,560 MGD, 151,095,000 tons of coal and 15,933,000 KWh of electricity consumed — the last full snapshot of the mixed steam/electric-era fleet before Lake View's phase-out began [\[2\]](#src-2).
- **1955–56** — Construction begins on a 30-million-gallon underground reservoir at Western Avenue, the first storage reservoir anywhere in the Chicago water system (51% complete by year-end 1955); two new 60-MGD pumps and a 650-horsepower boiler added system-wide; by July 1956 the city reports 11 stations (Southwest and Lexington not yet built), 63.6 miles of tunnel, 4,067 miles of mains, 51 suburban communities served, and — a striking contrast with today — a *higher* average daily pumpage (1.04 BGD) than the current ~750 MGD–1 BGD, reflecting substantially higher per-capita water use in the mid-1950s [\[38\]](#src-38).
- **1964–65** — Lake View pumping station (Clarendon/Montrose) placed in standby February 1964, pumps for the last time December 1964, officially retired May 31, 1965; demolished 1978–79 by Bongi Wrecking Co. [\[2\]](#src-2).
- **1964/1968** — Jardine (Central District) plant enters full operation, eventually rated the world's largest conventional filtration plant at 1.4 BGD [\[12\]](#src-12).
- **1986–1992** — DuPage Water Commission agreement (1986) leads to construction of the Lexington Pumping Station (bedrock excavation to 90 ft, 36-month build) and a 9.3-mile, 90-in./72-in. transmission main to Elmhurst; system reaches full operation May 1, 1992 [\[22\]](#src-22)[\[23\]](#src-23)[\[24\]](#src-24).
- **2009–2011** — Federal STAG and state DCEO grants fund Thomas Jefferson pumping-station improvements (~$485,000) and Lexington station work (~$250,000); MeterSave voluntary metering program expands citywide January 1, 2010 [\[5\]](#src-5).
- **2011 (in progress) → ~2021** — Automated Meter Reading (AMR) rollout: a 2021 Badger Meter contract for roughly 162,000 residential/commercial meters (Orion radio-frequency AMR) extended the earlier 2007–11 AMR program [\[5\]](#src-5)[\[29\]](#src-29).
- **2012** — Springfield Avenue pumping station converts from steam to electric power, announced under Mayor Rahm Emanuel [\[20\]](#src-20).
- **2017** — Springfield Avenue conversion publicly unveiled: ~70% carbon-emissions cut (≈17,380 tons/yr), an estimated $4.5M/yr savings, plus solar panels, permeable pavers, underground retention and a green roof; three more of the city's steam stations slated for the same conversion under "Building a New Chicago" [\[21\]](#src-21).
- **c. 2015–early 2020s** — Central Park pumping station undergoes a ~$115 million steam-to-electric conversion (design/engineering by Greeley and Hansen; construction partners including Helm Group, IDCS, Aldridge Electric), installing variable-frequency-drive (VFD) motors and saving an estimated $4 million/year in operating costs [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[18\]](#src-18)[\[19\]](#src-19).
- **2020** — $74.5 million renovation approved for the Sawyer plant's 1941 building and 1964 addition, including roof restoration [\[13\]](#src-13).
- **2022** — Chicago Water Partners Advisory Council (CWPAC) established by DWM to formalize coordination with its wholesale suburban customers — reported as 123 (FY2022–2026 CIP), 125 (2011 transition report) or 126 (2021 academic paper) depending on source and year — meeting quarterly [\[4\]](#src-4)[\[5\]](#src-5)[\[8\]](#src-8)[\[34\]](#src-34).
- **2022–2026 Capital Improvement Program** — $1.7 billion allocated across the water system: $469.6M for the Jardine and Sawyer plants (incl. a new Joliet Tunnel Connection at Sawyer), $170.2M for pumping-station operations (continuing work at Central Park; Western Avenue and Mayfair in design phase), $760.9M for water distribution (including the start of the Lead Service Line Replacement Program), $96.0M for the New Meter Program, and $173.8M for water engineering [\[4\]](#src-4).
- **2024–2025** — Groundbreaking on the Chicago–Grand Prairie Water Commission regional pipeline project, connecting at the Southwest Pumping Station to deliver Lake Michigan water (~$1.5 billion project) to Joliet, Crest Hill, Channahon, Minooka, Shorewood and Romeoville [\[5\]](#src-5)[\[25\]](#src-25).
- **2025** — Chicago on pace to replace roughly 8,000 lead service lines for the year, after managing only 7,923 replacements over the prior four years combined. The city's *own* replacement plan — filed with the Illinois EPA under state law and keyed to an outdated first-Trump-administration federal rule — aims to finish replacing an estimated 400,000+ lead lines by 2076/77; this is not itself a federal deadline. The Biden-era EPA rule actually in force gives most utilities 10 years and Chicago roughly 20 (starting 2027, i.e., into the late 2040s) to finish, so Chicago's own plan runs about 30 years behind the deadline the current federal rule sets for Chicago specifically. As of mid-2025 the city had sent required customer notifications to only about 7% of the affected list despite $300 million-plus in unspent loan funds [\[27\]](#src-27)[\[28\]](#src-28).
- **2025–2029 Capital Improvement Program** — the successor five-year plan roughly doubles the prior cycle's water-system commitment to **$3.7 billion**: $482.9M for the Jardine and Sawyer plants, $231.5M for pumping-station operations (specifically naming Central Park's conversion to electric pumps and the Southwest Pumping Station's Grand Prairie Water Commission tunnel connection as program highlights), $2.3 billion for water distribution — including a target of **12 miles of new mains annually** and a stated goal of **10,000 lead-service-line replacements per year by 2027**, tied to the EPA's then-proposed Lead and Copper Rule Improvements — $356.7M for water engineering, and $280.5M for the New Meter Program; the plan puts the citywide lead-service-line count at **413,000** [\[40\]](#src-40).

## Current operations

Chicago's Department of Water Management delivers on the order of **750 MGD to just under 1 billion gallons per day** on average — against a combined rated treatment capacity at Jardine and Sawyer of roughly **2.1 billion gallons per day** — to Chicago's approximately 2.7 million residents and, per the most recent official count (the FY2022–2026 CIP), **123 suburban wholesale customers**. That count is not stable across sources: the 2011 DWM transition report gives 125 and a 2021 academic paper gives 126, so the precise current figure should be treated as approximate. Together these customers represent more than 5 million people and roughly 43% of Illinois's population [\[4\]](#src-4)[\[5\]](#src-5)[\[7\]](#src-7)[\[8\]](#src-8)[\[34\]](#src-34). Water leaves the two plants by gravity, is lifted and pressurized at the 12 pumping stations, and is pushed into roughly 4,300–4,400 miles of water mains via 47,000-plus hydrants and 48,000 valves [\[4\]](#src-4)[\[7\]](#src-7)[\[8\]](#src-8). Notably, this average delivery figure is *lower* than the system pumped seven decades ago: a 1956 city report puts 1955's average daily pumpage at 1,037,074,000 gallons — a legacy of substantially higher mid-century per-capita and industrial water use — even though the system now also serves a larger combined city-plus-suburban population [\[38\]](#src-38). DWM's own laboratories monitor water quality around the clock, and the department reports that its water consistently meets or exceeds US EPA, Illinois EPA and Illinois Department of Public Health standards [\[7\]](#src-7); its 2024 Consumer Confidence Report shows a 90th-percentile tap-water lead result of 7.1 ppb, well under the EPA action level of 15 ppb [\[42\]](#src-42).

Organizationally, DWM in 2011 comprised roughly 2,135 budgeted full-time positions under a Commissioner, a 1st Deputy/Chief Engineer, and three Managing Deputy Commissioners overseeing Engineering Services/Water Supply, Administrative Support/Meter Services, and Operations & Distribution [\[5\]](#src-5); the department's Water Fund and Sewer Fund together carried more than $250 million in annual revenue at that time, funded almost entirely by water and sewer rates rather than general tax dollars, with roughly 48% of water revenue historically generated by suburban wholesale sales [\[5\]](#src-5)[\[34\]](#src-34). DWM is legally and organizationally distinct from the MWRD: DWM supplies drinking water and conveys sewage/stormwater only as far as MWRD's interceptors, while MWRD owns and treats the wastewater side and the Deep Tunnel/TARP reservoir system [\[8\]](#src-8)[\[34\]](#src-34).

Of the 12 pumping stations, four (Springfield, Central Park, and — per city announcements — two further stations) were targeted for steam-to-electric conversion under a program begun in the Emanuel administration and continuing into the 2020s, motivated by reduced carbon emissions, lower maintenance costs, and the operational agility of variable-frequency-drive electric motors versus century-old steam turbines [\[4\]](#src-4)[\[19\]](#src-19)[\[21\]](#src-21). Western Avenue and Mayfair pumping stations were in the design phase for capital improvements as of the 2022–2026 Capital Improvement Program, while construction continued at Central Park [\[4\]](#src-4); the successor FY2025–2029 CIP names Central Park's conversion to electric pumps as an explicit program highlight, confirming it was still the active conversion project of the four as of that plan [\[40\]](#src-40). The system's aging infrastructure remains a persistent operational challenge: roughly half of Chicago-area water mains are more than 50 years old, some over a century, and the region loses an estimated 22 billion gallons of treated water annually to leaks; DWM fields on the order of 145,000 service calls per year [\[32\]](#src-32).

Lead service lines are the department's most consequential current modernization challenge. Chicago estimates roughly 400,000–413,000 of its approximately 490,000 total service lines are lead or lead-suspect — the FY2025–2029 Capital Improvement Program's own figure is 413,000 — a legacy of a municipal code that required lead until 1986 [\[27\]](#src-27)[\[28\]](#src-28)[\[40\]](#src-40). The 2077 (per some accounts, 2076) completion date frequently cited in press coverage is *not* a federal mandate: it is the pace of Chicago's own replacement plan, filed with the Illinois EPA under state law and based on an outdated first-Trump-administration federal rule. The Biden-era EPA rule actually now in force gives most water systems 10 years and Chicago roughly 20 (starting in 2027) to finish replacement — meaning Chicago's own plan runs about 30 years behind the deadline the *current* federal rule sets for Chicago specifically, not behind some universal federal pace [\[27\]](#src-27)[\[28\]](#src-28). The FY2025–2029 CIP itself sets a nearer-term operational target of **10,000 lead-service-line replacements per year by 2027**, explicitly tied to the EPA's then-proposed Lead and Copper Rule Improvements — a target roughly 25% above the "on pace for 8,000" figure reported for 2025 [\[40\]](#src-40). As of 2025 the department had over $300 million in unspent state revolving-fund loan dollars earmarked for the work and had replaced only 7,923 lines over the prior four years, though it reported being on pace for about 8,000 replacements in 2025 alone [\[27\]](#src-27)[\[28\]](#src-28). A parallel Automated Meter Reading (AMR) program — begun in the late 2000s and re-contracted in 2021 for roughly 162,000 additional meters using Badger Meter's Orion radio-frequency system — continues to convert Chicago's large stock of unmetered flat-rate accounts to metered billing [\[5\]](#src-5)[\[29\]](#src-29).

## Notable events & incidents

- **October 8–9, 1871 — Great Chicago Fire.** The wood-shingle roof of the original Chicago Avenue pumping station caught fire; the pumps were knocked out of commission on the afternoon of the second day of the fire and stayed out of service for eight days (the city's own 1933 statistics table records the city as "without water for eight days"), even as the adjacent Water Tower and standpipe survived essentially undamaged — one of very few structures left standing in the burned district [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[6\]](#src-6)[\[9\]](#src-9). An estimated 15,000 lead service connections citywide melted in the blaze [\[1\]](#src-1).
- **1908 — Waste investigation.** A city investigation found roughly 75% of pumped water was being lost to leakage or unmetered/unnecessary use, spurring the early-20th-century push toward universal metering [\[16\]](#src-16).
- **1909 — Dunne/Intermediate Crib fire** (see companion crib document for full account): killed an estimated 47–70 construction workers building the tunnel system that would feed the Roseland pumping station via the Southwest Land Tunnel [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[16\]](#src-16).
- **1932 — Reconstruction Finance Corporation loan.** A $2,327,000 federal RFC loan — one of the era's signature federal infrastructure interventions — financed the new Central Pumping Station (later Cermak), consolidating three obsolete 19th-century stations into one modern, all-electric plant during the depths of the Depression [\[1\]](#src-1).
- **1964–65 / 1978–79 — Lake View station phase-out and demolition.** Chicago's oldest annexed pumping-station complex (in service on that site since 1875) was placed on standby in 1964, formally retired in 1965, and physically demolished in 1978–79 — a rare fully documented "cradle to grave" record for a Chicago pumping station thanks to its HAER survey [\[2\]](#src-2).
- **1974 — Lake View chimney partial demolition.** The station's 215-foot chimney, built on four 5-foot-diameter caisson piers, was judged unsafe and had its top section removed in 1974, five years before the rest of the plant came down [\[2\]](#src-2).
- **2012–2017 — Springfield Avenue steam-to-electric conversion**, announced 2012 and unveiled 2017: Chicago's first completed pumping-station power-source conversion under the modern modernization program, publicly framed around a ~70% carbon-emissions cut [\[20\]](#src-20)[\[21\]](#src-21).
- **July 9–11, 2024 — Roseland Pumping Station mechanical failure and boil order.** A leak on a high-pressure water main inside the Roseland Pumping Station caused a pressure drop across parts of Beverly, Morgan Park and Auburn Gresham; DWM, in consultation with Illinois EPA, issued a precautionary boil-water order affecting an estimated 60,000 residents from the evening of July 9 until it was lifted the morning of July 11, 2024 — reported locally as the third such boil order affecting South Side neighborhoods in three years [\[30\]](#src-30).

## Open questions

- **The current "Lakeview Pumping Station" address discrepancy — still unresolved, but corroborated as a real, funded facility.** DWM's 2011 facility list shows an active, staffed, 24/7 "Lakeview Pumping Station" at 745 W. Wilson Ave. [\[5\]](#src-5), yet the HAER historical record states unambiguously that the historic Lake View Pumping Station at Clarendon & Montrose Avenues was retired in 1965 and demolished in 1978–79 [\[2\]](#src-2). The FY2025–2029 CIP independently confirms a "Lakeview PS" as a distinctly named, currently funded facility (capital project #39131, "Lakeview PS Electrical & Security Improvements," $542,405, design 2019–20) [\[40\]](#src-40), which rules out a mere clerical listing error but still does not explain when or how a replacement facility near Wilson Avenue was built. It is unclear from the sources found whether a new/replacement Lakeview station was built nearby (Wilson, Clarendon and Montrose Avenues are all within a few blocks of one another in Uptown) or whether the facility list entry reflects a different naming convention. Not resolved in any source consulted.
- **Southwest Pumping Station construction date — still unresolved; address itself now also in dispute.** No source found gives a build year for the Southwest Pumping Station; it does not appear by that name in the 1933 official history, suggesting it was built sometime between 1933 and 2009 (when it first appears in capital-project records alongside Roseland), but no confirming source was located. A secondary discrepancy has also surfaced: the 2011 DWM transition report gives the address as 8422 S. Kedvale Ave. [\[5\]](#src-5), while the FY2025–2029 CIP's own facility address list gives 8400 S Kedvale Ave [\[40\]](#src-40) — a one-digit conflict between two official City documents that neither source explains.
- **"Central Pumping Station" → "Cermak Pumping Station" renaming — now resolved, with a new date wrinkle.** A July 1956 City of Chicago report states explicitly that "the Harrison Street Pumping Station (rebuilt and called Cermak Station) was placed in service in 1889," directly confirming Cermak Station as a rebuild of Harrison Street specifically (not merely a three-station consolidation under a new name) — closing the identification this document previously had to reconstruct indirectly [\[38\]](#src-38). That same 1956 report, however, opens a new small puzzle: its Table 3 dates the Cermak Pumping Station's own in-service year to **1936**, diverging from the 1933 booklet's "expected...1934" and a 1935-dated photo caption elsewhere in this archive's sources [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[31\]](#src-31)[\[38\]](#src-38); no source reconciles the exact 1934/1935/1936 spread. Separately, the same 1956 table shows 22nd Street still listed as an active, electrically powered station with real 1955 pumpage (46.72 MGD average) — implying it survived in reduced service for roughly two decades after Cermak's opening rather than closing immediately, a detail no other source in this archive documents.
- **Western Avenue architect.** One low-authority secondary source names Charles Kallal as architect and describes a "Renaissance Revival" style, but the same source incorrectly labels the station an MWRD facility (drinking-water pumping has always been a City of Chicago/DWM function, not MWRD's), undermining confidence in the rest of its claims. A separate, independently verified source (DNAinfo Chicago, 2017) documents Charles Kallal as city architect of a *different* station — Mayfair (1915–18) [\[37\]](#src-37) — raising the likelihood that the low-authority source conflated the two projects. Western Avenue's actual architect is still not corroborated by any reliable source found.
- **Exact current per-station MGD capacities.** No modern (post-1990s) source found gives an official station-by-station capacity table comparable to the 1933, 1952 or (now) 1956 figures reproduced above; current combined system figures (12 stations, 2.1 BGD plant capacity, ~750 MGD–1 BGD average delivery) are well documented, but individual current station ratings — after decades of re-equipping — were not found in any public document.
- **Full list and start dates for the steam-to-electric conversion program.** Central Park and Springfield Avenue conversions are documented in detail; a 2022 city document states four stations total were slated for conversion, and the FY2025–2029 CIP independently reconfirms Central Park's conversion as still the active project of the four [\[40\]](#src-40), but the identity of the fourth station (beyond Central Park and Springfield) was not confirmed in any source found.
- **Precise current mileage of tunnel vs. main.** Sources from 2022–2026 consistently cite "65 miles of tunnels" and "4,300–4,400 miles of water mains," but none reproduce the granular per-tunnel table that the 1933 booklet provided, so it is unclear how much of the 1933-era tunnel inventory (much of it now 90–160 years old) remains in active service versus abandoned-in-place. A 1956 city report shows the total had already reached 63.6 tunnel miles and roughly 4,067 main miles by then [\[38\]](#src-38), meaning both figures have grown only modestly (tunnel) or essentially plateaued (mains) over the past seven decades — but the per-tunnel accounting stops there too.
- **Post-1964 North/Central District connector tunnels to Jardine — window narrowed, not closed.** The 1945, 2.80-mile "Connection to Filtration Plant" tunnel splicing the old South District network into the new Sawyer plant is explicitly documented [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[38\]](#src-38), but no equivalent document was found describing the analogous connector(s) presumably built after 1964 to route Jardine's output into the old North District (Lake View/Wilson Avenue) and Central District (Four Mile Lake/Chicago Avenue/Blue Island Avenue) tunnel networks feeding Chicago Avenue, Central Park, Springfield, Mayfair, Thomas Jefferson, Cermak and Lakeview. The 1956 report does at least establish a firm lower bound: as of July 1956 those tunnels were confirmed still running raw, chlorination-only water with no filtration-plant splice, so the connector(s) must date to the c. 1956–1968 window, not merely "sometime after 1945" [\[38\]](#src-38). `map-data/water-supply-feeders.json` marks every Jardine-to-North/Central-District-station route `approx: true` for this reason; a Bureau of Engineering as-built drawing or annual report from the 1960s would likely resolve it fully.
