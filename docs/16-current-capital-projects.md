# What's Being Built Right Now (2026): The System's Active Capital Projects

## Role in the system

Every other document in this archive narrates something essentially finished — a canal dug in 1900, a tunnel bored between 1975 and 2006, a reservoir completed in 2015. This document narrates the opposite: the system as a live construction site, captured in a single snapshot dated **July 2026**. Every dollar figure, percent-complete, and schedule date below is true as of that month and will drift — some quickly. A contract "under construction" in July 2026 may be finished by the time this is read; a project budgeted at one figure may be awarded at a materially different one (the O'Brien WRP Battery E project below moved from a $325.0 million engineer's estimate to a $409.4 million actual low bid within the same budget cycle — a live illustration of why this document is dated rather than definitive) [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[10\]](#src-10)[\[11\]](#src-11).

The value of pinning this snapshot into the archive is threefold. First, most of the system documented elsewhere here — the interceptors of doc [07](07-mwrd-interceptors-pumping-stations.md), the TARP tunnels of doc [09](09-tarp-deep-tunnel.md), the reservoirs of doc [10](10-tarp-reservoirs.md), the treatment plants of docs [11](11-stickney-wrp.md), [12](12-calumet-obrien-wrp.md), and [13](13-suburban-wrps.md) — is not actually finished; it is mid-rehabilitation, mid-expansion, or (in McCook Reservoir's case) still being excavated four decades after the federal design memorandum that authorized it. A "completed" 1930s pumping station or a "completed" 2015 reservoir is, this document shows, still generating tens of millions of dollars in active contract work every year. Second, a capital-projects snapshot is the cleanest way to see money and governance move across agency lines in real time: a Chicago Transit Authority transit-line extension is currently reshaping a Calumet WRP solids-handling contract; a U.S. Army Corps of Engineers pilot funding program is underwriting part of McCook Reservoir's federal share; a regional water commission's pipeline groundbreaking runs through the same Southwest Pumping Station this archive's water-distribution document already covers. Third, this document is a control on the rest of the archive: several facts recovered here — the exact contract number and value for the North Branch Pumping Station's 2023–2027 rehabilitation (open in doc [08](08-north-branch-pumping-station.md) as of its last revision), for instance — resolve open questions elsewhere by drawing on MWRD's and the City's own 2026 capital-budget documents rather than secondary reporting.

This document is organized by owning agency — MWRD, the City of Chicago Department of Water Management (DWM), and the U.S. Army Corps of Engineers (USACE) — because capital planning, funding, and procurement are agency-specific even where the underlying infrastructure is deeply interconnected (MWRD's TARP tunnels feed reservoirs built partly by USACE; DWM's Southwest Pumping Station is the Chicago-side terminus of a regional water commission's pipeline; USACE's Brandon Road project sits on the same Illinois Waterway that MWRD's effluent ultimately reaches). Dollar figures below are nominal (as-budgeted or as-awarded) unless noted; where MWRD's budget book gives both an engineer's estimate and a later actual award, both are shown.

## McCook Reservoir Stage 2 — the last piece of TARP

McCook Reservoir Stage 2 is the single largest piece of unfinished work anywhere in the system this archive documents, and it is unusual among the projects here in being gated not by money or design but by a commercial rock market. As doc [10](10-tarp-reservoirs.md) covers in detail, MWRD's 2003 mining agreement with Vulcan Materials Co. lets Vulcan crush and sell the reservoir's excavated stone, which is what makes MWRD's own out-of-pocket excavation cost (contract 73-161-EH, "Vulcan Rock Mining Hard Costs Less Royalty," $81,585,000 and still climbing as mining continues) far below what a conventional dig would cost — but it also means the pace of excavation tracks the regional aggregate market rather than a construction schedule MWRD fully controls [\[2\]](#src-2)[\[4\]](#src-4). MWRD's June 2025 TARP Status Report puts Stage 2 at roughly 72 percent excavated, and the District's own 2025 public update pushed the consent-decree completion date from December 31, 2029 to an estimated December 31, 2032, explicitly citing slowed stone demand rather than any funding or design shortfall [\[2\]](#src-2)[\[3\]](#src-3).

The federal side of Stage 2 has its own, separately noteworthy history. On January 31, 2019, USACE's Chicago District signed what both the Corps and MWRD described as a first-of-its-kind agreement: rather than USACE designing and constructing the remaining reservoir features itself (the arrangement used for Stage 1 and for Majewski Reservoir), the Corps transferred $33.8 million in federal funds directly to MWRD as Stage 2's federal cost-share, under a pilot program the Corps had never previously used, and MWRD assumed full design and construction responsibility for the remainder of the work [\[5\]](#src-5)[\[6\]](#src-6). MWRD's 2026 capital budget shows that federal-share arrangement continuing project-by-project rather than as a single lump sum: the 2025-completed McCook Reservoir Rock Wall Stabilization and Geotechnical Instrumentation contract (17-131-4FR, $21,862,000) carried a 53 percent federal share; the still-future McCook Reservoir Stage 2 Final Reservoir Preparation contract (17-132-4F, $24,796,000) is budgeted at a 69 percent federal share; and ongoing geotechnical professional services (19-151-4C, $1,491,000) carry a 75 percent federal share — the same historical rate as the original Stages 1–2 Army Corps construction-contract package (73-161-2H, $615,937,000) [\[1\]](#src-1). Total Phase II reservoir project cost across all three reservoirs stood at $1,525,491,000 in the 2026 budget book, essentially matching the $1.524 billion figure in MWRD's own June 2025 TARP Status Report [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[4\]](#src-4).

## O'Brien WRP Battery E — MWRD's largest contract ever, and a live case study in cost growth

The O'Brien WRP's Battery E Activated Sludge Facility is covered in construction and regulatory detail in doc [12](12-calumet-obrien-wrp.md); this document adds the primary-source contract detail behind the headline numbers. MWRD's own bid documents (Contract 21-092-3P, "Battery E Activated Sludge Facility," Terrence J. O'Brien Water Reclamation Plant, Skokie) set a bid-opening date of Tuesday, October 14, 2025, and describe the physical scope precisely: an 8-by-8-foot cast-in-place concrete influent conduit and utility tunnel, a three-pass fermenter tank with fourteen mechanical mixers, a one-pass anoxic pass with six mixers, an influent channel with four mixers and six motorized slide gates, six two-pass aeration tanks (each with two mixers, an FRP baffle wall, an internal mixed-liquor recirculation pump, and a fine-bubble membrane disc diffuser system), and a 63-inch-diameter HDPE return-activated-sludge conduit — a new aeration battery built essentially from scratch alongside the 98-year-old plant it will partly relieve [\[8\]](#src-8). The bid document specifies 1,460 calendar days to substantial completion after bond approval — just under four years, consistent with the budget book's separately stated January 2030 completion target [\[8\]](#src-8)[\[1\]](#src-1).

The cost story is where this project becomes a useful test case for how to read every other dollar figure in this document. MWRD's 2026 Final Budget Book — finalized before the contract was awarded — carried Battery E at an estimated construction cost of $325,005,000, with the contractor listed as "to be determined" and the award date projected for January 2026 [\[1\]](#src-1). Trade press covering the pre-bid project described an even wider engineering-estimate range of $308–373 million [\[10\]](#src-10). When MWRD's Board of Commissioners actually authorized the contract — at its June 18, 2026 meeting, under agenda item 26-0432, after the item had first been pulled from the June 4 agenda — the award went to Michels Construction at $409,420,079, roughly 26 percent above the budget book's own estimate and above even the high end of the pre-bid range [\[11\]](#src-11)[\[1\]](#src-1)[\[10\]](#src-10). Independent local reporting describes this as the largest single contract in MWRD's history [\[10\]](#src-10)[\[11\]](#src-11). (One naming quirk worth flagging: MWRD's own board observer coverage and agenda item title the June 2026 award a "sludge removal contract," which on its face reads like a narrower scope than "Battery E Activated Sludge Facility" — this document treats the two as the same underlying Contract 21-092-3P per the dollar figure and date match, but the discrepancy in framing was not independently reconciled against MWRD's own board minutes; see Open questions.)

## North Branch Pumping Station rehabilitation

Deep construction and hydraulic history for this station is doc [08](08-north-branch-pumping-station.md)'s subject; this entry exists only to log the current contract precisely, since doc 08 as last revised could not confirm the exact contract number, award date, or dollar value of its "2023–2026" rehabilitation phase. MWRD's 2026 capital budget resolves that gap directly: Contract 22-094-3D, "Rehabilitation of Elevated Deck and Boat Dock at North Branch Pumping Station, NSA," was awarded to Path Construction Company, Inc. in **March 2024** for an estimated construction cost of **$22,587,970**, with substantial completion targeted for **August 2027** [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[34\]](#src-34). The scope — replacing deteriorated exterior concrete decking, stairs, and the boat dock, plus flap-gate replacement at the adjacent Dropshaft DS-91 connecting structure — matches the contractor's own February 2026 project profile describing roughly 80 percent of exterior wall repairs complete and the old boat dock fully demolished by that date [\[12\]](#src-12). This is a separate, later contract from the 2018 Contract 16-079-3D ($4,613,450) that repaired the station's storm-pump-eroded concrete columns, which doc 08 already documents in full [\[1\]](#src-1).

## Thornton Reservoir odor control

The July 2026 storm cycle that filled Thornton Reservoir to a record 94 percent of capacity — covered in construction and hydrological detail in doc [10](10-tarp-reservoirs.md) — produced a parallel, still-unfolding capital-projects story: a slowly draining reservoir of raw combined sewage generating hydrogen-sulfide odor complaints severe enough that MWRD's existing aerator-and-sprayer systems were acknowledged by the District itself to only partially work at such fill levels [\[13\]](#src-13). MWRD's response, announced in mid-July 2026, was two new projects — a carbon-filtration system and a misting system — together budgeted at roughly $5 million and due for completion by the end of December 2026, bringing the reservoir's decade-long cumulative odor-mitigation spending to about $8.6 million [\[13\]](#src-13)[\[14\]](#src-14). These sit alongside the reservoir's existing, already-built odor infrastructure: a 2017 surface-aeration system and a 2020 program combining new odor-control systems with decommissioning of the temporary Thornton Transitional Reservoir, plus a $3,064,500 odor-control-system contract (17-273-4P) awarded in 2024 with an October 2024 completion listed in MWRD's 2026 budget book as already finished [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[13\]](#src-13).

## Stickney WRP — digesters, gas piping, and boilers

Stickney's 2009 Master Plan (12 projects, $993.6 million, covered in doc [11](11-stickney-wrp.md)) is now well into its execution phase, and MWRD's 2026 capital budget shows two large, currently active Stickney contracts squarely within that plan's solids-and-energy scope. **Digester Rehabilitation and Gas Piping Replacement, Phase II** (Contract 18-148-3P, $53,151,662, contractor IHC Construction Companies, LLC, awarded November 2023, substantial completion March 2030) replaces digester gas piping that recent inspection found extensively fouled with iron-sulfide buildup, sized to carry the additional gas volume generated since Stickney's original Imhoff tanks were converted to primary settling tanks; the project is explicitly sequenced to allow a later, separate contract to install a final gas-mixing system inside the digesters without draining them [\[1\]](#src-1). **Boilers 3, 4, and 5 and MCC Replacement** (Contract 19-155-3MR, $21,979,424, contractor Path Construction Co., awarded May 2023, substantial completion June 2027) replaces boilers that MWRD's own project justification describes as "at the end of their useful lives" with new units capable of co-firing digester gas and natural gas — maximizing use of the plant's own biogas rather than flaring or venting the surplus, plus a new deaerator for full system redundancy [\[1\]](#src-1). MWRD's budget narrative separately notes complementary work already underway: mechanical mixers installed in Aeration Batteries A, C, and D (with Battery B in progress) to support Stickney's now-successful biological phosphorus removal, and a pilot combined-heat-and-power (CHP) biogas project begun in 2024 at Egan WRP whose findings MWRD is evaluating for wider application, including potentially at Stickney [\[1\]](#src-1).

## Calumet WRP — an unlikely driver: the CTA Red Line extension

The Calumet Master Plan (2009, $507.16 million, ten projects, covered in doc [12](12-calumet-obrien-wrp.md)) has one active-in-2026 project with an origin story worth flagging on its own terms. The **Rebuild of Dewatering Facility, CWRP** (Contract 24-268-3P, $65,000,000, awarded January 2026, substantial completion August 2028) restores an existing centrifuge building to mechanically dewater digested sludge — but MWRD's own project justification states plainly that the driver is the Chicago Transit Authority's Red Line extension, which will make the plant's existing practice of trucking digested sludge from lagoons to drying beds "no longer practical" once the transit project is built. A companion contract, **Install Pipe Casing and Utilities Under Railroad Tracks to Dewatering Facility, CWRP** (Contract 24-269-3P, $13,200,000, awarded May 2026, substantial completion December 2027) physically threads new utility infrastructure under the same rail corridor [\[1\]](#src-1). Separately, **TARP Mechanical Equipment Improvements, CWRP** (Contract 26-822-31, $25,500,000, awarded January 2026, substantial completion December 2028) rehabilitates the original-to-1985 suction and discharge valves on the Calumet Tunnel and Reservoir Plan's main sewage pumps, whose leaking seals currently require dewatering the entire pump house to service individual units, plus unreliable medium-voltage-drive auxiliary chillers [\[1\]](#src-1).

## Collection Asset Management Plan (CAMP) — interceptor rehabilitation, systemwide

Doc [07](07-mwrd-interceptors-pumping-stations.md) already covers CAMP's 2013 origin as the successor to the 1991 Interceptor Inspection and Rehabilitation Program (IIRP); MWRD's 2026 capital budget shows the program is not a single line item but a continuously rolling set of individually contracted interceptor-segment rehabilitations, several running simultaneously. Contracts under construction or newly awarded as of 2026 include: **North Shore 1 Rehabilitation, NSA** (10-047-3S, $45,424,000, awarded January 2024); **39th Street Conduit Rehabilitation, Phase II, SSA** (01-103-AS, $41,109,000, awarded October 2024); **Salt Creek Intercepting Sewer No. 3 Rehabilitation, SSA** (20-161-3S, $20,079,000, awarded November 2024); **Upper Des Plaines Intercepting Sewer 14B Rehabilitation, NSA** (06-360-3SR, $36,328,000, awarded June 2023); and **Upper Des Plaines Intercepting Sewer 11D Rehabilitation, NSA** (12-369-3S, $17,000,000, budgeted for February 2026 award) — plus the three-phase **Kirie–Egan Solids Pipeline Rehabilitation** (Sections 1–3, contracts 23-416-2S/23-417-3S/23-418-3S, roughly $50 million combined across the three sections, already flagged in doc [13](13-suburban-wrps.md)'s upgrade timeline for Sections 1–2) [\[1\]](#src-1). Further interceptor rehabilitation contracts are already queued in MWRD's "Projects Under Development" list for 2027–2030 award, including Calumet Intercepting Sewer Nos. 2, 3, 4, 10, and 13; West Side Intercepting Sewer Nos. 2 and 3-D; Southwest Side Intercepting Sewer Nos. 15, 16, and 17B; Harms Road Intercepting Sewer Extension No. 1; and Lemont Intercepting Sewer No. 4 — indicating CAMP-driven interceptor work will remain a multi-hundred-million-dollar, decade-long program well past this document's 2026 snapshot date; MWRD's separately published 2026 Major Contracts pamphlet — a rolling list of contracts tentatively scheduled for advertisement that year — independently corroborates several of these same CAMP contract numbers, values, and target award months [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[9\]](#src-9).

## Kirie, Egan, and the suburban WRPs

Doc [13](13-suburban-wrps.md) already covers Kirie's February 5, 2026 board-approved $27 million contract with the Sollitt/Broadway Joint Venture for electrical-distribution and phosphorus-removal improvements; MWRD's 2026 budget book itemizes the phosphorus-removal component of that same scope as Contract 23-379-3E at an estimated $23,483,000 [\[15\]](#src-15)[\[1\]](#src-1). The 2026 budget's phosphorus-compliance table shows this Kirie work sitting inside a coordinated, systemwide phosphorus-removal capital push spanning five WRPs and running through 2030: Kirie's chemical-addition backup system (19-375-3P, $5.4M, awarded 2025) and electrical/phosphorus contract (2026); O'Brien's Battery E (2026) and prior Battery D modifications; and a not-yet-awarded $30 million phosphorus-removal project at Egan WRP (19-415-3P) slated for 2028 — all driven by NPDES permits phasing in a 1.0 mg/L monthly-average and, later, 0.5 mg/L annual-geometric-mean total-phosphorus limit across the District's plants [\[1\]](#src-1).

## City DWM — water main and sewer main replacement, current pace

Beyond the systemwide figures already in doc [05](05-water-pumping-distribution.md) and doc [06](06-city-sewer-network.md), DWM's own 2026 project list — a street-by-street guide published for residents — itemizes **75 water main replacement projects** and **47 sewer main replacement project areas** scheduled for calendar year 2026, spanning neighborhoods from the North Side (W. Dickens Ave., N. Artesian Ave.) to the South and Southwest Sides (S. Central Park Ave., W. 66th St.) [\[16\]](#src-16). The City's 2025–2029 Capital Improvement Program (a newer edition than the 2022–2026 CIP already cited elsewhere in this archive) puts the five-year **Water System** allocation at **$3.7 billion** total — $1.7 billion from bond funds, $1.6 billion from state and federal loans/grants, and $322.8 million from water fees — broken into a $2.3 billion **Water Distribution Program** (63 percent of the total, targeting roughly 12 miles of new water main installed annually, integrated with lead-line and meter replacement), $482.9 million for the Jardine and Sawyer purification plants, $356.7 million for water engineering, $280.5 million for new meters, and $231.5 million for pumping-station operations [\[17\]](#src-17).

## City DWM — lead service line replacement, current status

Doc [05](05-water-pumping-distribution.md) already covers the core numbers behind Chicago's lead-line problem and the "2076–77" completion-date controversy; this entry updates the funding and pace picture into 2026. The Water Distribution Program's stated aim is to reach the **10,000-replacement-per-year pace by 2027** that the U.S. EPA's proposed Lead and Copper Rule Improvements sets as the relevant benchmark for a system of Chicago's size — a pace the City had not yet reached as of 2025, when roughly 8,000 lines were replaced [\[17\]](#src-17)[\[19\]](#src-19). EPA's WIFIA program lists a **$336 million loan invitation** specifically for Chicago's combined water-main-and-lead-service-line program [\[20\]](#src-20); separately, a March 2026 announcement confirmed the related, larger Chicago–Grand Prairie Water Commission financing (below) closed a $610 million WIFIA loan of its own — a different program serving the southwest suburbs rather than City lead-line replacement, and not to be confused with the $336 million Chicago-specific figure [\[24\]](#src-24)[\[20\]](#src-20). In January 2026 the City announced its first four Lead Service Line Replacement **Small Business Initiative (SBI)** contracts, together worth just over $1.67 million, awarded to three small, minority- and women-owned local construction firms — a modest dollar figure relative to the program's multi-hundred-million-dollar scale, but notable as the City's first structured attempt to route lead-line replacement work through smaller local contractors rather than only its largest incumbent contractors [\[21\]](#src-21).

## City DWM — Central Park Pumping Station and the steam-to-electric queue

Doc [05](05-water-pumping-distribution.md) documents the Central Park conversion's roughly $115 million estimated cost and could not identify the fourth station (beyond Central Park and Springfield Avenue) slated for steam-to-electric conversion. The City's 2025–2029 Capital Improvement Program resolves that gap: DWM's pumping-station-conversion program is Central Park (project #3343, essentially complete), **Western Avenue** (project #3342, design 2024–2025 at $2,671,876, construction 2027–2030 at a further $149,000,000, for a $151,671,876 total), and **Mayfair** (project #3258, design 2028–2030, construction 2030–2034, $107,874,000 total) — three stations beyond Springfield Avenue (already converted, per doc 05), one already largely built, one about to begin construction, and one not starting design work until the end of this decade [\[17\]](#src-17). Central Park itself carries a project-book total of **$113,579,827** across its full 2014–2025 design-and-construction life, funded through a mix of City bond issues and two Illinois EPA state-revolving-fund loans (0F57, $53,656,000; 0F60, $49,999,999) [\[17\]](#src-17). Contractor project pages for the conversion (Helm Group as constructor, Aldridge Electric handling electrical/ductbank coordination) describe the work — refurbishing five existing pumps "like new," installing five new variable-frequency drives, a new medium-voltage electrical distribution system, and a small unit substation, all while keeping the century-old station operational throughout — in language consistent with a substantially finished project, though neither contractor page states an exact completion date, and this document did not independently confirm final commissioning against a DWM press release [\[22\]](#src-22)[\[23\]](#src-23). A separate, smaller **Cermak Pumping Station electrical improvements** project (design complete 2024, $3,903,405; construction 2026–2028, $29,000,000, state-IEPA funded) is also underway but is described in the City's own CIP book as electrical upgrades rather than a full steam-to-electric conversion [\[17\]](#src-17).

## Chicago–Grand Prairie Water Commission pipeline

This project — new construction rather than rehabilitation — will bring Lake Michigan water to six southwest-suburban communities (Joliet, Crest Hill, Channahon, Minooka, Shorewood, and Romeoville) currently reliant on a declining deep sandstone aquifer, replacing groundwater with the same Lake Michigan source Chicago itself draws from [\[24\]](#src-24)[\[25\]](#src-25). Sources vary on the project's exact scale — reported variously as a 62-mile or 65-mile transmission network, and as a $1.24 billion project (implied by a $610 million WIFIA loan reported as 49 percent of total funding) or a $1.45 billion project in Engineering News-Record's framing — a discrepancy this document presents rather than resolves [\[24\]](#src-24)[\[25\]](#src-25). Regional construction broke ground in 2024–2025; the U.S. EPA closed the Grand Prairie Water Commission's $610 million WIFIA loan in March 2026, and the first Chicago-side work package (the AWSP-01-01 Tunnel Extension) is scheduled for completion by the end of 2026, with regional member-community construction beginning in 2026 and the whole system targeted for completion around mid-2030 [\[24\]](#src-24)[\[25\]](#src-25).

On Chicago's side, the connection point is the **Southwest Pumping Station** (8422 S. Kedvale Ave.) already noted in doc [05](05-water-pumping-distribution.md), and the City's own capital-budget line item for its piece of the project — **Southwest Pumping Station Tunnel Connection** (project #43810) — is budgeted at **$67,690,073** across design (2022–2026, $2,690,073, City-funded) and construction (2025–2026, $65,000,000, split between City bonds and City pay-as-you-go funds), consistent with the City's own program narrative describing "the Southwest Pumping Station's tunnel connection for the Grand Prairie Water Commission" as one of the three defining projects in its 2025–2029, $231.5 million Pumping Station Operations Program [\[17\]](#src-17). Construction on the south side of Durkin Park, adjacent to the pumping station, is expected to run roughly two years starting in 2026, during which that section of the park will be closed while the rest remains open [\[24\]](#src-24).

## USACE — Brandon Road Interbasin Project

Doc [02](02-river-reversal-canals.md) already covers Brandon Road's purpose (a multi-layer Asian carp deterrent — electric barrier, acoustic deterrent, air-bubble curtain, flushing lock, and engineered channel — at the Brandon Road Lock & Dam in Joliet) and its roughly $1.2 billion estimated total cost; this entry updates construction progress into 2026. The project's first construction increment carries an estimated $274 million in federal funding plus $114 million in state funding (Illinois and Michigan) [\[26\]](#src-26). Site preparation and channel excavation — the first construction contract — was completed in July 2025 [\[27\]](#src-27). USACE awarded the second construction contract, valued at **$113.8 million**, to J.F. Brennan Company, Inc. of La Crosse, Wisconsin, on **April 11, 2026**, covering the project's "leading-edge deterrents": an automated barge-clearing system, a bubble-curtain deterrent, an acoustic deterrent, leading-edge support facilities, and an upstream boat ramp [\[27\]](#src-27). Later phases — still to be contracted — will add a flushing lock, walls, and the electric-barrier deterrent system; the Corps has described its approach as strategically layering these defenses across three separate construction increments rather than building all deterrent types simultaneously [\[27\]](#src-27)[\[28\]](#src-28). Congressional appropriators secured $28 million in FY2026 federal funding for Brandon Road in a January 2026 spending package — described by Illinois's senators as "enough to fully fund the project for FY26" [\[31\]](#src-31).

## USACE — Chicago Harbor Lock

Doc [02](02-river-reversal-canals.md) covers the Chicago Harbor Lock's basic facts (built 1936–1938, transferred to USACE operation in 1984, one of the two busiest locks in the United States) and flags that a dedicated cost figure for its rehabilitation history was hard to locate; this entry adds what surfaced in 2026-dated reporting. The lock has now undergone **three consecutive winters** of major structural maintenance — off-season work each year from roughly mid-November through mid-April, when the lock is closed to navigation — described by USACE's Chicago District Commander as "significant investments" rather than routine fixes, intended to keep the 85-plus-year-old structure "reliable and operable for the next 50 years" [\[29\]](#src-29)[\[30\]](#src-30). The most recent phase (November 16, 2025 – April 14, 2026) placed new concrete and anchors into the chamber floor, reinforced the structure around the gates, replaced the gates' hydraulic operating systems with more efficient modern units, and continued replacing the lock's main generator, with pavement repairs deferred to the 2026–2027 off-season [\[29\]](#src-29)[\[30\]](#src-30). This continues a longer rehabilitation history that includes a 2010–2011 sector-gate replacement (already in doc 02) and a 2020–2021 North Pier Stabilization project ($7 million, contractor Superior Construction) that drove new sheet-pile walls 50 feet deep, 100 anchored tie rods, and widened the pier from 35 to 70 feet after demolishing a 1938 utility tunnel [\[32\]](#src-32).

The lock's near-term maintenance funding is, as of this document's snapshot date, publicly contested. President Trump's FY2026 budget request proposed cutting the lock's operations-and-maintenance appropriation by more than 90 percent — from the roughly $3.85 million USACE itself had requested to less than $300,000 — prompting Illinois Senators Dick Durbin and Tammy Duckworth to file an amendment seeking $16.6 million (matching FY2024 levels) [\[33\]](#src-33). USACE indicated it could cover the immediate FY2026 shortfall by reallocating funds originally earmarked for electrical rehabilitation work and drawing on roughly $1 million in leftover FY2025 operating funds, but warned this approach "creates risks in future years," since later budgets are calculated from an escalating five-year average that a severely cut FY2026 baseline would depress [\[33\]](#src-33). Whether Congress's final FY2026 appropriation restored full funding, split the difference, or left the cut in place was not resolved in the sources reviewed for this document; see Open questions.

- **The precise scope boundary between the "Kirie–Egan Solids Pipeline Rehabilitation" three-section program and the separately reported "$27 million" Kirie electrical/phosphorus contract** (doc 13's figure) versus the 2026 budget book's $23,483,000 Contract 23-379-3E — these may be the same award reported with/without a contingency allowance, or genuinely different figures; not independently reconciled here.
- **A dedicated MWRD or USACE cost figure for the Chicago Harbor Lock's 2023–2026 three-winter rehabilitation program** was not located; only the North Pier Stabilization ($7M, 2020–2021) and the O&M-budget dispute figures were confirmed.
