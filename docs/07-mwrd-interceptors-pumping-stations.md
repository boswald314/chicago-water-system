# MWRD Intercepting Sewers & Sewage Pumping Stations

## Role in the system

The Metropolitan Water Reclamation District of Greater Chicago (MWRD, originally the Sanitary District of Chicago, chartered May 29, 1889) does not own the pipes under most Chicago-area streets. Those "local sewers" — combined sewers carrying both sanitary sewage and stormwater in a single pipe — are owned and maintained by the City of Chicago and 128 suburban municipalities, and they drain by gravity into a second, larger tier of pipe that the District does own: roughly 560 miles of **intercepting sewers and force mains**, ranging from 6 inches to 27 feet in diameter, fed by about 10,000 local-sewer connection points across an 882-square-mile service area [5][2]. Functionally, the intercepting sewers are "the superhighway of sewers" — they gather what the municipal system collects and convey it, largely by gravity along waterway corridors and beneath major streets, to one of the MWRD's seven water reclamation plants (WRPs) for treatment [2][5].

In dry weather this system works by gravity alone. In wet weather, when combined flow exceeds what the intercepting sewers and WRPs can carry, two backstops exist. First, since the 1970s–2000s, excess flow is diverted into the **Tunnel and Reservoir Plan (TARP)** — deep rock tunnels 150–300 feet underground that store combined sewage and stormwater until treatment capacity is available, then release it to three surface reservoirs and ultimately to the WRPs via TARP's own pumping stations [3][1]. Second, at several points where the interceptor system itself needs to lift flow to a higher elevation — or where a CSO (combined sewer overflow) has to be discharged to a waterway because both the interceptors and TARP are full — the District operates dedicated **sewage pumping stations**. These are a distinct engineering layer from TARP's tunnel-dewatering pumps, built decades earlier to solve a simpler problem: intercepting sewers running along the lakefront and river branches are often *below* the elevation of the receiving river, so gravity cannot finish the job.

Downstream, treated effluent from the WRPs (Stickney, Calumet, O'Brien/North Side, and four smaller plants) discharges to the Chicago Area Waterway System — the North Shore Channel, North Branch and main stem of the Chicago River, the Chicago Sanitary and Ship Canal, the Cal-Sag Channel, and the Calumet River — which flows away from Lake Michigan toward the Des Plaines and Illinois Rivers, a legacy of the 1900 reversal of the Chicago River [2][13]. The intercepting-sewer and pumping-station network described here is thus the connective tissue between ~130 municipal sewer systems upstream and the WRPs/TARP downstream.

## Original construction

**Intercepting sewers (1906 onward).** Before the Sanitary District built intercepting sewers, local sewers drained straight into the Chicago River and Lake Michigan. Reversing the Chicago River's flow via the Chicago Sanitary and Ship Canal (completed January 2, 1900, chief engineer Isham Randolph) solved the problem for sewage that reached the South Branch, but it did nothing for sewage that drained directly into the lake along the northern lakefront [15][2]. The District's response, beginning in 1906, was to build a dedicated network of intercepting sewers — masonry, brick, cast-in-place concrete (pre-1950) and later precast concrete pipe, up to 27 feet in diameter — running along the lakefront and river corridors to catch that flow and redirect it inland [5]. The first completed segment, built 1900–1907 along the lakefront, fed a new pumping station at Lawrence Avenue that lifted the intercepted sewage into the North Branch of the Chicago River instead of the lake [15][2]. In parallel, the District cut the 7.7-mile North Shore Channel from Lake Michigan at Wilmette south to the North Branch (construction began September 1907, channel completed November 1910) specifically to bring diluting lake water down to flush the newly intercepted sewage away from the city, with the Wilmette Pumping Station (built 1908–1910, lock added 1912) serving as the controlling structure regulating that lake diversion [19][6].

**Calumet system (1920s).** On the South Side, the District built the Calumet Sewage Treatment Plant, completed 1922 — the Sanitary District's first treatment plant — together with the Cal-Sag Channel (operational 1922), which reversed the Calumet and Little Calumet Rivers away from Lake Michigan on the same principle as the main canal [2]. The Calumet intercepting sewer system needed its own pumping infrastructure to lift flow to the treatment plant; construction of the 95th Street Pumping Station ran April–June 1924, and it went into service in 1925, powered by motor-generator sets that converted incoming AC grid power to DC for variable-speed pump control, with coal delivered by a dedicated Pennsylvania Railroad spur for its boilers [18]. A siphon carrying the interceptor under the Calumet River, with its own maintenance headhouse, was completed in June 1923 as part of the same push [18].

**North Branch (Lawrence Avenue) Pumping Station rebuild (1928–30).** The original 1907 lakefront pumping station was superseded by a larger structure at 4860 N. Francisco Avenue at Lawrence Avenue, built by the Sanitary District of Chicago beginning in 1928, completed in 1929, and placed in service in 1930 [15]. In its current configuration the station carries five 300-cfs storm discharge pumps that lift excess combined sewage into the North Branch during rain events [10].

**Racine Avenue Pumping Station (1939).** RAPS, on the South Side near Pershing Road, was "originally constructed in 1939" according to the MWRD's own 2015 press release on the building [11]. The station houses the main sewage pumps that convey South Side wastewater onward to the (later-built) Stickney Water Reclamation Plant, plus a coarse-screen building that removes trash from the flow before it reaches the pumps [11]. A secondary source (South Side Weekly) separately describes a "1964 Racine Avenue Pumping Station on Pershing" that "replaced an older pumping station with less capacity," serving a 36-square-mile drainage area from Western Avenue to the lakefront and from 87th Street to Taylor Street [20] — see Open Questions for how this squares with the 1939 date. (A local-history blog cataloguing MWRD facility numbers [17] lists a "Racine Ave" station as District facility #142 but does not itself state a construction date.)

**TARP (1972 planning; 1975–2006 tunnels).** By the 1960s the combined-sewer system was overflowing more than 100 days a year and causing chronic street and basement flooding. In 1967 the MWRD, the State of Illinois, Cook County, and the City of Chicago formed a Flood Control Coordinating Committee that evaluated (depending on the source) "50 alternatives" [1] or "23 alternative proposals" [12] before formally adopting the Tunnel and Reservoir Plan on October 20, 1972, at an original estimated cost of $1.223 billion [12][1]. Tunnel construction began in 1975. Newly developed tunnel-boring machines — rather than traditional blasting — were used to bore through Silurian dolomite bedrock 150 to 300 feet down (Wikipedia cites a maximum depth "up to 350 feet" for some segments), wide enough in places for tunnels 33 feet in diameter; cracks in the surrounding rock were sealed with grout curtains to protect groundwater, and the tunnels were lined in concrete [3][1][14]. Metropolitan Sanitary District figures associated with the program in contemporaneous (1979) press coverage included MSD president Nicholas Melas, deputy chief engineer Frank Dalton, and research director Dr. Cecil Lue-Hing [23]. The Upper Des Plaines (O'Hare) segment went online first, in 1981; the full 110-mile Phase I tunnel network was completed and operational by 2006 [1][7]. A 1979 magazine investigation pegged the ultimate program cost (tunnels, reservoirs, treatment-plant upgrades, suburban connections, interest, and engineering) at roughly $11 billion in contemporary dollars — a figure the MWRD's own later accounting did not reach, as actual completed-to-date construction costs are documented at $3.858 billion as of December 31, 2025 [23][7].

**Mainstream and Calumet TARP pumping stations.** The MWRD's December 2017 TARP Status Report itemizes the original construction contracts for both major dewatering pumping stations by contract number and awarded price (bid-year dollars, not adjusted for inflation) [8]:
- Mainstream Pumping Station Part I — contract 73-162-AH, joint venture of P. Kiewit & Sons, J.F. Shea Co., and Kenny Construction Co. — **$168,811,300**
- Mainstream Pumping Station Part II — contract 73-162-BH, joint venture of Healy, Ball, and Grow Tunneling Corp. — **$64,755,000**
- Mainstream Pumping Station Part III — contract 73-162-CH, joint venture of Morrison-Knudsen and Paschen Contractors — **$28,012,400**
(Mainstream PS construction thus totaled roughly **$261.6 million** in three contracts.) The pumps themselves were placed in service in May 1985, per a 2021 MWRD rehabilitation-contract description [9]. On the South Side, the Calumet Tunnel and Pump Station was built under a single contract, 74-206-2H, awarded to a joint venture of S.A. Healy Co. and Atlas-Gest International Inc., for **$54,841,825** [8]. That station's pump rooms sit roughly 365 feet below grade in Chicago's Riverdale neighborhood — described by local reporting as the lowest inhabitable point within Chicago city limits [21].

## Upgrades & modernization timeline

- **1900** — Chicago Sanitary and Ship Canal completed; Chicago River reversed away from Lake Michigan [2][15].
- **1906** — Construction begins on the MWRD intercepting sewer system [5].
- **1900–1907** — Lakefront intercepting sewers built; original Lawrence Avenue (North Branch) Pumping Station completed 1907 [15][2].
- **1907–1910** — North Shore Channel excavated (begun Sept. 1907, completed Nov. 1910); Wilmette Pumping Station built 1908–1910 as the channel's lake-diversion controlling structure [19][6].
- **1912** — Lock completed under the Sheridan Road bridge at Wilmette [19].
- **1922** — Calumet Sewage Treatment Plant completed; Cal-Sag Channel operational, reversing the Calumet River system [2].
- **1923–1925** — Calumet River siphon headhouse completed (June 1923); 95th Street Pumping Station built (Apr.–Jun. 1924) and placed in service (1925) [18].
- **1928–1930** — North Branch (Lawrence Avenue) Pumping Station rebuilt on a larger scale; completed 1929, in service 1930 [15].
- **1930** — Eastern mitre gates at Wilmette PS replaced with a slide gate [19].
- **1939** — Racine Avenue Pumping Station originally constructed [11].
- **1959** — Western mitre gates at Wilmette PS replaced with a roller lift gate [19].
- **1964** — A "Racine Avenue Pumping Station on Pershing," described in a secondary source as replacing an older, smaller-capacity station, begins serving a 36-square-mile South Side drainage area [20].
- **1972 (Oct. 20)** — TARP formally adopted after committee review of dozens of flood/pollution-control alternatives [1][12].
- **1975** — TARP tunnel-boring construction begins [1].
- **1979–1980** — Instream aeration stations placed in service at Devon Avenue (North Shore Channel, 1979) and Webster Avenue (North Branch, 1980) [6].
- **1981** — Upper Des Plaines (O'Hare) TARP tunnel system goes online, the first TARP segment in operation [1].
- **1985 (May)** — Mainstream Pumping Station's original pumps placed in service [9].
- **1986 (Oct. 3)** — A severe thunderstorm over-pressurized the newly completed tunnel system, producing geysers reported over 65 feet high near Wilmette and the Bahá'í Temple and a roughly 30-foot geyser downtown at Jefferson and Monroe that trapped a motorist in her flooding car; the MWRD subsequently installed watertight bulkheads throughout the system to prevent recurrence [14].
- **1991** — MWRD adopts the Interceptor Inspection and Rehabilitation Program (IIRP) for systematic CCTV/visual inspection and rehab of aging interceptor sewers; by the time of the District's 2022 O&M manual, roughly 45 miles had been rehabilitated under the program with ~30 more miles identified for future work [28].
- **1994** — Sidestream elevated pool aeration (SEPA) stations begin operating on the Cal-Sag Channel and the Little Calumet/Calumet Rivers to relieve dissolved-oxygen sags [6].
- **1996** — MWRD-wide computerized Maintenance Management System (MMS) installed [5]; Racine Avenue PS roof replaced with a 10-year-warranty tar roof [11].
- **1998** — Gloria Alitto Majewski Reservoir (Elk Grove Village), designed and built by the U.S. Army Corps of Engineers, completed — the first TARP Phase II/CUP reservoir [7].
- **2000** — Five temporary pumps (aggregate 50 cfs) installed at Wilmette PS after the original large pumps failed [6].
- **2002** — One original Wilmette PS pump rehabilitated for service, the temporary pumps having proven insufficient [6]; NPDES sampling program for North Branch PS discharges begins March 1, 2002 [5].
- **2003–2013** — Thornton Transitional Reservoir completed (March 2003) in the Thornton Quarry's West Lobe for interim flood relief; permanent Thornton Reservoir excavation in the North Lobe completed by a mining partner in 2013 [7].
- **2006** — TARP Phase I's full 110-mile tunnel network completed and operational [7][1].
- **2012** — $17.5 million Wilmette PS renovation: single 30-ft roller gate replaced with three smaller hydraulic lift gates for finer backflow control; two of the four original pump tunnels repurposed for bidirectional flow [19].
- **2013** — MWRD replaces IIRP with the Collection Asset Management Plan (CAMP), a broader NASSCO-standards-based, risk-scored framework covering intercepting sewers, TARP structures, tide gates, and other passive/active District assets; CAMP remains the current program as of the District's 2026 O&M manual [28][5].
- **2015 (Jan.)** — Green roof completed atop the Racine Avenue Pumping Station (33,000-sf building; ~4,500 sf vegetated) [11][27].
- **2015 (late)** — Thornton Composite (permanent) Reservoir becomes operational, providing 4.8 billion gallons for the TARP portion of storage (7.9 BG including the separate 3.1 BG Thorn Creek overbank-flood-relief portion) [7].
- **2016** — Calumet TARP Pump Station receives the Steel Tank Institute/Steel Plate Fabricators Association's Steel Water Pipe Fabrication Award for a custom-fabricated steel fitting installed alongside larger replacement pumps.
- **2017** — McCook Reservoir Stage 1 (3.5 BG) placed in operation [7]; 125th Street Pumping Station's 100-hp steam boilers replaced with high-efficiency fire-tube units as part of an MWRD energy-retrofit program.
- **2018 (Aug.)** — $4,613,450 contract (16-079-3D) awarded to Path Construction Co. to repair North Branch PS concrete columns, eroded up to 50% by decades of high-velocity discharge from its five 300-cfs pumps [10][22].
- **2021 (Apr.)** — $23,380,000 contract (18-144-3M) awarded to IHC Construction Companies to overhaul Mainstream TARP Pumps 1, 3, and 5 (in continuous service since 1985), including motors, discharge cone valves, and control upgrades [9].
- **2022 (Sept.)** — Thornton Transitional Reservoir decommissioned following installation of the Thorn Creek Diversion Tunnel's east plug; Thorn Creek Overflow Tunnel connected into the permanent Thornton Reservoir [7].
- **2025 (Dec. 31)** — McCook Reservoir Stage 2 approximately 72% excavated, targeted operational 2032; cumulative TARP/CUP program cost documented at $3.858 billion [7].
- **2026 (Jul. 2–4)** — Record-setting regional rainfall (locally up to ~8.5 inches over 48 hours) drove McCook Reservoir to full capacity (100%, more than 3.6 billion gallons — reported as low as 96–98% at other points that weekend) — its sixth time reaching capacity in 2026 alone, versus five times in the preceding five years combined — and Thornton Reservoir to 94% full (more than 7.4 billion gallons), the fullest it had been since opening in 2015 [25][30].
- **2026 (Jul. 10)** — MWRD announces $5 million in odor-mitigation projects at Thornton Reservoir (carbon-filtration system on nearby shafts; perimeter mist/odor-control system), targeted for completion by year-end 2026, in response to resident odor complaints following the early-July storms [29].

## Current operations

The MWRD today operates 23 sewage pumping stations alongside seven water reclamation plants, moving an average of roughly 1.2–1.5 billion gallons of wastewater per day through a combined treatment capacity exceeding 2.0–2.4 billion gallons per day [5][2]. The 560-mile intercepting sewer network is inspected and maintained jointly by the District's Maintenance & Operations and Engineering Departments; under the Collection Asset Management Plan (CAMP) — which replaced the 1991 Interceptor Inspection and Rehabilitation Program (IIRP) in 2013 — segments are prioritized for CCTV inspection, lining, or replacement using NASSCO-standard, risk-based scoring, and the District tracks asset age by decade (roughly 37% of interceptor mileage was installed 1961–1980, versus 6.9% before 1920) [5][28].

Each of the five major CSO pumping stations — North Branch, Racine Avenue, 95th Street, 122nd Street, and 125th Street — is subject to NPDES permit sampling requirements: whenever a station's first storm pump activates, MWRD Industrial Waste Division staff are dispatched to pull composite and grab samples (BOD5/TOC, suspended solids, cadmium, hardness, pH, fecal coliform, depending on the receiving water) from the discharge for regulatory reporting [5]. All are unmanned, remotely alarmed facilities inspected monthly, with level sensors triggering pump operation from wet-well elevation, debris screens (some with automatic raking) protecting the pump intakes, and backup generators and dehumidification equipment on site [5].

On the TARP side, the system now consists of four connected tunnel systems (Mainstream, Calumet, Des Plaines, and Upper Des Plaines) feeding three reservoirs (Majewski, Thornton, and the still-expanding McCook) with 15.15 billion gallons of design storage, of which all but McCook Stage 2 (6.5 of the 10 billion gallons planned for McCook) is complete [7]. The Mainstream and Calumet TARP pumping stations are the only points where stored combined sewage is lifted back out of the tunnel/reservoir system to the WRPs (Stickney and Calumet, respectively) for treatment; MWRD's own 50th-anniversary retrospective states the full system captured more than 153 billion gallons of combined sewer overflow between 2017 and 2021 alone, and MWRD's TARP fact sheet claims over 1 trillion gallons captured since the tunnels first went into service [12][1]. Regionally, TARP's operation is credited with reducing the frequency of combined sewer overflow events from roughly 100 days per year before construction to about 50 days per year system-wide, with overflows in the Calumet system's service area "nearly eliminated" since the Thornton Reservoir came online in 2015 [1].

## Notable events & incidents

**October 3, 1986 — Deep Tunnel geyser event.** A heavy thunderstorm dumped several inches of rain in a short period over the newly built (and not yet fully interconnected) tunnel system. Water surged unevenly through the tunnels, producing geysers reported at over 65 feet near Wilmette and the Bahá'í Temple, and a roughly 30-foot geyser erupted downtown at Jefferson and Monroe streets, filling a woman's car with water and trapping her inside. The MWRD subsequently installed a system of watertight bulkheads throughout TARP to prevent similar pressure events [14].

**2015–2016 — green infrastructure and manufacturing recognition.** The Racine Avenue Pumping Station's green-roof retrofit (2015) and the Calumet TARP Pump Station's award-winning custom steel pump fitting (2016) both reflect a period of MWRD investment in retrofitting Depression- and Cold War-era pumping infrastructure with modern equipment and sustainability features [11][27].

**2018–2021 — structural rehabilitation of the two oldest major pumping stations.** Both the ~1930 North Branch Pumping Station and the ~1985 Mainstream TARP pumps required major rehabilitation contracts within a few years of each other — the former because decades of 300-cfs discharge had eroded structural concrete columns by up to 50%, the latter because the original 1985 pump/motor sets were nearing the end of their design life [10][9].

**July 2–4, 2026 — record rainfall and near-capacity reservoirs.** A multi-day rain event over the Independence Day weekend brought locally heavy rainfall — up to roughly 8.5 inches in Aurora and nearly 8 inches in Naperville over 48 hours, well above the Chicago area's 3.7-inch average for the entire month of July — pushing the McCook Reservoir to full capacity (100%, more than 3.6 billion gallons; different outlets reporting at different points over the weekend put it as low as 96–98% full) — its sixth time reaching capacity in 2026 alone, versus five times in the preceding five years combined — and the Thornton Reservoir to about 94% full (more than 7.4 billion gallons), the fullest it had been since opening in 2015. MWRD officials acknowledged that if reservoirs filled completely, untreated water might have to be released to Lake Michigan or allowed to overflow into local waterways, risking beach closures; as of the storm's immediate aftermath, officials reported the system had contained the rainfall without requiring such releases. Nearby South Holland and Thornton residents reported a persistent, strong odor from the reservoir during and after the storms; MWRD subsequently announced $5 million in odor-mitigation projects (a carbon-filtration system on nearby shafts and a perimeter mist/odor-control system), expected complete by the end of 2026 [25][30][29][26].

## Open questions

- **Named "sub-interceptors."** This brief was framed around a North Side/North Branch/Northwest Side/West Side/Southwest/Calumet naming scheme for individual interceptor sewers. None of the sources consulted — including MWRD's own current O&M manual and CSO/UAA reports — refer to the system by those specific proper names with individual mileage or diameter breakdowns; the District's public materials describe the interceptor network only as a single 560-mile system, or by "service basin" (North/Central/South) and by the pumping stations each segment feeds. MWRD's engineering atlases likely contain interceptor-by-interceptor detail, but they were not accessible through the sources reviewed for this brief, and some District GIS/mapping materials are described elsewhere as FOIA-restricted.
- **Racine Avenue Pumping Station construction date.** MWRD's own January 2015 press release states RAPS "was originally constructed in 1939." A secondary source (South Side Weekly) instead describes a "1964 Racine Avenue Pumping Station" that "replaced an older pumping station with less capacity" and serves the 36-square-mile drainage area cited above. (Note: a local-history blog once cited alongside South Side Weekly for this claim [17] was re-checked directly and does not itself state 1964 or any RAPS construction date — it only lists MWRD's internal facility number for "Racine Ave" as #142 — so it has been removed as a supporting citation for the 1964 date.) It is unresolved from the sources reviewed whether 1964 represents a full replacement of the 1939 structure, a major expansion of it, or a misattribution in secondary reporting; this brief reports both dates.
- **122nd Street Pumping Station construction date** could not be found in any source reviewed (95th Street's 1924–25 date and 125th Street's 2017 retrofit were documented, but not 122nd Street's original build year).
- **Wilmette Pumping Station, 1959–2000.** Sources document gate/pump changes in 1930, 1959, 2000, 2002, and 2012, but not what (if anything) happened to the station's four original screw pumps between 1959 and their apparent failure some time before 2000.
- **HAER/HABS documentation.** No Historic American Engineering Record or Historic American Buildings Survey entry specific to any MWRD intercepting sewer or sewage pumping station was located. The one Chicago-area sewer-adjacent HAER record found (HAER IL-4, Lakeview Pumping Station) documents a *drinking-water* supply pumping station on the lakefront, unrelated to the sewage/TARP system covered here.
- **Engineer/contractor of record for the 1907 predecessor** to the North Branch (Lawrence Avenue) Pumping Station was not identified in any source reviewed.
- **Design engineer(s) of record for the original 1972 TARP plan.** Sources name Sanitary District figures associated with the program (Nicholas Melas, Frank Dalton, Dr. Cecil Lue-Hing) and note Black & Veatch's later involvement in specific TARP-related design work, but no single firm or engineer is documented as author of the original 1972 plan itself.
- ~~**Total interceptor mileage discrepancy.**~~ **Resolved.** The 554-mile/8-inch-to-27-foot figure is confirmed to come from an earlier edition of the same recurring document: MWRD's *Collection System O&M Manual*, dated March 31, 2022, states the interceptor system is "554 miles in length" ranging "from 8 inches to 27 feet in diameter" and serving "883.6 square miles" [28]. The District's 2026 edition of the same manual instead states 560 miles, 6 inches to 27 feet, and 882.1 square miles [5]. This is a genuine year-over-year revision within MWRD's own recurring manual (net +6 miles of interceptor added/reclassified, minimum pipe size redefined downward from 8 in. to 6 in., and service area reduced slightly, between the 2022 and 2026 editions), not a sourcing error; this brief uses the more current 2026 figures in the body text and cites the 2022 figures here for the record.
