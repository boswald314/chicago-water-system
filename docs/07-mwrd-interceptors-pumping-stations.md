# MWRD Intercepting Sewers & Sewage Pumping Stations

## Role in the system

The Metropolitan Water Reclamation District of Greater Chicago (MWRD, originally the Sanitary District of Chicago, chartered May 29, 1889) does not own the pipes under most Chicago-area streets. Those "local sewers" — combined sewers carrying both sanitary sewage and stormwater in a single pipe — are owned and maintained by the City of Chicago and 128 suburban municipalities, and they drain by gravity into a second, larger tier of pipe that the District does own: roughly 560 miles of **intercepting sewers and force mains**, ranging from 6 inches to 27 feet in diameter, fed by about 10,000 local-sewer connection points across an 882-square-mile service area [\[5\]](#src-5)[\[2\]](#src-2). Functionally, the intercepting sewers are "the superhighway of sewers" — they gather what the municipal system collects and convey it, largely by gravity along waterway corridors and beneath major streets, to one of the MWRD's seven water reclamation plants (WRPs) for treatment [\[2\]](#src-2)[\[5\]](#src-5).

In dry weather this system works by gravity alone. In wet weather, when combined flow exceeds what the intercepting sewers and WRPs can carry, two backstops exist. First, since the 1970s–2000s, excess flow is diverted into the **Tunnel and Reservoir Plan (TARP)** — deep rock tunnels 150–300 feet underground that store combined sewage and stormwater until treatment capacity is available, then release it to three surface reservoirs and ultimately to the WRPs via TARP's own pumping stations [\[3\]](#src-3)[\[1\]](#src-1). Second, at several points where the interceptor system itself needs to lift flow to a higher elevation — or where a CSO (combined sewer overflow) has to be discharged to a waterway because both the interceptors and TARP are full — the District operates dedicated **sewage pumping stations**. These are a distinct engineering layer from TARP's tunnel-dewatering pumps, built decades earlier to solve a simpler problem: intercepting sewers running along the lakefront and river branches are often *below* the elevation of the receiving river, so gravity cannot finish the job.

Downstream, treated effluent from the WRPs (Stickney, Calumet, O'Brien/North Side, and four smaller plants) discharges to the Chicago Area Waterway System — the North Shore Channel, North Branch and main stem of the Chicago River, the Chicago Sanitary and Ship Canal, the Cal-Sag Channel, and the Calumet River — which flows away from Lake Michigan toward the Des Plaines and Illinois Rivers, a legacy of the 1900 reversal of the Chicago River [\[2\]](#src-2)[\[13\]](#src-13). The intercepting-sewer and pumping-station network described here is thus the connective tissue between ~130 municipal sewer systems upstream and the WRPs/TARP downstream.

## Original construction

**Intercepting sewers (1906 onward).** Before the Sanitary District built intercepting sewers, local sewers drained straight into the Chicago River and Lake Michigan. Reversing the Chicago River's flow via the Chicago Sanitary and Ship Canal (construction completed 1898, opened January 1900; chief engineer Isham Randolph) solved the problem for sewage that reached the South Branch, but it did nothing for sewage that drained directly into the lake along the northern lakefront [\[15\]](#src-15)[\[2\]](#src-2). The District's response, beginning in 1906, was to build a dedicated network of intercepting sewers — masonry, brick, cast-in-place concrete (pre-1950) and later precast concrete pipe, up to 27 feet in diameter — running along the lakefront and river corridors to catch that flow and redirect it inland [\[5\]](#src-5). The first completed segment, built 1900–1907 along the lakefront, fed a predecessor pumping station at Lawrence Avenue (just east of Broadway) that lifted the intercepted sewage into the North Branch of the Chicago River instead of the lake [\[15\]](#src-15)[\[2\]](#src-2). Per the Sanitary District's own 1928 engineering report, that original Lawrence Avenue Sewage Pumping Station — with its crib ¼ mile offshore in Lake Michigan at the foot of Lawrence Avenue supplying dilution water — was actually built and initially owned by the *City of Chicago*, completed in 1908, and only transferred to Sanitary District operation on December 6, 1910; it drew a 6-sq-mi area and pumped 40–300 cfs via one screw flushing pump and three small centrifugal sewage pumps [\[35\]](#src-35). (A separate 1927 Sanitary District-commissioned engineering report locates this same predecessor station instead at "Lawrence and Racine Avenue" rather than Lawrence and Broadway — a minor discrepancy between two Sanitary District-linked primary sources that this brief has not been able to resolve further [\[37\]](#src-37).) In parallel, the District cut the 7.7-mile North Shore Channel from Lake Michigan at Wilmette south to the North Branch (construction began September 1907, channel completed November 1910) specifically to bring diluting lake water down to flush the newly intercepted sewage away from the city, with the Wilmette Pumping Station (built 1908–1910, lock added 1912) serving as the controlling structure regulating that lake diversion [\[19\]](#src-19)[\[6\]](#src-6). A separate, smaller Evanston Sewage Pumping Station (Elmwood/Orrington Ave. and Lake St., Evanston) was completed October 1920, six pumps, draining roughly 600 acres of Evanston's lakefront district into the North Shore Channel's 10-ft outfall sewer, at a cost of $505,900.95 [\[35\]](#src-35)[\[37\]](#src-37).

**Named sub-interceptors did exist historically, and MWRD still uses alphanumeric interceptor identifiers today.** The Sanitary District's 1928 *Engineering Works* report individually names and describes numerous discrete intercepting sewers with their own mileage, dimensions, service areas, and construction costs — among them the North Side Intercepting Sewer System (13.75 mi, built almost entirely in tunnel, $11,260,542.72 through Dec. 31, 1927, completed with the North Side Sewage Treatment Works by October 1928), the West Side Intercepting Sewer (58 sq mi design service area, three separate construction contracts totaling over $7 million), the Salt Creek Intercepting Sewer (serving 13 west-suburban communities), the Calumet Intercepting Sewer, the Broadview-Bellwood Outfall Sewer (13,600 ft, completed 1926, $458,727.23), and the (then-planned) Berkeley-Hillside Intercepting Sewer [\[35\]](#src-35). This naming convention persisted into modern engineering practice: 1976 EPA Environmental Impact Statements for TARP cite consulting-engineer reports referencing the "Southwest Side Intercepting Sewer 13A" (DeLeuw, Cather & Co., 1970) and the "Calumet Intercepting Sewer 18E, Extension A" (Harza Engineering Co., 1972) [\[38\]](#src-38); MWRD's own 2024 capital budget book lists an active construction project, "Calumet 18E Relief Connecting Structure and Sewer Work" (Contract 21-262-3S, Alsip, IL, contractor Rausch Infrastructure LLC, $2,863,408, awarded Dec. 2022, substantially complete June 2024), justified explicitly as relief for "Calumet Intercepting Sewer 18F ext. A, Calumet Intercepting Sewer 18F, Calumet Intercepting Sewer 18E, and the Palos Hills Pumping Station" [\[39\]](#src-39) — confirming both that MWRD still identifies interceptors by these alphanumeric designations internally and that a "Palos Hills Pumping Station" exists in the South/Calumet service basin beyond the five/six major CSO stations profiled in this brief's facilities table.

**Calumet system (1920s).** On the South Side, the District built the Calumet Sewage Treatment Plant, completed 1922 — the Sanitary District's first treatment plant — together with the Cal-Sag Channel (operational 1922), which reversed the Calumet and Little Calumet Rivers away from Lake Michigan on the same principle as the main canal [\[2\]](#src-2). The Calumet Intercepting Sewer, "with its two pumping stations at 95th Street and 125th Street," served the part of Chicago south of 87th Street and east of Halsted Street; the 1928 Engineering Works report states the system was built at a cost of $8,399,466.60, with part of it in operation by 1922 and the whole system by 1925 [\[35\]](#src-35). Its two pumping stations had distinct histories: the Calumet Sewage Pumping Station at 125th Street and Indiana Avenue (today's "125th Street Pumping Station") was completed in 1920 and placed in service January 1921, at a cost of $1,531,224.85, with three 250-hp and three 1,100-hp synchronous motors plus two 450-hp emergency motors; a stand-by Calumet Power Station (four 750-bhp diesel generator sets) adjoining it went into service June 1922 [\[35\]](#src-35). The 95th Street station had an earlier predecessor, operational by April 1921, which the District replaced in 1924–25 — construction of the current 95th Street Pumping Station's contract was let October 13, 1924, and it went into service in 1925, powered by motor-generator sets that converted incoming AC grid power to DC for variable-speed pump control, with coal delivered by a dedicated Pennsylvania Railroad spur for its boilers — "an electrically operated station of three times the old capacity" per the 1928 report, at a cost of $919,261.18 [\[18\]](#src-18)[\[35\]](#src-35). A siphon carrying the interceptor under the Calumet River, with its own maintenance headhouse, was completed in June 1923 as part of the same push [\[18\]](#src-18).

**39th Street Sewage Pumping Station (1905–1939) — the Racine Avenue Pumping Station's direct predecessor.** Before RAPS existed, a City of Chicago-built pumping station at 39th Street and Lake Michigan handled sewage and drainage for the South Side territory between 39th and 87th Streets, discharging west through a 20-foot conduit into the South Fork of the South Branch of the Chicago River (Bubbly Creek) "just west of Racine Avenue." The station and its 20-foot conduit were completed in 1905 and turned over by the City to the Sanitary District to operate on April 30, 1910; it drained about 22 square miles and pumped 100–1,500 cfs [\[35\]](#src-35)[\[36\]](#src-36). Its equipment — eight boilers, two 1,000-cfs flushing screw pumps, and four centrifugal sewage pumps — was enlarged in 1925–26 with two additional 250-cfs electric-driven pumps at a cost of $429,904.70, bringing the Sanitary District's total investment to $1,339,275.08 ($1.77 million including the City of Chicago's original outlay) [\[35\]](#src-35). Per the U.S. Army Corps of Engineers' 2020 Bubbly Creek feasibility report, "operations at the 39th Street Pumping Station were discontinued" in 1939 — the same year RAPS began operating a few blocks away — and the station is now eligible for the National Register of Historic Places, its structure repurposed as a Chicago Park District maintenance facility [\[36\]](#src-36).

**North Branch (Lawrence Avenue) Pumping Station rebuild (1928–30).** The original 1907 lakefront pumping station was superseded by a larger structure at 4860 N. Francisco Avenue at Lawrence Avenue, built by the Sanitary District of Chicago beginning in 1928, completed in 1929, and placed in service in 1930 [\[15\]](#src-15). In its current configuration the station carries five 300-cfs storm discharge pumps that lift excess combined sewage into the North Branch during rain events [\[10\]](#src-10).

**Racine Avenue Pumping Station (1939, expanded 1954).** RAPS, on the South Side near Pershing Road, was "originally constructed in 1939" according to the MWRD's own 2015 press release on the building [\[11\]](#src-11). This is independently confirmed — with substantially more engineering detail — by the U.S. Army Corps of Engineers' 2020 Bubbly Creek ecosystem-restoration feasibility report, a federal primary source prepared with MWRD's cooperation: "In 1939, RAPS began operation by discharging raw sewage to Bubbly Creek. A decade later, dry weather sewage was no longer discharged to Bubbly Creek and instead diverted to the West Southwest Sewage Treatment Works, now called the Stickney WRP" [\[36\]](#src-36). RAPS drains a 30-square-mile area of the South Side (downtown to 87th Street) via four large intercepting sewers that converge at the station; the station "originally had six pumps," expanded in 1954 to 14 centrifugal pumps — West Side: 3 units at 375 cfs, 2 at 400 cfs, 2 at 500 cfs (2,925 cfs); East Side: 3 units at 375 cfs, 4 at 500 cfs (3,125 cfs) — giving RAPS a documented maximum combined discharge of "6,000 cubic feet per second" in extreme storms, roughly double this brief's previously reported 3,125-cfs figure (which corresponds only to the station's *East Side* pump group, not its full capacity) [\[36\]](#src-36). Three TARP drop shafts sit adjacent to RAPS for wet-weather diversion, and the USACE report notes the Illinois SHPO has determined RAPS "eligible for listing on the National Register of Historic Places" [\[36\]](#src-36). The station houses the main sewage pumps that convey South Side wastewater onward to Stickney WRP, plus a coarse-screen building that removes trash from the flow before it reaches the pumps [\[11\]](#src-11). A secondary source (South Side Weekly) separately describes a "1964 Racine Avenue Pumping Station on Pershing" that "replaced an older pumping station with less capacity," serving a somewhat larger 36-square-mile drainage area (vs. USACE's 30 sq mi) from Western Avenue to the lakefront and from 87th Street to Taylor Street [\[20\]](#src-20). No source reviewed — including the federal 2020 USACE report, which documents 1939 construction and a 1954 pump expansion in detail — corroborates an additional 1964 rebuild; it remains possible "1964" in the secondary source is a transposition or conflation of the documented 1954 expansion (see Open Questions). (A local-history blog cataloguing MWRD facility numbers [\[17\]](#src-17) lists a "Racine Ave" station as District facility #142 but does not itself state a construction date.)

**TARP (1972 planning; 1975–2006 tunnels).** By the 1960s the combined-sewer system was overflowing more than 100 days a year and causing chronic street and basement flooding. In 1967 the MWRD, the State of Illinois, Cook County, and the City of Chicago formed a Flood Control Coordinating Committee that evaluated (depending on the source) "50 alternatives" [\[1\]](#src-1) or "23 alternative proposals" [\[12\]](#src-12) before formally adopting the Tunnel and Reservoir Plan on October 20, 1972, at an original estimated cost of $1.223 billion [\[12\]](#src-12)[\[1\]](#src-1). Tunnel construction began in 1975. Newly developed tunnel-boring machines — rather than traditional blasting — were used to bore through Silurian dolomite bedrock 150 to 300 feet down (Wikipedia cites a maximum depth "up to 350 feet" for some segments), wide enough in places for tunnels 33 feet in diameter; cracks in the surrounding rock were sealed with grout curtains to protect groundwater, and the tunnels were lined in concrete [\[3\]](#src-3)[\[1\]](#src-1)[\[14\]](#src-14). Metropolitan Sanitary District figures associated with the program in contemporaneous (1979) press coverage included MSD president Nicholas Melas, deputy chief engineer Frank Dalton, and research director Dr. Cecil Lue-Hing [\[23\]](#src-23). EPA's 1976 draft/final Environmental Impact Statements for the TARP tunnel systems include a detailed "Chronology of Program Development" (1967–1975) that names the consulting engineering firms behind the underlying planning studies — chiefly **Harza Engineering Company** and **Bauer Engineering, Inc.**, whose joint 1968 report "Chicagoland Deep Tunnel System for Pollution and Flood Control — First Construction Zone Definite Project Report" and 1971 "Report on the Impact of the Deep Tunnel Plan on the Water Resources of Northeast Illinois" underpin the plan's Alternative-lettered evaluations (roughly 18 named alternatives, A through T, several with pumped-storage variants, were formally evaluated 1967–71 before TARP's October 1972 adoption) [\[38\]](#src-38). The foundational technical paper proposing underground rock-tunnel storage was V.A. Koelzer, W.J. Bauer, and F.E. Dalton, "The Chicago Area Deep Tunnel Project — A Use of the Underground Storage Resource," *Journal of the Water Pollution Control Federation* (1969) — the same Frank Dalton later named as MSD deputy chief engineer in 1979 press coverage [\[38\]](#src-38)[\[23\]](#src-23). Additional named firms include Consoer, Townsend and Associates (a September 1971 treatment interim report) and DeLeuw, Cather and Company (a November 1970 tunnel-inspection report on the "Southwest Side Intercepting Sewer 13A," and 1973 preliminary plans for the O'Hare collection facility) [\[38\]](#src-38). No single engineer or firm is documented as sole author of the final 1972 TARP plan itself, but these EPA EIS chronologies are the most specific primary-source attribution located for the plan's technical authorship. The Upper Des Plaines (O'Hare) segment went online first, in 1981; the full 110-mile Phase I tunnel network was completed and operational by 2006 [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[7\]](#src-7). A 1979 magazine investigation pegged the ultimate program cost (tunnels, reservoirs, treatment-plant upgrades, suburban connections, interest, and engineering) at roughly $11 billion in contemporary dollars — a figure the MWRD's own later accounting did not reach, as actual completed-to-date construction costs are documented at $3.858 billion as of December 31, 2025 [\[23\]](#src-23)[\[7\]](#src-7).

**Westchester Pumping Station (1951).** Unlike the lakefront and river-corridor stations above, Westchester Pumping Station was not built to solve an elevation problem on a major waterway — it was built to relieve two overloaded western-suburban intercepting sewers. Per MWRD's own 2023 project-justification document for the station's relief sewer, "the Westchester Pumping Station was constructed in 1951 to provide relief to both the Berkley-Hillside Intercepting Sewer and the Broadview-Bellwood Intercepting Sewer" — sewers that (per the Sanitary District's 1928 *Engineering Works* report, describing the related Broadview-Bellwood and Des Plaines River outfall/intercepting sewers of that same era) originally routed the sewage of Broadview, Bellwood, Maywood, River Forest, Forest Park and part of Oak Park toward the old Des Plaines River Sewage Treatment Works near Maywood [\[31\]](#src-31)[\[35\]](#src-35). The Westchester station "relieves the intercepting sewers by discharging combined sewer overflow into Addison Creek" whenever wet-weather flow exceeds what those two intercepting sewers and downstream TARP capacity can carry — a small, gallons-scale CSO outfall (No. 150, TARP structure DS-D34-AI) compared to the billion-gallon-scale North Branch/Racine Avenue stations, which is why this archive's CSO log shows Westchester's 242 recorded events (2002–2025) measured in gallons rather than million gallons [\[31\]](#src-31)[\[32\]](#src-32). Dry-weather flow continues via the same two intercepting sewers toward Stickney WRP, whose service area includes Westchester [\[31\]](#src-31). In December 2023 the District awarded a $6,775,766 contract (21-168-3S, contractor Rausch Infrastructure LLC, financed by a Clean Water State Revolving Fund loan) to microtunnel roughly 275 linear feet of 45-inch glass-fiber-reinforced-plastic pipe connecting the Berkley-Hillside Intercepting Sewer directly to TARP Drop Shaft DS-D34-AI on the Des Plaines Tunnel System's "Roosevelt Road leg" — a relief sewer explicitly intended to reduce, not eliminate, the frequency of CSO events at Westchester's Addison Creek outfall [\[31\]](#src-31). Addison Creek is itself a tributary of Salt Creek (not a direct Des Plaines River tributary), joining Salt Creek near Elmhurst before Salt Creek in turn reaches the Des Plaines River near Lyons/Riverside [\[33\]](#src-33). A separate, much larger MWRD stormwater project — the 600-acre-foot (195-million-gallon) Addison Creek Reservoir at 2795 Washington Blvd. in Bellwood, completed 2023 at a cost of $81 million — provides general flood storage for six Addison Creek communities including Westchester, but is a distinct stormwater-management facility from the sanitary Westchester Pumping Station and is not itself part of the combined-sewer/TARP relief chain described above [\[34\]](#src-34).

**Mainstream and Calumet TARP pumping stations.** The MWRD's December 2017 TARP Status Report itemizes the original construction contracts for both major dewatering pumping stations by contract number and awarded price (bid-year dollars, not adjusted for inflation) [\[8\]](#src-8):
- Mainstream Pumping Station Part I — contract 73-162-AH, joint venture of P. Kiewit & Sons, J.F. Shea Co., and Kenny Construction Co. — **$168,811,300**
- Mainstream Pumping Station Part II — contract 73-162-BH, joint venture of Healy, Ball, and Grow Tunneling Corp. — **$64,755,000**
- Mainstream Pumping Station Part III — contract 73-162-CH, joint venture of Morrison-Knudsen and Paschen Contractors — **$28,012,400**
(Mainstream PS construction thus totaled roughly **$261.6 million** in three contracts.) The pumps themselves were placed in service in May 1985, per a 2021 MWRD rehabilitation-contract description [\[9\]](#src-9). On the South Side, the Calumet Tunnel and Pump Station was built under a single contract, 74-206-2H, awarded to a joint venture of S.A. Healy Co. and Atlas-Gest International Inc., for **$54,841,825** [\[8\]](#src-8). That station's pump rooms sit roughly 365 feet below grade in Chicago's Riverdale neighborhood — described by local reporting as the lowest inhabitable point within Chicago city limits [\[21\]](#src-21).

## Upgrades & modernization timeline

- **1900** — Chicago Sanitary and Ship Canal completed; Chicago River reversed away from Lake Michigan [\[2\]](#src-2)[\[15\]](#src-15).
- **1905** — 39th Street Sewage Pumping Station built by the City of Chicago at 39th St. and Lake Michigan, the direct functional predecessor of Racine Avenue Pumping Station [\[35\]](#src-35)[\[36\]](#src-36).
- **1906** — Construction begins on the MWRD intercepting sewer system [\[5\]](#src-5).
- **1900–1908** — Lakefront intercepting sewers built; original Lawrence Avenue (North Branch) Pumping Station, built by the City of Chicago, completed 1908 (this brief previously cited 1907 per a secondary source; the Sanitary District's own 1928 engineering report gives 1908) [\[35\]](#src-35)[\[15\]](#src-15)[\[2\]](#src-2).
- **1907–1910** — North Shore Channel excavated (begun Sept. 1907, completed Nov. 1910); Wilmette Pumping Station built 1908–1910 as the channel's lake-diversion controlling structure [\[19\]](#src-19)[\[6\]](#src-6).
- **1910 (Apr. 30)** — Sanitary District begins operating the 39th Street Sewage Pumping Station, taking it over from the City of Chicago [\[35\]](#src-35).
- **1910 (Dec. 6)** — Sanitary District begins operating the original Lawrence Avenue Sewage Pumping Station, taking it over from the City of Chicago [\[35\]](#src-35).
- **1912** — Lock completed under the Sheridan Road bridge at Wilmette [\[19\]](#src-19).
- **1920 (Oct.)** — Evanston Sewage Pumping Station completed (Elmwood/Orrington Ave. and Lake St.) [\[35\]](#src-35).
- **1920–1921** — Calumet Sewage Pumping Station (today's 125th Street Pumping Station), 125th St. & Indiana Ave., completed 1920, in service Jan. 1921; predecessor 95th Street Sewage Pumping Station also operational by Apr. 1921 [\[35\]](#src-35).

## Current operations

The MWRD today operates 23 sewage pumping stations alongside seven water reclamation plants, moving an average of roughly 1.2–1.5 billion gallons of wastewater per day through a combined treatment capacity exceeding 2.0–2.4 billion gallons per day [\[5\]](#src-5)[\[2\]](#src-2). The 560-mile intercepting sewer network is inspected and maintained jointly by the District's Maintenance & Operations and Engineering Departments; under the Collection Asset Management Plan (CAMP) — which replaced the 1991 Interceptor Inspection and Rehabilitation Program (IIRP) in 2013 — segments are prioritized for CCTV inspection, lining, or replacement using NASSCO-standard, risk-based scoring, and the District tracks asset age by decade (roughly 37% of interceptor mileage was installed 1961–1980, versus 6.9% before 1920) [\[5\]](#src-5)[\[28\]](#src-28).

Each of the five major CSO pumping stations — North Branch, Racine Avenue, 95th Street, 122nd Street, and 125th Street — is subject to NPDES permit sampling requirements: whenever a station's first storm pump activates, MWRD Industrial Waste Division staff are dispatched to pull composite and grab samples (BOD5/TOC, suspended solids, cadmium, hardness, pH, fecal coliform, depending on the receiving water) from the discharge for regulatory reporting [\[5\]](#src-5). All are unmanned, remotely alarmed facilities inspected monthly, with level sensors triggering pump operation from wet-well elevation, debris screens (some with automatic raking) protecting the pump intakes, and backup generators and dehumidification equipment on site [\[5\]](#src-5). A sixth, much smaller CSO pumping station, Westchester (WCPS), is separately monitored under MWRD's CSO Representative Monitoring and Reporting Plan for its single Addison Creek outfall (No. 150), reporting quarterly to IEPA via the same telemetry-based program that covers the five larger stations [\[32\]](#src-32).

On the TARP side, the system now consists of four connected tunnel systems (Mainstream, Calumet, Des Plaines, and Upper Des Plaines) feeding three reservoirs (Majewski, Thornton, and the still-expanding McCook) with 15.15 billion gallons of design storage, of which all but McCook Stage 2 (6.5 of the 10 billion gallons planned for McCook) is complete [\[7\]](#src-7). The Mainstream and Calumet TARP pumping stations are the only points where stored combined sewage is lifted back out of the tunnel/reservoir system to the WRPs (Stickney and Calumet, respectively) for treatment; MWRD's own 50th-anniversary retrospective states the full system captured more than 153 billion gallons of combined sewer overflow between 2017 and 2021 alone, and MWRD's TARP fact sheet claims over 1 trillion gallons captured since the tunnels first went into service [\[12\]](#src-12)[\[1\]](#src-1). Regionally, TARP's operation is credited with reducing the frequency of combined sewer overflow events from roughly 100 days per year before construction to about 50 days per year system-wide, with overflows in the Calumet system's service area "nearly eliminated" since the Thornton Reservoir came online in 2015 [\[1\]](#src-1).

## Notable events & incidents

**October 3, 1986 — Deep Tunnel geyser event.** A heavy thunderstorm dumped several inches of rain in a short period over the newly built (and not yet fully interconnected) tunnel system. Water surged unevenly through the tunnels, producing geysers reported at over 65 feet near Wilmette and the Bahá'í Temple, and a roughly 30-foot geyser erupted downtown at Jefferson and Monroe streets, filling a woman's car with water and trapping her inside. The MWRD subsequently installed a system of watertight bulkheads throughout TARP to prevent similar pressure events [\[14\]](#src-14).

**2015–2016 — green infrastructure and manufacturing recognition.** The Racine Avenue Pumping Station's green-roof retrofit (2015) and the Calumet TARP Pump Station's award-winning custom steel pump fitting (2016) both reflect a period of MWRD investment in retrofitting Depression- and Cold War-era pumping infrastructure with modern equipment and sustainability features [\[11\]](#src-11)[\[27\]](#src-27).

**2018–2021 — structural rehabilitation of the two oldest major pumping stations.** Both the ~1930 North Branch Pumping Station and the ~1985 Mainstream TARP pumps required major rehabilitation contracts within a few years of each other — the former because decades of 300-cfs discharge had eroded structural concrete columns by up to 50%, the latter because the original 1985 pump/motor sets were nearing the end of their design life [\[10\]](#src-10)[\[9\]](#src-9).

**July 2–4, 2026 — record rainfall and near-capacity reservoirs.** A multi-day rain event over the Independence Day weekend brought locally heavy rainfall — up to roughly 8.5 inches in Aurora and nearly 8 inches in Naperville over 48 hours, well above the Chicago area's 3.7-inch average for the entire month of July — pushing the McCook Reservoir to full capacity (100%, more than 3.6 billion gallons; different outlets reporting at different points over the weekend put it as low as 96–98% full) — its sixth time reaching capacity in 2026 alone, versus five times in the preceding five years combined — and the Thornton Reservoir to about 94% full (more than 7.4 billion gallons), the fullest it had been since opening in 2015. MWRD officials acknowledged that if reservoirs filled completely, untreated water might have to be released to Lake Michigan or allowed to overflow into local waterways, risking beach closures; as of the storm's immediate aftermath, officials reported the system had contained the rainfall without requiring such releases. Nearby South Holland and Thornton residents reported a persistent, strong odor from the reservoir during and after the storms; MWRD subsequently announced $5 million in odor-mitigation projects (a carbon-filtration system on nearby shafts and a perimeter mist/odor-control system), expected complete by the end of 2026 [\[25\]](#src-25)[\[30\]](#src-30)[\[29\]](#src-29)[\[26\]](#src-26).

## Open questions

- ~~**Named "sub-interceptors."**~~ **Resolved.** This brief was originally framed around a North Side/North Branch/Northwest Side/West Side/Southwest/Calumet naming scheme, which MWRD's current public materials do not use. However, the Sanitary District's own 1928 *Engineering Works* report shows the naming convention *did* exist historically — North Side Intercepting Sewer System, West Side Intercepting Sewer, Salt Creek Intercepting Sewer, Calumet Intercepting Sewer, Broadview-Bellwood Outfall Sewer, Berkeley-Hillside Intercepting Sewer, etc., each with individual mileage, dimensions, and cost figures [\[35\]](#src-35) — and that a related alphanumeric identifier system persists in MWRD's internal engineering practice today: 1976 EPA TARP EISs cite consulting reports on the "Southwest Side Intercepting Sewer 13A" and "Calumet Intercepting Sewer 18E, Extension A" [\[38\]](#src-38), and MWRD's 2024 capital budget book funds an active construction project relieving "Calumet Intercepting Sewer 18F ext. A, Calumet Intercepting Sewer 18F, [and] Calumet Intercepting Sewer 18E" [\[39\]](#src-39). The District's *public-facing* materials (O&M manual, CSO/UAA reports) still describe the network only in aggregate or by service basin; interceptor-by-interceptor detail lives in internal engineering atlases/GIS layers not accessible for this brief.
- **Racine Avenue Pumping Station construction date — largely resolved toward 1939.** MWRD's own January 2015 press release states RAPS "was originally constructed in 1939," and this is now independently corroborated in detail by a federal primary source: the U.S. Army Corps of Engineers' 2020 Bubbly Creek feasibility report states "In 1939, RAPS began operation by discharging raw sewage to Bubbly Creek," documents an *original* six-pump station expanded to 14 pumps in **1954**, and does not mention any 1964 event [\[36\]](#src-36). A secondary source (South Side Weekly) instead describes a "1964 Racine Avenue Pumping Station" that "replaced an older pumping station with less capacity" and serves a 36-square-mile drainage area (vs. USACE's documented 30 sq mi) [\[20\]](#src-20). Given that the federal engineering source documents a 1954 expansion and no 1964 rebuild, it is now more likely than not that "1964" in the secondary source is a transposition or conflation of the 1954 expansion, though this brief cannot rule out an undocumented later modification. (Note: a local-history blog once cited alongside South Side Weekly for this claim [\[17\]](#src-17) was re-checked directly and does not itself state 1964 or any RAPS construction date — it only lists MWRD's internal facility number for "Racine Ave" as #142.)
- **122nd Street Pumping Station construction date** still could not be found in any source reviewed. New evidence narrows the search: the Sanitary District's 1928 *Engineering Works* report — which documents the Calumet system in detail, naming only the 95th Street and 125th Street (Calumet) stations — does not mention a 122nd Street facility at all, suggesting it was built after 1928, independently of the original 1920s Calumet Intercepting Sewer system [\[35\]](#src-35).
- **Westchester Pumping Station pump count/capacity** (cfs or MGD rating, number of pumps) was not located in any source reviewed — only its 1951 construction date, function (relief for the Berkley-Hillside and Broadview-Bellwood Intercepting Sewers), and Addison Creek CSO outfall were documented. Unlike the five larger CSO stations, no MWRD fact sheet or UAA-report table entry specific to Westchester's pumping capacity was found; the 2013–2015-vintage Description of the Combined Sewer System report [\[6\]](#src-6) that tabulates cfs ratings for North Branch, Racine Avenue, 95th/122nd/125th Street, and Wilmette does not include Westchester.
- **Wilmette Pumping Station, 1959–2000.** Sources document gate/pump changes in 1930, 1959, 2000, 2002, and 2012, but not what (if anything) happened to the station's four original screw pumps between 1959 and their apparent failure some time before 2000.
- **HAER/HABS documentation — partially addressed.** No Historic American Engineering Record or Historic American Buildings Survey entry specific to any MWRD intercepting sewer or sewage pumping station was located; the one Chicago-area sewer-adjacent HAER record found (HAER IL-4, Lakeview Pumping Station) documents a *drinking-water* supply pumping station, unrelated to the sewage/TARP system covered here. Short of a HAER/HABS record, however, the U.S. Army Corps of Engineers' 2020 Bubbly Creek report documents that the Illinois State Historic Preservation Office has determined *both* Racine Avenue Pumping Station and the historic (1905, now-decommissioned) 39th Street Sewage Pumping Station eligible for the National Register of Historic Places [\[36\]](#src-36).
- **Engineer/contractor of record for the 1907 predecessor — partially resolved.** The original Lawrence Avenue Sewage Pumping Station (North Branch PS's predecessor) is now documented as a *City of Chicago* project — not a Sanitary District one — completed in 1908 (not 1907) and only transferred to Sanitary District operation on December 6, 1910, per the District's own 1928 engineering report [\[35\]](#src-35). No specific engineer or contracting-firm name for the City's original construction was identified in any source reviewed.
- **Design engineer(s) of record for the original 1972 TARP plan — substantially resolved.** EPA's 1976 EISs for the TARP tunnel systems document, via a detailed program chronology, that Harza Engineering Company and Bauer Engineering, Inc. authored the core 1968–1971 planning studies underlying TARP, and that V.A. Koelzer, W.J. Bauer, and F.E. Dalton co-authored the seminal 1969 JWPCF paper proposing underground rock-tunnel storage; Consoer, Townsend and Associates and DeLeuw, Cather and Company contributed further 1970–73 studies [\[38\]](#src-38). No single firm or engineer is credited as sole author of the final adopted 1972 plan, but this is the most specific primary-source attribution located.
- ~~**Total interceptor mileage discrepancy.**~~ **Resolved.** The 554-mile/8-inch-to-27-foot figure is confirmed to come from an earlier edition of the same recurring document: MWRD's *Collection System O&M Manual*, dated March 31, 2022, states the interceptor system is "554 miles in length" ranging "from 8 inches to 27 feet in diameter" and serving "883.6 square miles" [\[28\]](#src-28). The District's 2026 edition of the same manual instead states 560 miles, 6 inches to 27 feet, and 882.1 square miles [\[5\]](#src-5). This is a genuine year-over-year revision within MWRD's own recurring manual (net +6 miles of interceptor added/reclassified, minimum pipe size redefined downward from 8 in. to 6 in., and service area reduced slightly, between the 2022 and 2026 editions), not a sourcing error; this brief uses the more current 2026 figures in the body text and cites the 2022 figures here for the record.
