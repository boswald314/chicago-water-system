# The Suburban Satellite Plants: Kirie, Egan, Hanover Park, and Lemont Water Reclamation Plants

## Role in the system

The Metropolitan Water Reclamation District of Greater Chicago (MWRD) operates seven water reclamation plants (WRPs) across its roughly 882–883-square-mile service area covering Chicago and 125–128 Cook County suburbs (the exact community count varies slightly by fact-sheet vintage) [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[5\]](#src-5)[\[6\]](#src-6). Two of the seven — Stickney and Calumet — are the enormous, century-old plants that anchor the system's Deep Tunnel-fed core. The other five are smaller "satellite" plants built or expanded in the mid-to-late 20th century to serve suburban areas too far from the core interceptors to be economical to pipe downtown. This document covers four of those satellites, all in the northwest and southwest suburbs: **James C. Kirie WRP** (Des Plaines), **John E. Egan WRP** (Schaumburg), **Hanover Park WRP**, and **Lemont WRP** [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[2\]](#src-2)[\[3\]](#src-3)[\[4\]](#src-4).

- **Kirie WRP** (701 West Oakton Street, Des Plaines) receives sanitary flow from local sewers in Des Plaines, Elk Grove Village, Mount Prospect, Arlington Heights, Rolling Meadows, Wheeling, Prospect Heights, Buffalo Grove, Elk Grove Township, and the Forest River and Northfield Wood Sanitary Districts, treats it, and discharges the cleaned effluent to **Higgins Creek**, a small tributary of the Des Plaines River near O'Hare International Airport [\[10\]](#src-10). Kirie is a "satellite" plant in the specific engineering sense that it performs no on-site solids digestion: raw sludge pumped off during primary and secondary treatment is piped to the **Egan WRP** for digestion, then on to **O'Brien WRP**, and finally to **Stickney WRP** for final dewatering and drying [\[5\]](#src-5). Combined-sewer overflow (CSO) from Kirie's service area is captured by the Upper Des Plaines Tunnel System, a small, self-contained component of the Tunnel and Reservoir Plan (TARP) that stores flow in the Gloria Alitto Majewski Reservoir near O'Hare — 0.35 BG (350 million gallons) per MWRD's TARP fact sheet, though MWRD's own Kirie-specific fact sheet states 343 million gallons [\[5\]](#src-5)[\[9\]](#src-9)[\[23\]](#src-23)[\[24\]](#src-24) — before pumping it back to Kirie for treatment. Kirie's own 1975 planning-era environmental impact statement (filed when the plant was still called the "O'Hare WRP") describes the interceptor system feeding it in street-level detail: a tunnel running along Elmhurst Road between the Northwest Tollway and Weller Creek, then along Weller Creek itself between Elmhurst Road and Central Road, with a branch extending from Elmhurst Road/Lonnquist Boulevard east to William Street, then north on William Street to a point north of Rand Road and Isabella Street, and a further 9-foot-diameter branch running along Weller Creek from William Street to Mount Prospect Road — collectively the Upper Des Plaines Intercepting Sewer system, which was explicitly designed to "intercept and convey combined sewage overflows presently discharging to Weller Creek and Feehanville Ditch" [\[32\]](#src-32). Illinois EPA's 2013 NPDES public-notice fact sheet for Kirie (permit IL0047741) pins down the plant's outfalls precisely: main effluent Outfall 001 discharges to Higgins Creek at 42°01'10"N, 87°56'12"W; a secondary Outfall 002 discharges to an unnamed Higgins Creek tributary at 42°01'27"N, 87°56'33"W; and CSO Outfall 111 — Kirie's Weller Creek discharge point — sits at 42°03'28"N, 87°57'30"W, corresponding to TARP structure UDP-DS1 (gates K2-1 and K2-2) south of the Central Road bridge in Arlington Heights [\[10\]](#src-10)[\[33\]](#src-33). Higgins Creek itself joins Willow Creek a few miles downstream, near the Cook County villages surrounding O'Hare — and Willow Creek carries the combined flow to its confluence with the Des Plaines River near Rosemont [\[34\]](#src-34).

- **Egan WRP** (550 South Meacham Road, Schaumburg) receives flow from Schaumburg, Arlington Heights, Elk Grove Village, Hoffman Estates, Inverness, Palatine, Rolling Meadows, and Roselle, and discharges to **Upper Salt Creek**, part of the Des Plaines River watershed, under NPDES Permit No. IL0036340 (main discharge via Outfalls A01/B01/001) [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[6\]](#src-6)[\[36\]](#src-36). Egan is a hub in the suburban solids-handling chain: it digests not only its own sludge but also the sludge trucked/piped in from Kirie via the interconnecting Kirie-Egan Solids Pipeline (the subject of a $12.5 million rehabilitation contract active in MWRD's 2026 capital program), and its own digested residuals continue downstream to O'Brien WRP en route to Stickney for final processing [\[6\]](#src-6)[\[12\]](#src-12). The Upper Salt Creek watershed that Egan serves is itself the subject of ongoing MWRD stormwater and TARP planning, since Salt Creek has historically been prone to CSOs and flooding in the built-out Schaumburg/Elk Grove corridor [\[24\]](#src-24). Downstream of Egan's outfall, Salt Creek continues south and east through DuPage and Cook Counties before joining the **Des Plaines River near Riverside/Lyons** (documented mouth coordinates approximately 41°48'51"N, 87°50'7"W, per USGS GNIS) — the same Des Plaines River that receives Kirie's Higgins Creek effluent farther upstream [\[37\]](#src-37).

- **Hanover Park WRP** (1200 Sycamore Avenue, Hanover Park) receives flow from Hanover Park, Bartlett, Schaumburg, and Hoffman Estates in northwestern Cook and DuPage counties and discharges to the **West Branch of the DuPage River** via Discharge/Outfall 007, under NPDES Permit No. IL0036137 [\[3\]](#src-3)[\[7\]](#src-7)[\[38\]](#src-38). That permit requires Hanover Park's participation in the **DuPage River Salt Creek Workgroup (DRSCW)**, the multi-agency watershed group that coordinates water-quality planning across both the DuPage River and Salt Creek systems the four plants in this document collectively drain into [\[38\]](#src-38). Uniquely among the four plants, essentially all of its biosolids are consumed on-site: since 1969 the MWRD has applied Class B liquid biosolids by subsurface injection to the adjacent 200-acre **Fischer Farm**, which also captures roughly 75 million gallons of stormwater overflow in the on-site Upper DuPage Reservoir [\[3\]](#src-3)[\[7\]](#src-7).

- **Lemont WRP** (13 Stephen Street, Lemont) is the smallest of the seven plants and serves only the Village of Lemont, discharging to the **Chicago Sanitary and Ship Canal** — meaning its effluent flows directly into the engineered channel that reversed the Chicago River and ultimately reaches the Illinois and Mississippi Rivers via the Des Plaines and Illinois waterways [\[4\]](#src-4)[\[9\]](#src-9). Solids removed at Lemont — about 0.9 dry tons per day — are gravity-concentrated (not digested) on-site and then trucked to the Calumet or Stickney WRPs for digestion and further treatment and drying [\[4\]](#src-4)[\[8\]](#src-8). Since opening in 1961, the plant has cleaned more than 42 billion gallons of wastewater [\[8\]](#src-8).

All four plants sit downstream of local combined and separate sewer networks and upstream of MWRD's TARP "Deep Tunnel" system, which captures wet-weather combined sewage that would otherwise overflow directly into these same waterways, storing it until the plants have capacity to treat it [\[9\]](#src-9).

## Original construction

None of these four plants was part of the original Sanitary District of Chicago's 1889–1928 canal-and-dilution system; all were built decades later by the district under its 1955–1988 name, the **Metropolitan Sanitary District of Greater Chicago**, as suburban Cook County urbanized after World War II and Illinois (then federal) water-quality law increasingly required treatment rather than dilution [\[22\]](#src-22). (The district was renamed the Metropolitan Water Reclamation District of Greater Chicago effective January 1, 1989 [\[22\]](#src-22).)

**Hanover Park** was the earliest of the four, entering service July 19, 1963, in the midst of Chicago's postwar suburban building boom in northwestern Cook County [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[3\]](#src-3). Roughly a decade later it became nationally notable: a secondary source describes Hanover Park's sand-filtration tertiary system, installed around 1971, as "the first of their kind to go into service" and states the plant was "the nation's first major tertiary wastewater treatment facility" [\[15\]](#src-15); a second, independent local-history source corroborates the general idea but in softer language, describing the 1970 plant Nixon visited only as "one of the first of its kind in the nation" [\[31\]](#src-31). That claim is corroborated by an extraordinary documented event: on **February 6, 1970**, President Richard Nixon personally toured the "Hanover Sewage Treatment Works" as part of a Chicago-area environmental-policy trip, accompanied by the governors of Illinois (Richard Ogilvie), Indiana (Edgar Whitcomb), Michigan (William Milliken), and Wisconsin (Warren Knowles), White House aide John Ehrlichman, and Benjamin Sosewitz, then Acting General Manager of the Metropolitan Sanitary District [\[16\]](#src-16)[\[15\]](#src-15). H. R. Haldeman's diary records that area schools let out for the visit, crowds were "very enthusiastic," Nixon "did not bring his overcoat" despite freezing conditions, and the sound system failed, forcing Nixon to address the crowd with a bullhorn [\[16\]](#src-16). The visit came the same day as a meeting of the Cabinet Committee on the Environment and the Council on Environmental Quality held not at the White House but at the Field Museum of Natural History in Chicago — attended by CEQ Chairman Russell Train, the same four governors, and other Cabinet members — situating Hanover Park's advanced treatment technology as a showcase for the administration's emerging clean-water agenda a few months before the EPA's creation later that year [\[16\]](#src-16).

**Egan WRP** followed, entering service December 16, 1975, and receiving a formal dedication ceremony on **June 4, 1976** — bicentennial year, a detail later commemorated in a plant time capsule (see Notable events, below) [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[6\]](#src-6)[\[14\]](#src-14). The plant is named for **John E. Egan**, who was elected a Commissioner of the Metropolitan Sanitary District on November 6, 1962, and served as **President of the district's Board of Commissioners from December 6, 1966, to December 3, 1974** — meaning the plant that bears his name opened roughly a year after his board tenure ended, honoring the sitting district president under whom it was planned and largely built [\[21\]](#src-21)[\[1\]](#src-1). Egan's design incorporated what MWRD's own materials describe as "a novel two-stage secondary process" together with an "advanced tertiary system," at a time when few suburban Chicago-area plants offered tertiary treatment at all [\[6\]](#src-6). The design proved durable: in 1982, seven years after opening, the U.S. EPA's Region V office selected Egan as the "best operated and maintained" large treatment plant in the six-state region [\[13\]](#src-13).

**Kirie WRP** was the last and largest of the four to be built, opening **May 12, 1980**, as the MWRD's seventh and (at the time) newest plant overall [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[5\]](#src-5). It is named for **James C. Kirie**, elected a Sanitary District/MWRD Commissioner on December 8, 1970, re-elected in 1976 and 1982, and serving on the board until December 6, 1988; MWRD's own materials describe him as "a strong supporter of the MWRD's Tunnel and Reservoir Plan (TARP)" [\[5\]](#src-5)[\[21\]](#src-21). At its 1980 opening, Kirie was described by the district as the first of its plants "to feature fully automated operations," at a time when the district's other six plants (including the newly commissioned Egan) still ran on a mix of computerized and manual control [\[5\]](#src-5). Kirie was engineered from the outset as a satellite/"no digestion" plant, piping its solids to Egan rather than digesting on site, which kept its 108-acre footprint comparatively compact for a 52 MGD (110 MGD peak) facility [\[5\]](#src-5).

Kirie was in fact designed and built as a **two-stage nitrification facility**, not the single-stage plant its current NPDES paperwork describes: per a 2022 MWRD/Carollo Engineers phosphorus-optimization study, the 1st-stage activated-sludge aeration tanks and clarifiers ("Battery A," six tanks each 250 ft × 25.5 ft × 16 ft deep, with matching 153-ft-diameter circular settling tanks) were configured for BOD₅ removal, while an identical 2nd-stage train ("Battery B") was built purely for nitrification — matching a 1975 MSDGC design criterion, recorded in the plant's federal EIS, that the O'Hare (Kirie) plant "proceed as a two-stage activated plant providing for biological ammonia oxidation" at an original planning-stage average flow of 72 MGD (later reduced to the 52 MGD DAF actually built) [\[39\]](#src-39)[\[44\]](#src-44). At some point after opening, Battery B was taken out of aeration service and is now run as a pass-through stage, with both BOD removal and nitrification occurring incidentally in Battery A alone — the arrangement the current NPDES fact sheet and the 2012 IIT thesis describe as "single-stage" [\[10\]](#src-10)[\[20\]](#src-20)[\[44\]](#src-44). This resolves what otherwise reads as a contradiction between 1975-era planning documents and later descriptions of the plant: Kirie's *hardware* is two-stage; its *current operation* is effectively single-stage. Other original/current equipment specifics from the 2022 study: 6 raw-sewage centrifugal pumps (3 at 38 MGD/190 ft TDH, 3 at 56 MGD/190 ft TDH, 2,250 hp each), 4 multi-rake fine screens (8 ft wide, 3/16-in. clear opening), 3 gravity grit/detritus tanks (55 ft × 55 ft × 4.5 ft), and 5 single-stage centrifugal blowers (37,770 scfm, 2,500 hp) [\[44\]](#src-44).

Kirie's road to construction was unusually contentious for a suburban treatment plant. MSDGC's engineering staff — under then-General Superintendent Vinton Bacon — initially favored a remote site on undeveloped O'Hare International Airport land (roughly 1 percent of total airport acreage), chosen for its distance from residential areas and cost-effectiveness, but the City of Chicago, which owned the airport, rejected the request over uncertainty about future runway expansion [\[40\]](#src-40). MSDGC then evaluated nine candidate sites — six inspected directly plus three more suggested by residents — against 1965–66 criteria requiring at least 100 acres and the lowest combined land-acquisition, construction, and interceptor/outfall costs, settling on "Site 1," a 104-acre parcel east of Elmhurst Road and south of Oakton Street later divided by Wille Road into 65 usable acres north and 28 south — a footprint close to the 108 acres Kirie occupies today [\[32\]](#src-32). MSDGC purchased Site 1 in January 1966, the day after a meeting with Des Plaines officials at which the city's objections were aired [\[40\]](#src-40). Des Plaines fought the "Oakton-Elmhurst" site for roughly nine years, including two zoning lawsuits (filed November 1966 and August 1972; the first was decided for Des Plaines by the circuit and appellate courts but reversed in MSDGC's favor by the Illinois Supreme Court on January 25, 1971) and, per USEPA's own final-EIS hearing record, public testimony in which a Des Plaines resident recounted that Vinton Bacon himself — by then the *former* MSDGC superintendent — had turned against Site 1 and "expressed his concern for the health of his family" [\[32\]](#src-32)[\[40\]](#src-40). A Des Plaines alderman's 1975 comment letter, reproduced in a related USACE draft EIS, further documents more than 300 residents confronting MSD Superintendent Benjamin Sosewitz at an August 8, 1972 public meeting and a 906-signature petition delivered to the MSD Board on February 8, 1973 in an unsuccessful attempt to force a public hearing before final design plans were authorized; the same letter states MSDGC also floated building the plant underground in the Swedish style in 1973 — roughly six years into final design — citing a possible $5–10 million savings (an idea a Des Plaines alderman had first raised in 1967), but the above-ground design already in progress went forward instead [\[40\]](#src-40). MSDGC's engineering documents name three consulting firms across this process: **Brown and Caldwell**, hired July 27, 1967 to design the plant around the Oakton-Elmhurst site (design reports issued February and June 1968); **DeLeuw, Cather and Company**, which prepared preliminary O'Hare collection-facility plans in November 1972; and **Consoer, Townsend and Associates**, which prepared the November 1974 Environmental Assessment Statement for the plant and solids pipeline — the closest this report could get to identifying an engineer of record, though a general contractor and an isolated, plant-only bid cost for the 1977–1980 construction were still not found [\[39\]](#src-39)[\[40\]](#src-40). A 1974-era MSDGC cost estimate covering the O'Hare WRP together with the Upper Des Plaines Tunnels (Nos. 20, 20A, 20B, 21, and 22) and the Northwest-O'Hare Reservoir came to $197,010,000 (not broken out by component), with MSDGC separately estimating that each month of delay in resolving the site dispute cost the project roughly $1.0 million [\[39\]](#src-39).

Kirie's existence as a standalone satellite plant, rather than a simple extension of the Stickney intercepting-sewer network, traces to a specific mid-1960s engineering decision. A 1988 EPA/MSDGC evaluation of TARP's early operating history explains that "due to development of the northwest area of the MSDGC jurisdiction in the early 1960's, MSDGC investigated possible alternatives to provide relief to the existing sewers. The initial recommendation was to construct an intercepting sewer system to convey all of the sewage to the West-Southwest Treatment Works in Stickney. However, the cost and magnitude of the project would have required such implementation time as to necessitate temporary treatment plants in the northwest area, making this alternative not cost-effective." MSDGC instead divided the northwest area into four facility areas by drainage basin, one of which — the Upper Des Plaines (O'Hare) facility area, 88.7 square miles with 13.7 square miles of combined-sewer area and 119 CSO outfalls — became the service territory for what opened in 1980 as the O'Hare WRP and was later renamed Kirie [\[35\]](#src-35). The Upper Des Plaines TARP tunnel system built to serve it consisted of three named construction projects: Upper Des Plaines No. 20 (22,000 linear feet of 20-foot-diameter rock tunnel, 130–180 feet deep, with 5 drop shafts, capable of conveying 489 cfs of peak sanitary flow to the plant against an average dry-weather design flow of 204 cfs); Upper Des Plaines No. 20A (connecting structures and lateral sewers diverting flow from the existing intercepting sewers and CSO outfalls into the drop shafts and tunnels); and Upper Des Plaines No. 20B (6,000 linear feet of 5-foot-diameter earth tunnel diverting up to 86 cfs of peak sanitary flow from the facility area's northern and western portions — flow that had previously been conveyed instead to MSDGC's North Side [O'Brien] treatment plant) [\[35\]](#src-35).

**Lemont WRP** is something of an outlier chronologically, dating to **November 1961** — slightly earlier than Hanover Park — but the smallest by an order of magnitude (2.3 MGD average versus Hanover Park's 12 MGD) [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[4\]](#src-4). Lemont's siting on the Chicago Sanitary and Ship Canal put it within a few miles of the district's original 1900 drainage-canal engineering works around Lockport and Romeoville, but Lemont itself was a purpose-built, free-standing local plant rather than an extension of the Sanitary District's original canal/dilution infrastructure [\[4\]](#src-4). The November 1961 plant, however, was far more rudimentary than what operates today: an MWRD phosphorus-impact study describes the original works as consisting only of a pump station, grit chamber, two Imhoff tanks, a trickling filter, and a final settling tank — pre-activated-sludge technology serving a 20.6-square-mile Lemont Township basin. That plant was entirely demolished and replaced in **June 1972** by a new activated-sludge plant built on the original's foundations, which was itself upgraded and expanded in 1995 to the 2.3 MGD DAF/4.0 MGD DMF rating in service today; the plant discharges to the Canal at River Mile 300.6, about 10.5 miles upstream of the CSSC's confluence with the Des Plaines River [\[42\]](#src-42).

## Upgrades & modernization timeline

- **1966** — MSDGC purchases the 104-acre "Site 1" at Oakton Street and Elmhurst Road for what becomes Kirie WRP, after the City of Chicago rejects MSDGC's preferred remote site on O'Hare Airport land; the purchase, made the day after a meeting with Des Plaines officials, triggers roughly nine years of litigation and public opposition (see Original construction and Notable events) [\[32\]](#src-32)[\[40\]](#src-40).
- **1967** — MSDGC contracts with Brown and Caldwell (agreement dated July 27, 1967) to design the O'Hare (Kirie) plant around the Site 1 location; design reports follow in February and June 1968 [\[40\]](#src-40).
- **1969** — MWRD purchases the 200-acre Fischer Farm adjoining Hanover Park WRP and constructs the Upper DuPage Reservoir (≈75 million gallons of stormwater storage capacity), establishing what remains an active biosolids land-application program to this day [\[3\]](#src-3).
- **ca. 1971** — Hanover Park WRP installs sand-media tertiary filters, described by a secondary source as the first such filters in the nation to enter service; the installation prompts President Nixon's February 1970 visit and subsequent dedication-era attention (visit predates filter completion, consistent with a groundbreaking/preview visit) [\[15\]](#src-15)[\[16\]](#src-16).
- **June 1972** — Lemont's original 1961 works (pump station, grit chamber, two Imhoff tanks, trickling filter, final settling tank) is entirely demolished and replaced by a new activated-sludge plant built on the same foundations [\[42\]](#src-42).
- **1973** — MSDGC publicly floats, then does not pursue, building the O'Hare (Kirie) plant underground in the Swedish style for an estimated $5–10 million savings, roughly six years into final design of the above-ground plant that was ultimately built [\[40\]](#src-40).
- **June 4, 1976** — Formal dedication of Egan WRP, one year after it entered service; ceremony guest register, a same-day Chicago Tribune, a photo of the sitting Board of Commissioners, a Bicentennial coin proof/set, and 1976 postage stamps are sealed into a plant time capsule [\[14\]](#src-14).
- **1982** — U.S. EPA Region V names Egan WRP the "best operated and maintained" large treatment plant in the region, seven years after opening [\[13\]](#src-13).
- **August 1987** — Severe storm-driven sewer back-up flooding across the O'Hare TARP System's combined-sewer area damages an estimated 6,000 homes in Mount Prospect alone; the flood is later cited in USACE's Phase II design memorandum as evidence of the O'Hare System's ongoing basement-flooding problem, part of the case for the terminal reservoir that becomes the Majewski Reservoir [\[41\]](#src-41).
- **1989** — Congress's Water Resources Development Act of 1986 authorizes a minimum 1,050-acre-foot (≈342.1 million gallon) O'Hare Reservoir — larger than the 450-acre-foot design USACE's own National Economic Development cost-benefit analysis had favored — and USACE completes the Phase II General Design Memorandum, pricing the project's first cost at $20.52 million ($14.018 million federal, $6.502 million non-federal) [\[41\]](#src-41).
- **1995** — Lemont WRP is upgraded and expanded to its present 2.3 MGD design average flow / 4.0 MGD design maximum flow rating [\[42\]](#src-42).
- **1998** — TARP's Gloria Alitto Majewski Reservoir (350 million gallons per MWRD's TARP fact sheet; 343 million gallons per MWRD's Kirie fact sheet) is completed near O'Hare Field as the terminal storage element of the Upper Des Plaines Tunnel System, providing combined-sewage storage for the communities in Kirie WRP's service area before the stored flow is pumped to Kirie for treatment [\[5\]](#src-5)[\[9\]](#src-9)[\[23\]](#src-23)[\[24\]](#src-24).
- **2012** — Kirie WRP commissions a "sewerthermal" demonstration energy-recovery project developed with the University of Illinois at Chicago (led by UIC chemical-engineering department head Sohail Murad and engineering dean Peter Nelson), supported by an $87,500 grant from the Illinois Clean Energy Community Foundation. The system extracts heat from the plant's ~55°F aeration-tank effluent to precondition heating/cooling water for the administration building, cutting that building's heating/AC costs by about 50 percent; neither of the two sources describing the project quantifies a plant-wide (as opposed to administration-building) energy-savings percentage, so no plant-wide figure is asserted here [\[11\]](#src-11)[\[25\]](#src-25).
- **February 12, 2013** — Illinois Governor Pat Quinn announces a $30 million low-interest loan to MWRD under the Illinois Clean Water Initiative (part of a $250 million statewide package) to fund a new Wet Weather Treatment Facility and 5-million-gallon underground storage reservoir at Lemont WRP, aimed at reducing storm-driven overflow discharges to the Sanitary and Ship Canal [\[18\]](#src-18).
- **2013–2018** — Lemont's Wet Weather Treatment Facility and Reservoir is designed by CH2M Hill and built by general contractor Joseph J. Henderson & Son, Inc. under Contract 10-716-3P at a value of $29,663,517; the underground concrete reservoir measures roughly 65.5 × 50 × 9 meters deep with walls up to 1 meter thick, and the project includes a new pump station, an emergency high-level-overflow diversion structure, a separator, and a disinfection system. The project later receives the LCCA APEX (Award for Project Excellence) [\[19\]](#src-19)[\[18\]](#src-18).
- **2015** — Egan WRP earns the National Association of Clean Water Agencies (NACWA) Gold Award for 100 percent compliance with its NPDES permit for a full calendar year, and installs a donated 45-panel solar-thermal array generating an estimated 2,040 therms per year to preheat boiler water; a permeable parking lot is planned for the following year to improve on-site stormwater management [\[13\]](#src-13).
- **August 2016** — Egan WRP brings online an ANITA Mox deammonification system for sidestream (centrate) treatment, converting four previously unused dissolved-air-flotation thickening tanks into one equalization tank and four parallel ANITA Mox bioreactors to strip ammonia and nitrogen from digester sidestream before it re-enters the main treatment train, designed to treat 870 m³/d (0.23 MGD) of centrate at ~1,080 mg/L NH₃-N with a guaranteed removal efficiency above 75 percent [\[28\]](#src-28). (Note: the Wastewater Digest "40 years" retrospective [\[13\]](#src-13) predates this installation — published December 2015 — and does not describe it; the ANITA Mox startup is documented instead in a Water Environment Federation conference paper by MWRD/vendor engineers.)
- **April 2015 – July 2017** — MWRD pilot-tests biological phosphorus (Bio P) removal at Kirie WRP by converting portions of the 1st-stage aeration tanks to anaerobic zones — first with periodic "air-bump" mixing, then with temporary wood baffle walls — rather than adding chemical treatment; supplemental testing across four of the six 1st-stage tanks achieves roughly 90 percent phosphorus removal (effluent TP ≈0.3 mg/L against ≈3.0 mg/L influent), laying the operational groundwork MWRD later cites for meeting a 1.0 mg/L optimized-effluent goal without major capital construction [\[44\]](#src-44).
- **April 2022** — MWRD's retained engineer (Carollo Engineers, under contract 16-RFP-21) reports that Kirie and Egan WRPs have "recently received" a 1.0 mg/L monthly-average total-phosphorus limit in "newly reissued" NPDES permits, and separately evaluates facility improvements needed to meet potential future limits of 0.5 mg/L and 0.1 mg/L at both plants — the most direct confirmation found that the compliance-schedule figure appearing in Illinois EPA's 2013 draft-permit fact sheet for Kirie had, by this point, become a binding limit, consistent with Kirie's July 28, 2021 permit reissuance [\[44\]](#src-44)[\[10\]](#src-10).
- **2022 (permit modification, effective 2022)** — Kirie WRP's NPDES permit (No. IL0047741) is modified regarding a Total Phosphorus effluent limit of 1.0 mg/L monthly average (434 lb/day, based on the 52 MGD design average flow), part of Illinois's system-wide phosphorus-reduction push affecting major dischargers statewide. Note that this same 1.0 mg/L / 434 lb/day figure already appears in Illinois EPA's 2013 draft-permit fact sheet for Kirie, listed there as a "Special Condition" establishing a compliance schedule for a future phosphorus limit rather than an immediately enforced one; the 2022 modification most likely converts that long-standing compliance-schedule target into a final, enforceable limit, and the April 2022 Carollo Engineers report above corroborates that the transition to a binding limit had occurred by that date, even though this report still could not directly obtain the 2021/2022 permit text itself [10; permit expiration July 31, 2026].
- **February 2024** — MWRD approves a research partnership with Black & Veatch and Wastewater Microbiology Solutions at Egan WRP to pilot process optimizations for energy efficiency, phosphorus removal, activated-sludge densification, carbon management, and anaerobic-digestion energy recovery; pilot testing was slated to begin in Q3 2024 with a final report due winter 2026 [\[26\]](#src-26).
- **September 3, 2025** — MWRD marks Egan WRP's 50th anniversary, opening the 1976 dedication-ceremony time capsule described above [\[14\]](#src-14).
- **February 5, 2026** — The MWRD Board approves a $27 million construction contract with Sollitt/Broadway Joint Venture for electric-power-distribution and phosphorus-removal improvements at Kirie WRP, covering electrical system upgrades and biological-process amendments needed to meet new phosphorus-removal requirements [\[17\]](#src-17).
- **2026 (in progress)** — The "Kirie–Egan Solids Pipeline Rehabilitation, Section No. 2" contract ($12.5 million, bond-funded) rehabilitates the interconnecting solids pipeline between the two plants; a separate "Gate Control Equipment Upgrade at TARP Control Structures, KWRP" contract ($3 million) and a "Tunnel Rehabilitation, HPWRP" contract ($250,000) are also active in MWRD's current capital program, alongside a continuing "Biosolids Injection and Farming, HPWRP" service contract ($1.34 million) for the Fischer Farm program [\[12\]](#src-12).

## Current operations

All four plants remain in continuous operation as of 2026, treating a combined average of roughly 96 MGD against a combined design-average capacity of roughly 96 MGD and combined peak capacity above 186 MGD (Kirie 52/110, Egan 30/50, Hanover Park 12/22, Lemont 2.3/4) [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[5\]](#src-5)[\[6\]](#src-6)[\[3\]](#src-3)[\[4\]](#src-4). All employ the standard MWRD three-stage process — primary screening and grit/solids removal, secondary biological (activated-sludge) treatment, and tertiary polishing — with tertiary treatment tailored to each plant's receiving stream: dual-media sand/anthracite filtration at Egan, sand filtration at Hanover Park, and filtration plus chlorination/dechlorination at Kirie and Lemont [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[6\]](#src-6)[\[3\]](#src-3)[\[4\]](#src-4). Treatment time from raw sewage to discharge is cited by MWRD as roughly 24 hours at Kirie, 7.8 hours at Egan (for design average flow), 12 hours at Hanover Park, and 10 hours at Lemont [\[5\]](#src-5)[\[6\]](#src-6)[\[3\]](#src-3)[\[4\]](#src-4). A 2012 Illinois Institute of Technology master's thesis that built a process-simulation model of Kirie found the plant functions in practice as "a nitrification plant with no separate distinct denitrification process," with ammonia oxidation occurring incidentally in the aeration basins rather than in a dedicated nitrification unit, and that simulated wet-weather swings in flow and temperature — rather than any single design flaw — were the dominant drivers of effluent-quality variability [\[20\]](#src-20). Kirie's current average annual day flow is about 35.8 MGD — well below its 52 MGD design average — and its treatment configuration is in fact a two-stage plant (see Original construction) whose 2nd-stage aeration/clarifier train is presently run as a pass-through rather than an active nitrification stage [\[44\]](#src-44).

An earlier, independent before/after assessment — a 1994 USGS study using MWRD/IEPA monitoring data from 1978–88 — compared Higgins Creek and Salt Creek water quality before and after Kirie (May 1980) and Egan came online, and found no uniform improvement or decline: downstream of Kirie's outfall, statistically significant *increases* in chloride, ammonia-nitrogen, phosphorus, and cyanide concentrations were measured after 1980, while numerous other constituents (chemical oxygen demand, fecal coliform, suspended solids) fell significantly — a net effect the study attributed mainly to TARP's elimination of combined-sewer overflows rather than to the treatment process itself. The same study found no major changes to Kirie's treatment process between 1978 and 1988, and confirmed the plant has chlorinated its effluent since first operating in 1980, because Higgins Creek is a general-use (not effluent-dominated) stream [\[43\]](#src-43).

Staffing is lean and scales with plant size: Kirie has 49 employees, Egan 75, Hanover Park 20, and Lemont just 3 — reflecting Lemont's status as the smallest of the system's seven plants [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[5\]](#src-5)[\[6\]](#src-6)[\[3\]](#src-3)[\[4\]](#src-4). All four plants operate under NPDES permits issued by the Illinois EPA that are periodically reissued (Kirie's current permit, No. IL0047741, was issued July 28, 2021, modified twice since, and expires July 31, 2026) [\[10\]](#src-10). All four plants hold the National Association of Clean Water Agencies' Peak Performance Platinum Award (100 percent NPDES compliance) on long, unbroken streaks: NACWA's own CY2023 and CY2024 platinum-winner lists show Kirie at "Platinum 19" (2023) and "Platinum 20" (2024) — a streak dating to 2005 — Egan at "Platinum 10" (2023) and "Platinum 11" (2024) — dating to 2014 — Hanover Park at "Platinum 16" (2023) and "Platinum 17" (2024) — dating to roughly 2008 — and Lemont at "Platinum 27" (2023) and "Platinum 28" (2024) — dating to roughly 1997, the longest streak of the four [\[29\]](#src-29)[\[30\]](#src-30).

Solids handling remains centralized and interlinked: Kirie sends all of its solids to Egan for digestion; Egan digests both its own and Kirie's sludge and forwards digested residuals to O'Brien WRP en route to Stickney for final dewatering and drying; Lemont gravity-concentrates its solids on-site — MWRD's biosolids reports classify its output as undigested or partially digested sludge — and trucks them to Calumet or Stickney for digestion and further treatment and drying; and Hanover Park is the outlier, consuming essentially all of its own biosolids locally via subsurface injection into the adjacent Fischer Farm cropland (100 acres of corn and soybeans), with harvested grain going to feedstock, ethanol, and biodiesel markets [\[5\]](#src-5)[\[6\]](#src-6)[\[4\]](#src-4)[\[3\]](#src-3). In 2024, the broader Fischer Farm Utilization Program (which also receives biosolids trucked in from the much larger Stickney and Calumet plants) land-applied 14,521 dry tons of air-dried biosolids and 1,721 dry tons of composted biosolids [reported in MWRD monitoring documents; see gaps]. Egan alone generates roughly 20 dry tons of solids per day; Lemont about 0.9 dry tons per day [\[6\]](#src-6)[\[4\]](#src-4).

Regulatory pressure on nutrient discharge is an active, system-wide current-operations theme: Illinois's total-phosphorus effluent standards (a 1.0 mg/L monthly-average limit for major dischargers) are now embedded in the plants' NPDES permits, and MWRD frames the $27 million 2026 Kirie contract and Egan's ANITA Mox and Black & Veatch pilot work explicitly around meeting these phosphorus- and nitrogen-removal mandates [\[10\]](#src-10)[\[17\]](#src-17)[\[26\]](#src-26). A 2022 phosphorus-removal feasibility study MWRD commissioned from Carollo Engineers (contract 16-RFP-21) confirms both Kirie **and Egan** carry the 1.0 mg/L limit in their reissued NPDES permits, and evaluates what additional facility improvements each plant would need to meet potential future limits of 0.5 mg/L and 0.1 mg/L — suggesting today's 1.0 mg/L requirement may not be the final tightening either plant faces [\[44\]](#src-44). Historical Kirie effluent data cited in that study (2014–2017) already averaged 0.74 mg/L total phosphorus, with a low-cost biological (rather than chemical) removal pilot pushing effluent as low as ≈0.3 mg/L under test conditions — context for why MWRD pursued the 2026 Kirie contract's "biological-process amendments" rather than a full chemical-dosing buildout [\[44\]](#src-44). System-wide, MWRD recovers phosphorus as a marketable fertilizer product (branded Crystal Green, via Ostara "Pearl" reactors) at the much larger Stickney plant, and its own fact sheets state a district-wide capacity to recover "up to 10,000 tons per year" of phosphorus, but this recovery infrastructure is centered at Stickney rather than installed at Kirie, Egan, Hanover Park, or Lemont themselves [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[6\]](#src-6).

## Notable events & incidents

- **1966–1975** — Kirie's construction site (the "Oakton-Elmhurst" parcel at Elmhurst Road and Oakton Street) was the subject of a near-decade-long fight between the City of Des Plaines and MSDGC — arguably the most contentious pre-opening history of any of the four plants, rivaling the Nixon visit for historical color even though it drew far less attention at the time: two zoning lawsuits (the first ultimately reversed in MSDGC's favor by the Illinois Supreme Court, January 25, 1971), USEPA hearing testimony that former MSDGC superintendent Vinton Bacon had turned against the site over concerns for his own family's health, a public meeting where more than 300 residents confronted MSD Superintendent Benjamin Sosewitz (August 8, 1972), a 906-signature resident petition (February 8, 1973), and a since-abandoned 1973 proposal to build the plant underground in the Swedish style to save an estimated $5–10 million [\[32\]](#src-32)[\[40\]](#src-40).
- **February 6, 1970** — President Nixon's inspection tour of Hanover Park's sewage treatment works, detailed above, remains the single most historically notable *opening-era* event associated with any of the four plants: a sitting U.S. president touring a suburban Chicago sewage plant, accompanied by four state governors, amid freezing weather and a failed sound system [\[16\]](#src-16)[\[15\]](#src-15).
- **August 1987** — A severe storm caused sewer back-up flooding across the O'Hare TARP System's combined-sewer area, damaging an estimated 6,000 homes in the Village of Mount Prospect alone (Village crews hauled away more than 287,000 pounds of flood-damaged household goods, at an estimated $285,000 cost to the Village); USACE's 1989 Phase II design memorandum for what became the Gloria Alitto Majewski Reservoir cites the flood as evidence of the O'Hare System's ongoing basement-flooding problem that the reservoir was built to address [\[41\]](#src-41).
- **September 2008** — Heavy regional rainfall required staff to sandbag Hanover Park WRP buildings against flooding, illustrating the plant's location on the flood-prone West Branch DuPage River corridor even with the adjacent Upper DuPage Reservoir in place [\[15\]](#src-15).
- **2012–2015** — Kirie's sewerthermal pilot project drew an international technical delegation: in August 2012, Brazilian wastewater-treatment students and operators toured Kirie specifically to study the heat-recovery system, an early instance of the plant serving as a showcase for the district's energy-innovation program [\[25\]](#src-25).
- **June 4, 1976 / September 3, 2025** — The sealing and, fifty years later, reopening of Egan WRP's dedication-day time capsule bookend the plant's operating history with a rare piece of institutional memory: newspapers, a commissioners' photograph, and Bicentennial-year memorabilia sealed in 1976 were unsealed at a formal 50th-anniversary ceremony in 2025 [\[14\]](#src-14).
- No major bypass, spill, or NPDES-violation incidents specific to these four plants turned up in the sources consulted for this report; NACWA's own Peak Performance Platinum records instead document a long, unbroken run of 100-percent-compliance years at all four plants (see Current operations) [\[13\]](#src-13)[\[29\]](#src-29)[\[30\]](#src-30).

## Open questions

- ~~Original construction contractors/architects and nominal construction costs for Kirie~~ — **substantially resolved for Kirie**: 1975-era MSDGC/USEPA documents name Brown and Caldwell (design engineer, contracted July 1967), DeLeuw, Cather and Company (preliminary collection-facility plans, November 1972), and Consoer, Townsend and Associates (Environmental Assessment Statement, November 1974), and give a combined 1974-era cost estimate of $197,010,000 for the O'Hare WRP plus its associated tunnels and reservoir (not broken out by component) — see Original construction [\[39\]](#src-39)[\[40\]](#src-40). Still open: the general contractor and an isolated, plant-only bid cost for Kirie's actual 1977–1980 construction, and any equivalent contractor/architect/cost information for Egan (1975–76) and Hanover Park (1963), were not located in the sources consulted. MWRD's institutional archives, Metropolitan Sanitary District annual reports from the relevant years (likely held at the Chicago Public Library Municipal Reference Collection or in MWRD's own records center), or period Chicago Tribune coverage would be the logical next sources to check for those two plants.
- **The claim that Hanover Park was "the nation's first major tertiary wastewater treatment facility"** rests on a local-history blog rather than a primary MWRD, EPA, or academic citation; a second, independent local-history source (a Hanover Park community-history blog) corroborates the general claim but in softer language — describing the plant only as "one of the first of its kind in the nation" rather than definitively "the first" — and adds an otherwise-uncorroborated anecdote that Nixon refused to drink a glass of the plant's treated water offered to him during the visit [\[31\]](#src-31). Neither source is a primary MWRD, EPA, or academic citation, so the superlative claim remains unverified at that level.
- **A precise identification of the TARP tunnel/reservoir infrastructure serving Egan's Upper Salt Creek service area** (analogous to Kirie's well-documented link to the Upper Des Plaines Tunnel System and Majewski Reservoir) was not pinned down; Upper Salt Creek is described by MWRD as within Egan's service territory and subject to ongoing stormwater/TARP watershed planning, but no dedicated "Upper Salt Creek Tunnel System" analogous to the Upper Des Plaines system was confirmed in the TARP fact sheet consulted.
- **A discrepancy in Kirie's design maximum flow** appears in the record: MWRD's own fact sheets and the Illinois EPA NPDES public notice both state 110 MGD, but one secondary compilation of 2017 discharge-monitoring-report data lists Kirie's "capacity" as 100 MGD. This report treats 110 MGD as authoritative based on the primary regulatory source.
- **Decommissioned/predecessor facilities**: this research did not find evidence that Kirie, Egan, Hanover Park, or Lemont directly replaced an earlier, now-decommissioned suburban treatment plant on the same site or serving the same population (unlike, say, older Sanitary District plants near the Chicago core). An early, unrelated 1920–1931 Sanitary District experimental sewage-works site near Maywood (on the Des Plaines River, since demolished and converted to a Forest Preserve District maintenance yard) was identified but does not appear to be a direct predecessor to Kirie or any of the other three plants; it predates the modern MWRD suburban satellite-plant program by roughly three decades and served a different purpose (process testing, not permanent treatment) [\[27\]](#src-27). One partial exception found in this update: Lemont's own present-day plant is technically not the original 1961 structure — an MWRD phosphorus-impact study documents that the November 1961 works (pump station, grit chamber, two Imhoff tanks, trickling filter, final settling tank) was demolished and replaced by an entirely new activated-sludge plant built on the same foundations in June 1972 (itself upgraded again in 1995). This is an on-site rebuild rather than a relocation, so it does not change the site-level answer to this question, but it does revise the flat "1961" framing used elsewhere in this document and in MWRD's own current fact sheet [\[42\]](#src-42).
- ~~NACWA Peak Performance Award histories for Kirie and Egan~~ — **resolved**: directly verified against NACWA's own CY2023 and CY2024 Peak Platinum winner-list PDFs, which name all four plants explicitly with consecutive-year counts (see Current operations). One remaining soft spot: the "since" years given here for Hanover Park (~2008) and Lemont (~1997) are inferred by subtracting the listed streak-length from the award year rather than read directly off a document that states a start year, so they could be off by a year depending on NACWA's exact counting convention.
- **Hanover Park and Lemont's own current-decade capital projects** were less thoroughly documented in public sources than Kirie's and Egan's; beyond the Fischer Farm biosolids contract and a small tunnel-rehabilitation line item, this research did not surface a major recent (2020s) capital project narrative for Hanover Park comparable to Lemont's well-documented Wet Weather Treatment Facility or Kirie's phosphorus-removal contract.
- ~~The exact timing of Kirie's 1.0 mg/L total-phosphorus NPDES limit~~ — **substantially resolved**: Illinois EPA's 2013 draft-permit fact sheet for Kirie already lists a 1.0 mg/L monthly-average / 434 lb/day phosphorus figure in its load-limit table, flagged there as subject to a "Schedule for compliance with a phosphorus effluent limit" (Special Condition 22) rather than an immediately enforced limit. A 2022 MWRD-commissioned Carollo Engineers study independently states that Kirie *and* Egan had, by April 2022, "recently received" a 1.0 mg/L limit in "newly reissued" NPDES permits — consistent with Kirie's July 28, 2021 permit reissuance and confirming the compliance schedule had by then converted into a binding limit, though the 2021/2022 permit text itself was still not directly obtained [\[44\]](#src-44)[\[10\]](#src-10).
- **Independent verification of Egan's ANITA Mox deammonification system** relies on a single 2017 Water Environment Federation conference paper (Lai et al.) rather than an MWRD-authored primary source; MWRD's own current fact sheets and webpages do not mention the system.
