# The Suburban Satellite Plants: Kirie, Egan, Hanover Park, and Lemont Water Reclamation Plants

## Role in the system

The Metropolitan Water Reclamation District of Greater Chicago (MWRD) operates seven water reclamation plants (WRPs) across its roughly 882–883-square-mile service area covering Chicago and 125–128 Cook County suburbs (the exact community count varies slightly by fact-sheet vintage) [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[5\]](#src-5)[\[6\]](#src-6). Two of the seven — Stickney and Calumet — are the enormous, century-old plants that anchor the system's Deep Tunnel-fed core. The other five are smaller "satellite" plants built or expanded in the mid-to-late 20th century to serve suburban areas too far from the core interceptors to be economical to pipe downtown. This document covers four of those satellites, all in the northwest and southwest suburbs: **James C. Kirie WRP** (Des Plaines), **John E. Egan WRP** (Schaumburg), **Hanover Park WRP**, and **Lemont WRP** [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[2\]](#src-2)[\[3\]](#src-3)[\[4\]](#src-4).

- **Kirie WRP** (701 West Oakton Street, Des Plaines) receives sanitary flow from local sewers in Des Plaines, Elk Grove Village, Mount Prospect, Arlington Heights, Rolling Meadows, Wheeling, Prospect Heights, Buffalo Grove, Elk Grove Township, and the Forest River and Northfield Wood Sanitary Districts, treats it, and discharges the cleaned effluent to **Higgins Creek**, a small tributary of the Des Plaines River near O'Hare International Airport [\[10\]](#src-10). Kirie is a "satellite" plant in the specific engineering sense that it performs no on-site solids digestion: raw sludge pumped off during primary and secondary treatment is piped to the **Egan WRP** for digestion, then on to **O'Brien WRP**, and finally to **Stickney WRP** for final dewatering and drying [\[5\]](#src-5). Combined-sewer overflow (CSO) from Kirie's service area is captured by the Upper Des Plaines Tunnel System, a small, self-contained component of the Tunnel and Reservoir Plan (TARP) that stores flow in the Gloria Alitto Majewski Reservoir near O'Hare — 0.35 BG (350 million gallons) per MWRD's TARP fact sheet, though MWRD's own Kirie-specific fact sheet states 343 million gallons [\[5\]](#src-5)[\[9\]](#src-9)[\[23\]](#src-23)[\[24\]](#src-24) — before pumping it back to Kirie for treatment.

- **Egan WRP** (550 South Meacham Road, Schaumburg) receives flow from Schaumburg, Arlington Heights, Elk Grove Village, Hoffman Estates, Inverness, Palatine, Rolling Meadows, and Roselle, and discharges to **Upper Salt Creek**, part of the Des Plaines River watershed [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[6\]](#src-6). Egan is a hub in the suburban solids-handling chain: it digests not only its own sludge but also the sludge trucked/piped in from Kirie, and its own digested residuals continue downstream to O'Brien WRP en route to Stickney for final processing [\[6\]](#src-6). The Upper Salt Creek watershed that Egan serves is itself the subject of ongoing MWRD stormwater and TARP planning, since Salt Creek has historically been prone to CSOs and flooding in the built-out Schaumburg/Elk Grove corridor [\[24\]](#src-24).

- **Hanover Park WRP** (1200 Sycamore Avenue, Hanover Park) receives flow from Hanover Park, Bartlett, Schaumburg, and Hoffman Estates in northwestern Cook and DuPage counties and discharges to the **West Branch of the DuPage River** [\[3\]](#src-3)[\[7\]](#src-7). Uniquely among the four plants, essentially all of its biosolids are consumed on-site: since 1969 the MWRD has applied Class B liquid biosolids by subsurface injection to the adjacent 200-acre **Fischer Farm**, which also captures roughly 75 million gallons of stormwater overflow in the on-site Upper DuPage Reservoir [\[3\]](#src-3)[\[7\]](#src-7).

- **Lemont WRP** (13 Stephen Street, Lemont) is the smallest of the seven plants and serves only the Village of Lemont, discharging to the **Chicago Sanitary and Ship Canal** — meaning its effluent flows directly into the engineered channel that reversed the Chicago River and ultimately reaches the Illinois and Mississippi Rivers via the Des Plaines and Illinois waterways [\[4\]](#src-4)[\[9\]](#src-9). Solids removed at Lemont — about 0.9 dry tons per day — are digested on-site and then trucked to the Calumet or Stickney WRPs for further treatment and drying [\[4\]](#src-4)[\[8\]](#src-8). Since opening in 1961, the plant has cleaned more than 42 billion gallons of wastewater [\[8\]](#src-8).

All four plants sit downstream of local combined and separate sewer networks and upstream of MWRD's TARP "Deep Tunnel" system, which captures wet-weather combined sewage that would otherwise overflow directly into these same waterways, storing it until the plants have capacity to treat it [\[9\]](#src-9).

## Original construction

None of these four plants was part of the original Sanitary District of Chicago's 1889–1928 canal-and-dilution system; all were built decades later by the district under its 1955–1988 name, the **Metropolitan Sanitary District of Greater Chicago**, as suburban Cook County urbanized after World War II and Illinois (then federal) water-quality law increasingly required treatment rather than dilution [\[22\]](#src-22). (The district was renamed the Metropolitan Water Reclamation District of Greater Chicago effective January 1, 1989 [\[22\]](#src-22).)

**Hanover Park** was the earliest of the four, entering service July 19, 1963, in the midst of Chicago's postwar suburban building boom in northwestern Cook County [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[3\]](#src-3). Roughly a decade later it became nationally notable: a secondary source describes Hanover Park's sand-filtration tertiary system, installed around 1971, as "the first of their kind to go into service" and states the plant was "the nation's first major tertiary wastewater treatment facility" [\[15\]](#src-15); a second, independent local-history source corroborates the general idea but in softer language, describing the 1970 plant Nixon visited only as "one of the first of its kind in the nation" [\[31\]](#src-31). That claim is corroborated by an extraordinary documented event: on **February 6, 1970**, President Richard Nixon personally toured the "Hanover Sewage Treatment Works" as part of a Chicago-area environmental-policy trip, accompanied by the governors of Illinois (Richard Ogilvie), Indiana (Edgar Whitcomb), Michigan (William Milliken), and Wisconsin (Warren Knowles), White House aide John Ehrlichman, and Benjamin Sosewitz, then Acting General Manager of the Metropolitan Sanitary District [\[16\]](#src-16)[\[15\]](#src-15). H. R. Haldeman's diary records that area schools let out for the visit, crowds were "very enthusiastic," Nixon "did not bring his overcoat" despite freezing conditions, and the sound system failed, forcing Nixon to address the crowd with a bullhorn [\[16\]](#src-16). The visit came the same day as a meeting of the Cabinet Committee on the Environment and the Council on Environmental Quality held not at the White House but at the Field Museum of Natural History in Chicago — attended by CEQ Chairman Russell Train, the same four governors, and other Cabinet members — situating Hanover Park's advanced treatment technology as a showcase for the administration's emerging clean-water agenda a few months before the EPA's creation later that year [\[16\]](#src-16).

**Egan WRP** followed, entering service December 16, 1975, and receiving a formal dedication ceremony on **June 4, 1976** — bicentennial year, a detail later commemorated in a plant time capsule (see Notable events, below) [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[6\]](#src-6)[\[14\]](#src-14). The plant is named for **John E. Egan**, who was elected a Commissioner of the Metropolitan Sanitary District on November 6, 1962, and served as **President of the district's Board of Commissioners from December 6, 1966, to December 3, 1974** — meaning the plant that bears his name opened roughly a year after his board tenure ended, honoring the sitting district president under whom it was planned and largely built [\[21\]](#src-21)[\[1\]](#src-1). Egan's design incorporated what MWRD's own materials describe as "a novel two-stage secondary process" together with an "advanced tertiary system," at a time when few suburban Chicago-area plants offered tertiary treatment at all [\[6\]](#src-6). The design proved durable: in 1982, seven years after opening, the U.S. EPA's Region V office selected Egan as the "best operated and maintained" large treatment plant in the six-state region [\[13\]](#src-13).

**Kirie WRP** was the last and largest of the four to be built, opening **May 12, 1980**, as the MWRD's seventh and (at the time) newest plant overall [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[5\]](#src-5). It is named for **James C. Kirie**, elected a Sanitary District/MWRD Commissioner on December 8, 1970, re-elected in 1976 and 1982, and serving on the board until December 6, 1988; MWRD's own materials describe him as "a strong supporter of the MWRD's Tunnel and Reservoir Plan (TARP)" [\[5\]](#src-5)[\[21\]](#src-21). At its 1980 opening, Kirie was described by the district as the first of its plants "to feature fully automated operations," at a time when the district's other six plants (including the newly commissioned Egan) still ran on a mix of computerized and manual control [\[5\]](#src-5). Kirie was engineered from the outset as a satellite/"no digestion" plant, piping its solids to Egan rather than digesting on site, which kept its 108-acre footprint comparatively compact for a 52 MGD (110 MGD peak) facility [\[5\]](#src-5). Specific bid costs, architect-of-record, and general contractor for Kirie's original 1980 construction were not identified in the sources consulted for this report (see Open questions).

**Lemont WRP** is something of an outlier chronologically, dating to **November 1961** — slightly earlier than Hanover Park — but the smallest by an order of magnitude (2.3 MGD average versus Hanover Park's 12 MGD) [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[4\]](#src-4). Lemont's siting on the Chicago Sanitary and Ship Canal put it within a few miles of the district's original 1900 drainage-canal engineering works around Lockport and Romeoville, but Lemont itself was a purpose-built, free-standing local plant rather than an extension of the Sanitary District's original canal/dilution infrastructure [\[4\]](#src-4).

## Upgrades & modernization timeline

- **1969** — MWRD purchases the 200-acre Fischer Farm adjoining Hanover Park WRP and constructs the Upper DuPage Reservoir (≈75 million gallons of stormwater storage capacity), establishing what remains an active biosolids land-application program to this day [\[3\]](#src-3).
- **ca. 1971** — Hanover Park WRP installs sand-media tertiary filters, described by a secondary source as the first such filters in the nation to enter service; the installation prompts President Nixon's February 1970 visit and subsequent dedication-era attention (visit predates filter completion, consistent with a groundbreaking/preview visit) [\[15\]](#src-15)[\[16\]](#src-16).
- **June 4, 1976** — Formal dedication of Egan WRP, one year after it entered service; ceremony guest register, a same-day Chicago Tribune, a photo of the sitting Board of Commissioners, a Bicentennial coin proof/set, and 1976 postage stamps are sealed into a plant time capsule [\[14\]](#src-14).
- **1982** — U.S. EPA Region V names Egan WRP the "best operated and maintained" large treatment plant in the region, seven years after opening [\[13\]](#src-13).
- **1998** — TARP's Gloria Alitto Majewski Reservoir (350 million gallons per MWRD's TARP fact sheet; 343 million gallons per MWRD's Kirie fact sheet) is completed near O'Hare Field as the terminal storage element of the Upper Des Plaines Tunnel System, providing combined-sewage storage for the communities in Kirie WRP's service area before the stored flow is pumped to Kirie for treatment [\[5\]](#src-5)[\[9\]](#src-9)[\[23\]](#src-23)[\[24\]](#src-24).
- **2012** — Kirie WRP commissions a "sewerthermal" demonstration energy-recovery project developed with the University of Illinois at Chicago (led by UIC chemical-engineering department head Sohail Murad and engineering dean Peter Nelson), supported by an $87,500 grant from the Illinois Clean Energy Community Foundation. The system extracts heat from the plant's ~55°F aeration-tank effluent to precondition heating/cooling water for the administration building, cutting that building's heating/AC costs by about 50 percent; neither of the two sources describing the project quantifies a plant-wide (as opposed to administration-building) energy-savings percentage, so no plant-wide figure is asserted here [\[11\]](#src-11)[\[25\]](#src-25).
- **February 12, 2013** — Illinois Governor Pat Quinn announces a $30 million low-interest loan to MWRD under the Illinois Clean Water Initiative (part of a $250 million statewide package) to fund a new Wet Weather Treatment Facility and 5-million-gallon underground storage reservoir at Lemont WRP, aimed at reducing storm-driven overflow discharges to the Sanitary and Ship Canal [\[18\]](#src-18).
- **2013–2018** — Lemont's Wet Weather Treatment Facility and Reservoir is designed by CH2M Hill and built by general contractor Joseph J. Henderson & Son, Inc. under Contract 10-716-3P at a value of $29,663,517; the underground concrete reservoir measures roughly 65.5 × 50 × 9 meters deep with walls up to 1 meter thick, and the project includes a new pump station, an emergency high-level-overflow diversion structure, a separator, and a disinfection system. The project later receives the LCCA APEX (Award for Project Excellence) [\[19\]](#src-19)[\[18\]](#src-18).
- **2015** — Egan WRP earns the National Association of Clean Water Agencies (NACWA) Gold Award for 100 percent compliance with its NPDES permit for a full calendar year, and installs a donated 45-panel solar-thermal array generating an estimated 2,040 therms per year to preheat boiler water; a permeable parking lot is planned for the following year to improve on-site stormwater management [\[13\]](#src-13).
- **August 2016** — Egan WRP brings online an ANITA Mox deammonification system for sidestream (centrate) treatment, converting four previously unused dissolved-air-flotation thickening tanks into one equalization tank and four parallel ANITA Mox bioreactors to strip ammonia and nitrogen from digester sidestream before it re-enters the main treatment train, designed to treat 870 m³/d (0.23 MGD) of centrate at ~1,080 mg/L NH₃-N with a guaranteed removal efficiency above 75 percent [\[28\]](#src-28). (Note: the Wastewater Digest "40 years" retrospective [\[13\]](#src-13) predates this installation — published December 2015 — and does not describe it; the ANITA Mox startup is documented instead in a Water Environment Federation conference paper by MWRD/vendor engineers.)
- **2022 (permit modification, effective 2022)** — Kirie WRP's NPDES permit (No. IL0047741) is modified regarding a Total Phosphorus effluent limit of 1.0 mg/L monthly average (434 lb/day, based on the 52 MGD design average flow), part of Illinois's system-wide phosphorus-reduction push affecting major dischargers statewide. Note that this same 1.0 mg/L / 434 lb/day figure already appears in Illinois EPA's 2013 draft-permit fact sheet for Kirie, listed there as a "Special Condition" establishing a compliance schedule for a future phosphorus limit rather than an immediately enforced one; the 2022 modification most likely converts that long-standing compliance-schedule target into a final, enforceable limit, but this report could not directly confirm that transition from the 2021/2022 permit text itself [10; permit expiration July 31, 2026].
- **February 2024** — MWRD approves a research partnership with Black & Veatch and Wastewater Microbiology Solutions at Egan WRP to pilot process optimizations for energy efficiency, phosphorus removal, activated-sludge densification, carbon management, and anaerobic-digestion energy recovery; pilot testing was slated to begin in Q3 2024 with a final report due winter 2026 [\[26\]](#src-26).
- **September 3, 2025** — MWRD marks Egan WRP's 50th anniversary, opening the 1976 dedication-ceremony time capsule described above [\[14\]](#src-14).
- **February 5, 2026** — The MWRD Board approves a $27 million construction contract with Sollitt/Broadway Joint Venture for electric-power-distribution and phosphorus-removal improvements at Kirie WRP, covering electrical system upgrades and biological-process amendments needed to meet new phosphorus-removal requirements [\[17\]](#src-17).
- **2026 (in progress)** — The "Kirie–Egan Solids Pipeline Rehabilitation, Section No. 2" contract ($12.5 million, bond-funded) rehabilitates the interconnecting solids pipeline between the two plants; a separate "Gate Control Equipment Upgrade at TARP Control Structures, KWRP" contract ($3 million) and a "Tunnel Rehabilitation, HPWRP" contract ($250,000) are also active in MWRD's current capital program, alongside a continuing "Biosolids Injection and Farming, HPWRP" service contract ($1.34 million) for the Fischer Farm program [\[12\]](#src-12).

## Current operations

All four plants remain in continuous operation as of 2026, treating a combined average of roughly 96 MGD against a combined design-average capacity of roughly 96 MGD and combined peak capacity above 186 MGD (Kirie 52/110, Egan 30/50, Hanover Park 12/22, Lemont 2.3/4) [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[5\]](#src-5)[\[6\]](#src-6)[\[3\]](#src-3)[\[4\]](#src-4). All employ the standard MWRD three-stage process — primary screening and grit/solids removal, secondary biological (activated-sludge) treatment, and tertiary polishing — with tertiary treatment tailored to each plant's receiving stream: dual-media sand/anthracite filtration at Egan, sand filtration at Hanover Park, and filtration plus chlorination/dechlorination at Kirie and Lemont [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[6\]](#src-6)[\[3\]](#src-3)[\[4\]](#src-4). Treatment time from raw sewage to discharge is cited by MWRD as roughly 24 hours at Kirie, 7.8 hours at Egan (for design average flow), 12 hours at Hanover Park, and 10 hours at Lemont [\[5\]](#src-5)[\[6\]](#src-6)[\[3\]](#src-3)[\[4\]](#src-4). A 2012 Illinois Institute of Technology master's thesis that built a process-simulation model of Kirie found the plant functions in practice as "a nitrification plant with no separate distinct denitrification process," with ammonia oxidation occurring incidentally in the aeration basins rather than in a dedicated nitrification unit, and that simulated wet-weather swings in flow and temperature — rather than any single design flaw — were the dominant drivers of effluent-quality variability [\[20\]](#src-20).

Staffing is lean and scales with plant size: Kirie has 49 employees, Egan 75, Hanover Park 20, and Lemont just 3 — reflecting Lemont's status as the smallest of the system's seven plants [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[5\]](#src-5)[\[6\]](#src-6)[\[3\]](#src-3)[\[4\]](#src-4). All four plants operate under NPDES permits issued by the Illinois EPA that are periodically reissued (Kirie's current permit, No. IL0047741, was issued July 28, 2021, modified twice since, and expires July 31, 2026) [\[10\]](#src-10). All four plants hold the National Association of Clean Water Agencies' Peak Performance Platinum Award (100 percent NPDES compliance) on long, unbroken streaks: NACWA's own CY2023 and CY2024 platinum-winner lists show Kirie at "Platinum 19" (2023) and "Platinum 20" (2024) — a streak dating to 2005 — Egan at "Platinum 10" (2023) and "Platinum 11" (2024) — dating to 2014 — Hanover Park at "Platinum 16" (2023) and "Platinum 17" (2024) — dating to roughly 2008 — and Lemont at "Platinum 27" (2023) and "Platinum 28" (2024) — dating to roughly 1997, the longest streak of the four [\[29\]](#src-29)[\[30\]](#src-30).

Solids handling remains centralized and interlinked: Kirie sends all of its solids to Egan for digestion; Egan digests both its own and Kirie's sludge and forwards digested residuals to O'Brien WRP en route to Stickney for final dewatering and drying; Lemont digests on-site but trucks the resulting sludge to Calumet or Stickney for further treatment and drying; and Hanover Park is the outlier, consuming essentially all of its own biosolids locally via subsurface injection into the adjacent Fischer Farm cropland (100 acres of corn and soybeans), with harvested grain going to feedstock, ethanol, and biodiesel markets [\[5\]](#src-5)[\[6\]](#src-6)[\[4\]](#src-4)[\[3\]](#src-3). In 2024, the broader Fischer Farm Utilization Program (which also receives biosolids trucked in from the much larger Stickney and Calumet plants) land-applied 14,521 dry tons of air-dried biosolids and 1,721 dry tons of composted biosolids [reported in MWRD monitoring documents; see gaps]. Egan alone generates roughly 20 dry tons of solids per day; Lemont about 0.9 dry tons per day [\[6\]](#src-6)[\[4\]](#src-4).

Regulatory pressure on nutrient discharge is an active, system-wide current-operations theme: Illinois's total-phosphorus effluent standards (a 1.0 mg/L monthly-average limit for major dischargers) are now embedded in the plants' NPDES permits, and MWRD frames the $27 million 2026 Kirie contract and Egan's ANITA Mox and Black & Veatch pilot work explicitly around meeting these phosphorus- and nitrogen-removal mandates [\[10\]](#src-10)[\[17\]](#src-17)[\[26\]](#src-26). System-wide, MWRD recovers phosphorus as a marketable fertilizer product (branded Crystal Green, via Ostara "Pearl" reactors) at the much larger Stickney plant, and its own fact sheets state a district-wide capacity to recover "up to 10,000 tons per year" of phosphorus, but this recovery infrastructure is centered at Stickney rather than installed at Kirie, Egan, Hanover Park, or Lemont themselves [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[6\]](#src-6).

## Notable events & incidents

- **February 6, 1970** — President Nixon's inspection tour of Hanover Park's sewage treatment works, detailed above, remains the single most historically notable event associated with any of the four plants: a sitting U.S. president touring a suburban Chicago sewage plant, accompanied by four state governors, amid freezing weather and a failed sound system [\[16\]](#src-16)[\[15\]](#src-15).
- **September 2008** — Heavy regional rainfall required staff to sandbag Hanover Park WRP buildings against flooding, illustrating the plant's location on the flood-prone West Branch DuPage River corridor even with the adjacent Upper DuPage Reservoir in place [\[15\]](#src-15).
- **2012–2015** — Kirie's sewerthermal pilot project drew an international technical delegation: in August 2012, Brazilian wastewater-treatment students and operators toured Kirie specifically to study the heat-recovery system, an early instance of the plant serving as a showcase for the district's energy-innovation program [\[25\]](#src-25).
- **June 4, 1976 / September 3, 2025** — The sealing and, fifty years later, reopening of Egan WRP's dedication-day time capsule bookend the plant's operating history with a rare piece of institutional memory: newspapers, a commissioners' photograph, and Bicentennial-year memorabilia sealed in 1976 were unsealed at a formal 50th-anniversary ceremony in 2025 [\[14\]](#src-14).
- No major bypass, spill, or NPDES-violation incidents specific to these four plants turned up in the sources consulted for this report; NACWA's own Peak Performance Platinum records instead document a long, unbroken run of 100-percent-compliance years at all four plants (see Current operations) [\[13\]](#src-13)[\[29\]](#src-29)[\[30\]](#src-30).

## Open questions

- **Original construction contractors/architects and nominal construction costs** for Kirie (1980), Egan (1975–76), and Hanover Park (1963) were not located in the sources consulted. MWRD's institutional archives, Metropolitan Sanitary District annual reports from the relevant years (likely held at the Chicago Public Library Municipal Reference Collection or in MWRD's own records center), or period Chicago Tribune coverage would be the logical next sources to check.
- **The claim that Hanover Park was "the nation's first major tertiary wastewater treatment facility"** rests on a local-history blog rather than a primary MWRD, EPA, or academic citation; a second, independent local-history source (a Hanover Park community-history blog) corroborates the general claim but in softer language — describing the plant only as "one of the first of its kind in the nation" rather than definitively "the first" — and adds an otherwise-uncorroborated anecdote that Nixon refused to drink a glass of the plant's treated water offered to him during the visit [\[31\]](#src-31). Neither source is a primary MWRD, EPA, or academic citation, so the superlative claim remains unverified at that level.
- **A precise identification of the TARP tunnel/reservoir infrastructure serving Egan's Upper Salt Creek service area** (analogous to Kirie's well-documented link to the Upper Des Plaines Tunnel System and Majewski Reservoir) was not pinned down; Upper Salt Creek is described by MWRD as within Egan's service territory and subject to ongoing stormwater/TARP watershed planning, but no dedicated "Upper Salt Creek Tunnel System" analogous to the Upper Des Plaines system was confirmed in the TARP fact sheet consulted.
- **A discrepancy in Kirie's design maximum flow** appears in the record: MWRD's own fact sheets and the Illinois EPA NPDES public notice both state 110 MGD, but one secondary compilation of 2017 discharge-monitoring-report data lists Kirie's "capacity" as 100 MGD. This report treats 110 MGD as authoritative based on the primary regulatory source.
- **Decommissioned/predecessor facilities**: this research did not find evidence that Kirie, Egan, Hanover Park, or Lemont directly replaced an earlier, now-decommissioned suburban treatment plant on the same site or serving the same population (unlike, say, older Sanitary District plants near the Chicago core). An early, unrelated 1920–1931 Sanitary District experimental sewage-works site near Maywood (on the Des Plaines River, since demolished and converted to a Forest Preserve District maintenance yard) was identified but does not appear to be a direct predecessor to Kirie or any of the other three plants; it predates the modern MWRD suburban satellite-plant program by roughly three decades and served a different purpose (process testing, not permanent treatment) [\[27\]](#src-27).
- ~~NACWA Peak Performance Award histories for Kirie and Egan~~ — **resolved**: directly verified against NACWA's own CY2023 and CY2024 Peak Platinum winner-list PDFs, which name all four plants explicitly with consecutive-year counts (see Current operations). One remaining soft spot: the "since" years given here for Hanover Park (~2008) and Lemont (~1997) are inferred by subtracting the listed streak-length from the award year rather than read directly off a document that states a start year, so they could be off by a year depending on NACWA's exact counting convention.
- **Hanover Park and Lemont's own current-decade capital projects** were less thoroughly documented in public sources than Kirie's and Egan's; beyond the Fischer Farm biosolids contract and a small tunnel-rehabilitation line item, this research did not surface a major recent (2020s) capital project narrative for Hanover Park comparable to Lemont's well-documented Wet Weather Treatment Facility or Kirie's phosphorus-removal contract.
- **The exact timing of Kirie's 1.0 mg/L total-phosphorus NPDES limit is unresolved.** Illinois EPA's 2013 draft-permit fact sheet for Kirie already lists a 1.0 mg/L monthly-average / 434 lb/day phosphorus figure in its load-limit table, flagged there as subject to a "Schedule for compliance with a phosphorus effluent limit" (Special Condition 22) rather than an immediately enforced limit. This document's claim that the limit was "added" by a 2022 permit modification most likely describes that compliance schedule finally converting into a hard limit, but the 2021-issued/2022-modified permit text itself was not directly obtained to confirm this.
- **Independent verification of Egan's ANITA Mox deammonification system** relies on a single 2017 Water Environment Federation conference paper (Lai et al.) rather than an MWRD-authored primary source; MWRD's own current fact sheets and webpages do not mention the system.
