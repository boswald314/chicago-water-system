# Lake Michigan Water Intake Cribs — Every Crib Ever Built

*Research compiled July 2026. All sources accessed July 2026.*

## Role in the system

The water intake cribs are offshore structures — essentially armored islands — built to house and protect the mouths of water-supply tunnels bored beneath the bed of Lake Michigan. They are the very first link in Chicago's drinking-water chain: raw lake water enters through intake ports in the crib walls at depths of roughly 20 to 30 feet below the surface, drops down a central well shaft inside the crib, and flows **by gravity** through brick- or concrete-lined tunnels as much as 200 feet beneath the lakebed to intake basins on shore [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[16\]](#src-16). From those shore basins the water passes to the city's two purification plants — the **James W. Jardine Water Purification Plant** just north of Navy Pier (north/central system) and the **Eugene Sawyer Water Purification Plant** at 79th Street (south system) — and after treatment is lifted by the Department of Water Management's pumping stations into the distribution grid serving Chicago and about 120 suburban communities, roughly 5 million consumers in all, at a rate of almost 1 billion gallons per day [\[10\]](#src-10)[\[16\]](#src-16)[\[21\]](#src-21).

The system exists because of sewage. Nineteenth-century Chicago discharged its wastes into the Chicago River and thence the lake, fouling the shoreline intakes; every heavy rain pushed a plume of filth over the shore inlets, and the city suffered repeated cholera, dysentery and typhoid epidemics [\[2\]](#src-2)[\[18\]](#src-18). City engineer Ellis S. Chesbrough's solution of 1863 — go two miles offshore, beyond the sewage field, via a tunnel under the lake — created the crib-and-tunnel template that Chicago repeated eight more times over the following seventy years [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[2\]](#src-2)[\[18\]](#src-18). The cribs are thus the intake-side complement of the same sanitary strategy that later produced the Sanitary & Ship Canal and river reversal: keep the sewage moving away from the spot where the drinking water comes in.

Nine permanent cribs were built between 1865 and 1935 (plus at least one temporary "intermediate" construction crib, site of the deadly 1909 fire). Six still stand; exactly **two remain in active service** — the Edward F. Dunne Crib (paired with the inactive 68th Street Crib, feeding the Sawyer plant) and the William E. Dever Crib (paired with the unusable Carter H. Harrison Crib, feeding the Jardine plant) [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[10\]](#src-10).

## Original construction

Chicago's very first attempt at a lake-water intake crib predates Chesbrough by more than a decade and ended in failure. Engineer **William J. McAlpine** surveyed the city's original 1852 waterworks plan (estimated cost $335,500), which specified a timber crib **20 by 40 feet** to be sunk 600 feet offshore near Chicago Avenue, feeding a 30-inch wooden inlet main to the pump well; work began in the summer of 1852, but "the boisterous condition of the lake rendered it difficult to secure the crib in place," and the effort was abandoned, with water instead drawn through a pipe close to shore [\[32\]](#src-32).

The template that succeeded was set by **Ellis Sylvester Chesbrough** (1813–1886), Chicago's chief engineer of the Board of Sewerage Commissioners, who had built Boston's water system before Chicago recruited him in 1855 [\[2\]](#src-2)[\[24\]](#src-24). With the shore intakes fouled by sewage, Chesbrough's 1863 plan proposed what contemporaries considered folly: a brick-lined tunnel five feet in diameter, driven 10,567 feet (two miles) out under the lake through the blue clay of the lakebed, to a protected intake anchored offshore [\[2\]](#src-2)[\[18\]](#src-18)[\[23\]](#src-23).

The contract went to **Dull & Gowan of Harrisburg, Pennsylvania** (October 28, 1863) per one lighthouse-history source [\[7\]](#src-7) — though the City's own 1933 Bureau of Engineering retrospective instead names the contractors **J. J. Dull and James McGowan of Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania**, awarded the contract in October 1863 as the second-lowest of seven bidders at a bid price of **$315,139**; the finished project's total cost was **$457,845**, of which **$380,784** was actually paid to the contractors including extras [\[33\]](#src-33) — work commenced in spring 1864 [\[7\]](#src-7). The **crib** — the offshore anchor and armor for the intake shaft — gave the structures their name: a "crib" in Great Lakes practice is a timber box built like ship's hull. The Two-Mile Crib was a pentagonal timber structure 40 feet high with five 58-foot sides, three concentric bolted walls caulked and tarred like a ship, divided into 15 watertight compartments around a 25-foot central well; it consumed 618,325 board-feet of lumber (mostly white pine, with white oak) [\[2\]](#src-2). It was **built on shore, launched on July 24, 1865, towed out by steam tugs, and sunk on station by filling its compartments with stone ballast** [\[2\]](#src-2)[\[7\]](#src-7)[\[9\]](#src-9)[\[33\]](#src-33) — a lake storm a few days after the launch disturbed the crib's position before it was finally secured [\[33\]](#src-33). An iron cylinder was lowered down the well to form the intake shaft, and a lighthouse and keeper's quarters were built on top [\[7\]](#src-7)[\[9\]](#src-9).

Tunneling proceeded simultaneously landward from the crib shaft and lakeward from the Chicago Avenue shore shaft. Two shifts a day of miners hand-dug the clay, hauling spoil in small mule-drawn railcars, while a third shift of masons lined the five-foot bore with two rings of brick [\[2\]](#src-2)[\[5\]](#src-5)[\[18\]](#src-18). The headings met at **3:40 p.m. on November 30, 1866**, less than seven inches out of alignment — a celebrated feat of Victorian surveying [\[2\]](#src-2)[\[18\]](#src-18)[\[33\]](#src-33); Mayor **John B. Rice** laid the tunnel's last masonry stone in a formal ceremony on **December 6, 1866** [\[33\]](#src-33). Water was first let into the tunnel on **March 8, 1867** — not December 1866, as sometimes stated; only the horizontal portion was filled at first, "this precaution being taken to avoid too sudden pressure on the masonry" [\[36\]](#src-36)[\[33\]](#src-33) — and after the tunnel was pumped out for a final inspection it was officially dedicated and put into service on **March 25, 1867** [\[33\]](#src-33)[\[36\]](#src-36), to national acclaim as the "wonder of America and of the world," and the whole project closed out at $380,784 [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[2\]](#src-2)[\[18\]](#src-18)[\[19\]](#src-19). The full primary-source narrative is *The Great Chicago Lake Tunnel* (J.M. Wing & Co., 1867) [\[19\]](#src-19).

Every later crib elaborated on this method. The Four-Mile Crib (designed by **D.H. Hayes**, built 1887–1894) is a masonry-clad crib with Romanesque arched brick windows and an integral lighthouse, deliberately architectural because it stood in public view [\[4\]](#src-4)[\[15\]](#src-15); its intake shaft was protected by a crib of two concentric steel shells on a timber grillage, sunk in October 1889 in 39 feet of water, while contractor **A. Onderdonk**'s tunnel work ran into soft, swelling clay and quicksand and had to detour around an abandoned temporary construction crib with a 350-foot northward deflection [\[33\]](#src-33). The Carter H. Harrison Crib (1900) followed the same pattern at larger scale: the "Northeast Lake Tunnel" contract was let July 6, 1896, the tunnel completed January 1899, and the crib's five construction contracts (general work; steel and iron; heating/tanks/pumps; plumbing; electric plant) were let in July 1898 and finished by November 1899, under assistant engineer **H. H. Jackman**; the finished crib cost $192,441.40, and a temporary intermediate construction crib associated with this era of lake-tunnel building — removed under an August 1, 1899 contract for $78,061.81 including removal — was also documented in the City's own records, closing part of the long-standing question of what became of the era's other temporary construction cribs [\[32\]](#src-32)[\[34\]](#src-34)[\[35\]](#src-35). The twentieth-century cribs shifted to **steel caissons sunk in place**: the Wilson Avenue Crib (1915–18) began with a 90-foot-diameter steel caisson sunk to the lake bottom, then a granite-block superstructure; when the nearly finished superstructure was found to be **18 inches out of plumb** from uneven settling, contractors blasted away the concrete joining it to the caisson and re-leveled it with **300 jacks** (another account: hydraulic cement pumped beneath the low side), delaying completion to May 1, 1918 [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[12\]](#src-12)[\[31\]](#src-31). The Edward F. Dunne Crib's own Southwest Land & Lake Tunnel project — crib, tunnel, and three planned pumping stations together — was estimated in 1912 at "in the neighborhood of eight millions of dollars" [\[36\]](#src-36). The Dever Crib (1934–35) was a Depression-era steel-and-concrete design whose well house was fabricated onshore and sunk on station; one account puts the combined crib-plus-tunnel project cost at roughly **$13 million (1930s dollars)**, though this figure comes from a low-authority source and needs verification [\[8\]](#src-8)[\[22\]](#src-22) — its physical dimensions (75-ft outside dia., 190-ft shaft depth) are, however, independently confirmed by the City's own 1956 engineering description [\[37\]](#src-37). Its companion 16-foot-diameter Chicago Avenue lake tunnel (completed 1936) ran 11.5 miles at 130–200 feet down, blasted through bedrock with some **3,000,000 pounds of dynamite** — "one of the greatest single engineering jobs in the world," the Tribune boasted in 1938 [\[11\]](#src-11).

## Upgrades & modernization timeline

- **1874** — Second lake tunnel (7-ft dia.), total cost ~$1,000,000 [\[32\]](#src-32), laid 40 ft [\[32\]](#src-32) (other sources: 50 ft) from the 1867 bore, extended ~6 miles (31,490 ft total incl. land extension) inland to the 22nd St./Ashland Ave. (West Side) pumping station, doubling the Two-Mile Crib's draw [\[2\]](#src-2)[\[11\]](#src-11)[\[33\]](#src-33).
- **1889** — Four-Mile Crib's steel-and-timber intake shaft sunk in October [\[33\]](#src-33); Lake View and Hyde Park/Town of Lake waterworks (the future 68th St. system) annexed to the city June 29, 1889 [\[32\]](#src-32).
- **1891/92** — Four-Mile Crib and its new lake tunnel enter service (tunnelers fought running sand, silt and swelling clay); 68th Street Crib commissioned for the South Side (1892) [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[11\]](#src-11)[\[15\]](#src-15).
- **1895** — Two-Mile Crib gains a third tunnel (a second 7-ft bore, cross-connected to the Northwest Land Tunnel), giving it three tunnels total feeding the Chicago Avenue pumping station [\[33\]](#src-33).
- **1896** — Lake View tunnel completed after 8 years: 2 miles, 6-ft diameter, first Chicago water tunnel driven partly through solid rock and first to use pneumatic drills [\[11\]](#src-11).
- **1898** — Steam heating plant installed on the Four-Mile Crib, holding the well room at 70 °F to prevent ice formation and easing winter crewing [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[7\]](#src-7). Carter H. Harrison Crib's five construction contracts let (general work, steel/iron, heating/tanks/pumps, plumbing, electric plant); Northeast Lake Tunnel completed [\[34\]](#src-34).
- **1899–1900** — Carter H. Harrison Crib's superstructure completed (November 1899, cost $192,441.40) [\[34\]](#src-34), replacing the aging Two-Mile Crib on the Chicago Avenue tunnels [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[8\]](#src-8); lake water was first admitted through the new intake gates June 3, 1900 [\[35\]](#src-35). A temporary intermediate construction crib from this era of lake-tunnel work was removed under an August 1, 1899 contract, $78,061.81 total including removal [\[34\]](#src-34)[\[35\]](#src-35).
- **1906** — Construction begins (April) on the Southwest Land & Lake Tunnel system feeding the future Dunne Crib, budgeted at roughly $8 million for crib, tunnel, and three planned pumping stations; a temporary intermediate crib is placed 7,500 feet from shore to expedite excavation, served by an aerial cableway on 26 steel towers — 19 of which were swept away by lake ice on the night of January 30, 1908, and rebuilt by that August [\[36\]](#src-36).
- **1909** — Edward F. Dunne Crib completed 45–50 feet from the 68th Street Crib [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[6\]](#src-6)[\[38\]](#src-38), linked by steel footbridge so one keeper crew could service both. (The City of Chicago's own engineering records — the 1933 Bureau of Engineering booklet and the 1956 water-system report — instead date the crib's entry into service to 1911, the year its Southwest Land & Lake Tunnel, rated at 400 MGD, was actually placed in service [\[33\]](#src-33)[\[38\]](#src-38); the sources genuinely conflict, and both figures are presented in this archive.)
- **1915–1918** — Lawrence Avenue Crib (1915) and Wilson Avenue Crib (completed 1918) built for the North Side/Mayfair tunnel system; Wilson's ~8 miles (8.62 mi.) of new tunnels hand-dug through bedrock [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[12\]](#src-12)[\[33\]](#src-33).
- **1918** — A new, larger-diameter tunnel completed in Clarendon Ave. to supply the Lake View Pumping Station directly, making the offshore Lake View Crib and its 6-ft, 10,000-ft tunnel (in service since Lake View's 1889 annexation to Chicago) unnecessary [\[17\]](#src-17).
- **1922** — Lake View Crib's intake shaft domed over [\[17\]](#src-17).
- **1923** — 68th Street Crib navigational aids electrified [\[6\]](#src-6).
- **1924** — Lake View Crib demolished (HAER data page: "In 1924 the Lake View crib was demolished," citing HAER Photos 93–98; a related photo caption elsewhere in the same record states "The Wilson crib was put in during 1917. The Lake View crib was removed in 1924.") [\[17\]](#src-17).
- **1932** — Harrison Crib gets electrically driven air compressor and 36-inch revolving aero-beacon; Four-Mile Crib gets two-room second-story addition [\[8\]](#src-8)[\[15\]](#src-15).
- **1934–1936** — Dever Crib built and placed in operation (1935); Two-Mile Crib and tunnel closed and demolished (1936); new 16-ft Chicago Avenue tunnel completed (1936), lifting total system pumping capacity to 25,000 gal/sec [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[2\]](#src-2)[\[8\]](#src-8)[\[11\]](#src-11).
- **1947 / 1964** — South Water Filtration Plant (now Sawyer) and Central Water Filtration Plant (now Jardine) open; the cribs cease feeding raw water directly to pumping stations and instead feed the plants' shore intake basins [\[14\]](#src-14)[\[16\]](#src-16)[\[21\]](#src-21).
- **late 1980s–1990s** — Zebra mussel invasion forces removal of intake screens; resident crews progressively withdrawn. Sources disagree on the end of live-in crib tending: Lighthousefriends says final keeper crews left in **1990** [\[6\]](#src-6)[\[7\]](#src-7)[\[8\]](#src-8); Neil Steinberg found live-in crews still rotating on the Harrison-Dever complex in **1997** [\[13\]](#src-13); Chicagoist reports crib tenders left in **1999** [\[25\]](#src-25).
- **1997–1998** — Harrison Crib tunnel drained for inspection amid engineering controversy, then collapses; crib permanently removed from service; city spends **$5.3 million** filling the tunnel under Lake Shore Drive [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[8\]](#src-8)[\[13\]](#src-13).
- **c. 2002** — Post-9/11 security overhaul: cameras, motion sensors and USCG-enforced restricted zones around the active cribs, alarms wired to Chicago Police [\[14\]](#src-14)[\[25\]](#src-25).
- **2015–2018** — City Capital Improvement Programs fund demolition of the Wilson Avenue and Four-Mile cribs ($9.5M in the 2015–2019 CIP, $11.5M in 2016–2020, falling to $0 by the 2018–2022 CIP); bid in 2016 but never awarded; Preservation Chicago lists the cribs among its 2017 "Chicago 7" most endangered and urges landmarking and adaptive reuse [\[4\]](#src-4)[\[5\]](#src-5)[\[15\]](#src-15).

## Current operations

Today the crib system runs unmanned. The two active cribs — **Dunne** (south) and **Dever** (north) — continuously admit lake water through ports about 20–30 feet down, screened against debris, into their center wells and gravity tunnels; the Dever complex feeds Jardine through a tunnel about 20 feet in diameter, and Dunne feeds the Sawyer plant [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[10\]](#src-10)[\[16\]](#src-16). During the 1997 crisis, with the Harrison tunnel sealed off for repair and only the Dunne Crib still drawing lake water (feeding the 79th Street/Sawyer plant), Jardine was forced to draw its entire daily supply — about **750 million gallons** — directly from the shoreline rather than from the Dever Crib, at higher cost and lower quality; this stopgap, not a normal Dever/Harrison output figure, is the source of the 750 MGD figure sometimes cited for this period [\[13\]](#src-13). Jardine's rated capacity is 1.4 billion gallons per day — commonly described as the world's largest conventional water filtration plant — and Sawyer's is 720 MGD; combined they actually deliver a bit under 1 BGD to the city and 120 suburbs [\[16\]](#src-16)[\[21\]](#src-21).

Operations staff from the Department of Water Management still travel out "all the time" for inspection and maintenance — in winter historically by icebreaking tug — but no one lives aboard; monitoring is remote, and each crib sits inside a security perimeter of cameras and motion sensors that alert the Chicago Police Department if a boat crosses the line [\[10\]](#src-10)[\[14\]](#src-14)[\[25\]](#src-25). The four surviving inactive cribs (Four-Mile, 68th Street, Harrison, Wilson Avenue) are mothballed, deteriorating, and — as of the last public record found — still city-owned with no executed demolition or reuse plan [\[4\]](#src-4)[\[15\]](#src-15).

Life aboard in the staffed era is well documented. Crews of roughly two (summer) to four or five men (winter), plus a cook, rotated weekly — one man ashore every fourth or fifth week [\[6\]](#src-6)[\[9\]](#src-9)[\[13\]](#src-13). Summer work meant clearing fish and debris from the intake screens ("In the summer the trouble is with fish. In the winter, it's ice" [\[6\]](#src-6)); winter work meant breaking and **dynamiting ice** off the intake ports [\[9\]](#src-9)[\[13\]](#src-13)[\[25\]](#src-25). Head keeper Ray Perkins recalled the life as "boring. Fun at times... no TV. Mostly radio, and crossword puzzles" [\[13\]](#src-13). The Four-Mile Crib's 1898 steam plant made it the comfortable posting at a constant 70 °F [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[7\]](#src-7). The Encyclopedia of Chicago preserves 1910–1930 Chicago Daily News interior photographs of the Harrison well room and the 68th Street crew kitchen [\[9\]](#src-9).

The idle cribs have acquired an unplanned ecological role: **double-crested cormorants nest on the Wilson Avenue Crib, and state-endangered peregrine falcons have also nested there** [\[20\]](#src-20). Preservation Chicago's 2017 proposal explicitly floated converting retired cribs into bird sanctuaries, museums, restaurants, or education venues [\[4\]](#src-4)[\[5\]](#src-5). No formal bird-sanctuary designation was found — the "sanctuary" status is de facto, not legal [\[20\]](#src-20)[\[4\]](#src-4).

## Notable events & incidents

**The January 20, 1909 crib fire — Chicago's worst forgotten disaster.** During construction of the new southern tunnels to the Dunne Crib, contractor **George W. Jackson Inc.** housed its tunnel gangs in wooden barracks atop a temporary "Intermediate Crib" on the tunnel route — **7,500 ft from shore** per Wikipedia [\[1\]](#src-1), a figure independently corroborated by a 1912 city history that also gives the same 7,500-ft distance [\[36\]](#src-36) (~2 miles per the 1909 Chicago Examiner [\[3\]](#src-3); "about a mile and one-quarter" per a 1929 popular history, which also misdates the fire to January 8 — a likely error given the well-corroborated January 20 date elsewhere [\[39\]](#src-39)). The crib was serviced by an aerial cableway strung on 26 steel towers; a lake-ice storm on the night of January 30, 1908 — almost exactly a year before the fire — swept away 19 of the towers, which were rebuilt and back in operation by August 1908 [\[36\]](#src-36). About 108 men were aboard on the morning of January 20, 1909, roughly two-thirds asleep on the second floor, when an explosion — followed about three minutes later by fire — engulfed the barracks. Theories ranged from a match dropped in the powder magazine to gasoline sprinkled against bedbugs; dynamite was kept loosely about the structure, and firefighting gear amounted to "a few axes and some hand fire extinguishers." Men leapt to ice floes; the tug *T.T. Morford* pulled about 40 survivors off the ice, but many froze, drowned, or burned. **Death toll estimates range from 47 (early count) through ~60 (coroner's jury) to 67–70 (press and later accounts)**; two roughly contemporary general histories independently settle on "some seventy lives" [\[36\]](#src-36) and "seventy" [\[39\]](#src-39). An exact figure is impossible because Jackson's temporary laborers were never fully documented [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[3\]](#src-3)[\[6\]](#src-6)[\[26\]](#src-26)[\[36\]](#src-36)[\[39\]](#src-39). Most bodies were burned beyond identification; a few days later the largest public burial in South Chicago history took the unidentified dead to a mass grave at Mount Greenwood Cemetery [\[26\]](#src-26). The fire's origin remains officially unsolved [\[3\]](#src-3).

**The 1948 ice siege.** A crew fought a 30-hour losing battle against ice choking the intakes, ultimately expending 55 sticks of dynamite [\[13\]](#src-13).

**Zebra mussels (late 1980s–90s)** colonized and doomed the intake screens, one of the developments that made the resident tenders obsolete [\[13\]](#src-13).

**The 1997–98 Harrison tunnel collapse.** When the century-old tunnels serving the Harrison Crib were drained for inspection — over warnings from some engineers that dewatering could trigger failure — the tunnel collapsed. Northbound lanes of Lake Shore Drive were closed for months while the shore end was sealed, and the city spent $5.3 million filling the reach under the Drive; the Harrison Crib never returned to service [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[8\]](#src-8)[\[13\]](#src-13)[\[27\]](#src-27).

**Demolition threat and reprieve (2015–2018).** The city budgeted up to $11.5M to demolish the Four-Mile and Wilson Avenue cribs, bid the work in 2016, never awarded it, and zeroed the line item by 2018; both cribs still stand, decaying [\[4\]](#src-4)[\[15\]](#src-15).

## Open questions

- Exact demolition method for the Two-Mile Crib (1936, per Wikipedia's Lake Tunnel article: concrete poured into the well, final blasting broadcast by radio station WENR) — contemporary documentation beyond Wikipedia not located, despite a targeted search of the primary-source corpus (period engineering journals, DPW annual reports through the 1930s, the 1933 Bureau of Engineering booklet). The Lake View Crib's demolition sequence is now resolved via the HAER IL-4 data pages (Lake View Pumping Station): the crib and its 6-ft, 10,000-ft tunnel were rendered unnecessary when a new Clarendon Ave. tunnel was completed in 1918, the intake shaft was domed over in 1922, and the crib itself was demolished in 1924 [\[17\]](#src-17) — independently corroborated by the City's own 1933 retrospective [\[33\]](#src-33).
- **RESOLVED — Carter H. Harrison Crib construction cost:** $192,441.40 for "substructure, superstructure, and landing" per the City's 1899 annual report [\[34\]](#src-34), or $225,000 (within a $590,000 total lake-section cost) per a 1901 engineering-society paper [\[32\]](#src-32); contracts let 1896 (tunnel) and 1898 (crib, in five parts), under assistant engineer H. H. Jackman — no single general contractor's name was found, as the City awarded the crib's construction as five separate specialty contracts (general work, steel/iron, heating, plumbing, electric) rather than to one firm [\[34\]](#src-34). **PARTIALLY RESOLVED — Edward F. Dunne Crib:** no crib-alone figure was found, but a 1912 city history puts the *entire* Southwest Land & Lake Tunnel project (crib + tunnel + three planned pumping stations) at "in the neighborhood of eight millions of dollars" [\[36\]](#src-36). The Dever project's "$13 million" figure still needs a primary source, though the same low-confidence source's physical dimensions (75-ft outside dia., 190-ft shaft) are now confirmed by the City's own 1956 report [\[37\]](#src-37).
- The Lawrence Avenue Crib (1915) is the most obscure of the nine: its precise purpose (auxiliary/emergency inshore intake?), dimensions, and date/manner of abandonment are undocumented in sources found, including the newly added primary-source corpus (period engineering journals and DPW annual reports); a Chicago History Museum glass-negative photograph titled "[Water intake crib on Lake Michigan at Lawrence Avenue]," catalogued to **1913**, is held by the Chicago History Museum and viewable via Explore Chicago Collections, which would push the crib's origin two years earlier than the Wikipedia-sourced 1915 date in the table above [\[29\]](#src-29).
- Definitive date the last live-in crews left (1990 vs. 1997+ vs. 1999 conflict; possibly different dates per crib).
- Whether any demolition, stabilization, or reuse action has occurred since 2018 — no post-2018 CIP line items or contracts were found through July 2026.
- Definitive 1909 fire death toll (see corroborating "some seventy"/"seventy" figures now added above) [\[36\]](#src-36)[\[39\]](#src-39). **PARTIALLY RESOLVED — temporary construction cribs:** in addition to the well-known 1907–09 Intermediate Crib (Dunne project), the primary-source corpus documents at least two more: an "abandoned intermediate crib" that forced a 350-ft tunnel deflection during the 1887–92 Four-Mile Tunnel project [\[33\]](#src-33), and a "Lake View Intermediate Crib" / "temporary intermediate working crib" removed in 1899–1900 (contract let Aug. 1, 1899, $78,061.81 total including removal) associated with the multi-year Lake View and/or Northeast Lake Tunnel construction of the 1890s [\[34\]](#src-34)[\[35\]](#src-35) — the sources place this crib's removal in the same annual-report sequence as both the Harrison Crib and Lake View Crib narratives, and the exact project it served is not fully disambiguated. No comparable temporary crib was found documented for the 1918 Wilson Avenue project specifically.
- Which specific tunnels from the inactive cribs have been bulkheaded or filled, beyond the Harrison/LSD fill.
