# Stickney Water Reclamation Plant

## Role in the system

The Stickney Water Reclamation Plant (WRP), owned and operated by the Metropolitan Water Reclamation District of Greater Chicago (MWRD), sits on 413 acres at 6001 West Pershing Road in the suburb of Cicero, Illinois, immediately adjacent to the Chicago Sanitary and Ship Canal (CSSC) [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[3\]](#src-3). It is the flagship — and by acreage and flow the largest — of the MWRD's seven water reclamation plants, and is widely cited as the largest wastewater treatment plant in the world by treatment volume [\[2\]](#src-2)[\[13\]](#src-13).

**Inflow.** Stickney receives sanitary and combined sewage from central Chicago and 46 suburban communities across a 260-square-mile service area, serving roughly 2.2–2.3 million people (sources differ; see Open Questions) [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[2\]](#src-2). Wastewater and stormwater collected in local sewers is carried to Stickney through the MWRD's network of intercepting sewers and pumping stations. Major intercepting sewers historically feeding the plant include the Salt Creek intercepting sewer (7 ft diameter), the Argo sewer (10 ft), the West Side #1 sewer (17 ft), and the double-barreled Southwest #1 sewer (two 16 ft barrels) [\[8\]](#src-8). On the south side, the Racine Avenue Pumping Station — built in 1939 and substantially rebuilt/expanded in 1964 — collects sewage from a roughly 36-square-mile area stretching from Western Avenue east to the lakefront and from 87th Street north to Taylor Street, and lifts it into the interceptor system that feeds Stickney [\[8\]](#src-8)[\[17\]](#src-17). Solids and sludge generated at four smaller MWRD plants — the John E. Egan, James C. Kirie, Terrence J. O'Brien, and Lemont WRPs — are also piped or hauled to Stickney, where they are combined with Stickney's own solids and anaerobically digested [\[21\]](#src-21). During major storms, combined sewage and stormwater captured in the Tunnel and Reservoir Plan's (TARP) McCook Reservoir — situated a few miles from Stickney in Hodgkins/Bedford Park — is pumped to Stickney for treatment when the reservoir is dewatered [\[5\]](#src-5)[\[19\]](#src-19)[\[20\]](#src-20).

**Outflow.** After treatment, Stickney discharges its cleaned effluent directly to the Chicago Sanitary and Ship Canal, a segment of the Chicago Area Waterway System (CAWS) that carries the flow (via the Des Plaines and Illinois Rivers) toward the Mississippi River and ultimately the Gulf of Mexico [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[17\]](#src-17)[\[20\]](#src-20). Unlike two of MWRD's other large plants (Calumet and O'Brien), Stickney does not disinfect its effluent before discharge; this is discussed under Current Operations below. Roughly a quarter of Stickney's biosolids (29,867 of 119,234 dry tons produced in 2024, or about 25%) are converted to Class A "Exceptional Quality" pellets and marketed as fertilizer by a private on-site contractor; the remainder is trucked or piped to two nearby MWRD solids-management sites (Lawndale Avenue and Harlem Avenue) for lagooning, air-drying, and composting, or applied directly to farmland, or barged/trucked to the MWRD's roughly 15,000-acre land-reclamation site in Fulton County, Illinois, about 200 miles down the Illinois River [\[7\]](#src-7)[\[21\]](#src-21)[and see Upgrades/Current Operations].

## Original construction

Stickney's origin lies in the Sanitary District of Chicago's response to a legal and environmental crisis. The Sanitary District (chartered May 29, 1889, and renamed the Metropolitan Sanitary District of Greater Chicago in 1955 and the MWRD in 1989) had spent its first three decades relying almost entirely on dilution — reversing the Chicago River in 1900 via the 28-mile Chicago Sanitary and Ship Canal, and later the North Shore Channel (1910) and Cal-Sag Channel (1922) — to carry Chicago's raw sewage away from Lake Michigan and toward the Illinois and Mississippi Rivers [\[4\]](#src-4)[\[6\]](#src-6). That approach ran into its limit when a series of U.S. Supreme Court decisions in the 1920s and a final 1930 ruling forced a sharp reduction in the volume of Lake Michigan water the District was permitted to divert for dilution [\[6\]](#src-6)[\[9\]](#src-9). With less water available to dilute sewage, the District was compelled to build actual treatment plants. The 1920s accordingly saw a first wave of Sanitary District treatment works: the Calumet works (1922, the District's first treatment plant), the North Side works (1928), and — completing the shift — the West Side works, which entered service on June 2, 1930 (a few secondary sources, including the Encyclopedia of Chicago, date "West Side works" to 1931; MWRD's own fact sheet gives 1930) [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[4\]](#src-4)[\[6\]](#src-6).

The West Side plant used the German-designed Imhoff tank as its core treatment technology: two-story concrete chambers combining sedimentation in an upper flow-through chamber with anaerobic sludge digestion in a lower chamber, a design associated with engineer Karl Imhoff. Over time the plant's Imhoff tanks were organized into batteries lettered A through D, ultimately totaling roughly 108 individual tanks [\[5\]](#src-5)[6-analog][\[8\]](#src-8). Batteries A and B entered service with the rest of the West Side plant in 1930; construction of Battery C began in 1931 but was halted in 1932 for lack of funds, and only resumed after new federal financing was secured in late 1933 (below). That same reorganization folded in the flow of a separate "Des Plaines River plant," which was abandoned in 1931 [\[31\]](#src-31). As the West Side works filled to capacity treating growing volumes from the South and West Sides — including roughly half the waste of the Union Stock Yards — the District built a second, larger facility immediately to the south: the Southwest Sewage Treatment Works. It opened dramatically on September 7, 1939, when workers pulled a wooden bulkhead at the Western Avenue sewer at 38th Street, diverting in a single day roughly 75 million gallons of raw sewage — previously dumping about 40 tons of solid waste per day straight into the Sanitary and Ship Canal — into the new southwest intercepting sewer and treatment works; within weeks all sewage from the area between the canal and 87th Street was routed to the new plant [\[9\]](#src-9). Both the West Side and Southwest works were pieces of a roughly $162,000,000 sewage-disposal construction program the Sanitary District undertook after the Supreme Court-ordered diversion cuts of the early 1930s [\[9\]](#src-9). Within that broader program, a primary-source federal engineering review puts a plant-specific figure on the Southwest works for the first time: on December 16, 1933, the Sanitary District signed two bond-purchase-and-grant contracts with the U.S. government under Title II of the National Industrial Recovery Act (the statute creating the Public Works Administration), totaling $41,948,000 in bonds against $10,304,866 in federal grants; of the "new construction" tranche, $21,646,000 was earmarked specifically for the West-Southwest Sewage Treatment Works, itemized down to $780,000 for six sewage-pumping units and four blower units (1,500 cu ft/sec and 300,000 cu ft/min capacity respectively), a combined $7,676,000 for the pumping-station/blower-house sub- and superstructure, $500,000 for an outdoor electrical substation, $9,116,000 for two batteries of aeration and final settling tanks (about 250 MGD each, built expandable to 300 MGD each), and $960,000 for a roughly 4,500-ft, 15-ft-wide conduit linking the West Side Imhoff tanks to the new Southwest aeration tanks — the ancestor of the plant's present-day lift-pump and blower infrastructure [\[31\]](#src-31). The two adjoining plants operated under the combined name "West-Southwest Sewage Treatment Works" for decades; MWRD's own historical timeline dates their operational combination to 1949 specifically [\[32\]](#src-32). In 1988, as part of a district-wide rebranding tied to the Sanitary District's renaming as the Metropolitan Water Reclamation District, the combined plant was renamed for the small adjacent village of Stickney [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[7\]](#src-7).

The same 1933 federal financing produced a paper trail that, for the first time in this document, names the Sanitary District engineers behind the West-Southwest design. A Federal Emergency Administration of Public Works (FEAPW/PWA) commission chaired by prominent consulting engineer Daniel W. Mead reviewed the District's plans in spring 1934 and cites a March 14, 1933 report on the sewage-quantity design basis for the West-Southwest works submitted to Chief Engineer Philip Harrington by Sanitary Engineer Langdon Pearse and engineer L. C. Whittemore, with F. W. Mohlman contributing supplementary process studies; a 1956 MWRD retrospective separately credits Harrington, alongside the earlier George M. Wisner, as one of the District's most consequential 20th-century chief engineers [\[31\]](#src-31)[\[32\]](#src-32). This is distinct from Isham Randolph, chief engineer of the earlier 1892–1900 Sanitary and Ship Canal excavation, or L.E. Cooley, an earlier Sanitary District chief engineer — see Open Questions for what remains unresolved. One popular local blog describes the original 1930s settling-tank infrastructure as built "by the WPA" [\[10\]](#src-10); the federal contracts above resolve this as a plausible but mistaken conflation — the actual New Deal-era financing came from the Public Works Administration under the December 1933 National Industrial Recovery Act contracts, not the Works Progress Administration (WPA), which was not created until 1935, roughly two years after the Sanitary District's PWA financing contracts were signed [\[31\]](#src-31).

## Upgrades & modernization timeline

- **1930** — West Side Sewage Treatment Works enters service (June 2), using Imhoff tanks for combined settling/digestion [\[1\]](#src-1).
- **1939** — Southwest Sewage Treatment Works enters service (September 7), adding activated-sludge secondary treatment capacity and a new intercepting sewer system draining 12.5 miles of the city, including half the stockyards' waste [\[9\]](#src-9).
- **1939** — Racine Avenue Pumping Station (RAPS) built to lift South Side sewage into the interceptor system feeding the combined West-Southwest plant; per a 2020 USACE report, RAPS began operation with six pumps discharging raw sewage directly to Bubbly Creek [\[17\]](#src-17)[\[33\]](#src-33).
- **1940s** — Secondary (activated-sludge) aeration tanks progressively added across the site to supplement primary Imhoff-tank treatment, following broader adoption of activated-sludge technology by Sanitary District engineers [\[10\]](#src-10).
- **1949** — MWRD's own historical timeline dates the operational combination of the West and Southwest plants to this year; per USACE, this is also when RAPS stopped discharging dry-weather sewage to Bubbly Creek and began diverting it instead to the West-Southwest plant [\[32\]](#src-32)[\[33\]](#src-33).
- **1954** — RAPS expanded from 6 to 14 centrifugal pumps (West Side: 3 units at 375 cfs, 2 at 400 cfs, 2 at 500 cfs; East Side: 3 units at 375 cfs, 4 at 500 cfs), per a 2020 USACE report — a different expansion date than the "1964 rebuild" reported elsewhere; not reconciled [\[33\]](#src-33).
- **1964** — Racine Avenue Pumping Station rebuilt/expanded on Pershing Road to replace the smaller original structure, increasing its South Side interceptor capacity [\[17\]](#src-17).
- **1971** — MWRD's Fulton County "Prairie Plan" land-reclamation program begins (per MWRD's own historical timeline, superseding this document's earlier "1970" figure); it wins the ASCE's "Special Civil Engineering Achievement Award" in 1974 [\[32\]](#src-32).
- **1972** — MWRD (then Metropolitan Sanitary District) adopts the Tunnel and Reservoir Plan (TARP), the multibillion-dollar "Deep Tunnel" program that would eventually include the McCook Reservoir feeding into Stickney [\[4\]](#src-4)[\[6\]](#src-6).
- **1976–1979** — A federal Environmental Impact Statement for the TARP Calumet Tunnel System documents a parallel "West-Southwest treatment plant expansion project" estimated at $666.3 million, targeted for Step 3 federal (FWPCA) construction-grant funding by December 1979, within a combined roughly $1.02–1.13 billion Calumet/West-Southwest treatment-plant-improvement program — a 1970s capital-expansion episode not otherwise documented in MWRD's own retrospective materials [\[34\]](#src-34).
- **1984 (March 21)** — The Illinois Pollution Control Board grants the District a variance (PCB 84-16) to bypass a chlorine contact tank during construction work at the "West-Southwest Plant," then listed in the case record at 5901 W. Pershing Road, Stickney — an address that differs from the plant's current 6001 W. Pershing Road listing; not reconciled [\[37\]](#src-37).
- **1985** — MWRD ceases routine chlorination/dechlorination disinfection at its large plants (including the West-Southwest/Stickney works), judging that chlorine's toxicity to fish outweighed benefits on waterways with little recreational contact at the time [17-analog].
- **1988–1989** — The combined West-Southwest Sewage Treatment Works is renamed Stickney Water Reclamation Plant (1988), coinciding with the district-wide renaming of the Metropolitan Sanitary District of Greater Chicago to the Metropolitan Water Reclamation District of Greater Chicago, effective January 1, 1989 [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[4\]](#src-4)[\[7\]](#src-7).
- **2002** — MWRD's Research and Development Department runs a roughly three-month "Bubbly Creek Water Quality Improvement Demonstration Project," opening a gate at RAPS to draw an estimated 2.5 billion gallons of stagnant Bubbly Creek water through the station to Stickney for treatment (at an estimated cost of $625,000), measurably improving dissolved-oxygen levels in the creek [\[33\]](#src-33).
- **2005** — MWRD's Monitoring & Research Department completes an "Effluent Disinfection Report" comparing UV and ozone disinfection costs at North Side (predecessor to O'Brien), Stickney, and Calumet WRPs; Stickney's far larger flow priced UV capital costs at roughly $358 million (versus $100 million at Calumet) and ozone at roughly $497 million (versus $180 million at Calumet), a cost gap that helps explain why disinfection was ultimately built only at the smaller Calumet and O'Brien plants [\[35\]](#src-35)[\[36\]](#src-36).
- **2009 (revised)** — MWRD, under Executive Director Richard Lanyon, issues the "Stickney Master Plan," a long-range facility-improvements plan estimating $993,600,000 to implement 12 major projects (new circular primary tanks replacing Imhoff Batteries A and B, new solids-thickening facilities, additional air blowers, new digester-gas storage/utilization, grit-handling upgrades, West Side pump station replacement, and potential nutrient-removal facilities), driven partly by the anticipated loss of off-site drying capacity once the McCook Reservoir was built [\[5\]](#src-5).
- **2013–2018** — Contract 04-128-3P ("West Side Primary Settling Tanks 1–9 and Aerated Grit Facility") replaces the original 108 Imhoff tanks with nine new 160-ft-diameter circular primary settling tanks and six new 132-ft aerated grit tanks; reported project cost approximately $224.8 million; general contractors IHC Construction Companies and F.H. Paschen/S.N. Nielsen joint venture, with Greeley and Hansen (now part of TYLin/Sidara) as design engineer [\[8\]](#src-8)[\[23\]](#src-23).
- **2014–2016** — MWRD partners with Black & Veatch (design-builder) and Ostara Nutrient Recovery Technologies to design and build the Pearl® nutrient-recovery facility, converting phosphorus- and struvite-forming sidestreams into marketable Crystal Green® fertilizer; substantial completion February 25, 2016, final completion September 12, 2016; Ostara signed to a 20-year offtake agreement paying MWRD roughly $400 per ton of recovered fertilizer [\[15\]](#src-15)[\[16\]](#src-16).
- **2014–2023** — Open-windrow composting operates at the Harlem Avenue Solids Management Area before being discontinued in 2023, per MWRD's 2025 odor-monitoring report [\[41\]](#src-41).
- **2017** — McCook Reservoir Stage 1 (3.5 billion gallons) completed, beginning to relieve combined-sewer overflow pressure on the West Side sewersheds that feed Stickney [\[14\]](#src-14).
- **2021 (May)** — Stickney (tracked in program materials as "Stickney SW") joins a 10-plant pilot of Illinois's wastewater-based epidemiology program, testing sewage for SARS-CoV-2 and its variants in partnership with Argonne National Laboratory and the Illinois Department of Public Health [\[42\]](#src-42).
- **2022 (May 12)** — Illinois EPA issues Stickney a Bureau of Air Compliance Commitment Agreement (docket A-2021-00469) addressing a Clean Air Act Permit Program (CAAPP) deviation, the first of two such agreements in this document's source set [\[39\]](#src-39).
- **2022 (December 27)** — Fire at the privately operated Metropolitan Biosolids Management (MBM) heat-drying pelletizing facility on the Stickney site; contained by multiple responding fire departments; no injuries or evacuations [\[12\]](#src-12).
- **2023** — MWRD and research partner Gross-Wen Technologies install a pilot revolving algal biofilm (RAB) reactor and greenhouse at Stickney (the second such installation after an earlier trial at the O'Brien WRP) to test algae-based nutrient recovery and carbon-offset potential, funded partly by a $100,000 Imagine H2O Fourth Annual Urban Water Challenge grant [\[29\]](#src-29).
- **2024** — Illinois EPA conducts its triennial water-quality-standards review, formally taking up whether the Chicago Sanitary and Ship Canal segment downstream of Stickney should be reclassified for greater recreational use (and thus potentially require disinfection), following years of advocacy from Cicero Community Farm and area residents [\[18\]](#src-18).
- **2025** — Stickney receives a "Platinum 5" NACWA Peak Performance Award, marking its fifth straight year of 100% NPDES permit compliance; MWRD announced the award June 10, 2025, ahead of NACWA's July 23 ceremony [\[28\]](#src-28).
- **2025 (July 29)** — Illinois EPA issues Stickney a second Bureau of Air Compliance Commitment Agreement (docket A-2024-00171), citing sulfur dioxide emissions exceeding 1,365 lb/month and nitrogen oxide emissions exceeding 585 lb/month from two digester waste-gas-burner flares, plus a failure to promptly report the permit deviation as required under CAAPP Permit No. 95090075 Condition 7.4.10 [\[38\]](#src-38).
- **2026 (early July)** — Record regional rainfall (roughly 8+ inches over 30 days, including 3+ inches July 3–4) drives the McCook Reservoir to 96–100% capacity for the sixth time in 2026 alone; MWRD begins pumping the reservoir down to Stickney for treatment, a process expected to take on the order of a month [\[19\]](#src-19)[\[20\]](#src-20).

## Current operations

Stickney today operates as a conventional primary/secondary activated-sludge plant without a tertiary filtration step, treating a design average flow of 1,200 MGD (peak/permitted hydraulic capacity 1.44 BGD, i.e., roughly 1 million gallons per minute at max) and an actual 2024 annual average flow of 685 MGD [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[5\]](#src-5)[\[21\]](#src-21). Roughly 397 employees staff the 413-acre site [\[1\]](#src-1). Incoming wastewater passes coarse screens, aerated grit removal, and primary settling (removing an estimated 60–80% of solids), then flows by gravity into roughly 36 acres of activated-sludge aeration tanks where a cultivated community of microorganisms consumes dissolved and suspended organic matter, followed by final settling before discharge [\[1\]](#src-1). Solids removed at each stage — plus digested/dewatered solids piped or hauled in from the Egan, Kirie, O'Brien, and Lemont WRPs — are thickened, anaerobically digested (generating digester gas used to help heat plant buildings and digesters), dewatered by centrifuge, and then either air-dried in lagoons at the adjacent Lawndale Avenue Solids Management Area, composted at the Harlem Avenue Solids Management Area in Forest View, applied directly as Class B biosolids to farmland by contractors (Synagro Midwest and Stewart Environmental in 2024), or converted to Class A "Exceptional Quality" heat-dried pellets by the on-site, privately run Metropolitan Biosolids Management facility [\[21\]](#src-21). In 2024, Stickney's combined process train produced 119,234 dry tons of biosolids, of which 105,144 dry tons were beneficially reused (59,234 DT applied as Class B biosolids to farmland; 16,043 DT of Exceptional Quality biosolids — 14,321 DT air-dried, 1,721 DT composted — applied to golf courses, parks, and other urban land; and 29,867 DT dewatered and conveyed to the on-site MBM facility, where it was pelletized and sold as fertilizer) [\[21\]](#src-21). None of Stickney's biosolids went to landfill in 2024 [\[21\]](#src-21).

Notably, Stickney does not disinfect its effluent before discharge to the Chicago Sanitary and Ship Canal. MWRD's large plants stopped routine chlorination in 1985, both because chlorine residuals were toxic to fish and because the CSSC — an artificial, industrial waterway — was not then designated for primary recreational contact [\[17\]](#src-17). That regulatory picture has partly shifted: since 2011, MWRD has installed disinfection facilities at its Calumet and O'Brien WRPs to protect increasing recreational use of the North Branch Chicago River and Calumet-area waterways (operational for the 2016 recreational season) [\[4\]](#src-4). A 2005 MWRD engineering study comparing UV and ozone disinfection options at North Side (predecessor to O'Brien), Stickney, and Calumet WRPs quantifies why Stickney was left out: because of its far larger flow, Stickney's UV capital cost was estimated at roughly $358 million versus $100 million at Calumet, and ozone at roughly $497 million versus $180 million at Calumet, with total present-worth costs of about $1.3–1.6 billion at Stickney against $448–592 million at Calumet — a roughly 3-to-3.5-times cost gap that helps explain why disinfection was ultimately built only at the smaller plants [\[35\]](#src-35)[\[36\]](#src-36). Stickney has not received the same treatment, because the Illinois Pollution Control Board classifies the CSSC segment Stickney discharges to as "Incidental Contact Recreation Waters," a designation whose dischargers are not required to disinfect under current state regulations [\[18\]](#src-18). This has become a live environmental-justice controversy: Cicero Community Farm and area residents have petitioned the Illinois EPA since at least 2016 to reclassify the canal for fuller recreational use given growing kayaking, rowing, and fishing activity near the plant; Illinois EPA's 2024 triennial-review response acknowledged the issue's significance and committed to evaluating the CSSC's recreational designated use, without immediately mandating disinfection [\[18\]](#src-18).

Beyond its water-discharge permit, Stickney also operates under Illinois EPA Clean Air Act Permit Program (CAAPP) Permit No. 95090075, which governs air emissions from sources including its digester waste-gas burners/flares. Illinois EPA and MWRD have entered into a series of Bureau of Air and Bureau of Water Compliance Commitment Agreements addressing permit deviations at the plant — in 2019 (water, docket W-2019-50042), 2022 (air, docket A-2021-00469), and 2025 (air, docket A-2024-00171); the 2025 agreement found sulfur dioxide emissions exceeding 1,365 lb/month and nitrogen oxide emissions exceeding 585 lb/month from two waste-gas-burner flares, along with a failure to promptly notify Illinois EPA of the deviation as CAAPP Condition 7.4.10 requires [\[38\]](#src-38)[\[39\]](#src-39)[\[40\]](#src-40).

Stickney also anchors MWRD's phosphorus-recovery efforts: the on-site Ostara Pearl® nutrient recovery facility, commissioned in 2016 and marketed as the world's largest facility of its kind, uses three "Pearl 10K" fluidized-bed reactors to intercept phosphorus- and magnesium-rich sidestreams (centrate and, via the WASSTRIP® process, thickened waste activated sludge) before they can precipitate as struvite scale inside pipes and digesters, instead crystallizing them into Crystal Green® slow-release fertilizer at an installed capacity of up to roughly 9,000 tons per year (per Ostara's own case study; some secondary sources round to 10,000), while helping the plant meet a tightened 1.0 mg/L total-phosphorus effluent limit [\[15\]](#src-15)[\[16\]](#src-16). As of the mid-2020s, MWRD was also piloting algae-based nutrient recovery (a revolving algal biofilm system with research partner Gross-Wen Technologies, funded partly by a $100,000 Imagine H2O grant) at Stickney to explore carbon-offset and further nutrient-capture potential [\[29\]](#src-29).

Physically, Stickney functions as a relief valve for the region's Deep Tunnel (TARP) system: the adjacent McCook Reservoir, completed in stages (Stage 1, 3.5 billion gallons, in 2017; further stages targeted through the late 2020s/early 2030s), captures combined sewer overflow during storms and is drained down to Stickney for treatment afterward [\[5\]](#src-5)[\[14\]](#src-14)[\[19\]](#src-19). In early July 2026, record regional rainfall pushed McCook to full or near-full capacity multiple times in a matter of weeks, and MWRD spokespeople confirmed the reservoir would be pumped down to Stickney over a period of roughly a month, illustrating the plant's ongoing role absorbing not just steady-state sanitary flow but major wet-weather surges [\[19\]](#src-19)[\[20\]](#src-20).

## Notable events & incidents

- **September 7, 1939** — In a single-day operation, MWRD's predecessor agency diverted roughly 75 million gallons of raw sewage from the Chicago Sanitary and Ship Canal into the newly completed Southwest works by pulling a wooden bulkhead at Western Avenue and 38th Street — effectively the "switch-on" moment for the second half of what became Stickney [\[9\]](#src-9).
- **1930s Supreme Court diversion rulings** — The chain of U.S. Supreme Court decisions culminating in 1930, which sharply cut the Sanitary District's permitted Lake Michigan diversion, was the direct trigger for building the West Side and Southwest treatment works in the first place [\[6\]](#src-6)[\[9\]](#src-9).
- **December 27, 2022** — Fire at the privately operated Metropolitan Biosolids Management (MBM) heat-drying pelletizing facility on the Stickney site, at noon; multiple fire departments responded and contained the blaze; no injuries, no evacuations, no damage to other buildings; cause was under investigation [\[12\]](#src-12).
- **July 2023** — More than eight inches of rain (National Weather Service data cited in Illinois EPA's 2024 triennial-review response; a public commenter at the review's hearing described it as "nearly nine inches") fell on Cicero and Berwyn in a single day, overwhelming local storm sewers; floodwater then entered either the sanitary sewer system (treated at Stickney) or the TARP storage system (also ultimately treated at Stickney), while Illinois EPA acknowledged an unknown but significant portion of floodwaters never reached the plant at all — an episode cited by environmental-justice advocates in the ongoing Illinois EPA disinfection/recreational-use review [\[18\]](#src-18).
- **2018 methane explosion (Calumet WRP, not Stickney)** — A methane explosion (caused by a worker's torch igniting accumulated methane) collapsed the roof of a sludge-concentration building at MWRD's Calumet plant around 11 a.m. on August 30, 2018, injuring ten people, two of whom had to be extricated from the rubble; it is sometimes conflated with Stickney in casual references but occurred at a different MWRD facility and is included here only to flag that distinction [\[30\]](#src-30).
- **July 29, 2025** — Illinois EPA's Bureau of Air issued Stickney a Compliance Commitment Agreement (docket A-2024-00171) after the plant exceeded monthly sulfur dioxide and nitrogen oxide limits from two digester waste-gas-burner flares and failed to promptly report the deviation, the second such air-compliance agreement in three years (following one in May 2022) [\[38\]](#src-38)[\[39\]](#src-39).
- **Early July 2026** — Historic regional rainfall drove the McCook Reservoir (which drains to Stickney) to 96–100% capacity repeatedly within days, a record for the reservoir since it began operating; the event triggered widespread Chicago-area basement flooding complaints and combined sewer overflows even as TARP/Stickney absorbed a record volume of stormwater [\[19\]](#src-19)[\[20\]](#src-20).

## Open questions

- **Exact opening year of the West Side works**: MWRD's own current fact sheet and most agency-adjacent sources state June 2, 1930; the Encyclopedia of Chicago and some other secondary sources state 1931. Could not find a primary Sanitary District annual report or contemporary newspaper account to resolve this discrepancy definitively.
- **Named design engineer(s) for the West Side/Southwest works — largely resolved**: A 1934 Federal Emergency Administration of Public Works (FEAPW/PWA) commission report, found in this archive pass, names Chief Engineer Philip Harrington, Sanitary Engineer Langdon Pearse, engineer L. C. Whittemore, and F. W. Mohlman as the Sanitary District personnel behind the West-Southwest design-basis reports of 1933–34, with consulting engineer Daniel W. Mead chairing the independent federal review board [\[31\]](#src-31)[\[32\]](#src-32). This is distinct from Isham Randolph (1892–1900 canal era) or later 20th-century Sanitary District chief engineers. Not yet identified: a named architecture/engineering firm of record (as opposed to in-house Sanitary District engineers) for the structures themselves.
- **Precise construction cost breakdown — resolved for the Southwest works specifically**: A 1934 FEAPW commission report itemizes $21,646,000 in PWA-financed "new work" allocated specifically to the West-Southwest Sewage Treatment Works (Items 4A–4K of Sub-Application Docket No. 3622), broken out by pumping/blower equipment, structures, and tankage [\[31\]](#src-31) — see Original construction, above. The broader $162,000,000 figure (Connecting the Windy City blog, sourced from a Chicago Tribune-era account) still describes the wider Sanitary District sewage-disposal program of which the West Side and Southwest works were only a part. A separate, itemized cost for the earlier West Side works (1927–1930) construction specifically was not located.
- **Service population and community count discrepancies**: Current MWRD materials state "over 2.2 million people" and "46 other communities" (260 sq mi) [\[1\]](#src-1); other press and industry sources (some dated) cite "2.3 million people" and "43 suburban communities"; a 2009 MWRD master-planning document cites a year-2000 base population of 2,276,000 [\[5\]](#src-5). The Ostara case study's claim of "over 4.5 million residents served" appears to describe the wider MWRD service area rather than Stickney's own service area specifically, and should not be taken as a Stickney-specific figure.
- **Acreage discrepancy**: MWRD's current fact sheet states Stickney occupies 413 acres [\[1\]](#src-1); one secondary source (Gizmodo, citing an older figure) states 570 acres. Not resolved; 413 acres, sourced to the agency's own current fact sheet, is treated as authoritative here.
- **"Metrogro" barge program**: The research brief's lead suggested MWRD/Stickney biosolids were marketed or shipped under a "Metrogro" brand. This appears to be a conflation with the Madison Metropolitan Sewerage District's (Wisconsin) unrelated "Metrogro" liquid-biosolids program. Chicago/MWRD's actual historical barge-based biosolids program moved sludge via the Illinois River to Liverpool, Illinois, in Fulton County (see Facilities table), under the MWRD's own "Prairie Plan" branding — no evidence was found that MWRD ever used the name "Metrogro."
- **Exact final settling tank count and total aeration-tank count**: The figure of 96 final settling tanks appears in only one secondary source (a hobbyist industrial-history blog) and could not be cross-verified against an MWRD engineering document; treat as approximate.
- **No dedicated HAER/HABS record identified**: Extensive searching did not turn up a Library of Congress Historic American Engineering Record (HAER) or Historic American Buildings Survey (HABS) documentation package specifically for the West Side/Southwest/Stickney treatment works, unlike, for example, the Sanitary and Ship Canal's Western Avenue Bridge (HAER ILL,16-CHIG,163-) or the Lockport Powerhouse (HAER IL-164-H). It is possible Stickney has simply never been HAER-documented, or that records exist under a name/number not surfaced by search. Adjacent but distinct: a 2020 USACE report notes that the Racine Avenue Pumping Station, while not listed on the National Register of Historic Places, has been determined eligible for listing by Illinois's State Historic Preservation Officer — a different federal recognition track (NRHP, not HAER/HABS) that nonetheless indicates the site's historic significance has been formally assessed [\[33\]](#src-33).
- **Full text of Sanitary District of Chicago annual reports covering plant construction (1927–1940)** was not directly reviewed; HathiTrust and archive.org host period Sanitary District "Engineering Data" volumes (e.g., a 1921 edition, pre-dating Stickney) but a construction-era (1928–1940) volume specifically documenting the West Side/Southwest works was not located and fetched in this pass. A related 1934 FEAPW federal review of the same construction program was located and is now cited throughout this document [\[31\]](#src-31).
- **Racine Avenue Pumping Station construction date remains contradictory across sources — refined but not resolved**: some (per search-engine summaries of MWRD-adjacent materials) describe the current structure as "originally constructed in 1939," while South Side Weekly and an industrial-history blog describe a distinct "1964 Racine Avenue Pumping Station" that replaced a smaller, older station. A 2020 USACE report adds a third data point: RAPS was built with 6 pumps in 1939 and expanded to 14 centrifugal pumps by 1954 [\[33\]](#src-33) — neither confirming nor ruling out a further 1964 rebuild. A dedicated search for a primary MWRD engineering or board record did not resolve whether 1964 was a full rebuild, an expansion beyond the 1954 total, or a reporting error; treated in this document as "built 1939 (6 pumps); expanded to 14 pumps by 1954 (USACE); also reported as rebuilt 1964 elsewhere (unreconciled)." The same USACE report also gives RAPS's service area as 30 sq mi, versus the 36 sq mi figure used elsewhere in this document from MWRD-adjacent sources — also unreconciled.
- **Stickney's street address is inconsistently given across eras**: a 1984 Illinois Pollution Control Board opinion lists the "West-Southwest Plant" at 5901 W. Pershing Road, Stickney, Illinois, while MWRD's current fact sheet gives 6001 West Pershing Road, Cicero, Illinois [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[37\]](#src-37). This may reflect a genuine address renumbering, a change in which entrance/boundary point is used as the official address, or simply an error in one source; not resolved in this pass.
- **Minor design-capacity discrepancy newly identified**: a 2015 MWEA conference presentation co-authored by MWRD, Black & Veatch, and Ostara staff (source 15, already cited for the nutrient-recovery facility) states Stickney's "design capacity" as 1,400 MGD, versus the 1.44 BGD (1,440 MGD) peak/permitted figure given in MWRD's current fact sheet (source 1) and used throughout this document. The ~40 MGD (about 3%) gap is most likely a rounding difference in an informal slide deck rather than a substantive disagreement, but it was not reconciled against a dated engineering record.
- **McCook Reservoir ultimate capacity resolved**: MWRD's current (May 2025) McCook Reservoir fact sheet states the total capacity as 10 billion gallons (3.5 BG Stage 1 + 6.5 BG Stage 2), which supersedes the 10.5-billion-gallon figure given in MWRD's older 2009 Stickney master plan; the Facilities table above has been updated accordingly.
