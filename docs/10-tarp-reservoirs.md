# TARP Phase II: The Reservoirs (Majewski, Thornton, McCook)

*Research compiled July 2026. All web sources accessed July 2026.*

## Role in the system

TARP Phase II is the storage half of the Metropolitan Water Reclamation District of Greater Chicago's (MWRD) Tunnel and Reservoir Plan ("Deep Tunnel"). Phase I — 110.4 miles of concrete-lined rock tunnels, 8–33 ft in diameter, 150–300 ft underground, built 1975–2006 at a construction cost of $2.332 billion — intercepts combined-sewer overflows (CSOs) from a ~360-square-mile combined-sewer area covering Chicago and 51 suburbs, but the tunnels themselves store only 2.32 billion gallons (BG) [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[4\]](#src-4). Phase II/CUP adds three terminal reservoirs that receive the tunnels' overflow and are "intended primarily for flood control… but also considerably enhance pollution control benefits being provided under Phase I" [\[4\]](#src-4). The federal partner interest was defined by the U.S. Army Corps of Engineers' (USACE) Chicagoland Underflow Plan (CUP), Final Phase I General Design Memorandum of 1986 [\[4\]](#src-4). When McCook Stage 2 is finished the whole system will store 17.5 BG — reservoirs 15.15 BG (TARP share) plus tunnels 2.32 BG [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[4\]](#src-4).

Upstream-to-downstream, the four tunnel systems map onto the three reservoirs as follows [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[2\]](#src-2)[\[3\]](#src-3):

- **Upper Des Plaines Tunnel System** (6.6 mi, 70 million gallons (MG), 9–20 ft diameter; first TARP segment online, 1981) serves an 11-sq-mi combined-sewer area in Des Plaines, Mount Prospect and Arlington Heights near O'Hare, and discharges to the **Gloria Alitto Majewski Reservoir** (0.35 BG), an open basin adjacent to the James C. Kirie Water Reclamation Plant (WRP). Stored sewage is pumped back to Kirie WRP for treatment [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[4\]](#src-4)[\[20\]](#src-20).
- **Mainstream Tunnel System** (40.5 mi, 1.2 BG, 8–33 ft diameter, Wilmette to McCook; completed in stages 1985–2006) and **Des Plaines Tunnel System** (25.6–26.6 mi, 0.4 BG) together drain a 258-sq-mi service area — central Chicago plus 36 suburbs, 3.1 million people — and both feed the **McCook Reservoir** (3.5 BG operating; 10 BG at completion) in Bedford Park, wedged between the Chicago Sanitary & Ship Canal and the Des Plaines River. McCook is dewatered through distribution tunnels to the TARP Mainstream Pumping Station at 6100 River Road, Hodgkins, which lifts flow to the Stickney WRP [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[2\]](#src-2)[\[4\]](#src-4)[\[21\]](#src-21).
- **Calumet Tunnel System** (36.7 mi, 0.63 BG, 9–30 ft diameter; built 1986–2006) drains a 91-sq-mi area of the Far South Side and south suburbs and feeds the **Thornton Composite Reservoir** (7.9 BG) in the north lobe of the Thornton Quarry. Thornton's stored sewage is pumped ~5 miles north to the Calumet WRP in Riverdale for treatment and release to the Little Calumet River [\[3\]](#src-3)[\[4\]](#src-4). Thornton additionally takes overbank floodwater directly from **Thorn Creek** via the Thorn Creek Overflow Tunnel (connected September 2022) — the non-TARP "composite" half of its mission [\[3\]](#src-3)[\[4\]](#src-4).

In a large storm, local combined sewers surcharge past the MWRD intercepting sewers and spill into TARP dropshafts; the tunnels convey flow by gravity toward the reservoirs; gates at the reservoir inlet tunnels open when tunnel storage is exhausted; and after the storm the TARP pumping stations empty tunnels and reservoirs back to the WRPs as treatment capacity allows [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[2\]](#src-2)[\[7\]](#src-7). Fill events are formally defined by water-surface elevations on the Chicago City Datum (CCD): Thornton counts a fill event above −280 ft CCD; McCook enters "high-stage operation" at −220 ft CCD [\[22\]](#src-22). Draining is much slower than filling: McCook filled in roughly 20 hours in a March 2018 event [\[12\]](#src-12), while MWRD estimated "at least a month" to draw down Thornton after the July 2026 storm, constrained by Calumet WRP throughput (Stickney can treat more than three times what Calumet can in a day) [\[18\]](#src-18).

## Original construction

**Majewski (the pilot reservoir).** Under the CUP cost-share, MWRD as local sponsor acquired land rights and USACE Chicago District designed and built the reservoir next to Kirie WRP; it was completed in 1998 for about $45 million ($40.8M USACE contract, 75% federally funded, plus $4.0M in MWRD "betterments" — a control building, reservoir outflow control gates, and a monitoring system; nominal late-1990s dollars) [\[4\]](#src-4). Originally the "O'Hare Reservoir" (the O'Hare CUP unit), it was renamed for Gloria Alitto Majewski, the MWRD commissioner (1984–2010) who was the District's first woman finance chair [\[8\]](#src-8)[\[23\]](#src-23). It is the only one of the three that is a conventional excavated open basin rather than a converted quarry. USACE's July 1989 Phase II General Design Memorandum/EIS for the "O'Hare Reservoir" — first cost estimated at $20.52M in October 1989 prices, against a 1.15 benefit-cost ratio — documents the design in detail: a 105-acre parcel bounded by the Northwest Tollway and Elmhurst Road, of which the reservoir itself occupies roughly 30 acres at ground elevation; storage of 1,050 acre-feet (342.1 MG, the design figure later rounded to the "350 MG" cited everywhere else) between elevations −2.0 and +58.0 ft CCD, a working pool 22 ft below the average +80 ft CCD ground surface; excavation with a 15-ft bench at +46 ft CCD and 1-on-2 side slopes; and a 30-in. recompacted-clay bottom liner (minimum permeability 1×10⁻⁷ cm/sec) topped with 10 in. of roller-compacted concrete [\[28\]](#src-28)[\[29\]](#src-29). This resolves most of the "Majewski physical dimensions" open question via a primary USACE source — including independently corroborating the 1,050-acre-foot figure that a low-confidence web lead had put forward — though the exact date/ordinance renaming the reservoir for Commissioner Majewski still was not located.

**Thornton (quarry conversion, world's largest CSO reservoir).** The Thornton Quarry was first worked in the 1830s–40s (Wikipedia dates the earliest pits there to 1836, 1846 and 1850) and owned by Material Service Corp. from 1938 [\[11\]](#src-11); MWRD's own Thornton fact sheet instead says the quarry "dates to the 1860s" — the two cited sources disagree and neither is reconciled here [\[3\]](#src-3)[\[11\]](#src-11). It exposes a Silurian reef dolomite prized for aggregate; the pit complex is about 1.5 miles long, 0.5 miles wide and up to 450 ft deep [\[11\]](#src-11). MWRD chose it because bedrock rises nearly to the surface there [\[3\]](#src-3). Under a 1998 mining agreement, operator Hanson Material Service (now Heidelberg Materials) mined roughly 76 million tons of stone from the north lobe to a depth of ~340 ft between 1998 and 2013, selling the aggregate commercially — the excavation cost MWRD only ~$65.2M in "mining, land and Corps costs" because the stone paid for the dig [\[4\]](#src-4)[\[9\]](#src-9)[\[10\]](#src-10). MWRD's own year-by-year board mining-progress tables (filed under Contract 77-235-2F) show token tonnage — 30,000 tons in 1996 and 2.37M tons in 1997 — already being pulled before the formal June 1998 agreement (counted as the contract's "Year 1"); by the end of 2009 the operator had mined 62.6M of an estimated ~71.2M mineable tons, 87.9% complete [\[34\]](#src-34). The contract's final hard-cost-plus-capital-cost accounting, approved by the board in September 2023, put Total Mining Costs at $54,506,673 — a subset of the broader $65.2M "mining, land and Corps costs" line, which also covers items like the $15.5M "lost reserves payment" to the operator and real-estate taxes on the west lobe [\[35\]](#src-35). A Project Cooperation Agreement with USACE was signed September 18, 2003, but "due to inadequate USACE funding and the urgent need for the reservoir," MWRD assumed design and construction itself in June 2004 and is pursuing federal reimbursement through the Water Resources Development Act [\[4\]](#src-4); by the 2026 budget cycle it had received $39.2M in cumulative USACE reimbursement toward the reservoir's design and construction, including a $20.0M installment in 2024 [\[31\]](#src-31). Meanwhile the temporary Thornton Transitional Reservoir (TTR, ~3.1 BG, a Natural Resources Conservation Service flood-control project) opened in the west lobe in March 2003 ($54.7M) to give nine communities interim Thorn Creek overbank relief [\[4\]](#src-4)[\[20\]](#src-20). Conversion of the north lobe (2013–2015) required: plugging two mining haul tunnels with concrete; building a 32,000-cu-yd roller-compacted-concrete dam beneath the I-80/294 Tri-State Tollway to separate the reservoir from the still-active main quarry ($80.75M with the grout curtain); drilling a double-row grout curtain around the ~2-mile perimeter — holes up to 500 ft deep at 15° angles about 5 ft apart, with a second row 20 ft away angled opposite — tying into a natural impermeable shale layer ~500 ft down; driving the ~30-ft-diameter inlet/outlet tunnels with four ~100-ton isolation gates ($147M); and pouring an erosion-control apron rated for 30-ft/s inflows ($63.5M final prep) [\[3\]](#src-3)[\[4\]](#src-4)[\[9\]](#src-9). The reservoir was dedicated September 1, 2015 and began filling for the first time on Thanksgiving, November 26, 2015 [\[9\]](#src-9)[\[26\]](#src-26). Total Thornton cost: $429M projected at opening [\[9\]](#src-9); $427M reported in 2020 [\[5\]](#src-5); $450M in the June 2025 status report [\[4\]](#src-4) (nominal dollars in all cases).

**McCook (dig-your-own quarry).** The PCA with USACE was signed May 10, 1999: USACE designs and builds the reservoir "features," MWRD provides "the massive hole" [\[4\]](#src-4). MWRD sited the hole in the lagoons of its Lawndale Avenue Solids Management Area (LASMA), sacrificing 17 biosolids drying lagoons [\[2\]](#src-2)[\[12\]](#src-12). In October 2003 MWRD signed a mining agreement with Vulcan Materials Co., whose McCook Quarry sits just across the Des Plaines River; MWRD bought the mining plant itself — a primary crusher ($1.6M, 2005), mining trucks and loaders ($15.5M, 2007–08), a 2,000-ft rock conveyance tunnel under I-55 and the Des Plaines River ($5.4M, 2006) and the conveyor system ($32.4M, 2008) — so Vulcan could crush and convey reservoir rock to its quarry for processing and sale [\[4\]](#src-4)[\[12\]](#src-12). Full production mining began March 2008 with an expected ~20-year run [\[4\]](#src-4). Some 9.4–10 million cubic yards of overburden were removed (LASMA overburden contract $66.3M, finished 2010; Stage 2 removals 2012–2016), and in 2015 MWRD hauled 1.8 million cu yd of dirt to build a hilly 11-mile segment of the Centennial Trail near Willow Springs [\[2\]](#src-2)[\[4\]](#src-4)[\[12\]](#src-12)[\[13\]](#src-13). USACE contracts ($615.9M line item for Stages 1–2 land, engineering and construction) built the groundwater protection and connections: a 3-ft-wide bentonite slurry cutoff wall through the overburden, a double-row pressure-grouted curtain to ~350–370 ft depth keyed into shale around the 3-mile perimeter, rock highwall stabilization, retaining walls, distribution tunnels to the Mainstream Pumping Station, main tunnels to the Mainstream Tunnel, final reservoir prep with the aeration system, and added pumps and motors at the pumping station [\[2\]](#src-2)[\[4\]](#src-4)[\[13\]](#src-13). To keep the stored sewage from going anoxic and generating hydrogen sulfide, USACE and USGS modeled the basin (CUP 0-D, MAC3D and Dynamic Lake Model studies) and specified a coarse-bubble aeration system with airflow of at least 1.2 m³/s whenever combined sewage is stored [\[14\]](#src-14); USACE also ran hydraulic model studies of a morning-glory inlet and manifold outlet for the reservoir [\[24\]](#src-24). An earlier (1991) USACE Waterways Experiment Station numerical water-quality model had sized the aeration system against a 450 tons/day peak oxygen demand, computing a need for 5,276 diffuser heads (7-in.-dia. ceramic, 1.4 scfm gas flow each) — a design-stage figure from a pre-construction study, not confirmed here as the as-built diffuser count [\[33\]](#src-33). Stage 1 (3.5 BG) mining finished in 2016 and the stage was dedicated December 4, 2017 [\[4\]](#src-4)[\[31\]](#src-31); the ASCE Illinois Section named it the 2018 Outstanding Civil Engineering Achievement [\[12\]](#src-12). Stage 1 filled for the first time in a major way in early 2018 (photos of the January/March 2018 first fills are on MWRD's site), capturing 27.2 BG in 2018 alone [\[12\]](#src-12).

## Upgrades & modernization timeline

- **1981** — Upper Des Plaines (O'Hare) tunnel online: first operating TARP segment, later the feeder for Majewski [\[1\]](#src-1).
- **1986** — USACE CUP General Design Memorandum defines federal interest in TARP Phase II [\[4\]](#src-4).
- **1998** — Majewski Reservoir completed (~$45M); Thornton north-lobe mining agreement signed with Hanson [\[4\]](#src-4)[\[9\]](#src-9).
- **1999 (May 10)** — McCook PCA signed with USACE [\[4\]](#src-4)[\[25\]](#src-25).
- **2001–2002** — Thornton enabling works: Vincennes Avenue relocation ($4.4M), TTR groundwater monitoring wells; McCook site prep, 73rd Street tunnel relocation ($15.1M), Willow Springs berm [\[4\]](#src-4).
- **2003 (March)** — Thornton Transitional Reservoir online ($54.7M) [\[4\]](#src-4); **(Sept 18)** Thornton PCA signed [\[4\]](#src-4); **(Oct)** Vulcan mining agreement for McCook [\[4\]](#src-4).
- **2004 (June)** — MWRD takes over Thornton design/construction from USACE [\[4\]](#src-4).
- **2005–2008** — McCook mining plant procured; conveyance tunnel (2006) and conveyor/maintenance facilities (2008); full-production mining from March 2008 [\[4\]](#src-4).
- **2006** — MWRD board expands McCook Stage 2 from the federally authorized 3.5 BG to 6.5 BG (total 10 BG) [\[4\]](#src-4).
- **2010** — LASMA overburden removal complete ($66.3M) [\[4\]](#src-4).
- **2011 (Dec 14)** — Clean Water Act consent decree lodged (U.S. and Illinois v. MWRD): $675,000 civil penalty, $25–50M green-infrastructure program, TARP completion schedule made federally enforceable; entered January 6, 2014 [\[6\]](#src-6)[\[15\]](#src-15).
- **2013** — Thornton north-lobe excavation complete (76M tons removed since 1998) [\[3\]](#src-3)[\[4\]](#src-4)[\[10\]](#src-10).
- **2015** — Thornton dam/grout curtain ($80.8M), inlet-outlet tunnels/gates ($147M) and final prep ($63.5M) complete; reservoir operational end of 2015 (first fill Nov 26) [\[4\]](#src-4)[\[9\]](#src-9)[\[26\]](#src-26); McCook Stage 1 & 2 overburden work done [\[4\]](#src-4).
- **2016–2017** — McCook Stage 1 mining complete (2016); expanded Stage 2 overburden removal (2016); Thornton surface aeration installed ($1.9M, 2017); McCook Stage 1 operational end of 2017 [\[4\]](#src-4).
- **2018–2019** — McCook Stage 2 slope stabilization ($8.9M, 2019); ASCE award for Stage 1 (2018) [\[4\]](#src-4)[\[12\]](#src-12).
- **2020** — Thornton odor-control systems installed under the $9.1M odor control/TTR decommissioning program [\[5\]](#src-5); last CSO recorded in Thornton's service area [\[3\]](#src-3)[\[7\]](#src-7).
- **2022** — Des Plaines Inflow Tunnel to McCook completed ($109.9M) [\[4\]](#src-4); Thorn Creek Overflow Tunnel connected to Thornton (Sept), completing the composite system; TTR decommissioned (Sept; contract closed 2023 at $26.1M) [\[3\]](#src-3)[\[4\]](#src-4).
- **2023** — Thornton rock dam treatment and geotechnical instrumentation completed ($6.1M) [\[4\]](#src-4).
- **2019–2025** — McCook Stage 2 rock wall stabilization (Contract 17-131-4FR), under construction as of the June 2025 status report ($22.4M, 53% federal), reported complete in the December 2025 status report at a final cost of $21.862M [\[4\]](#src-4)[\[30\]](#src-30); Stage 2 final reservoir prep (Contract 17-132-4F, $24.8M, 69% federal) remained a future contract as of Dec. 2025 [\[30\]](#src-30).
- **2025** — MWRD announces Stage 2 operational date slipping from the consent-decree Dec 31, 2029 to an estimated **Dec 31, 2032**, because Vulcan's mining pace tracks regional stone demand, which slowed; the 2014 decree anticipated that "the long-term market for stone can be volatile and is outside of the MWRD's control" [\[6\]](#src-6).
- **2026 (July)** — After record fills and severe odor complaints, MWRD announces two projects totaling $5M (carbon-filter odor capture and a misting system) at Thornton, due by end of December 2026, bringing decade-long odor mitigation spending there to $8.6M [\[19\]](#src-19).

## Current operations

TARP is complete and fully operational except McCook Stage 2 [\[6\]](#src-6). MWRD publishes live reservoir levels (a McCook live stream and a TARP reservoir-levels page) and NOAA maintains a gauge on the Mainstream Deep Tunnel at McCook [\[7\]](#src-7). Combined sewage enters the reservoirs only after tunnel storage is exhausted; afterwards the water is pumped out — McCook via distribution tunnels to the Mainstream Pumping Station in Hodgkins and on to Stickney WRP; Thornton to Calumet WRP; Majewski to Kirie WRP — at rates limited by plant treatment capacity [\[2\]](#src-2)[\[3\]](#src-3)[\[21\]](#src-21). Drawdown of a full Thornton takes on the order of a month; the blog-documented pumping pace in July 2026 was roughly 2 BG per 10 days [\[12\]](#src-12)[\[18\]](#src-18). Water quality in storage is managed with the McCook coarse-bubble aeration system [\[14\]](#src-14), floating solar-powered surface aerators that oxygenate the top layer to control odors at the reservoirs [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[2\]](#src-2)[\[3\]](#src-3), sprayers/misting, and (from 2026) carbon filtration at Thornton [\[19\]](#src-19). Groundwater protection is verified through an IEPA-approved monitoring program: 111 wells along the tunnels plus reservoir-specific networks — four wells at Majewski (QK-1 through QK-4, at the reservoir's four corners, plus nine privately owned water-supply wells within 1,000 ft tracked for reference) sampled for six consecutive weeks after each fill event, seven wells plus the Main Quarry Sump at Thornton (fill threshold −280 ft CCD), and five to seven wells at McCook (high-stage threshold −220 ft CCD) [\[22\]](#src-22)[\[38\]](#src-38).

Performance to date: CSO frequency system-wide has fallen from ~100 days per year pre-TARP to ~50 with the tunnels alone [\[1\]](#src-1); Thornton has captured over 99% of the volume entering its system and eliminated CSOs in its service area since 2020, protecting 556,000 people in 14 communities and roughly 182,000 structures [\[3\]](#src-3)[\[4\]](#src-4)[\[9\]](#src-9); Majewski has captured ~8.0 BG lifetime [\[4\]](#src-4)[\[31\]](#src-31); McCook Stage 1 ~137.9 BG through September 2025 [\[6\]](#src-6)[\[31\]](#src-31). Benefit estimates (nominal-dollar figures from different years, tracking successive MWRD/USACE re-evaluations as the project grew): Thornton ~$40M/yr in flood-reduction benefits [\[20\]](#src-20), with $713.7M in cumulative flood-damage-reduction benefits to its 14 communities reported through December 2025 [\[31\]](#src-31); McCook was projected at $90M/yr in an April 2008 early-CUP-planning snapshot [\[20\]](#src-20), stated as "more than $143 million per year" in MWRD's TARP Status Report as of June 30, 2020 [\[5\]](#src-5), and "more than $175 million per year" in the 2025 fact sheet, status report and 2026 budget-in-brief [\[2\]](#src-2)[\[4\]](#src-4)[\[31\]](#src-31); MWRD's engineering chief has cited a benefit-cost ratio above 3 for McCook [\[13\]](#src-13). Majewski's cumulative flood-damage-reduction figure — $116M as of the April 2008 planning snapshot [\[20\]](#src-20) — has since climbed steadily in MWRD's own annual reporting: over $560M as of the 2021 budget cycle [\[32\]](#src-32), over $652M through end of 2022 [\[36\]](#src-36), over $730.2M through end of 2023 [\[37\]](#src-37), and over $824.6M as of the 2026 budget cycle [\[31\]](#src-31) — closing the gap this doc had previously flagged as "not independently updated."

## Notable events & incidents

- **May 2020 storms.** With the system overwhelmed, MWRD reversed the waterways into Lake Michigan on May 15 and May 17–18, 2020 — the backstop action TARP is designed to avoid [17 (context)][\[8\]](#src-8). (Reservoir-era reversals remain rare.)
- **June 2019.** Thornton's previous fill record, roughly 55% of capacity [\[16\]](#src-16).
- **July 2–4, 2026 storm (record fill).** After 8+ inches of rain in 30 days including 3+ inches on July 3–4, McCook hit 100% on July 3 — its sixth time at capacity in 2026 alone, versus five times in all of 2021–2025 — and Thornton reached 94% (~7.4 BG), by far its record; the system as a whole held over 13 BG, "nearly 20,000 Olympic-sized swimming pools," while Majewski stayed comparatively empty [\[16\]](#src-16)[\[17\]](#src-17). CSOs still reached the Chicago River over three days, and the city logged ~400 basement-flooding and ~600 street-flooding complaints [\[16\]](#src-16). Illinois state climatologist Trent Ford warned that saturated soils and nearly full reservoirs raise regionwide flood risk even from moderate rain [\[16\]](#src-16).
- **July 2026 odor crisis at Thornton.** A nearly full, slowly draining reservoir of raw combined sewage produced a "rotten eggs" hydrogen-sulfide stench that residents of Thornton/South Holland called "horrific" and "putrid"; MWRD deployed large aerators, acknowledged its aerator-and-sprayer systems only partially work at such fill levels, and announced the $5M carbon-filter and misting projects (due Dec 2026, $8.6M cumulative odor spend) [\[8\]](#src-8)[\[19\]](#src-19)[\[27\]](#src-27). Drawdown was expected to take at least a month (Thornton was down to 82% and McCook to 54% by July 11) [\[18\]](#src-18).
- **Stage 2 schedule slip.** The consent-decree McCook completion (mining by Dec 31 2028, operational Dec 31 2029) has been pushed to an estimated Dec 31, 2032 because commercial demand for Vulcan's stone — not construction funding — sets the excavation pace [\[6\]](#src-6).
- **A useful contrast:** Thornton was excavated *for free* in rock-sale terms (Hanson bought the right to mine 76M tons) [\[9\]](#src-9)[\[10\]](#src-10), while McCook cost MWRD ~$81.6M (updated from ~$79.4M as of the June 2025 report) in "Vulcan rock mining hard costs less royalty," still rising because Stage 2 mining continues, plus the entire mining plant, because there MWRD owns the hole and pays for extraction net of stone royalties [\[4\]](#src-4)[\[30\]](#src-30).

## Open questions

- ~~Majewski physical dimensions~~ — largely resolved: USACE Chicago District's July 1989 Phase II GDM/EIS for the "O'Hare Reservoir" [\[28\]](#src-28)[\[29\]](#src-29) gives a primary-source figure set — 105-acre parcel, ~30-acre reservoir footprint, 1,050 acre-feet (342.1 MG) between −2.0 and +58.0 ft CCD, a 22-ft-below-grade working pool, 15-ft excavation bench at +46 ft CCD, 1-on-2 side slopes, and a 30-in. clay/10-in. RCC liner — which independently corroborates the previously "uncorroborated" 1,050-acre-foot figure. Still open: the exact date/ordinance renaming the O'Hare Reservoir for Commissioner Majewski was not located in this pass.
- **The "Des Plaines fly-ash" lead could not be confirmed.** No source tying fly ash disposal to the McCook Reservoir site, its Stage 2 dig, or the Vulcan/Hanson McCook quarries surfaced in this pass; regional coal-ash stories found relate to Joliet/Romeoville quarries on the lower Des Plaines. Needs checking against Illinois EPA records or MWRD board proceedings.
- **Mainstream Pumping Station rated capacity** — still unresolved for an MGD figure. MWRD's own facility page gives no MGD figure; the Industrial History blog's 568 MGD figure remains uncorroborated. A widely-repeated "1200 MGD" figure associated with the pumping station in some secondary sources (e.g. the Ven Te Chow Hydrosystems Lab page) turns out on close reading to describe the Stickney WRP's treatment capacity, not the pumping station's own rate — a dead end, noted here so it isn't re-chased. One characterization did surface: a 1992 peer-reviewed paper on TARP Phases I–II describes the station's dewatering rate as emptying a full Mainstream Tunnel "within two to three days," citing a 1986 USACE source — a performance description, not a rated MGD figure, so the open question stands [\[39\]](#src-39).
- **Whether the 2014 consent decree has been formally modified** to reflect the 2032 McCook date — checked MWRD's own news release on the schedule slip [\[6\]](#src-6): its language ("such delays were anticipated under the consent decree") indicates MWRD is relying on the decree's built-in market-volatility/force-majeure-type provisions rather than announcing a court-approved amendment, but no independent court record confirming (or ruling out) a formal modification was located.
- ~~Exact aeration hardware counts at each reservoir~~ — largely resolved for original design (not confirmed as-built): a 1991 USACE Waterways Experiment Station numerical model specified 5,276 diffuser heads (7-in. ceramic) for McCook against a 450 tons/day peak oxygen demand [\[33\]](#src-33); the 1989 O'Hare (Majewski) GDM specified 8 floating surface aerators (250 lbs O₂/hr each, 2,000 lbs/hr total capacity against a 1,300 lbs/hr peak demand) [\[28\]](#src-28). Still open: whether these design-stage counts match the as-built/current hardware, and the July 2026 emergency aerator deployment details at Thornton.
- ~~Precise TTR capacity~~ — resolved: 3.1 BG is corroborated across three independent-vintage sources (Stormwater Solutions, April 2008 [\[20\]](#src-20); TARP Status Report, June 2020 [\[5\]](#src-5); TARP Status Report, June 2025 [\[4\]](#src-4), which frames it as the "Thorn Creek overflow" portion of the now-composite Thornton Reservoir). The "~" hedge in the facilities table can be read as a rounding note, not genuine uncertainty.
- ~~Reconciliation of McCook benefit figures~~ — resolved: $90M/yr (McCook alone, April 2008 planning-era estimate) → $143M/yr (per the June 2020 TARP Status Report) → $175M/yr (per the June 2025 status report and McCook fact sheet) is a documented progression across three MWRD-sourced snapshots as the project's scope/valuation was updated, not merely "likely" successive re-evaluations.
