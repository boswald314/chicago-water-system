# River Reversal, Canals, Locks & Lakefront Controlling Works

*Chicago region water & sewer infrastructure research archive — Topic 02. Research completed July 2026.*

## Role in the system

The Chicago Area Waterway System (CAWS) is a roughly 78-mile network of natural rivers and man-made canals whose defining feature is that it flows **backwards**: instead of the Chicago and Calumet rivers draining into Lake Michigan, lake water, treated wastewater effluent, and stormwater are drawn *away* from the lake and sent southwest across the subcontinental divide into the Des Plaines–Illinois–Mississippi river system [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[9\]](#src-9).

**What flows in:** (a) Lake Michigan water admitted by gravity at three lakefront control points — the Chicago River Controlling Works/Chicago Harbor Lock at the river mouth, the T.J. O'Brien Lock & Dam on the Calumet River, and the Wilmette Pumping Station at the head of the North Shore Channel [\[27\]](#src-27); (b) treated effluent from MWRD's water reclamation plants (O'Brien WRP discharges to the North Shore Channel, Stickney WRP to the Sanitary & Ship Canal, Calumet WRP to the Little Calumet near the Cal-Sag) [\[26\]](#src-26); (c) stormwater and combined-sewer overflows from the tributary area [\[26\]](#src-26).

**What flows out:** essentially everything exits at the system's single downstream throat at Lockport, where the MWRD's Lockport Powerhouse and the adjacent Lockport Controlling Works discharge the entire flow of the CAWS into the Des Plaines River just above Joliet, exploiting a ~40-foot drop in elevation [\[2\]](#src-2)[\[3\]](#src-3)[\[10\]](#src-10). From there water passes the U.S. Army Corps of Engineers' (USACE) Lockport Lock and continues down the Illinois Waterway to the Illinois and Mississippi rivers [\[29\]](#src-29)[\[36\]](#src-36).

**Internal connections:** the North Shore Channel (Wilmette → North Branch at Foster/Lawrence Ave) feeds the North Branch of the Chicago River; the Main Stem and South Branch feed the Chicago Sanitary and Ship Canal (CSSC) at Damen Avenue (historic Robey Street); the Cal-Sag Channel carries the reversed Little Calumet/Calumet river flow 16 miles west to "Sag Junction," where it joins the CSSC at canal mile ~12 above Lockport [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[15\]](#src-15)[\[26\]](#src-26). The whole system is deliberately sloped to drain "downhill" toward Lockport [\[27\]](#src-27).

The system is also the legal conduit for the **Lake Michigan diversion**, capped by U.S. Supreme Court decree at 3,200 cubic feet per second (cfs) averaged over 40 years — just over 2 billion gallons per day for all Illinois uses combined (domestic supply, direct diversion, and stormwater runoff from the diverted watershed) [\[23\]](#src-23)[\[24\]](#src-24)[\[25\]](#src-25).

## Original construction

**Before the big canal.** Chicago's first reversal was accomplished with the Illinois & Michigan Canal (opened 1848). Its summit level was fed by steam pumps at Bridgeport that could lift about 100 cfs from the South Branch, and from 1861–1870 the city paid to run the pumps an extra 45 days per year purely to flush sewage away from the lake [\[34\]](#src-34). In 1871 the I&M's "deep cut" was completed, lowering the summit so lake water flowed by gravity to Lockport — the first true reversal — but the channel was far too small and the river soon relapsed, notably during heavy storms [\[34\]](#src-34). The catalyzing event was the storm of August 2, 1885 (over 6 inches of rain), which washed the river's filth far out toward the water-supply cribs; the oft-repeated claim that a cholera/typhoid epidemic killed ~12% of the city that year is repeated in some sources [\[30\]](#src-30) but is regarded by historians as a myth — the storm's real effect was political panic [\[38\]](#src-38).

**The Sanitary District and the Main Channel, 1892–1900.** The Illinois General Assembly created the Sanitary District of Chicago (SDC, today's MWRD) on May 29, 1889; voters approved it that November [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[9\]](#src-9). The enabling act mandated a channel able to maintain a continuous flow of not less than 80,000 cubic feet per minute at a depth of at least 14 feet and a current no faster than 3 mph — the "dilution solution," sized on Rudolph Hering's standard of 3.3 cfs per 1,000 population [\[1\]](#src-1). That 80,000 cu ft/min was only the statutory *minimum*; the 1907 International Waterways Commission report gives the Main Channel's full designed capacity as 600,000 cu ft/min (10,000 cfs), flowing at 1.25 mph in the earth sections and 1.9 mph in rock [\[45\]](#src-45) — precisely the ceiling the District was later accused of illegally exceeding by the late 1920s (see Notable events). Lyman E. Cooley served as first Chief Engineer (he resigned under board pressure in December 1890 and was succeeded by Benezette Williams; Isham Randolph became Chief Engineer in 1893 and saw the work through) [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[5\]](#src-5).

Ground was broken with ceremony — "Shovel Day" — on September 3, 1892, two miles west of Lemont on the Cook/Will county line [\[5\]](#src-5)[\[6\]](#src-6). The 28-mile Main Channel from Robey Street (Damen Ave) to Lockport was split into ~one-mile contract sections in three geological classes (earth; earth-and-rock; rock — nearly 15 miles of Niagaran dolomite from Willow Springs to Lockport) [\[1\]](#src-1). At peak, roughly 8,500 men worked on the channel [\[6\]](#src-6)[\[7\]](#src-7). Excavation totals: HAER reports slightly over 26 million cu yd of glacial drift plus 12 million cu yd of solid rock for the Main Channel proper, with river-diversion and Lockport/Joliet work bringing the total above 42 million cu yd [\[1\]](#src-1); Chicagology tabulates 28.5M cu yd drift + 12.91M cu yd rock + 43.5M cu yd for the Des Plaines River diversion, and 380,050 cu yd of retaining-wall masonry [\[5\]](#src-5); the Encyclopedia of Chicago rounds this to "29 million cubic yards of soil and 12 million of rock" — the largest earth-moving operation yet undertaken in North America [\[6\]](#src-6). Record months: August 1894 saw 1,201,688 cu yd of drift and 418,164 cu yd of rock removed [\[7\]](#src-7); Section 14's contractors took out 86,400 cu yd of solid rock in April 1895 alone [\[5\]](#src-5).

**The "Chicago School of Earth Moving."** The rock work forced contractors to invent machinery that engineering journals collectively dubbed the Chicago school of earth moving [\[1\]](#src-1). Documented plant included: 50+ steam shovels (at least 26 Bucyrus and 20+ Marion machines) [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[7\]](#src-7); twenty Lidgerwood traveling cableways running on tower-cars along track [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[30\]](#src-30); Brown and McMyler cantilever conveyors; the celebrated Mason & Hoover double-cantilever conveyor with a 320-foot bridge, designed by Arthur J. Mason and Frank K. Hoover [\[1\]](#src-1); 88 channeling machines (54 Sullivan) and 134 Ingersoll-Sergeant rock drills plus 150 Rand "Little Giant" drills, with forcite gelatin dynamite and Aetna/Atlas powder for blasting [\[1\]](#src-1); a grading machine pulled by 16 horses [\[7\]](#src-7). Machines and men from this project went on to build the Panama Canal — Isham Randolph himself joined the Panama board of consulting engineers in 1905 [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[8\]](#src-8)[\[30\]](#src-30).

**Cost.** Chief Engineer Randolph's accounting put Main Channel construction paid-and-estimated to January 1, 1900 at $23,693,014 (HAER rounds to $23.3 million, in ~1900 dollars) against an initial estimate of $26.8 million [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[5\]](#src-5). The SDC's own July 1921 internal *Engineering Data* pamphlet gives a later, more complete total for the original Main Channel — right of way, construction, bridges, controlling works and engineering combined — of $27,440,096.59 [\[46\]](#src-46). Statements at the opening put the Sanitary District's total spending, with land and auxiliary works, at about $33 million [\[7\]](#src-7)[\[39\]](#src-39); a contemporary eyewitness account in the *Farmers' Review* gave a more precise figure of $33,525,691 as of January 10, 1900, projecting a final cost near $44 million and favorably comparing the canal's cost and depth to the Suez ($100M), Erie ($300M cumulative), Manchester ($77M), Welland ($56M) and Kiel/North Sea ($37.44M) canals [\[50\]](#src-50). Labor was not peaceful: an 1893 strike involving ~2,000 workers ended with at least five men killed (named individuals and context below, Notable events) [\[8\]](#src-8)[\[48\]](#src-48), and a separate dynamite explosion at Willow Springs in July 1895 killed three more laborers [\[49\]](#src-49).

**Lockport Controlling Works (1895–1899).** Because the canal stood essentially at lake level for its whole length and then dropped ~40 feet at Lockport, the District built regulating works at the canal's terminus: designed by SDC assistant engineer Thomas T. Johnston with metal details by Alfred Noble; general contract to Christie & Lowe (Dec 1895), bear-trap foundation to Griffiths & McDermott ($60,335) [\[2\]](#src-2). As built it comprised a 160-foot-crest bear-trap dam — reportedly the largest of its type in the world, its two hinged steel leaves weighing 299,000 and 338,000 lbs — plus seven hand-operated modified Stoney sluice gates, each 30' wide with 20' of vertical travel, in fifteen framed bays (eight left empty for the future). Total cost: $373,000 [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[2\]](#src-2).

**Opening, January 1900.** Learning in December 1899 that Missouri would seek an injunction, the trustees moved quickly and quietly: on January 2, 1900 they ordered the needle dam at the Chicago River end cut down, letting the river begin to fill the channel; on January 16–17 the Lockport controlling gates were lowered/opened, establishing full flow (HAER says the gates were ordered lowered January 16; other accounts give January 17 for full opening) [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[5\]](#src-5)[\[8\]](#src-8). The SDC's own 1956 institutional history splits the difference: trustees traveled to Lockport on the night of January 16, and the gates were actually lowered "early the next morning" — i.e., January 17 — hours before Missouri's injunction suit was filed [\[43\]](#src-43). A preliminary, smaller opening the week before (reported by the *Farmers' Review* on January 10, 1900) had already visibly changed the Chicago River's color "from black to grey with hints of blue," convincing skeptical Chicagoans the project worked [\[50\]](#src-50). The steamer *Juliet* made the first transit January 20, 1900 [\[5\]](#src-5).

**Main Channel Extension & Lockport Powerhouse (1903–1907).** An act of July 14, 1903 authorized extending the channel from the Controlling Works through Lockport toward Joliet and developing water power [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[3\]](#src-3). Work began October 29, 1903; contracts went to Joseph J. Duffy (Sections 1–3) and Lorimer & Gallagher (Section 4). The head-and-tailrace works cost $4.7–4.77 million; the powerhouse, lock and movable dams roughly $800,000 more; the Butterfly Dam $242,000 [\[1\]](#src-1). The powerhouse (built 1904–1908 by Hayes Brothers of Janesville, WI; Randolph designing, G.M. Wisner acting chief after 1905, movable dams by E.L. Cooley, architect Frederick L. Barrett per NRHP documentation) was energized November 26, 1907, transmitting power ~30 miles at 34,000 V over aluminum cables — touted as the first large aluminum transmission east of the Mississippi — to a Western Avenue terminal station [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[3\]](#src-3)[\[11\]](#src-11). Original equipment: horizontal 6,400-hp turbine units with 54" Jolly-McCormick runners, each able to swallow 100,000 cu ft/min, driving 4,000-kW, 6,600-V Crocker-Wheeler generators ($101,366 contract), plus three 350-kW exciter sets [\[3\]](#src-3).

**North Shore Channel (1907–1910).** After the legislature added Evanston, Wilmette, Kenilworth, Winnetka, Hubbard Woods and Glencoe to the District in 1903, the SDC dug an 8-mile channel from Lake Michigan at Wilmette to the North Branch — 80' wide at the waterline, 30' at the bottom, 13' deep, right-of-way averaging 500' — for $4.1 million, completed November 1910 [\[12\]](#src-12)[\[14\]](#src-14). Excavation used steam shovels and cableway excavators; spoil dumped along the shore created 22 acres of new lakefill that is now much of Gillson Park [\[12\]](#src-12)[\[13\]](#src-13). The Wilmette Pumping Station and a 30-foot lock were built beneath the Sheridan Road bridge (station 1908–1910, lock finished 1912) to admit lake water and originally to pass boats [\[12\]](#src-12)[\[13\]](#src-13). At the channel's other end, where it rejoined the natural North Branch at River Park, the SDC also built a low dam in 1910 — a roughly four-foot drop needed to hold the channel's lower, canal-controlled water surface apart from the higher natural river upstream — which stood as the only "waterfall" on the Chicago River system until Friends of the Chicago River, the U.S. Army Corps of Engineers, MWRD, and the Chicago Park District removed it in 2018 to restore fish passage (see the North Branch Pumping Station document in this archive for the full removal story) [\[41\]](#src-41).

Upstream of the mapped system and entirely outside MWRD's jurisdiction, Lake County's North Shore Water Reclamation District discharges treated effluent from its Clavey Road plant (Highland Park) into the Skokie River, which flows south through the Chicago Botanic Garden and Skokie Lagoons to form the North Branch of the Chicago River that this channel joins — one of several non-MWRD inflows along this stretch, alongside the independent Lake Michigan water-supply intakes of Evanston, Wilmette (which also wholesales to Kenilworth), Winnetka, and Glencoe [\[42\]](#src-42).

**Cal-Sag Channel (1911–1922).** To reverse the Calumet system, the District dug the 16.2-mile Calumet-Saganashkee Channel from the Little Calumet at Blue Island to the CSSC — 20' deep, 60' wide through rock (36' bottom / 116' waterline in earth) — guarded at its east end by the 50' × 350–360' Blue Island Lock and controlling works [\[4\]](#src-4)[\[15\]](#src-15). Gates opened August 18, 1922; the channel was fully operational by August 26 [\[16\]](#src-16). The SDC's own 1956 retrospective gives the original construction cost as $14,291,500 — $11,945,600 for channel work, $565,800 for controlling works, $1,126,200 for bridges, and $653,900 for right-of-way — and the excavation as 13.8 million cu yd of spoil (10.3M cu yd earth, 3.5M cu yd rock); completing the Cal-Sag brought the District's total of navigable canals to 71 miles and its cumulative channel investment to about $100 million [\[43\]](#src-43).

**Chicago River Controlling Works / Chicago Harbor Lock (1936–1938).** The 1930 Supreme Court decree (below) required Illinois to end uncontrolled diversion and prevent storm reversals into the lake, so the Sanitary District built a lock and controlling works at the river mouth: a 600' × 80' chamber ~22–23' deep with two pairs of 30-foot sector gates, flanked by eight 10' × 10' sluice gates through the adjoining piers [\[17\]](#src-17)[\[18\]](#src-18)[\[22\]](#src-22).

**T.J. O'Brien Lock & Dam (1957–1965).** Under the 1946 Rivers & Harbors Act Calumet-Sag Modernization, USACE replaced the Blue Island Lock with a new lock and controlling works on the Calumet River at East 130th Street, sized 110' × 1,000' for 14-barge tows, with sector gates chosen because they can operate with flow in either direction and permitted an innovative lightweight cofferdam that saved money (winning 1958 bid was $800,000 below the next lowest) [\[4\]](#src-4)[\[19\]](#src-19). Built 1957–1960 — HAER reports the work as 44% complete at a cumulative cost of $2,645,500 in 1959, reaching 96% complete in 1960 (no final cost given); Wikipedia's $6,954,700 total project cost is consistent with that trajectory once the remaining ~56% of work is prorated, so the two figures likely aren't a real conflict, just different progress snapshots [\[4\]](#src-4)[\[19\]](#src-19). It opened to traffic in 1965 when Blue Island closed [\[4\]](#src-4)[\[19\]](#src-19). HAER gives sector-gate weight as 865 tons each; a commonly repeated 216-tons-each figure attributed to Wikipedia could not be relocated in the current live Wikipedia article and may reflect an older revision or a conflation with another structure — treat as unconfirmed [\[4\]](#src-4)[\[19\]](#src-19).

- **1957–1965** — O'Brien Lock & Controlling Works built and commissioned; Blue Island Lock demolished [\[4\]](#src-4)[\[19\]](#src-19).
- **1960s** — Powerhouse turbines 3, 4 and 7 removed [\[3\]](#src-3).
- **1976–77** — Powerhouse turbine 5 retired in place [\[3\]](#src-3).
- **ca. 1986** — 20'-wide motorized vertical-lift sluice gate replaces the original 48' movable dam at the powerhouse; the 12' dam removed and bay filled [\[3\]](#src-3).
- **1984** — Operation and maintenance of Chicago Harbor Lock transfers from MWRD to USACE (MWRD keeps the sluice gates) [\[17\]](#src-17).
- **Late 1990s** — Lockport Controlling Works modernization (MWRD Contract 96-882-2M): gates 4–7 shortened 4', new electric operating machinery with remote operation, control-room addition atop the 1890s tower house; powerhouse gets outdoor transformer banks and 6,000-V breakers [\[2\]](#src-2)[\[3\]](#src-3).
- **ca. 2000** — Two modern vertical Kaplan turbine-generator units installed at Lockport Powerhouse [\[3\]](#src-3)[\[11\]](#src-11).
- **2002 / 2009 / 2011** — Electric fish dispersal barriers at Romeoville: demonstration barrier (2002), Barrier IIA (2009), Barrier IIB (2011) [\[31\]](#src-31).
- **2004** — Lockport Lock, Dam and Power House Historic District listed on the National Register (March 10) [\[11\]](#src-11).
- **2010–11** — Chicago Harbor Lock sector gates replaced in a major overhaul (McHugh Construction) [\[37\]](#src-37).
- **2012** — Wilmette Pumping Station rebuilt for $17.5 million: two new pumps (four judged unnecessary), three hydraulic lift gates replace the single 30' roller gate, carp-exclusion grates on unused pump tunnels [\[13\]](#src-13).
- **2017** — USACE completes Lockport Lock major rehabilitation, $150,280,294 [\[36\]](#src-36).
- **2024** — Chicago Harbor Lock winter maintenance/construction completed for the 2024 season [\[18\]](#src-18).

## Current operations

MWRD operates the system hydraulically; USACE operates the two navigation locks. In normal conditions lake water enters by gravity at the three lakefront structures (the lake typically stands ~2–4 feet above the maintained canal level, about −2 ft Chicago City Datum), flows downhill through the CAWS, and exits at Lockport, spinning the powerhouse turbines [\[13\]](#src-13)[\[26\]](#src-26)[\[27\]](#src-27). MWRD sets outflow at Lockport with the powerhouse, its sluice gates, and the Controlling Works gates; the USACE Water Control Manual governs coordination with the downstream Lockport Lock [\[27\]](#src-27)[\[29\]](#src-29). Hydroelectric revenue is real but declining — and briefly collapsed outright: 41.6M kWh ($1.30M) in 2015; 41.1M kWh ($1.09M) in 2019; 21.9M kWh ($836,182) in 2022 [\[10\]](#src-10); then generator outages took the powerhouse to zero kWh generated in 2023 (that year's $645,618 in "revenue" came entirely from renewable-energy-credit sales, not power sold), only 343,733 kWh in 2024, with 29.1M kWh ($1.33M) budgeted for 2025 and 32.4M kWh ($1.51M) targeted for 2026 as the units are brought back online [\[54\]](#src-54)[\[55\]](#src-55).

Before forecast storms MWRD pre-draws the canals ("drawdown") by increasing Lockport discharge and closing the lake gates, creating storage; during extreme events, when the canals threaten to overtop, MWRD orders the lakefront sluice gates — and in the worst cases the locks themselves — opened to release flood water to Lake Michigan (a "backflow" or gravity reversal) [\[27\]](#src-27)[\[28\]](#src-28). The Chicago Harbor Lock's annual traffic figures vary by year and source: an older USACE figure put it at ~40,000 vessels and ~900,000 passengers a year, while USACE reported that the 2025 season alone saw 9,859 lockages serving over 72,000 vessels and nearly 1.3 million passengers [\[17\]](#src-17)[\[18\]](#src-18)[\[40\]](#src-40). It is commonly cited as the nation's fourth-busiest lock for commercial traffic and second-busiest for recreational traffic — USACE describes it and Seattle's Hiram M. Chittenden Locks as the two busiest locks in the country [\[17\]](#src-17)[\[18\]](#src-18)[\[40\]](#src-40). Diversion is metered under the 1980 amended decree: Illinois' total take is capped at 3,200 cfs on a 40-year running average, administered by IDNR allocation permits with USACE performing Lake Michigan Diversion Accounting; within that, MWRD holds 305 cfs at the lakefront structures — 270 cfs "discretionary diversion" to keep the canals from going septic (dilution/water-quality flow) plus 35 cfs navigation make-up water, per a 2008 MWRD planning report [\[25\]](#src-25)[\[26\]](#src-26). The decree's accounting has three legs — domestic pumpage not returned to the lake, stormwater runoff from the diverted ~673-square-mile watershed, and direct diversion at the three lakefront structures (itself split into lockage, discretionary flow, navigation make-up, and uncontrolled leakage) — and layers annual limits on top of the 40-year average: a 3,680-cfs annual ceiling that may be exceeded only a limited number of times, an absolute 3,840-cfs annual maximum never to be exceeded, and a maximum permitted cumulative deficit of 2,000 cfs-years over the averaging period's first 39 years [\[52\]](#src-52). Even nominally "closed" gates leak: a 1993–94 USGS acoustic Doppler study measured mean uncontrolled leakage of roughly 130–190 cfs through the Chicago River Controlling Works, ~20 cfs at O'Brien, and 15–59 cfs at Wilmette (dropping after MWRD sealed unused pump bays there in mid-1993) — all of which counts against the diversion cap [\[53\]](#src-53). The 270-cfs figure is dated: the 2024 USACE Lockport Water Control Manual states the discretionary allowance has since been reduced to 220 cfs through 2030 [\[29\]](#src-29) — a different number and schedule than the 101-cfs-from-2015 figure that circulates in some secondary sources. Since TARP tunnels came online in 2006, combined sewer overflow events to the waterways have roughly halved (from ~100 to ~50 days/year) [\[8\]](#src-8).

## Notable events & incidents

- **Jan 2 & 16–17, 1900** — The stealth opening. Trustees cut the Kedzie needle dam ahead of Missouri's injunction; Lockport gates opened two weeks later [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[5\]](#src-5). Missouri sued (injunction bill filed January 1900); the Supreme Court allowed the case (*Missouri v. Illinois*, 180 U.S. 208 (1901)) but ultimately dismissed it on the merits — Holmes finding Missouri could not prove Chicago's sewage caused St. Louis typhoid — on February 19, 1906 (*Missouri v. Illinois*, 200 U.S. 496) [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[20\]](#src-20).
- **1901–1925 diversion battles** — The SDC had operated since the reversal under a Secretary of War diversion permit issued May 8, 1899 [\[43\]](#src-43); the Secretary then ordered discharge cut to 200,000 cu ft/min (April 9, 1901), then permitted 250,000 cu ft/min (≈4,167 cfs) on December 5, 1901; the District ignored the cap, diverting ~8,500–10,000 cfs by the 1920s (peak ~10,000 cfs in 1928); Canada filed six diplomatic protests 1912–24 [\[21\]](#src-21)[\[22\]](#src-22). In *Sanitary District of Chicago v. United States*, 266 U.S. 405 (Jan 5, 1925), a unanimous Court (Holmes) upheld the federal injunction limiting the diversion to the permitted amount [\[21\]](#src-21); a new Secretary of War permit (March 3, 1925) allowed 8,500 cfs conditionally while treatment plants were built [\[22\]](#src-22)[\[24\]](#src-24).
- **1929–1930** — *Wisconsin v. Illinois*: the Court held the diversion unlawful (1929) and Chief Justice Hughes' April 21, 1930 decree stepped it down: 6,500 cfs (+ domestic pumpage) from July 1, 1930; 5,000 cfs from Dec 31, 1935; 1,500 cfs (+ domestic pumpage) after Dec 31, 1938 — the deadline that forced construction of sewage treatment works and the Chicago Harbor Lock [\[22\]](#src-22)[\[24\]](#src-24).
- **1954–1956** — With the 1930 decree's 1,500-cfs floor felt as inadequate for waterway sanitation, Congress twice took up bills to grant a temporary 1,000-cfs diversion increase for a 3-year experimental period under Corps of Engineers supervision; a 1954 version passed but was vetoed by the President, and a modified bill had passed the House and was pending in the Senate as of late 1956 — a preview of the pressure that led to the 1967 decree [\[43\]](#src-43).
- **1967 / 1980** — The June 12, 1967 decree (388 U.S. 426) reset the cap at 3,200 cfs *including* domestic pumpage; the December 1, 1980 amendment (449 U.S. 48) added the 40-year running-average accounting and flexibility for new suburban allocations. Illinois later ran cumulative overdrafts and agreed in a 1996 MOU with the Great Lakes states to pay the excess back [\[22\]](#src-22)[\[23\]](#src-23)[\[24\]](#src-24)[\[25\]](#src-25).
- **Backflows to the lake** — MWRD records show gravity reversals to Lake Michigan at the lakefront structures on: Sept 13–16 and Dec 27–28, 2008 (remnants of Hurricane Ike — the largest modern release); Feb 26–27, Mar 8 and Jun 19, 2009; Jul 24, 2010; May 29 and Jul 23, 2011; Apr 18–19, 2013; Jun 30–Jul 1, 2014; Jun 15–16, 2015; Jul 24, 2016; Apr 29–30 and Oct 14–15, 2017; Oct 3, 2019; May 15 and 17–18, 2020 [\[28\]](#src-28).
- **Asian carp emergency, 2009–2010** — eDNA of Asian carp detected above the electric barrier Nov 20, 2009; a December 2009 rotenone kill during barrier maintenance and a live bighead carp found in Lake Calumet (June 2010) triggered lawsuits by Michigan and other states demanding closure of the Chicago and O'Brien locks; the Supreme Court declined to reopen the original diversion decree [\[8\]](#src-8)[\[22\]](#src-22)[\[31\]](#src-31).
- **1893 strike violence** — canal laborers near Lockport, angered by pay deception (a promised 30 cents/hour for an 8-hour day that in practice often ran over 13 hours) and poor camp conditions, struck; when state militia fired on the picket lines on June 10, twelve workers were wounded and five killed — Gregor Kilka, Jakob Ignatz, Thomas Moorski, Mike Berger, and 17-year-old bystander John Kluga, all immigrants — halting construction for nearly a week [\[8\]](#src-8)[\[48\]](#src-48).
- **July 23, 1895 — Willow Springs dynamite explosion** — a premature blast while tamping dynamite on Section 2 (contractor McArthur Bros.) instantly killed three laborers — William Kelly (32), Thomas Soaker (45), and Joseph Smith (35) — and seriously injured a fourth, Matthew Healy, per a contemporary newspaper account [\[49\]](#src-49).
- **Emergency diversion increases for downstream navigation, 1930–1956** — on four documented occasions the District (or the Corps, drawing on the diversion) temporarily raised flow above the decreed limit to relieve low water on the Illinois/Mississippi system, then made it up with reduced diversion later: Aug 14–Sept 18, 1930 (to 9,030 cfs, low water in the Illinois River; authorized baseline then 6,500 cfs); July 8–Aug 7, 1936 (+1,500 cfs, low water above the Illinois's mouth to aid Mississippi lock/dam construction; baseline 5,000 cfs); Jan 25–27, 1944 (1,450 cfs raised to a planned 5,000 cfs for 20 days — cut short after 2 days by rain — to give Naval craft 9 feet of draft at Chain of Rocks during a wartime drought); and Oct 23–Nov 1, 1956 (+1,900 cfs for 10 days, prolonged Missouri/upper-Mississippi drought, after Illinois's governor petitioned the Secretary of Defense) [\[51\]](#src-51).
- **1939 power collapse** — diversion cuts halved Lockport generation, ending the powerhouse's role as a major municipal power source [\[3\]](#src-3).

## Open questions

- Resolved (July 2026): Original Cal-Sag Channel (1911–22) construction cost and excavation volume, previously "not found in accessible sources," turned up in the SDC's own 1956 retrospective pamphlet: $14,291,500 total cost and 13.8M cu yd of spoil moved — see Facilities & capacities and Original construction above [\[43\]](#src-43). The earlier, more detailed 1928 *Engineering Works* report and Lanyon's manuscript were not re-checked for this pass but are no longer needed to close this question.
- Total worker deaths over the 1892–1900 construction remain unknown in aggregate, though the documented toll is larger than previously recorded here: five killed in the 1893 strike (now named) plus three more in a July 1895 dynamite explosion at Willow Springs — see Notable events [\[48\]](#src-48)[\[49\]](#src-49).
- Nominal construction cost of the Chicago Harbor Lock (1936–38) and of the 2010–11 gate replacement. A dedicated HAER data page for the Chicago Harbor Lock/Chicago River Controlling Works (paralleling the CSSC/Lockport/O'Brien HAER reports) does not appear to exist. The USACE fact-sheet PDF (lrc.usace.army.mil/Portals/36/docs/navigation/ChicagoLock.pdf) again failed to load in July 2026 with a server-certificate mismatch, so this structure's sourcing still rests on Wikipedia, USACE news releases, and a hobbyist blog.
- Reconciling Lockport Powerhouse footprint (386'×69' vs 160'×386' contract dimensions vs 142'×390' in 1905 Engineering News, vs a 2019 Shaw Local retrospective's 385'×70') [\[3\]](#src-3)[\[4\]](#src-4)[\[35\]](#src-35) — now a further three-way conflict on height, since the SDC's own July 1921 *Engineering Data* pamphlet gives "385 ft. x 70 ft. x 47 ft. high" [\[46\]](#src-46), close to the 385–386'×69–70' footprint but with a notably lower building height than the ~100' figure elsewhere. (The O'Brien sector-gate-weight and lock-cost figures below are now largely reconciled — see Original construction.)
- Whether the O'Brien dam's "93'-long reinforced concrete control works" (HAER, describing just the poured-concrete gate section) relates to the ~293'–297' overall dam length given by Wikipedia — not reconciled [\[4\]](#src-4)[\[19\]](#src-19).
- Largely resolved (July 2026): whether full flow at Lockport began Jan 16 or Jan 17, 1900. The SDC's own 1956 institutional history has trustees traveling to Lockport the night of Jan 16 and the gates actually being lowered "early the next morning" — i.e., Jan 17 — which reconciles HAER's "ordered lowered Jan 16" with other accounts' "Jan 17" as order vs. execution [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[8\]](#src-8)[\[43\]](#src-43).
- Resolved (July 2026): the "post-2015 101-cfs" discretionary-diversion figure previously flagged here does not appear in the current authoritative source. The 2024 USACE Lockport Water Control Manual instead documents a cut from 270 cfs to 220 cfs "through 2030" [\[29\]](#src-29) — see Facilities & capacities and Current operations above. Whether a 101-cfs figure exists in some other document, and how it relates to the 220-cfs figure, is unresolved.
- Resolved (July 2026): the "1956–58 temporary diversion increases for Mississippi River navigation" turned out to be one of a documented series of four emergency increases (1930, 1936, 1944, 1956) plus a separate, never-enacted 1954–56 legislative push for a standing 3-year increase — see Notable events and the Upgrades timeline above [\[43\]](#src-43)[\[51\]](#src-51).
