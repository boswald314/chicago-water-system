# TARP Phase I: The Deep Tunnel System

*Research compiled July 2026. All web sources accessed July 2026.*

## Role in the system

TARP Phase I — universally nicknamed "Deep Tunnel" — is the conveyance-and-interception half of the Metropolitan Water Reclamation District of Greater Chicago's (MWRD, formerly the Metropolitan Sanitary District, MSD) Tunnel and Reservoir Plan. It is a separate, later-built system from the 19th-century intercepting sewers described elsewhere in this archive: those shallow interceptors (built after 1907) still do the first-line job of carrying dry-weather sewage to the water reclamation plants (WRPs) [\[1\]](#src-1). TARP Phase I exists for the moments when a storm overwhelms that shallow system. Combined sewers — carrying stormwater and sanitary sewage in the same pipe across roughly 360 square miles of Chicago and 51 suburbs — surcharge past the capacity of the intercepting sewers and the WRPs; historically this backed up into basements or spilled untreated, via combined sewer overflows (CSOs), into the Chicago River, the Calumet River, the Des Plaines River, and ultimately Lake Michigan [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[2\]](#src-2). TARP intercepts that overflow before it reaches the waterways: local sewers drain into MWRD connecting structures, many with regulating gates or weirs, which under normal flow route water to the shallow interceptors but under storm flow divert the excess down vertical drop shafts into 109.4–110.4 miles of rock tunnels 8 to 33 feet in diameter, lying 150 to 300 feet below the waterways they parallel [\[2\]](#src-2)[\[3\]](#src-3)[\[6\]](#src-6)[\[26\]](#src-26). From the tunnels, flow moves by gravity toward pumping stations and, since the 1980s–2010s, into the three Phase II reservoirs (Majewski, Thornton, McCook — covered in a companion document in this archive) for temporary storage until it can be pumped back up to a WRP for treatment [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[2\]](#src-2)[\[4\]](#src-4).

Upstream to downstream, Phase I is four separate tunnel systems that converge on shared low points:

- The **Upper Des Plaines (O'Hare) System**, the smallest, drains an 11-square-mile combined-sewer area around O'Hare Airport (Des Plaines, Mount Prospect, Arlington Heights) and discharges toward the James C. Kirie Water Reclamation Plant in unincorporated Des Plaines Township, from 1998 onward via the Gloria Alitto Majewski Reservoir [\[2\]](#src-2)[\[3\]](#src-3).
- The **Des Plaines System** runs along the Des Plaines River and its North Branch tributary, draining a wider swath of the northwest suburbs, and — together with Mainstream — feeds toward the McCook area [\[2\]](#src-2)[\[4\]](#src-4).
- The **Mainstream System** is the spine of the network: it runs from the Wilmette Pumping Station and North Shore Channel south under the North Branch and South Branch of the Chicago River and the Chicago Sanitary & Ship Canal to the McCook/Hodgkins area, where the TARP Mainstream Pumping Station lifts captured flow roughly 300 feet to the Stickney Water Reclamation Plant — the world's largest sewage treatment plant [\[4\]](#src-4)[\[19\]](#src-19)[\[21\]](#src-21).
- The **Calumet System** drains the Far South Side and south suburbs along the Calumet Sag Channel, the Little Calumet and Grand Calumet Rivers to near the Indiana state line, converging on a Calumet TARP Pumping Station roughly 365 feet below grade that lifts flow to the Calumet Water Reclamation Plant in Riverdale [\[3\]](#src-3)[\[19\]](#src-19).

Downstream of Phase I, since the 1980s–2010s, three of the four systems also feed Phase II storage: Upper Des Plaines feeds Majewski Reservoir, Calumet feeds Thornton Composite Reservoir, and Mainstream/Des Plaines feed McCook Reservoir — but the tunnels themselves were built, funded, and largely completed as a stand-alone pollution-control system fifteen to thirty years before their reservoirs existed [\[2\]](#src-2)[\[4\]](#src-4). That original 1972 plan, in fact, envisioned a very different storage endpoint than what was ultimately built: a single 57,000-acre-foot rock quarry reservoir at McCook-Summit with three internal basins, a small quarry reuse at the old Stearns Quarry near 28th and Halsted, and a modest reservoir at the O'Hare plant — not the three separate Majewski/Thornton/McCook reservoirs that Phase II eventually became [\[7\]](#src-7).

## Original construction

**Origins.** Planning traces to 1967, when officials of the newly named Metropolitan Sanitary District of Greater Chicago, the State of Illinois, Cook County, and the City of Chicago formed a Flood Control Coordinating Committee and, that November, a Technical Advisory Committee to evaluate the region's flood and pollution options; after 1967–68 discussions stalled, the effort was reactivated in November 1970 [\[2\]](#src-2)[\[7\]](#src-7). The specific "deep tunnel" concept, however, predates that committee: **Vinton W. Bacon**, general superintendent of the Metropolitan Sanitary District of Greater Chicago from 1962 to 1970, is widely credited as the idea's originator, and Engineering News-Record profiled him as the sanitary engineer who "cleaned house" at the District during that tenure [\[22\]](#src-22). The technical proposal that gave the idea real shape was the **Harza-Bauer Report of May 1968** (Harza Engineering Company), which outlined a series of tunnels in the Niagaran and Galena-Platteville rock strata converging on a single mined/surface storage complex in the Calumet area — labeled "Alternate A, Original Deep Tunnel Plan" in the 1972 planning document [\[7\]](#src-7). By the time the plan was finalized in 1972, Bacon was gone and the roster had changed: Ben Sosewitz was MSD General Superintendent, Forrest Neil was Chief Engineer, and **Frank E. Dalton** — later Dalton's own MWRDGC obituary credits him with having "conceived and led" the "$3.2 billion" Deep Tunnel project — held the title of Assistant Chief Engineer [\[7\]](#src-7)[\[21\]](#src-21). In practice, both narratives are defensible and not mutually exclusive: contemporary reporting in 1979 also names Dalton as the MSD's "deputy chief engineer" who steered TARP "into construction and through the public arena," while Bacon is the earlier-tenure name credited with first floating a deep-tunnel concept for Chicago [\[10\]](#src-10)[\[22\]](#src-22). Dalton went on to serve roughly a decade as MWRDGC General Superintendent later in his career, overseeing Deep Tunnel's continued build-out [\[21\]](#src-21).

**The 1972 Chicago Underflow Plan.** The Flood Control Coordinating Committee's consulting team — engineers from the City, MSD, County, State and federal agencies, plus outside firms Consoer Townsend and Associates, Greeley and Hansen, Harza Engineering, Metcalf and Eddy, and Warren and Van Praag — evaluated 23 distinct alternatives (labeled A through T, with several "pumped-storage" sub-variants), including full sewer separation (rejected at an estimated cost "upwards of four billion dollars" with no flood-control benefit), a Leffler Plan of Lake Michigan diking, a Sheaffer Plan of piping all sewage to central Illinois farmland for spray irrigation, and several rival "Chicago Underflow Plan" quarry-storage configurations proposed separately by the City of Chicago's Bureau of Engineering [\[7\]](#src-7). In January 1972 the Technical Advisory Committee's interim "Evaluation Report of Alternative Systems" recommended a composite of Alternatives G, H, J and S (all City of Chicago "Underflow Plan" variants) at "Mod 3" storage sizing (sufficient to prevent lake backflow without further waterway channel work); the Flood Control Coordinating Committee unanimously adopted this recommendation, and the MWRD formally adopted the resulting **Chicago Underflow Plan**, soon renamed the Tunnel and Reservoir Plan, on **October 20, 1972** [\[2\]](#src-2)[\[7\]](#src-7)[\[10\]](#src-10). The December 1972 final report — "Development of a Flood and Pollution Control Plan for the Chicagoland Area," published by the Flood Control Coordinating Committee under chairman William F. Cellini — priced the recommended plan at $1,048,200,000 for surface collection/drop shafts ($93M), tunnels ($567.2M), storage facilities ($350M) and pumping stations ($38M), plus $100M contingency and $75M for engineering/legal/administrative costs, for a **total of $1,223,200,000 in 1972 dollars** — the widely cited "$1.223 billion" figure [\[2\]](#src-2)[\[6\]](#src-6)[\[7\]](#src-7). That 1972 report envisioned roughly 120 miles of tunnel, 10 to 42 feet in diameter, and a single primary 57,000-acre-foot McCook-Summit reservoir (with a smaller Stearns Quarry basin and an O'Hare-area reservoir) — dimensions and a storage architecture that differ noticeably from the 109.4–110.4-mile, 8–33-foot-diameter Phase I tunnels and the three separate Phase II reservoirs (Majewski, Thornton, McCook) actually built over the following five decades [\[2\]](#src-2)[\[4\]](#src-4)[\[7\]](#src-7).

**Financing.** The plan was designed to exploit the newly enacted Federal Water Pollution Control Act Amendments of 1972 (the modern Clean Water Act), whose Title II construction-grants program offered up to 75 percent federal funding for qualifying water-pollution-control facilities — a rate contemporary reporting says directly shaped Chicago's 1972 timing [\[10\]](#src-10). The 1972 report's own funding exhibit shows a ten-year, 1973–1982 financing schedule totaling roughly $2.65 billion for the combined Underflow Plan and associated treatment-plant upgrades, drawing on federal appropriations (contingent on the House/Senate reconciliation that became the 1972 CWA amendments on October 18, 1972, just before the report went to press), a $750 million Illinois State Bond Issue (passed November 3, 1970), and a $380 million MSD Bond Issue (approved September 22, 1969) [\[7\]](#src-7). In practice the federal share eroded badly during the Nixon-to-Reagan years: a 1985 Christian Science Monitor account of the tunnel's dedication reported that federal funding "practically dried up during the Reagan administration," forcing schedule and financing renegotiation [\[12\]](#src-12).

**Construction.** Ground was broken in 1975, sequenced so that finished tunnel segments could go into service while other segments were still under construction elsewhere [\[2\]](#src-2)[\[6\]](#src-6). Tunneling used two methods depending on rock conditions and size: purpose-built tunnel boring machines (TBMs, "moles") for most bores, and conventional drill-and-blast for the largest-diameter sections, chosen specifically to cut vibration, damage and cost relative to blasting [\[2\]](#src-2)[\[17\]](#src-17). MWRD's own TBM-performance comparison, tabulated across contracts awarded from 1977 to 2001, shows advance rates climbing over that span from about 48 linear feet per day (a 32.25-ft-diameter machine, 1977 contract) to over 160 linear feet per day (a 24.25-ft machine, 1995 contract) as machines, geotechnical understanding and contractor experience improved; a Little Calumet leg TBM reportedly bored 452 feet in a single day in 2002 — a record for the project [\[18\]](#src-18)[\[17\]](#src-17). The Robbins Company, a major American TBM manufacturer, supplied machines up to 10.8 meters (35.4 ft) in diameter for the project — among the largest TBMs built at the time — and cites more than 30 TBMs used across the roughly 20-year construction span [\[17\]](#src-17). Photographer Michael Earley documented the Morrison-Knudsen joint venture's construction of the Calumet-area tunnel in October 1982, showing a 40-foot cutting head TBM guided by laser, a "trailing floor" system for hauling excavated rock out by rail with a rotating dump mechanism at the shaft, and workers 400 feet below grade [\[23\]](#src-23). Nearly all bores stayed within the Silurian (Niagaran-age) dolomite bedrock; where infiltration risk was higher, crews grouted fractures and lined tunnels in concrete, and drop shafts were engineered with a split vertical shaft — one side for falling water, one for displaced air, with slotted walls to insufflate air into the water and cut impact energy at the bottom, plus a rooftop vent chamber to vent air on filling and admit it on dewatering [\[7\]](#src-7).

MWRD's own contract ledger (Table II of its status reports) names the general contractors and joint ventures for every completed Phase I contract. Selected examples: the **Mainstream Pumping Station** was split across three simultaneous contracts — Part I to a Peter Kiewit & Sons/J.F. Shea/Kenny Construction joint venture ($168.8M), Part II to a Healy/Ball/Grow Tunneling Corp. joint venture ($64.8M), Part III to a Morrison-Knudsen/Paschen Contractors joint venture ($28.0M) [\[2\]](#src-2). Mainstream tunnel segments went to rotating joint ventures of S.A. Healy, Ball Corp., Horn Construction, Paschen Contractors, Morrison-Knudsen, Kenny Construction, J.F. Shea and Peter Kiewit & Sons; the North Branch Chicago River tunnel and connecting structures went to a Perini/ICA/O&G joint venture ($167.9M) [\[2\]](#src-2). On the Calumet System, the Crawford-to-Calumet-Plant tunnel went to Traylor Brothers with Ferrera & Resco ($79.3M); the 140th Street industrial-area tunnel/shafts/connecting structures to a Kenny/Kiewit/Shea joint venture ($194.5M, the single largest Phase I contract); and the Little Calumet Leg — tunnels, shafts and connecting structures — to a Jay-Dee/Affholder joint venture ($168.7M), Jay Dee Contractors' first contract over $100 million [\[2\]](#src-2)[\[29\]](#src-29). On the Des Plaines System, Impregilo/Ebasco/Losinger built the Cermak-to-Fullerton segment ($147.7M) and Kenny/Kiewit/Shea the Fullerton-to-Prairie segment ($141.1M) [\[2\]](#src-2). On the Upper Des Plaines (O'Hare) System, Healy/Ball/Greenfield built Tunnel #20 ($35.7M), McHugh Construction built Tunnel #21 ($21.4M), a Jay-Dee/Kenny joint venture built the smaller #20B tunnel ($2.7M), and a separate Jay-Dee/Jay-Dee of Illinois contract built the #20A connecting structures ($4.6M) [\[2\]](#src-2). In total these completed Phase I contracts sum to $2,332,154,822 in nominal, bid-year dollars — not adjusted for inflation, financing costs, or escalation, and thus considerably below the higher "total program" figures ($3.8–$12.5 billion at various points) that critics and the GAO cited when engineering, financing, interest and Phase II reservoir costs were folded in [\[2\]](#src-2)[\[10\]](#src-10)[\[14\]](#src-14).

- **1990** — Phase II reservoir construction formally begins (per the Encyclopedia of Chicago's account), running in parallel with the final Phase I tunnel segments [\[9\]](#src-9).
- **1998** — Gloria Alitto Majewski Reservoir (feeding the Upper Des Plaines system) completed, the first Phase II facility [\[2\]](#src-2)[\[4\]](#src-4).

- **2022** — Thorn Creek Overflow Tunnel connects to Thornton Reservoir, completing the Calumet system's "composite" (CSO-plus-overbank-flood) mission [document 10 of this archive].
- **2023 (July 2)** — A storm exceeding local sewer and tunnel capacity forces roughly 20 CSO outfalls open across the county and releases over 1.1 billion gallons of combined sewage into Lake Michigan despite the completed tunnel/reservoir system, prompting renewed climate-adequacy criticism [\[15\]](#src-15)[\[16\]](#src-16).

## Current operations

In dry weather and light rain, local combined sewers drain through MWRD connecting structures into the shallow intercepting-sewer system exactly as they have since the early 20th century, bypassing TARP entirely [\[26\]](#src-26). When flow exceeds interceptor or treatment-plant capacity, regulating gates, weirs, or orifices at the connecting structures divert the excess to nearby drop shafts, which carry it down 150–300 feet into the rock tunnels below the waterways [\[7\]](#src-7)[\[26\]](#src-26). Air displaced by the rising water escapes through the drop shafts' separate air-side channel and rooftop vent chamber (with louvers added in 1985–86 to manage this more precisely), while water races through the tunnel network by gravity toward the pumping stations and Phase II reservoirs [\[7\]](#src-7)[\[2\]](#src-2). Once tunnel storage and, since the 2010s, reservoir storage are both exhausted in a severe event, remaining overflow spills at the connecting structures directly to the waterways as a CSO — the residual roughly 15% of pollution load the tunnels alone do not capture [\[2\]](#src-2)[\[3\]](#src-3). After a storm subsides, the Mainstream Pumping Station (dewatering Mainstream/Des Plaines tunnels and McCook Reservoir to Stickney WRP), the Calumet TARP Pumping Station (dewatering the Calumet tunnels, and since 2015 Thornton Reservoir, to Calumet WRP), and the O'Hare/Upper Des Plaines pumping arrangement (dewatering to Kirie WRP via Majewski Reservoir) empty the system back down at whatever rate the receiving WRP's treatment capacity allows [\[2\]](#src-2)[\[3\]](#src-3)[\[19\]](#src-19)[\[21\]](#src-21). MWRD's Collection Asset Management Plan (2022 update) formalizes annual inspection of drop manholes and connecting structures, condition assessment of TARP tunnel liners (noted as "relatively good" where limited manned entries have occurred, though the oldest tunnel segments — now 40-plus years old — are flagged for a more systematic asset-management approach going forward), and maintenance of the roughly 45 miles of intercepting sewer already rehabilitated under the District's infiltration/inflow reduction program [\[26\]](#src-26).

Phase I's steady-state performance figure, cited consistently across MWRD sources from the 2010s through 2025, is that the tunnels alone capture and enable treatment of about 85 percent of the combined sewer overflow pollution in the roughly 360-square-mile service area — down from essentially 0 percent capture (aside from the shallow interceptors' dry-weather role) before TARP, and reducing system-wide CSO days from roughly 100 per year to roughly 50 per year with tunnels alone (further reductions have come from the Phase II reservoirs, covered in the companion document in this archive) [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[2\]](#src-2)[\[4\]](#src-4). The Mainstream Pumping Station remains, per MWRD-adjacent reporting, the largest facility of its kind, with eight pumps (two pump houses of four, largest at 17,500 horsepower) capable of moving more than a billion gallons per day up roughly 300 feet through 14-inch force mains fed by dual redundant 138,000-volt utility feeds [\[19\]](#src-19). Contract 18-144-3M ("Mainstream TARP Pumps Rehabilitation," let 2021) reflects ongoing modernization of that original 1970s–80s pumping plant [\[30\]](#src-30). MWRD offers public tours of the Mainstream Pumping Station (Thursdays, roughly two hours, by email/phone reservation) [\[31\]](#src-31).

## Notable events & incidents

- **October 3, 1986 — the Jefferson/Monroe geyser.** A heavy thunderstorm produced air-water geysers exceeding 65 feet at several points along the operating (but not yet complete) tunnel system, including a roughly 30-foot geyser at the downtown intersection of Jefferson and Monroe that lifted a 61-year-old motorist's Pontiac Bonneville and nearly drowned her in the ejected water; the tunnel system itself "performed satisfactorily," absorbing the storm's volume, but the air-entrapment/transient-pressure dynamics of rapid filling became a lasting cautionary example cited in wastewater-engineering literature on deep-tunnel design generally [\[8\]](#src-8)[\[18\]](#src-18).
- **1979–1985 — the cost-and-schedule controversy.** A series of GAO reports dating from May 1978 tracked the full-program cost estimate (tunnels, reservoirs, associated treatment-plant work, and financing/escalation combined — the same basket as the 1972 report's own $2.65 billion total-program figure) climbing to $8.5 billion in GAO's May 1979 report and to $10.2 billion (1980 dollars) in GAO's CED-81-51, dated January 21, 1981 — a letter report to Sen. Charles Percy that this pass obtained and read in full. Using a 10-percent annual inflation assumption, that same report projected the total would reach $12.5 billion "by 1983," almost certainly the basis for the $12.5 billion figure the Washington Post reported as a current GAO estimate in November 1982 — more, contemporary reporting noted, than the Trans-Alaska Pipeline had cost [\[13\]](#src-13)[\[14\]](#src-14). The same GAO letter separately notes the Army Corps of Engineers' own estimate to complete TARP rose from $6.4 billion (1975) to over $10 billion (1980), while the Sanitary District's own estimate climbed from $2.6 billion (1972) to over $4 billion (1977) [\[13\]](#src-13). The Sierra Club and Environmental Defense Fund publicly opposed aspects of the program on grounds that the money would be poorly spent relative to the pollution benefit, while the Better Government Association's Terry Brunner called the project effectively "dead" as of 1985 even as MSD project superintendent Raymond Rimkus insisted it would eventually be finished [\[10\]](#src-10)[\[12\]](#src-12). MSD President Nicholas Melas defended the project on CBS's *60 Minutes* in 1982 during the height of this scrutiny [\[33\]](#src-33).
- **1985 — dedication amid an unfinished system.** The formal dedication of the tunnel system that spring came with only 47 of the planned 110 miles complete and federal Clean Water Act construction-grant funding "practically dried up" under the Reagan administration, forcing MSD to seek other financing to complete the remaining segments over the following two decades [\[12\]](#src-12).
- **1985 — labor and safety issues during construction.** Contemporary reporting noted an Illinois Attorney General lawsuit alleging the District improperly kept more than 300 workers in "temporary" job classifications to avoid civil-service testing requirements, and at least one worker death from carbon dioxide gas exposure during tunnel construction — a reminder of the routine underground-construction hazards (rock fall, gas accumulation, flooding) inherent to a project ultimately employing dozens of contractors over three decades [\[12\]](#src-12)[\[2\]](#src-2).
- **July 2, 2023 — a storm outruns the completed system.** Despite Phase I's completion in 2006 and McCook Reservoir Stage 1 online since 2017, up to 8 inches of rain in parts of the western suburbs forced roughly 20 CSO outfalls open and released more than 1.1 billion gallons of combined sewage into Lake Michigan; MWRD opened the Wilmette sluice gates and the Chicago River locks at Navy Pier to relieve pressure on the system, and hydrological engineers were quoted arguing the fixed-capacity tunnel-and-reservoir system, sized to 1960s–70s rainfall statistics, cannot fully absorb the more frequent, more intense storms now observed in the region [\[15\]](#src-15)[\[16\]](#src-16).
- **2026 — renewed strain.** As documented in the companion Phase II report in this archive, the July 2026 storm sequence pushed McCook and Thornton Reservoirs to record fill levels; while that event centers on Phase II reservoir capacity, it also illustrates the tunnels' own throughput limits, since tunnel storage (2.32 BG system-wide) fills and passes flow to the reservoirs well before either component is exhausted [document 10 of this archive].

## Open questions

- **Exact drop-shaft and connecting-structure counts remain unreconciled across sources.** One peer-reviewed conference paper (Schmidt/Fitzpatrick et al., 2009) cites "256 drop shafts" and "over 600 connecting structures, pumping stations, and other appurtenances" for the TARP system as a whole (Phase I tunnels plus the reservoirs then existing); a University of Illinois hydrosystems-lab summary instead cites "over 250 drop shafts" and "over 400 connecting structures." Neither MWRD status report reviewed here gives an authoritative Phase-I-only count; MWRD's Collection Asset Management Plan references a complete inventory in "Appendix 8" that was not accessible in this pass [\[26\]](#src-26)[\[27\]](#src-27)[\[28\]](#src-28).
- **The precise 1985 "first Mainstream segment" mileage/endpoints could not be independently confirmed.** Secondary sources describe "the first tunnel" coming online in 1985 and the Christian Science Monitor's 1985 dedication article puts 47 of 110 miles complete by that date, but no primary source located here states the specific length or exact endpoints (e.g., "Wilmette to Hodgkins") of that first operational Mainstream segment with certainty.
- **The Bacon-versus-Dalton "who conceived it" question is genuinely contested in the sources**, not merely a research gap: Bacon (MSD general superintendent 1962–1970) is credited in several secondary accounts as originator of the deep-tunnel concept, while Dalton's own MWRDGC obituary claims he "conceived and led" the project; the 1968 Harza-Bauer engineering report is the earliest concrete technical proposal located. This document presents both attributions with their respective sourcing rather than resolving the dispute.
- **Individual pumping-station design capacities in MGD or cfs were not found in any primary MWRD document reviewed.** The "more than one billion gallons per day" and "8 pumps / 17,500 hp" figures for the Mainstream Pumping Station trace to an enthusiast engineering blog (Industrial History / industrialscenery.blogspot.com) based on a pre-operational tour, not an MWRD-published spec sheet; MWRD's own facility page gives no MGD rating. This should be corroborated against an MWRD or USACE engineering document if one becomes available.
- **Total nominal system-wide cost figures vary widely and are not always comparable**: MWRD's contract-by-contract accounting shows Phase I tunnels alone at $2.332 billion (bid-year dollars, no interest or engineering markup); the 1972 planning document's own "Total Project Cost" figure of $1.223 billion already included contingency and engineering (or $2.653 billion including associated treatment-plant upgrades); and GAO's own full-program figures escalated from $8.5 billion (1979) to $10.2 billion (1980, per CED-81-51, now directly sourced — see Notable events) with a $12.5 billion 1983 projection, while the Encyclopedia of Chicago separately cites "~$4 billion" as of 1999 and Frank Dalton's obituary cites "$3.2 billion." These appear to bundle in Phase II reservoirs, financing/interest costs, and inflation escalation to differing, not always specified, degrees and different report years. Care is needed comparing any of these figures across sources.
- **The claim (found in one AI-summarized search result, not confirmed in an original document) that a system of "watertight bulkheads" was installed specifically to prevent recurrence of 1986-style geysering could not be verified against a primary engineering source**; the contract record does show Mainstream and Calumet "Bulkhead Removal" contracts in 1982 (removing what were more likely temporary construction bulkheads) followed by drop-shaft louver and slide-gate installation contracts in 1985–86, which is suggestive but not proof of a direct geysering-response engineering program.
- **HAER/HABS documentation appears not to exist for TARP.** No Library of Congress Historic American Engineering Record was located for any TARP Phase I facility in this pass — plausibly because the system, still under active construction into the 2000s and expansion into the 2030s, has not yet been treated as a historic engineering resource by that federal program.
