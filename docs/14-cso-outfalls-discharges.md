# CSOs, Outfalls, Discharges, Flooding & the Modern Waterway

## Role in the system

The Chicago Area Waterway System (CAWS) — the Chicago River (North, South and Main branches), the North Shore Channel, the Chicago Sanitary and Ship Canal (CSSC), the Cal-Sag Channel and the Calumet River — is the receiving water for two separate but interlocked discharge systems: treated effluent from the Metropolitan Water Reclamation District of Greater Chicago's (MWRD) seven water reclamation plants (WRPs), and combined sewer overflows (CSOs) that spill untreated when storms overwhelm the combined sewers of Chicago and 51 suburban satellite communities [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[2\]](#src-2)[\[3\]](#src-3).

**Upstream inputs.** Roughly 99.5% of the City of Chicago's sewer system is "combined" — a single pipe network carrying sanitary sewage and stormwater together — and it feeds MWRD's intercepting sewers, which in turn feed the Tunnel and Reservoir Plan (TARP, "Deep Tunnel") and the seven WRPs [\[4\]](#src-4)[\[5\]](#src-5). In dry weather essentially all flow reaches a plant for treatment. In wet weather, once TARP's tunnels and reservoirs and the WRPs are full, excess combined flow spills through fixed-elevation weirs at combined sewer outfalls directly into the CAWS, bypassing treatment entirely [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[4\]](#src-4).

**Downstream outputs.** Each WRP discharges its treated effluent to a specific CAWS reach: Stickney WRP (the world's largest wastewater plant) to the Chicago Sanitary and Ship Canal; the Terrence J. O'Brien WRP (formerly "North Side") to the North Shore Channel; Calumet WRP to the Cal-Sag Channel/Calumet River system; and the smaller Kirie, Egan, Hanover Park and Lemont plants to their local receiving streams in the Des Plaines and Salt Creek watersheds [\[6\]](#src-6)[\[7\]](#src-7)[\[8\]](#src-8)[\[9\]](#src-9). From the CSSC, flow continues southwest through the Des Plaines and Illinois Rivers to the Mississippi — the entire point of the 1900 reversal was to send Chicago's waste away from Lake Michigan, the region's drinking-water source, rather than into it [\[10\]](#src-10)[\[11\]](#src-11).

**The lake connection.** Because the reversed river system is gravity- and pump-controlled at three lakefront control points — the Wilmette Pumping Station (North Shore Channel), the Chicago River Controlling Works (CRCW, downtown), and the O'Brien Lock and Dam (Calumet River) — extreme storms can force MWRD to open locks and sluice gates and let the swollen waterway drain back into Lake Michigan, "re-reversing" the flow and sending a mix of stormwater and diluted combined sewage into the lake that supplies the region's drinking water [\[12\]](#src-12)[\[13\]](#src-13)[\[14\]](#src-14). This tension — protect the lake vs. protect the streets and basements — is the central engineering trade-off documented in this file.

**How each outfall actually works: regulators, tide gates, and drop shafts.** The several hundred CSO outfalls in the MWRD system are not a network that water is *delivered to* — each one is the tail end of a local combined trunk sewer that, before the Sanitary District existed, discharged straight into the nearest river or lakeshore. When MWRD built its intercepting sewers in the early twentieth century it did not replace those local sewers; it built a **connecting structure** (also called a **regulator**) at each old outlet, sitting at the junction of the local sewer, the interceptor, and the original river outfall [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[5\]](#src-5). The regulator, not a pump, decides where the water goes, purely by water level:

- **Dry weather and light rain:** a weir, orifice, or gate inside the structure routes the flow *into the interceptor*, which carries it by gravity to a water reclamation plant for treatment [\[67\]](#src-67).
- **Heavy rain:** once combined flow exceeds the interceptor's capacity, the excess backs up over the weir (or through the regulating gate) and spills out the old outfall pipe to the waterway — that is the combined sewer overflow. A **tide gate** (a hinged flap gate) on the outfall keeps the swollen river from flowing *backward* into the sewer when river levels are higher than the sewer [\[67\]](#src-67). MWRD's own inventory counts on the order of 97 flow-control/regulator devices across the system (see Facilities table) [\[1\]](#src-1).

TARP inserted one more step *ahead* of that spill. At many connecting structures a **drop shaft** was added: excess combined flow that would formerly have overflowed to the river now plunges 150–300 feet down the shaft into the Deep Tunnel first, admitted through **sluice gates**. MWRD's 1988 TARP evaluation groups these tunnel drop-shaft sluice gates into thirteen control areas and describes the collecting-structure/tide-gate arrangement in detail [\[67\]](#src-67). Only when the tunnel *and* the reservoirs downstream are also full does flow finally reach the waterway as a CSO — and during a fill event MWRD may deliberately *close* selected sluice gates to keep additional stormwater out of a nearly-full tunnel and prevent hydraulic surging or geysering, as documented in the USGS monitoring study of the Evanston/Riverside reaches [\[68\]](#src-68). A detailed hydraulic model of four North Branch sewersheds in series illustrates the whole local-sewer → regulator → drop-shaft → tunnel cascade and how real-time gate control trades off CSO volume against basement flooding [\[69\]](#src-69). This is why the CSO outfalls on the archive's geographic map each carry a `TARP_CONNECTION` code from MWRD's GIS — `DS-*` for a drop shaft, `TG-*` for a tide gate, `CDS-*`/`CI-*` for connecting structures: those codes *are* the regulator hardware at each outfall.

Geysering is not a solved, historical problem — MWRD engineers and outside modelers presented dated, located examples from the modern Mainstream Tunnel System at a December 2024 M&R seminar: drop shaft DSM-84 geysered on 5/3/2022, DSM-79 (near the Diversey/Logan and Montrose/Harding intersections on the North Branch) geysered on 9/11/2022 during a 2.19-in. storm while the tunnel and McCook Reservoir were both essentially empty, and a drop shaft near the South Branch/Loop geysered on 7/2/2023 during the 5.13-in. storm that also drove that day's lake reversal. The team is testing hydraulic metrics — a dimensionless flow parameter (Q*), the Froude number, and bidirectional-filling detection — to predict geyser risk by location and inform mitigation, but reports no reliable predictive threshold yet identified [\[75\]](#src-75).

### Non-MWRD stormwater pumping stations (village/municipal)

Not every wet-weather discharge into the CAWS is an MWRD-permitted CSO outfall. Where a satellite community's sewers are fully separated — storm drains independent of the sanitary system, rather than Chicago's combined design — that community typically owns, operates and NPDES-permits (Illinois EPA general MS4 permit) its own local stormwater pumping station, outside the CSO-outfall counts tabulated above.

**Village of Wilmette Storm Water Pumping Station (SWPS).** West Wilmette — about 2.8 sq. mi. west of Ridge Road, built out 1930–1950, before modern stormwater practice — drains through a fully separated storm/sanitary sewer system rather than combined sewers; all of its storm and street runoff collects at a single Village-owned pumping station at 4000 Lake Avenue, on Wilmette's western border, which lifts the water into a discharge channel flowing by gravity to the North Branch of the Chicago River near the Lake Avenue overpass [\[63\]](#src-63)[\[65\]](#src-65)[\[66\]](#src-66). Built in 1936, the station runs five pumps automatically on sump-level signals from its intake chamber; a 2020 Village hydrologic/hydraulic engineering study rates its combined capacity at 585 cfs, comfortably above the roughly 290–295 cfs 10-year/100-year storm flows the same study modeled for the drainage area — though the separate storm-sewer network feeding the station has only about 2-year capacity, so street flooding (2+ ft. deep over 310 structures, per the study's 10-year-storm model) still occurs in larger events [\[65\]](#src-65). The station is monitored remotely from the Village's C.P. Dubbs Water Treatment Plant, with Public Works staff dispatched during significant rainfall [\[63\]](#src-63). In October 2022 Village trustees approved a $1.93 million contract (Connelly Electric) to replace the station's switchgear — last replaced in 1991, roughly a decade past its typical 30-year service life — and the variable-frequency drive on one of its five pumps, installed in 2001 and already past its rated 15–20-year life; the project ran about 28% ($432,000) over its 2022 budget on equipment and labor-cost escalation, with switchgear work completed in late 2023 [\[64\]](#src-64). This Village-owned facility is unrelated to MWRD's own Wilmette Pumping Station, the lakefront lock/control structure at the head of the North Shore Channel discussed above (see also docs 02 and 07 in this archive) — the two share only a village name, one district-owned and lakefront, the other Village-owned and on the North Branch [\[63\]](#src-63).

## Original construction

The discharge/flooding architecture described here is the second great re-engineering of the Chicago waterway, layered on top of the 1900 Sanitary and Ship Canal reversal (covered in depth elsewhere in this archive). By the 1960s the combined-sewer system built around that reversal was itself failing: Chicago-area sewers were overflowing to the rivers more than 100 days a year, and flooding had become chronic as paved development sent ever more stormwater into pipes sized for a smaller, less-developed city [\[15\]](#src-15). In 1967 the MWRD, the State of Illinois, Cook County and the City of Chicago formed the Flood Control Coordinating Committee, which evaluated 50 alternative solutions — including simply separating storm and sanitary sewers, judged too costly and disruptive — before settling on TARP as the most cost-effective approach [\[15\]](#src-15). The MWRD formally adopted TARP on October 20, 1972, as the region's plan for complying with new federal and state water-quality standards [\[15\]](#src-15)[\[16\]](#src-16).

Tunnel construction began in 1975. Because completed segments could be brought online while work continued elsewhere, the Upper Des Plaines Tunnel System near O'Hare went into service first, in 1981 [\[15\]](#src-15)[\[18\]](#src-18). The tunnels — driven 150 to 300 feet down through dolomite bedrock (up to 350 feet in some segments, per secondary accounts) — were bored, not blasted, using newly developed tunnel-boring machines to limit vibration and surface disruption; cracks in the limestone were sealed with grout curtains to protect groundwater, and the finished bores were concrete-lined [\[15\]](#src-15). Phase I (the full 110.4-mile tunnel network, 2.3 billion gallons of conveyance/storage) was declared complete in 2006 [\[15\]](#src-15)[\[16\]](#src-16). The USACE's 1986 Chicagolands Underflow Plan (CUP) defined the federal interest in Phase II, the three large flood-control reservoirs: Majewski (built by USACE, completed 1998), Thornton (District-managed after USACE funding proved inadequate; permanent reservoir completed 2015) and McCook (under a 1999 Project Cooperation Agreement with USACE, mined out in partnership with a commercial limestone quarry operator, Stage 1 completed 2017, Stage 2 consent-decree target 2029, since revised to Dec. 31, 2032) [\[16\]](#src-16). As of the December 31, 2024 TARP status report, cumulative construction cost stood at $3.856 billion — $2.332 billion for Phase I tunnels and appurtenant facilities, $1.524 billion for the three Phase II reservoirs ($45 million Majewski, $450 million Thornton, $1.029 billion McCook) [\[16\]](#src-16); the December 31, 2025 status report puts the total at $3.858 billion ($1.526 billion of it reservoirs — $45M Majewski, $450M Thornton, $1.031 billion McCook), with only $7 million of McCook Stage 2 unspent [\[71\]](#src-71).

Alongside TARP, the City of Chicago's own combined-sewer collection system is far older: 25% of it predates 1900 and another 23% dates to 1900–1920, with brick sewers (785 mi.) and vitrified clay (2,750 mi.) still the dominant pipe materials citywide [\[1\]](#src-1). That aging, largely unlined network — built to a design standard of roughly 2 inches of rain in 24 hours — is the fundamental reason CSOs and basement backups persist even after TARP's tunnels were completed [\[19\]](#src-19)[\[20\]](#src-20). A National Weather Service analysis presented to MWRD's Monitoring & Research seminar in September 2025 quantifies how that design standard has eroded in relative terms as storms intensify: a network engineered in the 1940s to handle a 10%-annual-exceedance-probability (roughly 10-year) storm now provides, given measured 2020s rainfall statistics, only the protection level associated with a roughly 33%-annual-exceedance-probability storm — and even sewers designed as late as the 1980s and 2000s have seen effective service level fall from a 10% design standard to roughly 18% and 13% AEP, respectively [\[76\]](#src-76).

## Upgrades & modernization timeline

- **1972** — MWRD adopts TARP as the region's Clean Water Act compliance plan after the Flood Control Coordinating Committee's multi-year alternatives study [\[15\]](#src-15).
- **1975** — TARP tunnel construction begins using tunnel-boring machines [\[15\]](#src-15).
- **1981** — Upper Des Plaines Tunnel System (O'Hare area) is the first TARP segment to go into service [\[15\]](#src-15)[\[18\]](#src-18).
- **1998** — Gloria Alitto Majewski Reservoir, the smallest of the three Phase II reservoirs, completed by USACE [\[15\]](#src-15)[\[16\]](#src-16).
- **2004** — Illinois Public Act 93-1049 grants MWRD countywide stormwater-management authority in Cook County, ending a fragmented, municipality-by-municipality approach [\[21\]](#src-21)[\[22\]](#src-22).
- **2004** — Friends of the Chicago River, the City of Chicago and USACE launch the Bubbly Creek Recovery Initiative to plan restoration of the historically industrial South Fork [\[23\]](#src-23).
- **2006** — TARP Phase I tunnel system (110.4 miles) fully operational; MWRD reports CSO frequency thereafter falls from a pre-TARP average of ~100 days/year to ~50 days/year [\[15\]](#src-15)[\[16\]](#src-16).
- **2007** — MWRD Board adopts the Cook County Stormwater Management Plan (CCSMP), establishing Watershed Planning Councils, Detailed Watershed Plans for six major watersheds, a Stormwater Management Capital Improvement Program, the Small Streams Maintenance Program and the Watershed Management Ordinance [\[21\]](#src-21).
- **2011 (June)** — MWRD Board votes to add disinfection at the Calumet and North Side (O'Brien) WRPs — chlorination/dechlorination at Calumet, UV irradiation at North Side — after decades without effluent disinfection [\[24\]](#src-24)[\[25\]](#src-25).
- **2011 (Dec. 14)** — DOJ/EPA announce a Clean Water Act consent decree with MWRD over untreated CSO discharges, mandating disinfection, floatables controls and a Long-Term Control Plan (TARP completion) by December 31, 2029, at an estimated cost of more than $3 billion, plus a $25–50 million Green Infrastructure Program and a $675,000 civil penalty ($350,000 federal / $325,000 state) [\[26\]](#src-26)[\[27\]](#src-27).
- **2013 (April 18–19)** — Record-setting reversal event: 10,719.5 million gallons (≈10.7 BG) released to Lake Michigan across all three control points, the second-largest event in the 1985–2021 MWRD record [\[12\]](#src-12).
- **2014 (Jan. 6)** — After two years of litigation, U.S. District Judge George M. Marovich approves and formally enters the 2011 consent decree over the objections of intervening environmental groups — NRDC, Sierra Club and Prairie Rivers Network ("NRDC Group"), plus the Alliance for the Great Lakes and Environmental Law & Policy Center — who argued its green-infrastructure component was inadequate ("meager," per the NRDC Group's own brief). As entered, the decree requires MWRD to distribute 15,000 low-/no-cost rain barrels within 5 years (10,000 within 3 years; 825,000 gal. combined capacity) and to implement green-infrastructure projects retaining 2 million gal. of storm water per storm within 5 years, 5 million within 10 years and 10 million within 15 years — plus a containment boom at one specific CSO outfall — while leaving TARP's own build-out, at 17.45 billion gallons, as the plan's primary control mechanism [\[73\]](#src-73).
- **2014** — Center for Neighborhood Technology's RainReady program partners with the Chicago Department of Planning and Development on the Residential Flood Assistance Program, offering free flood-mitigation retrofits to homeowners hit by the April 2013 flood [\[28\]](#src-28).
- **2014** — Space to Grow schoolyard partnership (MWRD, Chicago Public Schools, Chicago Dept. of Water Management, Healthy Schools Campaign, Openlands) begins converting asphalt CPS schoolyards into stormwater-capturing green space [\[29\]](#src-29)[\[30\]](#src-30).
- **2015** — Thornton Composite Reservoir (permanent, 7.9 BG) completed; MWRD reports CSOs "nearly eliminated" in its service area from this point [\[15\]](#src-15)[\[16\]](#src-16).
- **2015 (Dec.)** — Disinfection construction complete at Calumet and North Side WRPs, in service for the 2016 recreational season; combined capital cost ~$109 million, under half the original estimate; the North Side facility is billed as the world's largest ultraviolet wastewater-disinfection installation [\[24\]](#src-24)[\[25\]](#src-25)[\[31\]](#src-31).
- **2017** — McCook Reservoir Stage 1 (3.5 BG) operational [\[15\]](#src-15)[\[16\]](#src-16).
- **2021 (Sept.)** — H2NOW Chicago, the first real-time microbial (E. coli/fecal-coliform) water-quality monitoring system for an urban U.S. waterway, launches on the Chicago and Calumet Rivers — a 20-plus-partner effort led by the nonprofit Current, with Shedd Aquarium providing laboratory validation [\[32\]](#src-32)[\[33\]](#src-33).
- **2022 (Sept.)** — Thornton Transitional Reservoir decommissioned after the Thorn Creek Overflow Tunnel connection completes the permanent Thornton system [\[16\]](#src-16).
- **2022** — Cook County allocates $6 million for RainReady green-infrastructure projects in six flood-prone Calumet Corridor communities, delivered via CNT [\[34\]](#src-34).
- **2023 (July 2–3)** — Major storm (3–8+ inches of rain regionally) forces reversal at all three control points: Wilmette gates open 2:16 p.m.–9:47 p.m. July 2; downtown CRCW locks open 4:00 p.m. July 2–2:30 a.m. July 3 [\[13\]](#src-13)[\[35\]](#src-35).
- **2024 (April 1)** — Chicago's citywide CSO NPDES permit (IL0045012) reissued with updated Operations & Maintenance Plan requirements, effective through February 28, 2029 [\[1\]](#src-1).
- **2025 (Sept. 21)** — First sanctioned open-water swim in the Chicago River in 98 years (Chicago River Swim), credited to sustained water-quality gains; a second annual swim is scheduled for September 20, 2026 [\[36\]](#src-36)[\[37\]](#src-37).
- **2026 (July 2–6)** — 8–10 inches of rain over 48 hours drives McCook Reservoir to ~98% capacity (its sixth fill-to-capacity event of 2026 alone, versus five cumulative fills in the previous five years) and Thornton Reservoir to a record 94–98% capacity — the fullest since Thornton opened in 2015 — prompting MWRD to announce a $5 million odor-mitigation project (carbon filtration and misting systems) for Thornton, targeted for completion by year-end 2026 [\[38\]](#src-38)[\[39\]](#src-39)[\[40\]](#src-40).

## Current operations

Day to day, MWRD balances the CAWS through "downstream control" (metering outflow at the Lockport Powerhouse, the canal's terminus) and "upstream control" (sluice gates at the three lakefront structures) [\[14\]](#src-14). Ahead of forecast storms, operators draw down canal levels to create in-channel storage capacity, and, during a storm, route flow into TARP — whose tunnels (2.32 BG) and on-line reservoirs (8.65 BG as of December 2024) together hold on the order of 11 billion gallons — before treatment plants and reservoirs are exhausted [\[39\]](#src-39)[\[16\]](#src-16). (An earlier revision of this document put the pre-storm in-channel storage at "roughly 5 billion gallons"; that figure could not be traced to any source during verification and has been removed pending a direct MWRD operational statement.) Only when waterway levels exceed Lake Michigan's does MWRD open locks/gates to reverse flow back to the lake, a step system operators describe as rare and used only when water threatens to overtop banks into homes and businesses [\[13\]](#src-13)[\[14\]](#src-14).

Combined sewage that cannot be captured discharges through the ≈370 CSO outfalls across the district. Chicago's own 184 outfalls are inspected at least annually, with monthly spot monitoring by the Chicago Department of Water Management and tide-gate alarm monitoring by MWRD at most City outfalls; illegal dry-weather discharges can be reported via 311 or MWRD's 1-800-332-DUMP hotline [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[41\]](#src-41). Consent-decree "floatables control" is a standing waterborne operation, not just a permit clause: MWRD's Channel Maintenance Unit runs a 36-ft. debris boat with barge and crane, two 23-ft. skimmer boats, a utility work boat and a pontoon boat across the CAWS's 76 navigable miles; under the decree's Floatables Control Plan, two skimmer boats (two-person crews) patrol daily on weekdays from mid-April through mid-October collecting smaller floating debris, while larger items are flagged for the debris boat, and the decree separately requires a containment boom at one specific CSO outfall [\[74\]](#src-74). Public notification runs through two channels: MWRD's CSO email/text alert system (re-platformed June 17, 2025, requiring re-registration) and the CSO Events/Quick View map on MWRD's GeoHub, which plots confirmed and suspected CSO events by CAWS reach [\[42\]](#src-42)[\[43\]](#src-43)[\[44\]](#src-44). H2NOW Chicago supplements this with continuous fluorescence-sensor E. coli estimates, refreshed every 15 minutes at monitoring points on the North, South and Main branches, validated monthly against Shedd Aquarium lab cultures [\[32\]](#src-32)[\[33\]](#src-33).

Green infrastructure is now a standing MWRD program rather than a one-off mitigation. As of the mid-2020s, the Space to Grow partnership has transformed more than 40 CPS schoolyards, each typically capturing 200,000-plus gallons per storm event — collectively equivalent to more than 13 Olympic pools [\[29\]](#src-29)[\[30\]](#src-30). RainReady (Center for Neighborhood Technology) runs parallel household- and community-scale retrofit programs, including the Calumet Corridor initiative funded by Cook County [\[28\]](#src-28)[\[34\]](#src-34). All of it sits under the Cook County Watershed Management Ordinance framework MWRD has administered since gaining countywide stormwater authority in 2004 [\[21\]](#src-21)[\[22\]](#src-22).


For the six MWRD pumping stations that discharge CSOs directly (North Branch, Racine Avenue, 95th St., 122nd St., 125th St., and Westchester), the District's own *Pumping Station Activity* log provides an event-level record — 988 confirmed discharge events from 2000 through July 2026, totaling ≈230 billion gallons, three-quarters of it at Racine Avenue alone — reproduced in this archive's [data section](../data/) as a flagged, faithful CSV [\[62\]](#src-62).

## Notable events & incidents

- **April 13, 1992 — Great Chicago (Loop) Flood.** A contractor's piling, driven near the Kinzie Street bridge in September 1991, punctured the roof of a disused turn-of-the-century freight-tunnel network beneath the Chicago River. The breach went undiscovered for months; when it failed, an estimated 250 million gallons of river water flooded Loop basements and the underground Pedway, shutting down the financial district for days. Crews eventually drained the flooded freight tunnels into the still-unfinished Deep Tunnel network below. Remediation and business-interruption costs have been estimated at roughly $2 billion in 1992 dollars [\[45\]](#src-45)[\[46\]](#src-46).
- **1985–2025 reversal record (MWRD's own tally, updated).** A January 2026 update to MWRD's official "Reversals to Lake Michigan" table extends the ledger three years past the archive's original 1985–2021 source and shows **zero reversal events in 2022, 2024 and 2025** — only the July 2023 storm broke that streak. Recomputed across the full 1985–2025 series, the CAWS reversed to the lake at least once in 23 of 41 years, across 36 discrete dated events summing to ≈52.5 billion gallons; the largest single event remains **September 13–16, 2008** (11,049.1 million gallons combined) and the second-largest **April 18–19, 2013** (10,719.5 million gallons) [\[12\]](#src-12)[\[72\]](#src-72). Other cited totals in secondary press coverage still don't reconcile — MWRD's then-executive director told reporters in 2014 that reversal frequency had "increased in the past five years" versus a historical average of about once a year [\[47\]](#src-47), and separate news accounts describe "about 50" reversal events since 1985 [\[13\]](#src-13) versus a "27 reversals... 44 billion gallons" figure attributed to MWRD elsewhere [\[48\]](#src-48) — neither matches the direct count/sum of the source-12/72 tables even with the 2022–2025 update, so the discrepancies likely reflect different counting rules (gate reversals vs. lock reversals, per-event vs. per-date tallies) rather than missing data; this file reports the MWRD-published table as the primary figure.
- **July 2–3, 2023.** A storm dropping 3–8+ inches regionally left an estimated 70,000-plus homes flooded across Chicago's West Side, South Side and suburbs; $426 million in federal disaster aid was later awarded to Chicago and $244 million to Cook County, though a 2026 investigative report found essentially none of it had yet been spent [\[19\]](#src-19)[\[35\]](#src-35). MWRD's own reversal ledger records this event's lake-bound total precisely at **1,142.6 million gallons** — 911.8 MG through the Chicago River Controlling Works and 230.8 MG through Wilmette — with **no O'Brien Lock volume logged for this date**, correcting this file's earlier framing of the storm as a reversal "at all three control points" to two of three by MWRD's own volumetric accounting [\[72\]](#src-72).
- **February 2018.** A winter storm filled the newly opened 3.5-billion-gallon McCook Reservoir Stage 1 and still forced an additional 3.8 billion gallons of combined sewage into local waterways — cited by TARP critics as evidence the system remains outrun by the largest storms even after a $3.86 billion, half-century construction program [\[16\]](#src-16) [\[20\]](#src-20).
- **September 2025.** First sanctioned open-water swim in the Chicago River since the 1920s, contingent on real-time water-quality clearance from MWRD/H2NOW data — framed by city officials and researchers as a visible marker of the CAWS's multi-decade recovery [\[36\]](#src-36)[\[37\]](#src-37).
- **July 2–6, 2026.** Holiday-weekend storms (8–10 inches of rain in 48 hours) pushed McCook Reservoir to ~98% capacity — its sixth fill event of the year alone — and Thornton Composite Reservoir to a record 94–98% capacity, its fullest since opening in 2015. South Holland and other south-suburban residents near Thornton reported persistent hydrogen-sulfide ("rotten egg") odors; MWRD spokesman Patrick Thomas estimated a full month to draw the reservoir back down, and MWRD Board President Kari Steele told press "there's no one silver bullet" for flood control, while the district announced a $5 million carbon-filtration/misting odor-control retrofit targeted for completion by the end of 2026 [\[38\]](#src-38)[\[39\]](#src-39)[\[40\]](#src-40).

## Open questions

- **Does the total reversal count/volume reconcile? (Substantially resolved — 2022–2025 data now located.)** A January 2026 MWRD update (source 72) extends the reversal ledger through 2025 and closes the archive's earlier data gap: 2022, 2024 and 2025 each had zero reversal events, and the sole event in that window — July 2–3, 2023 — totaled a precisely quantified 1,142.6 million gallons (911.8 MG CRCW + 230.8 MG Wilmette; no O'Brien Lock volume). Recomputed across the full 1985–2025 series this yields 23 years with at least one event (not 27) and a cumulative volume of roughly 52.5 billion gallons — still reconciling with neither the press citation of "~50 reversals since 1985" [\[13\]](#src-13) nor the separately reported "27 reversals / 44 billion gallons" figure attributed to MWRD [\[48\]](#src-48), which confirms the discrepancy is a counting-method difference (calendar dates vs. discrete events vs. lock-only vs. gate-plus-lock) rather than missing 2022–2026 data. No reversal volume for the July 2026 storm was located — the source-72 table's cutoff is January 2026, before that event [\[12\]](#src-12)[\[13\]](#src-13)[\[48\]](#src-48)[\[72\]](#src-72).
- **What is the true current fish-species count?** Sources range from "more than 60" (Wikipedia, citing Chicago Tribune/WTTW) to "more than 70" (MWRD, Friends of the Chicago River) to "77" (one Shedd/CBS-linked figure), with a distinct 2025 Shedd Aquarium finding of 24 species successfully spawning to the larval stage. These appear to be different metrics (species observed vs. species reproducing) rather than a contradiction, but the underlying methodology/dataset for each number was not confirmed [\[49\]](#src-49)[\[50\]](#src-50)[\[51\]](#src-51)[\[52\]](#src-52).
- **Bubbly Creek restoration status (updated).** USACE's 2020 feasibility study recommended a roughly 40–44-acre ecological restoration (30.7 ac. substrate capping with rounded river rock/quarried stone/sand, plus riparian, emergent and submergent plantings), but the project stalled over liability concerns tied to sediment contamination, and a $11.6 million congressional authorization has sat unspent pending resolution [\[23\]](#src-23)[\[53\]](#src-53)[\[61\]](#src-61). As of a February 2025 update, EPA's Great Lakes National Program Office, USACE and the City of Chicago began a joint pre-design investigation — sediment characterization across roughly 22 acres plus a sediment-capping pilot — intended to help unblock the liability impasse and inform USACE's restoration design; EPA states final pre-design investigation reports are expected in fall 2027, meaning the restoration itself had not begun construction as of this research pass (July 2026). Separately, the nonprofit Urban Rivers installed floating wetland islands in Bubbly Creek in 2022 as an interim, smaller-scale habitat/water-quality measure [\[53\]](#src-53)[\[61\]](#src-61).
- **Manhole count discrepancy.** Chicago's own CSO O&M Plan states both "146,000 manholes" (Section 2.3 prose) and "148,000" (Section 2.3.5 Structure Inventory table) within the same document; not resolved here — independently confirmed present in both forms in the source PDF during this fact-check pass.
- **Minor MWRD-internal figure mismatch on the Des Plaines Tunnel System.** MWRD's TARP fact sheet (source 5/15) lists the Des Plaines Tunnel System at 25.6 miles / 0.4 BG capacity, while the more detailed *TARP Status Report as of December 31, 2024* (source 16) lists it at 26.6 miles / 420 MG — a roughly 1-mile/20-million-gallon difference not explained in either document. This file follows the status report's figures (which are also the only ones that reconcile exactly to the system-wide 110.4-mile/2,320-MG totals both documents otherwise agree on) but flags the fact sheet's slightly different numbers here.
- **Deep Tunnel effectiveness — the live debate.** MWRD engineers and board leadership describe TARP as an "unreserved success" that protects 1.5 million structures and has cut CSO pollutant loading by an estimated 85% [\[16\]](#src-16)[\[20\]](#src-20). Critics — including the late state hydrologist Stanley Changnon, NRDC's Karen Hobbs, and former city resilience officer Aaron Koch — argue the system was sized for a mid-20th-century climate and city, that local sewer "straws" remain the binding constraint regardless of Deep Tunnel capacity downstream, and that the decades-long, multibillion-dollar tunnel program crowded out investment in distributed green infrastructure. Post-2006 data cited by critics show nearly 19 billion gallons of overflow reached Lake Michigan in the four years after Phase I's completion, versus 12 billion in the prior 22 years — though this comparison's baseline methodology was not independently verified here [\[20\]](#src-20). Both positions are represented in this file; neither should be read as this archive's conclusion. A primary-source data point for the "success" side, not previously in this file: a 1991–92 Illinois State Water Survey study for MWRD found minimum dissolved-oxygen levels in the Illinois Waterway at Lockport rose from 0.00 mg/l (1971/72, effectively anoxic) to 1.71 mg/l (1989/90) after TARP Phase I began operating, and from 2.30 to 5.68 mg/l over the same period at Chillicothe, with modeled CSO-Phase I raising minimum Peoria Pool DO by at least 1.50 mg/l during high-CSO conditions; the same study's 1958–1988 simulation put average total-service-area overflow frequency at 29 days/year with no TARP, 9 days/year with Phase I alone, and 1 day/year with Phase II complete — a different metric, on a different (modeled, pre-1988) baseline, from MWRD's often-cited "100 days to 50 days" measured figure, but directionally consistent with it [\[70\]](#src-70). On the "still flooding" side: a September 2025 National Weather Service analysis presented to MWRD's own M&R seminar attributes persistent flooding not to TARP's capacity being exhausted but to eroding local storm-sewer design standards under rainfall intensification (see Original construction, above), and MWRD's own Dec. 2024 engineering seminar on Mainstream Tunnel geysering acknowledges the phenomenon remains active and only partially understood in the completed post-2006 system [\[75\]](#src-75)[\[76\]](#src-76).
