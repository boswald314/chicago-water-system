# Early Chicago Sewerage History, 1850s–1900

## Role in the system

Between 1855 and 1900 Chicago built the first comprehensive, planned underground sewer system of any large American city [\[7\]](#src-7)[\[10\]](#src-10)[\[12\]](#src-12). It was a **combined system**: a single network of pipes designed to receive household wastes ("all stagnant water, all liquids from kitchens and manufactories, and the contents of all privies") plus street surface runoff captured through catch basins at block corners [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[7\]](#src-7). Flow was entirely by gravity — Chesbrough explicitly rejected deep sewers requiring pumps — so the pipes had to sit near or above the natural ground surface of a city that stood only a few feet above lake level [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[7\]](#src-7).

**What flowed in:** sanitary sewage from house drains (2,194 private house-drain connections existed by January 1861 [\[2\]](#src-2)), industrial liquid wastes (notably distillery, tannery and packinghouse offal along the river branches [\[4\]](#src-4)), and stormwater from streets via catch basins [\[1\]](#src-1).

**What flowed out, and where:** the sewers of all three city divisions discharged primarily into the **Chicago River** — the South Division draining west into the South Branch and north into the Main Stem, the North Division through mains on Rush, Clark and Franklin Streets into the Main Stem and the North Branch, and the West Division into both branches; only one of the two Michigan Avenue mains had an outlet directly into **Lake Michigan**, at Twelfth Street (the other Michigan Avenue main discharged to the river) [\[1\]](#src-1). The river in turn emptied into the lake — the same lake from which the city drew its drinking water, initially at an intake near the river mouth, and after 1867 through a two-mile tunnel to an offshore crib [\[7\]](#src-7). This supply/disposal collision drove every subsequent decision.

**Downstream re-routing:** from 1871 onward the city tried to send the river's sewage load *away* from the lake and down the **Illinois & Michigan Canal** at Bridgeport — first by pumping, then by the gravity "deep cut" of 1871, then by rebuilt pumps in the 1880s — so that diluted sewage flowed to the Des Plaines and Illinois Rivers and ultimately the Mississippi [\[4\]](#src-4)[\[7\]](#src-7)[\[18\]](#src-18). The permanent solution, the Sanitary District's 28-mile Main Drainage Channel (opened January 1900), replaced the canal as the receiving artery and permanently reversed the Main Stem and South Branch [\[7\]](#src-7)[\[15\]](#src-15)[\[17\]](#src-17). The 1850s–1890s network described here is the direct ancestor of today's City of Chicago combined-sewer system, whose overflows the MWRD's Deep Tunnel (TARP) now intercepts [\[7\]](#src-7).

## Original construction

**The crisis.** Chicago in the early 1850s was a boomtown on a wet clay flat barely above the level of Lake Michigan; streets were mud sloughs ("No bottom here" read a famous sign) and drainage was by open gutter into a sluggish river [\[10\]](#src-10)[\[16\]](#src-16). Waterborne disease was chronic: cholera struck in 1849 and repeatedly thereafter, and in 1854 it killed **1,424 people** — with another 210 deaths recorded as "diarrhea" and 242 as "dysentery" that may also have been cholera — while the city's overall death rate hit 53.9 per 1,000 [\[2\]](#src-2)[\[4\]](#src-4)[\[9\]](#src-9)[\[10\]](#src-10). Six consecutive years of cholera and dysentery epidemics pushed the legislature to act [\[10\]](#src-10).

**The institution.** Illinois had empowered sewage commissioners for the city as early as 1852 [\[7\]](#src-7) — though the actual June 28, 1852 act, per a Department of Public Works retrospective, created a *county* drainage commission (Henry Smith, Geo. W. Snow, James H. Reed, George Steele, H. L. Stewart, Isaac Cook and Charles V. Dyer) empowered to build ditches, culverts and roads across roughly 100,000 acres of swamp land in Cook County townships 37–40 N, ranges 12–14 E — a regional land-reclamation body (it spent about $100,000 in its first two years) distinct from the city sewer commission that followed three years later [\[22\]](#src-22); on **February 14, 1855** the legislature created the **Board of Sewerage Commissioners** — William B. Ogden (Chicago's first mayor), J. D. Webster and Sylvester Lind — empowered to issue up to **$500,000** in bonds [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[10\]](#src-10)[\[12\]](#src-12)[\[22\]](#src-22). The board recruited **Ellis Sylvester Chesbrough** (1813–1886), then Boston's first city engineer, as chief engineer, with **William H. Clark** as principal assistant engineer [\[10\]](#src-10)[\[12\]](#src-12)[\[22\]](#src-22).

**The plan.** Chesbrough delivered his *Report and Plan of Sewerage for the City of Chicago* on December 26, 1855; the board adopted it December 31, 1855 [\[1\]](#src-1). He weighed four disposal schemes: (1) drainage into the river and thence to the lake; (2) directly into the lake; (3) into reservoirs to be pumped and sold as farm manure; (4) into "the proposed steamboat canal" to the Illinois River — an uncanny forecast of the 1900 solution, which he rejected in 1855 only because its cost would exceed "sewers for five times the present population" [\[1\]](#src-1). He chose plan (1) as cheapest, proposing to keep the South Branch tolerable by driving lake water through it [\[1\]](#src-1). The system was combined and gravity-flow, with circular brick mains of 5 feet (one 6-foot main on Clark Street), 2-foot brick branches, and 12–15-inch glazed pipe laterals; junctions for every present and future house were to be built in from the start; **manhole entrances at every street intersection** and catch basins at every block corner completed the design — features that made the system a national model [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[12\]](#src-12). His cost estimate for the initial district was $157,893 (South Division), $156,522 (North) and $188,831 (West) — roughly $500,000 — with about **$2,300,000** projected to sewer the whole territory then contemplated (1855 dollars) [\[1\]](#src-1). The initial district as platted in 1855 was bounded on the north by Division Street, on the west by "Reuben Street" (renamed Ashland Avenue), on the south by "North Street" (renamed Sixteenth Street), and on the east by Lake Michigan — an independent 1891 retrospective glossing the same boundary names, which confirms the 1855 report's "North street" (see Open Questions) was printed correctly, not garbled by OCR [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[22\]](#src-22).

**Raising the city.** Because the mains had to stay high enough to drain to the river yet carry at least two feet of cover, Chesbrough's report required raising street grades — an average of about 18 inches to 3 feet across much of the district as first laid out [\[1\]](#src-1) — and the city's grade ordinances of 1855–56 and 1857–58 ultimately lifted streets on the South Side and parts of the North and West Sides by four to five feet on average, locally eight feet or more (contemporary accounts speak of new grades 10–14 feet above low water downtown) [\[8\]](#src-8)[\[13\]](#src-13)[\[16\]](#src-16). Sewers were laid essentially on the surface, buried in fill largely dredged from the river bottom, and new streets built on top [\[7\]](#src-7)[\[10\]](#src-10). Raising the *buildings* was left to private owners: the first large masonry building went up on jackscrews in January 1858; George M. Pullman made his reputation lifting the 320-foot Lake Street row (35,000 tons, 6,000 jackscrews, 600 men) in 1860 and the six-story Tremont House (500 men, 1,000 jackscrews) in 1861, hotels operating throughout [\[8\]](#src-8)[\[13\]](#src-13). Frame buildings by the hundreds were simply rolled to the outskirts [\[13\]](#src-13).

**Execution and cost experience.** Construction began in the **spring of 1856** [\[7\]](#src-7)[\[10\]](#src-10). The first sewerage bonds — $100,000 — sold March 19, 1856, and the first sewer contracts went to Ives & Lonergan (State Street, Randolph Street to the river) and S. S. Wiltsee & Co. (North Clark Street, North Water to Erie; and West Randolph Street, the river to Desplaines Street) [\[22\]](#src-22). By January 1, 1861 the board had built 283,586 lineal feet — almost 53¾ miles — of sewers with 1,174 catch basins and 2,102 manholes at an actual cost of $686,277.30, financed by $900,000 in 6 and 7 percent sewerage bonds [\[2\]](#src-2). Unit prices fell dramatically with experience and competition: a 6-foot sewer cost $5.76 per lineal foot in 1856 but $2.88 in 1860; a 3-foot sewer fell from $3.19 to $1.45 [\[2\]](#src-2). Quicksand, relocation of water and gas pipes, and the preservation of "old wooden sewers" (the city's scattered pre-1855 plank drains) were the main construction difficulties [\[2\]](#src-2) — Chicago's actual first sewers, per the 1891 retrospective, were triangular wooden box sewers built in 1850 along Clark, La Salle and Wells Streets, from the river to the alleys south of Randolph Street, costing $2,871.90, paid entirely by special assessment on the property benefited (see Open Questions) [\[22\]](#src-22). The Board of Health's 1873 sewerage-extension report tabulates feet of sewer built annually from 1856 through 1872, extending the mileage curve past 1861: cumulative construction reached 850,563 ft (≈161.1 mi) by the close of 1871 — the year of the Great Fire — and 893,330 ft (≈169.2 mi) by the close of 1872 [\[5\]](#src-5).

**The European study tour.** In the winter of 1856–57 the commissioners sent Chesbrough to Europe (instructed December 19, 1856; his report to the board is dated March 25, 1858) [\[22\]](#src-22); he examined the sewerage of London, Liverpool, Manchester, Glasgow, Paris, Amsterdam, Hamburg, Berlin and other cities and published his findings in 1858 (*Chicago Sewerage: Report of the Results of Examinations Made in Relation to Sewerage in Several European Cities in the Winter of 1856–7*) — one of the earliest systematic American surveys of European sanitary practice [\[3\]](#src-3)[\[10\]](#src-10). In 1861 the Board of Sewerage Commissioners was folded into the new **Board of Public Works**, and Chesbrough became its engineer (and later City Engineer), serving until 1879 [\[4\]](#src-4)[\[7\]](#src-7)[\[12\]](#src-12). The abolishing act was approved March 20, 1861; at an election held the third Tuesday of that April, Benjamin Carpenter, Frederick Letz and John G. Gindele were chosen the first Board of Public Works commissioners, and the new board organized and assumed full control of sewerage and all other public works on May 6, 1861 [\[22\]](#src-22).

## Upgrades & modernization timeline

- **1855 (Feb 14):** Board of Sewerage Commissioners created; Chesbrough hired; plan adopted Dec 31 [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[10\]](#src-10).
- **1855–1858:** street-grade-raising ordinances passed and executed in two stages (1855–56, 1857–58) [\[8\]](#src-8)[\[16\]](#src-16).
- **1856 (spring):** first sewer construction begins; river dredged for fill [\[7\]](#src-7)[\[10\]](#src-10).
- **1858–1866:** building-raising era — first masonry raise Jan 1858; Lake Street row 1860; Tremont House 1861; Robbins Building (27,000 tons, 1,584 jackscrews) Nov 1865; Briggs House 1866 [\[13\]](#src-13).
- **1858:** larger pumps rated over 20,000 cu ft/min installed at Bridgeport, cost split between the city and the I&M Canal trustees, to draw more lake water through the South Branch as the city's sewage load grew [\[25\]](#src-25).
- **1861 (Jan 1):** 53¾ miles of sewers in service, cost $686,277 [\[2\]](#src-2). Sewerage functions pass to Board of Public Works [\[7\]](#src-7).
- **1861 (Mar 20 – May 6):** abolishing act approved March 20; Benjamin Carpenter, Frederick Letz and John G. Gindele elected Public Works commissioners in the following April election; new Board of Public Works organized and assumed full control of sewerage May 6, 1861 [\[22\]](#src-22).
- **1861 (April):** Chesbrough reports drier streets/cellars and a death rate fallen from 53.9 per 1,000 (1854) to 18.8 (1860) [\[2\]](#src-2)[\[4\]](#src-4).
- **1863:** erysipelas epidemic along the South Branch traced to packinghouse waste; slaughtering at Bridgeport had grown from 306,428 head (1860) to 1,029,948 (1863) [\[4\]](#src-4).
- **1865 (Jan 9):** commission appointed "to devise the best plan for cleansing the Chicago river"; its March 6, 1865 report weighed intercepting sewers, flushing canals with pumps, and cutting down the I&M Canal summit — recommending the canal deep cut [\[4\]](#src-4).
- **1865 (Sept 2):** deep-cut contracts let — Fox, Howard & Walker (sections 1–44; 33¢/cu yd earth, $2 rock) and Sanger, Steele & Co. (sections 45–64, rock, $1.64¾/cu yd) [\[4\]](#src-4).
- **1871 (July 15):** temporary dam at Bridgeport cut away; deep cut complete; the river's water "entirely changed" in ~36 hours; city expenditure $3,300,883.71 per Brown's narrative (p. 91) — though Brown's own construction-cost summary table (p. 252) separately gives $3,800,883.71 for "1866 to 1871, by the City of Chicago," a genuine disagreement within the same 1894 source; the state had earlier spent $5,139,492.03 (1836–42) and the canal trustees $1,429,606.21 (1843–48) on the canal [\[4\]](#src-4)[\[17\]](#src-17). The reversal proved unreliable: the Des Plaines flooded back through the Ogden‑Wentworth ditch and low lake levels sapped the current [\[7\]](#src-7)[\[18\]](#src-18).
- **1873 (June 25):** idle Bridgeport pumps sold for $2,500 [\[4\]](#src-4). Board of Health's 1873 report urges extension of sewerage into undrained working-class districts; cumulative sewerage spending listed year by year (to 1861: $665,188; 1869 alone: $654,141) [\[5\]](#src-5).
- **1874–1880:** **Fullerton Avenue conduit** built to flush the North Branch — 12-ft brick tunnel, 11,898 ft, reversible 15,000 cu ft/min pumps; first contractor (George F. Norris & Co., $343,284) quit after building only ~4,000 ft, claiming conditions had been misrepresented and the price set too low (released June 14, 1875); FitzSimons & Connell finished; machinery started **January 9, 1880** (a 1911 retrospective misdates it "about 1870," likely conflating Chesbrough's 1870 *recommendation* of the conduit with its completion) [\[4\]](#src-4)[\[6\]](#src-6).
- **1876 (Sept 18):** Board of Public Works abolished by ordinance and replaced by the Department of Public Works, Mayor Monroe Heath serving as acting commissioner until Charles S. Waller's appointment under Mayor Carter H. Harrison (May 19, 1879); a new data point on the mileage curve: 265.80 miles of sewers in place as of Dec. 31, 1876 [\[22\]](#src-22).
- **1880 (Mar 29):** Common Council appropriates $100,000 toward new Bridgeport pumping works (eventual contract cost over $280,000, plus ~$70,000/yr to operate) after State Board of Health findings (Dr. John H. Rauch) on canal filth; pumps contracted August 1881 and nominally "completed 1883–84" in secondary retrospectives, but per a contemporary 1892 account not accepted until a December 1886 trial, and the contract-required 60,000 cfm capacity was reportedly never reliably achieved (see Open Questions) [\[4\]](#src-4)[\[6\]](#src-6)[\[18\]](#src-18)[\[23\]](#src-23).
- **1880 (Dec.):** the Citizens' Association's Main Drainage Committee (John B. Sherman, George C. Morgan, S. B. Reed, aided by engineer A. J. Mathewson) reports, proposing a gravity "New river" canal from Mud Lake to the Des Plaines at Lockport, estimated to cost $6.85M ($12M "to develop the scheme in all its necessary details"); Brown (1894) credits this report — not the 1885 one below — with having "suggested the idea which developed into the law of 1889" [\[4\]](#src-4)[\[21\]](#src-21).
- **1885 (Aug 2–3):** a 2024 National Weather Service/Illinois State Water Survey rainfall reanalysis puts the storm at 5.63 in. over 19 hours (approx. 2 a.m.–7 p.m., Aug. 2) and 6.36 in. over the full two-day period — a major contributor to August 1885's record monthly total of 11.28 in., a record that stood until October 1954 and remains 6th-highest as of 2023 — closely bracketing Brown's contemporary "nearly six inches" and Chicagology's 5½ inches (see Open Questions) [\[4\]](#src-4)[\[17\]](#src-17)[\[26\]](#src-26); Des Plaines floodwater flushed the river's "concentrated filth" into the lake; sewage reached and surrounded the two-mile crib for weeks [\[4\]](#src-4)[\[14\]](#src-14)[\[17\]](#src-17).
- **1885 (Aug 27):** a second, separately constituted Citizens' Association committee (Ossian Guthrie, Lyman E. Cooley, F. W. Reilly, William Rutherford, Charles A. McDonald, David Bradley, J. J. Glessner, Edwin Lee Brown) reports on water-supply pollution and documents crib contamination [\[4\]](#src-4).
- **1886 (Jan 27):** City Council creates the **Drainage and Water Supply Commission**; Mayor Carter Harrison appoints **Rudolph Hering** chief engineer with Benezette Williams and Samuel G. Artingstall consulting [\[4\]](#src-4).
- **1887 (Jan):** Hering Commission preliminary report recommends a gravity dilution channel to the Des Plaines valley sized for the future metropolis [\[4\]](#src-4).
- **1889 (May 29):** General Assembly approves the Sanitary District Enabling Act (effective July 1) [\[15\]](#src-15)[\[17\]](#src-17).
- **1889 (Nov 5, most likely):** referendum creates the **Sanitary District of Chicago** — 70,958 for, 242 against [\[15\]](#src-15)[\[17\]](#src-17). Brown's (1894) narrative text quotes Judge Prendergast's October 14 order verbatim as setting the vote for "November 6, 1889," but Brown's own back-of-book chronological summary table gives "November 5, 1889" for the same event — the primary source disagrees with itself (see Open Questions). District 185 sq mi, reaching beyond city limits because "the natural drainage area did not conform to Chicago's political boundary lines" [\[4\]](#src-4)[\[7\]](#src-7).
- **1889 (Dec 12):** nine trustees elected — Murray Nelson, Richard Prendergast, Frank Wenter, John J. Altpeter, Arnold P. Gilmore, Christoph Hotz, John A. King, William H. Russell and Henry J. Willing; Nelson became the District's first president (Feb. 1–Dec. 2, 1890), succeeded by Prendergast [\[24\]](#src-24); first meeting **January 18, 1890**; Lyman E. Cooley chief engineer [\[4\]](#src-4).
- **1892 (Sept 3):** "Shovel Day" — Main Channel construction begins near Lemont [\[17\]](#src-17).
- **1896:** Mayor Swift halts new lake-outfall sewers (70th/73rd St.) and appoints a commission to plan **intercepting sewers** routing sewage to the river and future channel [\[6\]](#src-6).
- **1900 (Jan 2):** Main Drainage Channel opened; Main Stem and South Branch permanently reversed; 41.41 million cu yd excavated; cost $23,693,014 [\[15\]](#src-15)[\[17\]](#src-17).

## Current operations

The 1855-pattern combined sewers are not a museum piece: the City of Chicago's Department of Water Management still operates a combined sewer system whose oldest brick trunk lines date to the Chesbrough era, and by 1930 that system was described as "the most extensive in the world" [\[10\]](#src-10)[\[11\]](#src-11). Because combined sewers overflow in storms, the 19th-century design choice led directly to the MWRD's Tunnel and Reservoir Plan (TARP/"Deep Tunnel"), whose Phase I pollution-control tunnels went into service in 1985 to capture the overflows the Victorian system sends riverward [\[7\]](#src-7). The raised street grades remain a permanent feature of the Loop and near-neighborhood topography (vaulted sidewalks, below-grade original floors) [\[8\]](#src-8)[\[13\]](#src-13). The I&M Canal, sewage carrier of 1871–1900, ceased navigation in the 1930s and is today a National Heritage Corridor, while the Sanitary District's channel and the reversed river remain the region's operating disposal spine under the Metropolitan Water Reclamation District [\[7\]](#src-7)[\[15\]](#src-15)[\[18\]](#src-18). The Chicago Public Library's Chicago Sewers Collection (13.5 linear feet, 951 photographs, 121 glass-plate slides, including Chesbrough's 1855 plan) is the principal municipal archive of the early system [\[10\]](#src-10)[\[11\]](#src-11).

## Notable events & incidents

- **Cholera era (1849–1873):** epidemics in 1849, 1850 (420 deaths), 1851 (216), 1852 (630), 1854 (1,424 cholera deaths plus contiguous "diarrhea"/"dysentery" mortality) and 1866 (990 deaths, the next major outbreak after the Civil War) — cholera was killing hundreds of Chicagoans annually even before the sewer system existed [\[9\]](#src-9)[\[10\]](#src-10)[\[27\]](#src-27); dysentery killed ~1,600 between 1854 and 1860 [\[9\]](#src-9)[\[10\]](#src-10). Cholera's last Chicago appearance killed 116 in summer 1873 — after which "Chicago's cholera days were over" [\[9\]](#src-9).
- **Mortality turnaround:** citywide death rate fell from 53.9 per 1,000 (1854) to 18.8 (1860) as sewers, grade raising and water works advanced — a decline Chesbrough himself documented in April 1861 [\[2\]](#src-2)[\[4\]](#src-4).
- **The Great Fire (October 1871)** destroyed buildings connected with the works and municipal records but left the buried system essentially intact; the legislature's Oct 20, 1871 appropriation of $2,955,340 relieved the canal lien to help the burned-out city [\[4\]](#src-4).
- **The August 1885 storm** contaminated the water-supply crib for weeks and galvanized the sanitary-district movement [\[4\]](#src-4)[\[17\]](#src-17). **But the associated "epidemic" is a myth:** the oft-repeated claim that ~90,000 Chicagoans (12% of the city) died of cholera/typhoid after the 1885 storm has no basis — no cholera deaths occurred, typhoid deaths never exceeded 1,000 in any 1880s year, and researcher Libby Hill's work prompted a Chicago Tribune retraction on September 29, 2005. The tale traces to a 1941 remark by a sewer superintendent and a 1957 Sanitary District pamphlet [\[14\]](#src-14).
- **The real typhoid catastrophe came later:** 1891, when typhoid fever killed roughly 2,000 Chicagoans — about 174 per 100,000, the city's worst year — threatening the upcoming World's Columbian Exposition and underscoring that the canal-dilution regime had failed [\[9\]](#src-9)[\[20\]](#src-20).
- **Population outran everything:** Chicago grew from 29,963 (1850) to 112,172 (1860), 298,977 (1870), 503,185 (1880), 1,099,850 (1890) and 1,698,575 (1900) [\[19\]](#src-19); by 1893 roughly 1.5 million people drained to the river system, with 270,000 draining straight to the lake along 32 miles of shore [\[4\]](#src-4). Each engineering fix (1856 sewers, 1871 deep cut, 1880s pumps) was overwhelmed within a decade [\[7\]](#src-7).

## Open questions

- **Deep-cut cost figure — resolved as a genuine source-internal discrepancy, not OCR noise:** direct inspection of Brown's (1894) full text (not just the scanned page images) confirms both figures are actually printed in the book: $3,300,883.71 in the narrative describing the July 1871 completion (p. 91), and $3,800,883.71 in Brown's own summary table of canal construction costs 1836–1871 (p. 252, "1866 to 1871, By the City of Chicago"). The book itself is inconsistent. Only the Board of Public Works annual report for 1871 could adjudicate which is the audited figure [\[4\]](#src-4)[\[17\]](#src-17).
- **Sanitary District referendum date — most likely November 5, 1889:** MWRD and Chicagology both give Nov 5, and so does Brown's (1894) own chronological summary table; but Brown's narrative text, quoting Judge Prendergast's order verbatim, gives "November 6, 1889" for the same event — so Brown's book disagrees with itself. November 5, 1889 was the standard Tuesday general-election day that year, which favors Nov 5 as correct and "November 6" in Brown's narrative passage as a transcription error [\[4\]](#src-4)[\[15\]](#src-15)[\[17\]](#src-17).
- **Sewer mileage between 1861 and 1892 — partly closed, one more data point added:** the Board of Health's 1873 report (source [\[5\]](#src-5)) tabulates feet of sewer built annually from 1856 through 1872, giving cumulative totals of 850,563 ft (≈161.1 mi) at year-end 1871 (the Fire year) and 893,330 ft (≈169.2 mi) at year-end 1872. An 1891 Dept. of Public Works retrospective adds a further waypoint: 265.80 miles in place at year-end 1876 [\[22\]](#src-22). The gap between 1877 and Jan. 1, 1892 (888.3 mi) remains open; Board of Public Works / Dept. of Public Works annual reports on HathiTrust for the intervening years would fill the rest of the curve.
- **Pre-1855 wooden/plank sewers — resolved:** an 1891 Dept. of Public Works retrospective identifies Chicago's actual first sewers: triangular wooden box sewers built in 1850 along Clark, La Salle and Wells Streets, from the main river to the alleys south of Randolph Street, costing $2,871.90, paid entirely by special assessment on the property benefited — almost certainly the "old wooden sewers" the 1860 report says complicated construction [\[2\]](#src-2)[\[22\]](#src-22).
- **The 1855 plan's southern boundary street — resolved, not an OCR artifact:** an independent 1891 Dept. of Public Works retrospective glosses the same 1855 plan boundaries explicitly — "bounded ... on the west by (Reuben street) Ashland avenue, on the south by (North street) Sixteenth street" — confirming "North Street" (soon renamed Sixteenth Street) really was the plan's printed southern edge and the route of the proposed flushing canal, not a scanning error [\[1\]](#src-1)[\[22\]](#src-22).
- **Fullerton conduit completion date discrepancy** ("about 1870" in the 1911 WSE paper vs. Jan 9, 1880 in Brown) is resolved in favor of 1880; the likely source of the 1911 paper's error is that City Engineer Chesbrough first *recommended* building the Fullerton conduit in his report for the year ending March 31, 1870 — Brown's narrative makes clear the 1870 date is for the recommendation, not construction, which didn't begin until 1874 [\[4\]](#src-4)[\[6\]](#src-6).
- **1885 storm rainfall — resolved:** a 2024 National Weather Service/Illinois State Water Survey technical reanalysis using Hazen's 1893 compilation and contemporary Tribune reports gives 5.63 in. over 19 hours (Aug. 2–3) and 6.36 in. over the full two-day period — squarely between Brown's "nearly six inches" and Chicagology's 5½ inches, confirming both were essentially correct [\[4\]](#src-4)[\[17\]](#src-17)[\[26\]](#src-26).
- **New Bridgeport pumping works — completion-date discrepancy (open):** secondary engineering retrospectives (the 1911 WSE paper, Illinois DNR history) date completion to 1883–84, but D. W. Jackson's contemporary 1892 pamphlet states the pumps, contracted in August 1881, were not completed until 1885; that an April 1885 acceptance trial found them insufficient; and that they were found compliant — and even then only "in every essential respect," not fully — at a second trial in December 1886. It is possible pumping began at reduced capacity by 1883–84 while formal contract acceptance dragged on for years; Common Council or Board of Public Works annual reports for 1883–86 would settle the sequence [\[6\]](#src-6)[\[18\]](#src-18)[\[23\]](#src-23).
